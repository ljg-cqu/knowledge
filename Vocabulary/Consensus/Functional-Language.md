1. **Functional Expression**: I'd like to suggest that... (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to propose an idea or course of action for consideration.
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I propose that..., May I suggest that..., I would recommend that..., Perhaps we could...
   
   **Antonyms**: I insist that..., You must..., I demand that...
   
   **When to Use**: Professional settings, meetings, collaborative discussions, when proposing ideas diplomatically
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Emergency situations requiring direct commands; very casual conversations (use "How about..." instead); when making demands
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd like to propose that...
   - I would suggest that...
   - May I suggest that...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses negative politeness strategy (minimizing imposition); softens suggestion through conditional "would" and personal framing "I'd like to"; respects others' autonomy while contributing ideas
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's a good idea / I think that could work
   - I'm not sure that would be feasible / I have some concerns about that
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd like to suggest that we meet earlier next time.
   - I'd like to suggest that everyone review the document before we discuss it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'd like to suggest that we establish a working group to address these concerns.
   - I'd like to suggest that the committee consider alternative implementation frameworks.

1. **Functional Expression**: Would it be possible to...? (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A very polite way to make a request, asking if something can be done.
   - *Formality*: formal/very formal
   - *Directness*: very indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Could we possibly..., Would you be able to..., Is it possible to..., Might we...
   
   **Antonyms**: Do this, You need to..., I require that you...
   
   **When to Use**: Formal settings, making requests to superiors or unfamiliar people, when high politeness is required
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts with friends (too formal); emergency situations; when directness is required
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would it be feasible to...
   - Could it be possible to...
   - Is it possible to...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: High negative politeness strategy; uses question form to minimize imposition; conditional "would" adds distance and politeness; frames request as inquiry about possibility rather than direct demand
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, certainly / Of course, that would be fine
   - I'm afraid that won't be possible / That might be difficult, because...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would it be possible to reschedule our meeting?
   - Would it be possible to get everyone's input on this?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would it be possible to extend the consultation period to ensure comprehensive stakeholder engagement?
   - Would it be possible to revisit this provision in light of the concerns raised?

1. **Functional Expression**: I see your point, but... (function: disagreeing/hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A diplomatic way to acknowledge someone's viewpoint before expressing disagreement or a different perspective.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I understand what you're saying, however..., That's a fair point, but..., I appreciate that perspective, though..., While I see your reasoning...
   
   **Antonyms**: You're wrong, That's incorrect, I disagree completely, That doesn't make sense
   
   **When to Use**: Discussions, debates, meetings, when disagreeing respectfully, maintaining positive relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When complete agreement is required; very formal academic writing (too conversational); when strong disagreement is appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I understand your point, but...
   - I see what you mean, however...
   - That's a good point, although...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness strategy; acknowledges and validates other's perspective before disagreement; "but" signals upcoming contrast; softens disagreement to maintain face and relationship
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, I understand your concern as well
   - Perhaps we can find a way to address both perspectives
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I see your point, but I think we need more time to decide.
   - I see your point, but have you considered the cost implications?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I see your point regarding implementation timelines, but I believe we must balance speed with thoroughness.
   - I see your point about efficiency; however, we must also ensure inclusive stakeholder engagement.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's explore the possibility of... (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A collaborative way to suggest investigating or considering an option without committing to it.
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's consider..., How about we look into..., We might want to explore..., Perhaps we could examine...
   
   **Antonyms**: We must do..., The only option is..., I've decided that...
   
   **When to Use**: Meetings, collaborative planning, brainstorming, when proposing ideas for consideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When firm decisions are needed; situations requiring immediate action; when exploration isn't appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's look into the possibility of...
   - Let's consider the option of...
   - We could explore...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Inclusive language using "let's" promotes collaboration; "explore" and "possibility" keep options open without commitment; reduces perceived risk of suggestion; positive politeness through inclusion
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's worth exploring / Let's do that
   - I'm not sure that's the right direction / What specifically would that involve?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's explore the possibility of meeting monthly instead of weekly.
   - Let's explore the possibility of combining the two proposals.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let's explore the possibility of establishing a joint working committee to address these concerns.
   - Let's explore the possibility of a phased implementation approach to accommodate diverse stakeholder needs.

1. **Functional Expression**: I was wondering if we could... (function: requesting/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A very tentative and polite way to make a request or suggestion.
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: very indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I was thinking perhaps we could..., Would it be possible to..., Could we perhaps..., I'd like to ask if...
   
   **Antonyms**: We need to..., You must..., I require that...
   
   **When to Use**: Polite contexts, tentative suggestions, when high diplomacy is needed, uncertain proposals
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Assertive situations; urgent matters; very casual contexts (too tentative); when confidence is important
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I was thinking we could...
   - I was wondering whether we might...
   - I wonder if we could...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Past progressive ("was wondering") adds distance and tentativeness; question form minimizes imposition; highly indirect approach respects autonomy; appropriate when power distance exists
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, I think that would work / Sure, let's do that
   - I'm not sure that's feasible / Let me think about that
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was wondering if we could postpone the deadline a bit.
   - I was wondering if we could revisit that decision.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I was wondering if we could schedule additional consultation sessions to address outstanding concerns.
   - I was wondering if we could consider alternative governance mechanisms that might accommodate diverse perspectives.

1. **Functional Expression**: If I may add... (function: interrupting/contributing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to interrupt or add to a discussion; requesting permission to contribute.
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: May I add..., If I could just say..., May I contribute..., Could I add something...
   
   **Antonyms**: *interrupts without acknowledgment*, Hold on..., Listen...
   
   **When to Use**: Formal meetings, professional discussions, when adding to ongoing conversation, panel discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations with friends (too formal); when you have the floor; emergencies
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - If I may interject...
   - If I could add something...
   - May I add a point...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conditional "if I may" requests permission; shows respect for current speaker; acknowledges turn-taking norms; appropriate for formal settings and power-distance situations
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Of course, please go ahead
   - *speaker yields floor*
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If I may add, we should also consider the timing.
   - If I may add something here, there's another option we haven't discussed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If I may add, the stakeholder feedback indicated concerns regarding implementation timelines.
   - If I may add a perspective from the working group, we identified several alternative approaches.

1. **Functional Expression**: Just to clarify... (function: clarifying/checking)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A phrase used to ensure understanding or to explain something more clearly.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To clarify..., Let me make sure I understand..., For clarification..., To be clear...
   
   **Antonyms**: *no direct antonym—related to obscuring rather than clarifying*
   
   **When to Use**: Any context where clarity is needed, meetings, confirming understanding, explaining details
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When clarity exists; when used excessively (becomes filler); in highly emotional situations (may seem dismissive)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just to be clear...
   - For clarification...
   - Let me clarify...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Just" softens the clarification request; signals that misunderstanding may exist; can be face-saving for both parties; neutral formality makes it widely applicable
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, that's correct / Actually, what I meant was...
   - *provides clarification*
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just to clarify, we're meeting next Tuesday, right?
   - Just to clarify, does everyone need to vote, or just the committee members?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Just to clarify, the consensus applies to strategic principles but not implementation details, correct?
   - Just to clarify the stakeholder position, are you requesting additional consultation or proposing alternative frameworks?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm afraid I have to disagree (function: disagreeing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite and formal way to express disagreement; softens the impact of disagreement.
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I respectfully disagree, I must disagree, I have a different view, I see it differently
   
   **Antonyms**: I completely agree, You're absolutely right, That's correct
   
   **When to Use**: Professional settings, formal disagreements, when maintaining relationship is important, respectful debates
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts (too formal); when strong disagreement is appropriate; with close friends (unnecessarily distant)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm afraid I don't agree
   - I'm afraid I see it differently
   - I'm afraid I must disagree
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I'm afraid" expresses regret about disagreement; softens face-threatening act; shows respect while maintaining position; "have to" suggests obligation rather than personal opposition
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I respect your perspective / I understand your concerns
   - Could you explain your reasoning? / What are your main concerns?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm afraid I have to disagree with that assessment.
   - I'm afraid I have to disagree about the timeline—I think we need more time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'm afraid I have to disagree with the proposed implementation framework, as it may not adequately address stakeholder concerns.
   - I'm afraid I have to disagree with that interpretation of the consensus agreement.

1. **Functional Expression**: Could we hear from...? (function: inviting contribution)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to request input from a specific person or group; facilitates inclusive discussion.
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Would it be possible to hear from..., Can we get input from..., I'd like to invite... to share..., Perhaps... could share their perspective
   
   **Antonyms**: *direct command: Tell us...*
   
   **When to Use**: Meetings, group discussions, facilitating participation, ensuring all voices are heard
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When person doesn't have relevant input; when putting someone on the spot inappropriately; very casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could we hear [name]'s perspective?
   - Could we get input from...
   - Can we hear from...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Question form is polite request rather than command; acknowledges value of person's input; facilitates inclusive discussion; appropriate for facilitators or leaders
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *invited person speaks*
   - Yes, I'd like to hear that perspective as well
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could we hear from the marketing team on this?
   - Could we hear from everyone before making a decision?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could we hear from the minority stakeholder representatives regarding their concerns?
   - Could we hear from the technical working group before finalizing the framework?

1. **Functional Expression**: I take your point (function: acknowledging/conceding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An expression acknowledging that someone has made a valid argument or observation.
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I see your point, I understand your argument, That's a fair point, I acknowledge that
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't see the relevance, That doesn't make sense, I reject that argument
   
   **When to Use**: Debates, discussions, meetings, when acknowledging valid arguments, showing receptiveness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you completely disagree; very casual contexts (somewhat formal); when concession is inappropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I take that point
   - Point taken
   - I see your point
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows active listening and respect for others' views; doesn't necessarily indicate full agreement; can precede further discussion or counter-arguments; builds rapport
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you for understanding / I appreciate that
   - So can we move forward on this basis?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I take your point about the cost, but we also need to consider quality.
   - I take your point—we should include more people in the discussion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I take your point regarding implementation challenges, and we should address those in the revised framework.
   - I take your point about the need for additional consultation before finalizing the decision.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let me play devil's advocate (function: presenting counter-argument)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A phrase used to signal that you will present an opposing or challenging viewpoint for the sake of discussion, not necessarily because you believe it.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Let me offer a different perspective, Let me challenge that assumption, To consider the other side, Playing devil's advocate...
   
   **Antonyms**: I completely agree, That's absolutely right, No question about it
   
   **When to Use**: Brainstorming, testing ideas, encouraging critical thinking, exploring alternatives, group discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When counter-arguments might be offensive; with sensitive topics; when consensus is fragile; very formal settings (too colloquial)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Playing devil's advocate here...
   - Let me offer a counterpoint...
   - To play devil's advocate...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals that following argument is exploratory, not personal position; protects face by distancing speaker from argument; encourages critical thinking; traditional phrase with specific discourse function
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's a good point to consider / I see what you mean
   - Yes, we should think about that possibility
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me play devil's advocate—what if the plan doesn't work as expected?
   - Let me play devil's advocate: couldn't we just use the existing system?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let me play devil's advocate: have we adequately considered the implementation risks associated with this accelerated timeline?
   - *In very formal contexts, prefer: "Let me present an alternative perspective"*

1. **Functional Expression**: Can we table this for now? (function: postponing/deferring)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A request to postpone discussion of a topic for later consideration.
   - *Note: In American English means "postpone"; in British English can mean "bring forward for discussion"*
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Can we postpone this discussion?, Can we come back to this later?, Could we defer this?, Shall we revisit this later?
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's decide this now, We need to resolve this immediately, We must address this today
   
   **When to Use**: Meetings when discussion is unproductive, when more information is needed, time constraints, managing agendas
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For urgent matters; when repeatedly avoiding difficult topics; cross-cultural contexts (British/American difference)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Can we come back to this?
   - Can we defer this discussion?
   - Should we table this?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Question form softens directive; "for now" implies temporary postponement; useful for managing difficult discussions diplomatically; be aware of British/American difference in meaning
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, let's move on / I think we should resolve this first
   - When should we revisit this?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we table this for now and discuss it next week?
   - Let's table this for now—we're not making progress.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given time constraints, can we table this item for now and revisit it at the next meeting?
   - Can we table this discussion pending additional stakeholder consultation?
