1. **Preposition**: according to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* As stated, reported, or described by a particular source or authority.
   - *(Abstract)* In a way that agrees with a rule, plan, or method.
   
   **Synonyms**: as reported by, as described in, in line with, in accordance with (more formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, against, in conflict with
   
   **When to Use**: In research writing when attributing information to a source ("according to Smith, 2020") or describing actions that follow a protocol ("according to the procedure").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overusing it at the start of every sentence; vary attribution with "Smith (2020) argues that...".
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: according to the data; according to prior research; according to the survey
   - **Verb + prep**: reported according to; classified according to
   - **Adjective + prep**: consistent according to (rare; prefer "consistent with")
   - **Noun + prep**: estimates according to the model
   
   **Root Analysis**: accord (agreement) + -ing (present participle) + to (preposition indicating reference)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French 'acorder' (to agree). "According to" became a fixed phrase meaning "as stated by" in Middle English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to the survey, most people prefer shorter emails.
   - According to our logs, hardly anyone used that feature.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to prior meta‑analyses, effect sizes in this domain are typically small.
   - Outcomes were categorized according to established diagnostic criteria.

1. **Preposition**: in line with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In agreement or consistent with something, especially previous findings or standards.
   
   **Synonyms**: consistent with, in accordance with, in keeping with, aligned with
   
   **Antonyms**: at odds with, inconsistent with, contrary to
   
   **When to Use**: When comparing your results or decisions to prior work, guidelines, or expectations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very informal speech where "matches" or "fits" is easier; do not use when there is actually a clear conflict.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in line with previous studies; in line with expectations; in line with the theoretical model
   - **Verb + prep**: remain in line with; bring into line with
   - **Noun + prep**: policy in line with regulations
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (inside) + line (alignment) + with (association)
   
   **Etymology**: From physical alignment in a straight line; extended to abstract agreement in Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our results are pretty much in line with what others have found.
   - The new numbers are in line with our earlier estimates.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These findings are broadly in line with previous longitudinal studies.
   - The analysis plan was developed in line with pre‑registered protocols.

1. **Preposition**: with respect to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning, regarding, or in relation to a specific aspect.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, in relation to, as regards
   
   **Antonyms**: irrespective of, regardless of (ignoring that aspect)
   
   **When to Use**: When narrowing a statement to a particular variable, subgroup, or dimension in academic writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very informal oral language; "about" or "regarding" is simpler there.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with respect to time; with respect to gender; with respect to performance
   - **Verb + prep**: vary with respect to; differ with respect to
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (regarding) + respect (regard) + to (direction/referring)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin 'respectus' (regard, consideration). The phrase "with respect to" is common in formal English, especially in mathematics and research.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The two groups didn't differ much with respect to age.
   - We saw big changes with respect to how often people used the app.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - No significant differences were observed with respect to gender or prior experience.
   - The model parameters were estimated with respect to maximum likelihood.

1. **Preposition**: based on (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Using something as the foundation, evidence, or starting point for a decision or conclusion.
   
   **Synonyms**: grounded in, derived from, informed by, built on
   
   **Antonyms**: independent of, ignoring, contrary to
   
   **When to Use**: When stating what evidence or theory supports a claim, design choice, or interpretation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid stacking many "based on" phrases; vary with "drawing on," "using," or "given" for style.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: based on these results; based on prior work; based on the survey data
   - **Verb + prep**: decide based on; conclude based on
   - **Noun + prep**: decision based on evidence
   
   **Root Analysis**: base (foundation) + -ed (having) + on (preposition of support)
   
   **Etymology**: From "base" (foundation). "Based on" became a frequent multi‑word preposition in 20th‑century English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Based on the pilot, we cut three questions from the survey.
   - We changed the design based on user feedback.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Based on these findings, we recommend a staged rollout of the intervention.
   - Inclusion criteria were defined based on established clinical guidelines.

1. **Preposition**: in terms of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* From the point of view of a particular measure, category, or aspect.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, in relation to, as measured by, with respect to
   
   **Antonyms**: irrespective of, regardless of (ignoring that measure)
   
   **When to Use**: When specifying the dimension along which you are comparing groups or outcomes (e.g., in terms of accuracy, cost, or time).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid vague overuse ("in terms of, like...") in speech; be specific about which aspect you mean.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in terms of accuracy; in terms of sample size; in terms of practical impact
   - **Verb + prep**: differ in terms of; improve in terms of
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (inside) + terms (conditions, ways of describing) + of (genitive preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: "Terms" from Latin 'terminus' (limit, boundary). The phrase "in terms of" is common in academic and technical English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new method is better in terms of speed but worse in terms of clarity.
   - In terms of effort, this version is much heavier for the team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The two algorithms differ primarily in terms of computational complexity.
   - In terms of practical significance, the observed effect is relatively small.
