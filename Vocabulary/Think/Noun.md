1. **Noun**: thought (C/U, pl: thoughts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A single idea, opinion, or mental image that appears in your mind.
   - *(U)* The process of thinking in general, especially careful or serious mental activity.
   
   **Synonyms**: idea, notion, reflection, consideration, mental image
   
   **Antonyms**: action, deed, forgetfulness, mindlessness
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about individual ideas you have, or about thinking as a process (deep thought, careful thought) in everyday, academic, or professional contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you need a more specific word like decision, plan, or belief; do not overuse in formal writing where precise terms (hypothesis, conclusion, argument) are clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deep thought
   - train of thought
   - random thought
   - thought process
   - give something some thought
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English root related to *think*; no clear modern prefix or suffix.
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *þoht* / *gehðoht* (the act of thinking, a thought), related to the verb *think*; used for both individual ideas and the general activity of thinking since early English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I had a strange thought while I was on the bus this morning.
   - Give it some thought before you say yes to the offer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author traces the development of philosophical thought from antiquity to the present.
   - Interruptions can significantly disrupt a person’s train of thought during complex problem‑solving.

1. **Noun**: idea (C, pl: ideas)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A suggestion, plan, or possible course of action that you think of.
   - A concept or mental picture of something, especially as part of a broader way of thinking.
   
   **Synonyms**: concept, notion, proposal, suggestion, insight
   
   **Antonyms**: ignorance, confusion, emptiness (of mind), misunderstanding
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about specific suggestions, creative proposals, or mental pictures in casual, professional, or academic discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing where more precise terms like hypothesis, construct, or framework are needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - come up with an idea
   - a brilliant / terrible idea
   - have no idea
   - develop an idea
   - main idea
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Greek *idea* (form, pattern); no modern prefix or suffix.
   
   **Etymology**: Greek → from *idea* (form, appearance, kind of thing) via Latin and French into English; later used broadly for mental concepts and creative suggestions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That’s a great idea—let’s try it tomorrow.
   - I have no idea what he’s thinking right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The central idea of the theory is that small changes in assumptions can lead to very different outcomes.
   - Innovative ideas often emerge when people have time and space to think freely.

1. **Noun**: concept (C, pl: concepts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An abstract idea or general notion that helps you organize or understand something.
   - *(In academic and technical writing)* A clearly defined mental construct used to build theories or models.
   
   **Synonyms**: notion, construct, principle, abstraction, mental model
   
   **Antonyms**: concrete example, detail, instance, fact (in contrast to abstraction)
   
   **When to Use**: In academic, technical, and professional contexts when discussing abstract ideas, theoretical frameworks, or key mental categories.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech where idea sounds more natural; do not use when you refer to a physical object rather than an abstraction.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - key concept
   - abstract concept
   - understand a concept
   - concept formation
   - concept map
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + cept (taken, seized) from Latin *concipere* (to take in, conceive).
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *conceptus* (something conceived), related to *concipere*; came to mean “general idea” or “notion” in philosophical and educational writing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The concept of infinity is hard to really imagine.
   - Kids often struggle with the concept of time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Critical thinking requires a clear grasp of core concepts such as evidence, inference, and validity.
   - The study introduces a new concept to describe how teams share mental models.

1. **Noun**: insight (C/U, pl: insights)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A clear, deep understanding of a complicated problem or situation.
   - *(C)* A specific, often sudden realization that reveals something previously hidden or unclear.
   
   **Synonyms**: understanding, realization, illumination, perception, intuition
   
   **Antonyms**: misunderstanding, blindness, ignorance, confusion
   
   **When to Use**: When describing moments of clarity, valuable observations, or deep understanding in personal reflection, research, strategy, and coaching.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for simple facts or surface‑level observations; do not use when no real depth of thought is involved.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - gain insight into something
   - valuable insight
   - deep insight
   - offer insights
   - insightful comment
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + sight (seeing) → metaphorically “seeing into.”
   
   **Etymology**: Old English roots for *in* and *sight*; the mental sense of “inner vision, deep understanding” developed in Middle and Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Talking with her gave me a lot of insight into how the team really feels.
   - That book had a few surprising insights about how we make decisions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis provides new insights into how experts organize their knowledge.
   - Qualitative interviews generated rich insights into participants’ thinking processes.

1. **Noun**: perspective (C, pl: perspectives)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A particular way of seeing or thinking about something; a point of view.
   - A mental position that shapes how someone interprets information or experiences.
   
   **Synonyms**: viewpoint, standpoint, angle, outlook, point of view
   
   **Antonyms**: narrowness, one‑sided view, bias (in the sense of distortion)
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing different ways of looking at a situation, especially in problem‑solving, conflict resolution, design, and cross‑cultural communication.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you simply mean opinion without reference to the underlying way of seeing; do not confuse with physical perspective in art unless the context is visual.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - from my perspective
   - different perspectives
   - gain perspective
   - see things from someone’s perspective
   - put something in perspective
   
   **Root Analysis**: per- (through) + spect (look) → “to look through.”
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *perspectiva* (science of optics), from *perspicere* (to look through); later extended metaphorically to mental points of view.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Talking to people from other teams really changed my perspective.
   - From his perspective, the decision made perfect sense.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The paper compares three theoretical perspectives on human decision‑making.
   - Cross‑cultural perspectives can reveal hidden assumptions in our thinking.

1. **Noun**: assumption (C, pl: assumptions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that you accept as true without proof, often unconsciously, and then use as a basis for your thinking.
   - *(In logic and research)* A starting belief or condition that is taken for granted in an argument, model, or study.
   
   **Synonyms**: presumption, belief, premise, supposition, taken‑for‑granted idea
   
   **Antonyms**: verified fact, evidence, proof, tested conclusion
   
   **When to Use**: When analyzing arguments, models, and everyday thinking, especially when you want to question what is being taken for granted.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual talk where guess or belief is clearer; do not use when something has already been tested and supported by strong evidence.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - challenge an assumption
   - hidden assumptions
   - underlying assumptions
   - make an assumption
   - base something on an assumption
   
   **Root Analysis**: as- (toward) + sume (take) from Latin *assumere* (to take up, adopt).
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *assumptio* (a taking up), via Old French into English; the logical and critical‑thinking sense of “taken‑for‑granted belief” became central in modern philosophy and science.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I realized I was working with the wrong assumption about what the customer wanted.
   - Don’t make assumptions about what people are thinking—ask them.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The model relies on several simplifying assumptions about human rationality.
   - Critical thinking involves identifying and examining the assumptions that shape our reasoning.

1. **Noun**: reasoning (U, also C for specific arguments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The process of thinking about something in a logical, organized way in order to reach a conclusion.
   - *(C)* A set of reasons or arguments that explain why someone thinks something.
   
   **Synonyms**: logic, argumentation, line of thought, rationale, thought process
   
   **Antonyms**: irrationality, guesswork, emotional reaction, illogic
   
   **When to Use**: When focusing on how people move from evidence to conclusions in everyday life, problem‑solving, mathematics, law, and philosophy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you only mean feeling or intuition without structured thought; do not confuse with simple calculation or memorization.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - logical reasoning
   - flawed reasoning
   - explain your reasoning
   - reasoning skills
   - line of reasoning
   
   **Root Analysis**: reason (thinking, explanation) + -ing (process or activity).
   
   **Etymology**: Developed in English from the noun *reason*; used widely in philosophy, mathematics, and education to describe structured thought.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don’t follow your reasoning—can you walk me through it again?
   - Her reasoning was solid, even if I didn’t like the conclusion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines the reasoning strategies students use when solving complex problems.
   - Transparent reasoning helps readers evaluate whether the conclusions are justified.

1. **Noun**: bias (C/U, pl: biases)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A tendency to think in a particular way that is not neutral or fair, often without realizing it.
   - *(In psychology and decision science)* A systematic deviation in thinking that leads to predictable errors in judgment.
   
   **Synonyms**: prejudice, inclination, slant, distortion, predisposition
   
   **Antonyms**: fairness, neutrality, objectivity, open‑mindedness
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing thinking errors, unfair opinions, or systematic distortions in judgment in everyday life, social issues, and cognitive science.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you simply mean preference without unfairness (use preference or taste); do not accuse people of bias lightly in sensitive contexts without evidence.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unconscious bias
   - confirmation bias
   - cognitive bias
   - biased thinking
   - reduce bias
   
   **Root Analysis**: Possibly from Old French *biais* (slant, slope), no clear prefix/suffix in modern English.
   
   **Etymology**: Old French → from *biais* (slant, sideways), originally used in games and textiles; later adopted in English to describe mental “slant” or unfair leaning in thought.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We all have some bias in how we think about other people.
   - Try to notice your own bias before you judge the situation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The paper reviews common cognitive biases that influence human decision‑making.
   - Training programs aim to raise awareness of implicit bias in hiring and evaluation processes.

1. **Noun**: mindset (C, pl: mindsets)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A set of attitudes or fixed ideas that someone has and that strongly influences how they think and behave.
   - A general mental framework or way of approaching problems and experiences.
   
   **Synonyms**: attitude, mentality, way of thinking, outlook, cognitive frame
   
   **Antonyms**: open perspective (for rigid mindsets), flexibility, growth (in contrast to fixed mindset)
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about stable patterns in how people think—such as growth vs. fixed mindset—in education, coaching, personal development, and leadership.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very technical writing where more specific constructs (schema, belief system) are required; do not use when describing a single momentary thought.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - growth mindset
   - fixed mindset
   - change your mindset
   - adopt a new mindset
   - mindset shift
   
   **Root Analysis**: mind (mental activity) + set (established position or arrangement).
   
   **Etymology**: English compound; became especially popular in psychology and education in the late 20th and early 21st centuries, influenced by research on growth vs. fixed mindsets.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With the right mindset, it’s easier to learn from your mistakes.
   - She has a very fixed mindset about what success looks like.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Interventions that target students’ mindsets can influence their persistence and learning strategies.
   - Leadership mindsets shape how managers interpret challenges and make decisions.
