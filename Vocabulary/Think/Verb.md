1. **Verb**: think
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T/I)* To use your mind to form ideas, make judgments, or consider something carefully.
   - *(I)* To have a particular opinion or belief about a person, situation, or question.
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, believe, suppose, reckon, contemplate
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, dismiss, forget, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Everyday and professional contexts when you talk about using your mind, forming opinions, or processing information.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing when a more precise verb is needed (use analyze, evaluate, examine); avoid for brief physical actions where notice or see is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - think about a problem
   - think of an idea
   - think clearly
   - think ahead
   - think differently
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English root with no transparent modern prefix or suffix; related to the idea of strong emotion and mental activity.
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English thencan (to conceive in the mind, consider), related to thought and thank; core verb for mental activity in Germanic languages.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need some time to think before I answer that question.
   - Do you think this plan will actually work?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were asked to think silently about the scenario before rating their agreement.
   - When managers think strategically, they evaluate both short‑term results and long‑term consequences.

1. **Verb**: reflect
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(I)* To think carefully and deeply about something, especially in order to understand it or learn from it.
   - *(T)* To show or express a particular quality, attitude, or result.
   
   **Synonyms**: contemplate, mull over, ponder, consider, review
   
   **Antonyms**: rush, react impulsively, disregard, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing slow, careful thinking about experiences, decisions, or information; common in learning, leadership, and personal development contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for quick, surface‑level thinking (use think or consider); do not use when talking about light or images bouncing off a surface in a metaphor‑free context unless you mean the physical sense.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reflect on your mistakes
   - reflect on past experience
   - take time to reflect
   - reflective practice
   - reflect deeply
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + flect (bend) → literally “bend back.”
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin reflectere (to bend back), via Late Latin and Old French into English; the mental sense of “think back over something” developed from the physical image.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the meeting, I took a walk to reflect on what everyone had said.
   - She likes to reflect on her day before going to sleep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students were encouraged to reflect on how the feedback influenced their final projects.
   - Reflective journals can help practitioners think critically about their professional decisions.

1. **Verb**: consider
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T)* To think about something carefully, especially before making a decision.
   - *(T)* To regard someone or something in a particular way.
   
   **Synonyms**: contemplate, weigh, examine, review, regard
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, discount, reject without thought, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: When highlighting deliberate, often decision‑oriented thinking in everyday, professional, or academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech when a lighter verb like think about sounds more natural; do not use when no real thought is involved.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - carefully consider your options
   - consider the consequences
   - consider doing something
   - consider X as Y
   - be considered important
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + sider (star) in Latin, originally linked to observing the stars for guidance.
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin considerare (to look at closely, observe the stars), later “examine, reflect on” → Old French → English; now the neutral verb for deliberate thought before action.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’ll consider your suggestion and get back to you tomorrow.
   - Have you considered talking to a mentor about this?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before implementing any policy change, organizations must consider its impact on different stakeholders.
   - The study considers three alternative explanations for the observed trend.

1. **Verb**: reason
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(I)* To think in a logical way in order to reach a conclusion or judgment.
   - *(T)* To work something out by applying logic or evidence.
   
   **Synonyms**: infer, deduce, logically think, work out, rationalize
   
   **Antonyms**: guess blindly, assume without evidence, react emotionally
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing logical, step‑by‑step thinking, especially in problem‑solving, philosophy, mathematics, or debate.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very informal talk where think it through or figure out sounds more natural; do not use for purely emotional reactions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reason logically
   - reason from evidence
   - reason your way through a problem
   - ability to reason
   - reason things out
   
   **Root Analysis**: reason (noun: thinking, explanation) used as a verb; from Latin ratio (reckoning, calculation).
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from ratio (reckoning, understanding) via Old French raison into English; verb use developed to describe the act of using rational thought.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Give me a minute to reason this out before we decide.
   - Kids learn to reason about cause and effect as they grow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were asked to reason through a series of logic puzzles under time pressure.
   - The ability to reason abstractly is strongly linked to performance on complex problem‑solving tasks.

1. **Verb**: analyze
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T)* To examine something in detail in order to understand it or explain it.
   - *(In thinking and research)* To break information into parts, identify patterns, and draw reasoned conclusions.
   
   **Synonyms**: examine, break down, evaluate, scrutinize, dissect
   
   **Antonyms**: skim, overlook, summarize only, misinterpret
   
   **When to Use**: When focusing on systematic, often data‑driven or logical thinking about ideas, arguments, or problems in academic, professional, or technical settings.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech where look at or go through is more natural; do not use when no detailed thinking is involved.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - analyze an argument
   - analyze a situation
   - critically analyze
   - analyze patterns in data
   - analyze pros and cons
   
   **Root Analysis**: ana- (up, throughout) + lys- (loosen, break apart) + -e (verb ending in English).
   
   **Etymology**: Greek → from analysis (a breaking up) → French analyser → English analyze; strongly associated with systematic, rational thinking.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let’s analyze what went wrong before we try again.
   - She likes to analyze every decision in detail.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We analyzed participants’ thinking processes using protocol analysis.
   - The report analyzes how different assumptions shape strategic decisions.

1. **Verb**: brainstorm
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(I/T)* To generate many ideas quickly, usually in a group, without judging them at first.
   - *(I)* To think freely and creatively about possible solutions or options.
   
   **Synonyms**: generate ideas, ideate, think freely, come up with ideas, bounce ideas around
   
   **Antonyms**: block creativity, shut down ideas, over‑filter, dismiss ideas
   
   **When to Use**: In creative and collaborative thinking contexts such as workshops, meetings, design sessions, and problem‑solving discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing; do not use when you mean careful evaluation rather than free idea generation.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - brainstorm ideas
   - brainstorm solutions
   - group brainstorm
   - quick brainstorm
   - brainstorm together
   
   **Root Analysis**: brain (mind) + storm (sudden, intense activity); English compound.
   
   **Etymology**: English → originally used for a sudden, sometimes confusing idea; later adopted in business and creativity contexts to describe intense group idea generation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let’s brainstorm some ideas for the presentation after lunch.
   - We spent an hour brainstorming ways to make the app easier to use.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Teams were asked to brainstorm potential solutions before moving to structured evaluation.
   - In the ideation phase, participants brainstorm a wide range of options without immediate criticism.
