1. **Functional Expression**: What do you think about (…)? (function: asking for an opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Invites the listener to share their thoughts, evaluation, or judgment on a specific topic.
   - *Formality*: neutral/informal (suitable for most everyday and workplace situations).
   - *Directness*: direct but polite; signals genuine interest in the other person’s view.
   
   **Synonyms**: What’s your view on (…)? , How do you feel about (…)? , I’d like to hear your thoughts on (…); Could I get your opinion on (…)?
   
   **Antonyms**: I don’t care what you think. , It doesn’t matter what you think. (shutting down opinion), Just do it. (ignoring input)
   
   **When to Use**: In conversations, meetings, and feedback sessions when you genuinely want someone’s perspective before deciding; useful for collaborative thinking, coaching, and checking alignment.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you have already made a firm decision and cannot change it (can sound fake); in very formal speeches or documents (prefer: We would value your opinion on …).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - more formal version: What is your view on (…)?
   - less formal version: So, what do you think?
   - emphatic version: I really want to know what you think about (…).
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive‑politeness strategy that shows respect for the other person’s thinking; can soften authority differences by treating the listener as a partner in reasoning.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I think it’s a good idea because (…).
   - From my point of view, (…).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What do you think about taking a day just to slow down and think things through?
   - What do you think about his way of solving problems?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - What do you think about including a short reflection session after each workshop?
   - From a strategic perspective, what do you think about investing more in critical‑thinking training?

1. **Functional Expression**: I tend to think (that) … (function: expressing a cautious opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Signals a personal view or belief while leaving room for other perspectives and for changing your mind.
   - *Formality*: neutral (appropriate in professional and academic discussion, as well as everyday talk).
   - *Directness*: indirect; softens the strength of the statement and reduces potential conflict.
   
   **Synonyms**: My view is that … , I’m inclined to think that … , I generally believe that … , It seems to me that …
   
   **Antonyms**: I’m absolutely sure that … , There’s no doubt that … (strong, categorical claims)
   
   **When to Use**: When offering your thinking in discussions, meetings, or writing but signalling openness to other views; useful in collaborative reasoning and critical‑thinking contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you must state a firm conclusion based on strong evidence (better: The evidence shows that …); in high‑stakes decisions where uncertainty must be clearly quantified rather than vaguely hedged.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - more formal version: I am inclined to think that …
   - less formal version: I kinda/sort of think that …
   - emphatic version: I still tend to think that … even after hearing the counter‑arguments.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Classic hedging device in English; protects face by avoiding direct confrontation and acknowledges that your thinking is provisional, which is valued in critical, evidence‑based discussion.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: I tend to think that you’re right about this. / I tend to think that this approach makes sense.
   - Disagreeing: I see why you tend to think that, but I look at it a bit differently.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tend to think that people learn best when they talk through their ideas.
   - I tend to think too much before I make even small decisions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Based on the data, we tend to think that reflection time improves decision quality.
   - I tend to think that students benefit from explicitly practising their thinking processes.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let me think for a second. (function: buying time / managing turn‑taking)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely asks for a short pause to organize your thoughts before answering or deciding.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral (works in conversation and many meetings; can be softened in very formal contexts).
   - *Directness*: direct about your need to pause, but polite and self‑focused (I‑message).
   
   **Synonyms**: Give me a moment to think. , Let me just think this through. , I need a second to process that. , Allow me a moment to consider this.
   
   **Antonyms**: I’ll answer right away. , Off the top of my head, … (immediate response), No need to think about it. (refusing to reflect)
   
   **When to Use**: In Q&A sessions, meetings, coaching conversations, or interviews when a question is complex or sensitive and you want to answer thoughtfully instead of reacting.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In emergencies where quick action is required; with impatient speakers who may interpret long pauses as lack of preparation (briefen or rephrase: This is an important question—may I take a moment to think?).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - more formal version: This is an important point; may I take a moment to think?
   - less formal version: Uh, let me think for a sec.
   - emphatic version: Let me really think about that for a moment before I answer.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Useful self‑management strategy to protect thinking quality; also signals to others that you take the question seriously. In some cultures, silence while thinking is respected; in others, it helps to verbalize this request.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - After pause (answering): Thanks. I think the main issue is (…).
   - Requesting more time: I’ll need longer to think this through—can I get back to you later today?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me think for a second… Okay, I’d probably talk to my manager first.
   - Hmm, let me think for a second before I give you advice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let me think for a second. The most relevant example might be last year’s project review.
   - Let me think for a moment about how this impacts our overall strategy.
