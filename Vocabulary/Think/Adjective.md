1. **Adjective**: thoughtful (comp: more thoughtful, sup: most thoughtful)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; usually attributive & predicative)* Showing that you think carefully about things and consider other people’s needs or feelings.
   - *(Gradable)* Involving serious, careful thinking about a subject.
   
   **Synonyms**: considerate, reflective, attentive, mindful, deliberate
   
   **Antonyms**: thoughtless, careless, insensitive, impulsive
   
   **When to Use**: Describing people who think about others, or work (comments, essays, decisions) that shows depth and care in thinking; suitable for everyday, professional, and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you only mean intelligent or clever; do not use for quick, automatic reactions without reflection.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - thoughtful comment
   - thoughtful decision
   - thoughtful gift
   - thoughtful reflection
   - be thoughtful about something
   
   **Root Analysis**: thought (noun) + -ful (full of, characterized by).
   
   **Etymology**: Formed in English from *thought* + *-ful*; originally “full of thought or care,” later also “considerate toward others.”
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That was a really thoughtful question—you clearly listened carefully.
   - She’s very thoughtful about how her words might affect people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The paper offers a thoughtful analysis of how experts structure their thinking.
   - Thoughtful reflection on past decisions can improve future judgment.

1. **Adjective**: rational (comp: more rational, sup: most rational)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; predicative & attributive)* Based on clear reasons, evidence, or logic rather than emotion.
   - *(Of a person)* Able to think in a clear, logical way and make sensible decisions.
   
   **Synonyms**: logical, reasonable, analytical, sound, clear‑headed
   
   **Antonyms**: irrational, illogical, emotional (when it overrides logic), unreasonable
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing thinking or decisions that follow logic, evidence, or clear arguments; common in academic, professional, and everyday critical‑thinking contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when talking about creative leaps or purely emotional responses; do not use as a personal insult (“you’re not rational”) in sensitive situations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rational decision
   - rational argument
   - rational explanation
   - rational behavior
   - remain rational
   
   **Root Analysis**: ration- (reason, thinking) + -al (relating to).
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *rationalis* (of or belonging to reason), from *ratio* (reason, reckoning); adopted into English via Old French.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let’s try to be rational and look at the facts first.
   - It’s hard to stay rational when you’re really stressed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Classical economics assumes that individuals are fully rational decision‑makers.
   - The authors argue that what looks irrational may be rational given people’s limited information and time.

1. **Adjective**: analytical (comp: more analytical, sup: most analytical)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; primarily attributive, also predicative)* Involving breaking something into parts to understand it; relating to careful, structured examination.
   - *(Of a person)* Good at analyzing information logically and systematically.
   
   **Synonyms**: systematic, logical, methodical, structured, detail‑oriented
   
   **Antonyms**: impressionistic, unstructured, intuitive‑only (without analysis), superficial
   
   **When to Use**: Describing thinking styles, approaches, or tasks that require breaking problems down, examining details, and drawing reasoned conclusions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when talking about purely creative or emotional responses; do not use when only light, surface‑level thinking is involved.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - analytical thinker
   - analytical skills
   - analytical approach
   - analytical mindset
   - analytical framework
   
   **Root Analysis**: analytic- (from analysis, breaking down) + -al (relating to).
   
   **Etymology**: Greek → from *analysis* (a breaking up) via Late Latin and French; analytical emerged in English to describe reasoning and problem‑solving that relies on structured analysis.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She’s very analytical—she likes to break every problem into small pieces.
   - His analytical side comes out when he’s planning a big project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The role requires strong analytical skills and the ability to interpret complex data.
   - Analytical thinking helps experts identify patterns that novices often miss.

1. **Adjective**: critical (comp: more critical, sup: most critical)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; attributive & predicative)* Involving careful judgment about the good and bad parts of something, especially arguments, evidence, or ideas.
   - *(In education and philosophy)* Focused on questioning assumptions and evaluating reasoning rather than accepting things at face value.
   
   **Synonyms**: evaluative, questioning, skeptical, discerning, analytical
   
   **Antonyms**: uncritical, accepting, naive, unquestioning
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about thinking that evaluates evidence, detects weaknesses, and questions assumptions; central in “critical thinking,” “critical reading,” and “critical analysis.”
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be careful not to confuse with the sense “very important” (critical issue); avoid using it only to mean negative or complaining.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - critical thinking
   - critical analysis
   - critical reader
   - critical reflection
   - be critical of an idea
   
   **Root Analysis**: critic- (judge, decide) + -al (relating to).
   
   **Etymology**: Greek → from *kritikos* (able to judge), from *krinein* (to separate, decide); in English, *critical* became central in education and philosophy to describe evaluative thinking.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Try to be more critical when you read things online.
   - He’s pretty critical of his own ideas, which helps him improve them.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The course aims to develop students’ critical thinking skills.
   - A critical perspective requires examining underlying assumptions in the argument.

1. **Adjective**: intuitive (comp: more intuitive, sup: most intuitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; predicative & attributive)* Based on feelings or instinct rather than conscious reasoning.
   - *(Of ideas or interfaces)* Easy to understand or use without needing detailed explanation.
   
   **Synonyms**: instinctive, gut‑based, natural, self‑evident, easy to grasp
   
   **Antonyms**: counter‑intuitive, confusing, forced, overly complex
   
   **When to Use**: Describing thinking that “just feels right,” decisions made quickly from experience, or designs that people can understand without much thought.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal contexts where you must explain the reasoning; do not rely on “intuitive” as an excuse for not analyzing.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - intuitive feeling
   - intuitive decision
   - intuitive interface
   - intuitive sense
   - highly intuitive
   
   **Root Analysis**: intuit- (to look at, contemplate) + -ive (having the quality of).
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *intueri* (to look at, contemplate); in English, *intuitive* came to mean “known directly without conscious reasoning.”
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I had an intuitive feeling that something was wrong.
   - The new app is really intuitive—you don’t need a manual.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Experts often rely on intuitive judgments that are grounded in years of experience.
   - Designers aim to create intuitive workflows that reduce cognitive load.

1. **Adjective**: open‑minded (comp: more open‑minded, sup: most open‑minded)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; predicative & attributive)* Willing to consider new ideas, different opinions, or evidence that challenges your current beliefs.
   
   **Synonyms**: receptive, flexible, tolerant, broad‑minded, willing to listen
   
   **Antonyms**: closed‑minded, rigid, dogmatic, narrow‑minded
   
   **When to Use**: Describing people, groups, or approaches that welcome different viewpoints, especially in learning, collaboration, and problem‑solving.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when someone has firm views *based on strong evidence* rather than simple rigidity; do not use as polite cover for “has no clear opinion.”
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - be open‑minded about something
   - remain open‑minded
   - open‑minded attitude
   - open‑minded discussion
   - open‑minded approach
   
   **Root Analysis**: open (not closed) + mind + -ed (having); compound adjective.
   
   **Etymology**: English compound; became common in 19th–20th century writing about tolerance and flexibility in thinking.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Try to stay open‑minded while you hear everyone’s ideas.
   - She’s very open‑minded and loves learning from people who disagree with her.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Open‑minded discussion encourages participants to examine their assumptions.
   - Effective leaders cultivate an open‑minded attitude toward alternative solutions.

1. **Adjective**: biased (comp: more biased, sup: most biased)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; predicative & attributive)* Having or showing an unfair tendency to believe that some people, ideas, or things are better than others.
   - *(In cognitive science)* Systematically influenced by mental shortcuts or prior beliefs in a way that distorts judgment.
   
   **Synonyms**: prejudiced, one‑sided, skewed, unfair, partial
   
   **Antonyms**: unbiased, objective, neutral, balanced
   
   **When to Use**: When you need to highlight unfair or distorted thinking, especially in evaluation, decision‑making, or interpretation of evidence.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid as a personal attack without evidence; be cautious in sensitive contexts and consider more precise terms (e.g. incomplete, limited) if you mean something softer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - biased thinking
   - biased sample
   - heavily biased
   - biased toward / against
   - consciously / unconsciously biased
   
   **Root Analysis**: bias (noun: a slant) + -ed (having); no clear morphological breakdown beyond the noun base.
   
   **Etymology**: From the noun *bias* (slant, tendency), ultimately from Old French; used in English to describe mental slant or unfair leaning in judgments.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I might be biased because she’s my friend, but I think her idea is the best.
   - Try not to be biased toward your own team’s solution.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Biased interpretations of data can lead to misleading conclusions.
   - The sample is biased toward highly educated participants, which limits generalizability.

1. **Adjective**: objective (comp: more objective, sup: most objective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable; predicative & attributive)* Based on facts rather than feelings or personal opinions; not influenced by personal bias.
   - *(Of thinking or reporting)* Aiming to describe things as they are, not as someone wants them to be.
   
   **Synonyms**: impartial, unbiased, neutral, fact‑based, detached
   
   **Antonyms**: subjective, biased, partial, opinion‑based
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about thinking, evaluation, or reporting that tries to minimize personal bias; common in science, journalism, law, and decision‑making.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid promising complete objectivity where it is unrealistic; do not use when personal experience or perspective is central (e.g. reflective writing).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - objective view
   - remain objective
   - objective assessment
   - objective evidence
   - be as objective as possible
   
   **Root Analysis**: object (thing presented) + -ive (having the quality of); from Latin *objectivus*.
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin → from *objectivus* (relating to the object), from *objectum* (something put before the mind); in modern English, used for fact‑based, unbiased thinking or reporting.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It’s hard to be objective about your own work.
   - Let’s try to be objective and look at the numbers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study aims to provide an objective evaluation of different teaching methods.
   - Complete objectivity may be impossible, but researchers can make their reasoning transparent.
