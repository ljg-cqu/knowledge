1. **Preposition**: during (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* For the whole or part of a period of time when something else is happening, for example during a war or during a reign.
   - *(Abstract)* Within the context of a broader historical phase or process.
   
   **Synonyms**: in (the course of), over (the period of), throughout (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, after
   
   **When to Use**: When locating events inside a larger stretch of time, such as during the Middle Ages, during the crisis, or during the emperor's rule.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use it to introduce a specific date or moment (use 'on' for dates and 'at' for exact times); avoid 'during' when cause-and-effect is the main idea (use 'because of' or 'as a result of').
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: during the war; during this period; during the dynasty
   - **Verb + prep**: live during; rule during; suffer during
   - **Noun + prep**: events during; stability during; changes during
   
   **Root Analysis**: Related to 'while, enduring a period'; built from 'dur-' (to last) with prepositional function.
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French 'durant' (lasting) based on Latin 'durare' (to last). It developed into a preposition in English meaning 'throughout the time of'.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Many people went hungry during the war.
   - Life in the city changed quickly during the industrial boom.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Social hierarchies hardened during this phase of imperial expansion.
   - Agricultural output declined sharply during the final years of the regime.

1. **Preposition**: before (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Earlier than a particular time, event, or period.
   - *(Abstract)* In advance of a development or change in conditions.
   
   **Synonyms**: prior to, ahead of (time), in advance of
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following
   
   **When to Use**: When contrasting conditions or institutions that existed earlier with those that came later, for example before the revolution or before independence.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use 'before' when you mean 'in front of' in a physical sense in formal writing; use spatial prepositions like 'in front of' for location.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: before the war; before independence; before the reforms
   - **Verb + prep**: exist before; collapse before; emerge before
   - **Noun + prep**: years before; period before; situation before
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + fore (in front of, earlier than)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English 'beforan' (in front of, earlier than). The temporal sense became central in historical description.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Before the revolution, most people had very few rights.
   - The city was small and quiet before the factories arrived.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before 1914, European powers still believed that a major war could be avoided.
   - Landholding patterns before the reform act are difficult to reconstruct.

1. **Preposition**: after (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Later than a particular time, event, or period.
   - *(Abstract)* In the wake of or as a consequence of some earlier development.
   
   **Synonyms**: following, subsequent to, in the aftermath of
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to
   
   **When to Use**: When describing reactions, reforms, or new arrangements that came later than a key historical turning point, such as after the war or after the collapse of the empire.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it when you need to emphasize direct causation rather than simple sequence; use 'because of' or 'as a result of' for clear cause-and-effect.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: after the war; after the crisis; after independence
   - **Verb + prep**: rebuild after; reorganize after; emerge after
   - **Noun + prep**: period after; years after; decades after
   
   **Root Analysis**: aft (behind, later) + -er (comparative/relational ending)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English 'Ã¦fter' (following in time or place). In history writing it marks later phases or consequences.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the war, many soldiers struggled to find work.
   - The country changed its flag after it became independent.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After the revolution, land ownership was redistributed on a large scale.
   - The decades after unification saw rapid economic growth.

1. **Preposition**: throughout (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In every part of a place or region.
   - *(Temporal)* For the whole of a period of time, from beginning to end.
   - *(Abstract)* Widely present across a society or institution.
   
   **Synonyms**: all over, across, during the whole of, over the entire period of
   
   **Antonyms**: only in part of, only at times, briefly during
   
   **When to Use**: When stressing that a condition or practice was widespread in place or constant in time, such as throughout the empire or throughout the nineteenth century.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid if evidence only covers a few regions or a short segment of the period; in that case, more limited phrases like 'in many areas' or 'for much of the century' are better.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: throughout the empire; throughout Europe; throughout the century
   - **Verb + prep**: spread throughout; occur throughout; rule throughout
   - **Noun + prep**: resistance throughout; unrest throughout; reforms throughout
   
   **Root Analysis**: through (from side to side, from beginning to end) + out (completely)
   
   **Etymology**: From Middle English 'thurghout', combining 'through' and 'out' to give the sense of completeness in space or time.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Taxes were collected throughout the kingdom.
   - People celebrated the festival throughout the region.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Similar patterns of urban growth can be observed throughout this period.
   - The policy remained largely unchanged throughout the dynasty.

1. **Preposition**: under (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Directly below something or covered by it.
   - *(Abstract)* Subject to the control, authority, or influence of a person, law, or system, as in under foreign rule.
   
   **Synonyms**: beneath, underneath, subject to (in contexts of control), in the time of (in some historical collocations)
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, independent of, free from
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about people or regions controlled by a ruler, law, or system, such as under the emperor, under colonial rule, or under martial law.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid it when you need a neutral time marker like 'during'; 'under' usually implies power relations or control.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: under imperial rule; under the king; under occupation
   - **Verb + prep**: live under; suffer under; govern under
   - **Adjective + prep**: subject under; dependent under (less common; usually 'subject to')
   - **Noun + prep**: rule under; control under; administration under
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (from Old English, meaning lower in place or status)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English 'under' (beneath, subordinate to). In historical writing it often signals subordination, as in 'under foreign domination'.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - People under colonial rule had limited political rights.
   - Under the new king, taxes went up sharply.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Large parts of the region remained under military occupation for decades.
   - Peasants under feudal lords owed labor and other obligations.
