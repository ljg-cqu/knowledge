1. **Verb**: conquer
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T)* To take control of a country, region, or people by military force.
   - *(In historical writing)* To defeat another power and impose new political rule, often followed by administration and cultural influence.
   
   **Synonyms**: defeat, subdue, occupy, seize, bring under control
   
   **Antonyms**: surrender, lose, yield, be defeated, withdraw
   
   **When to Use**: When describing military expansion, campaigns, or the creation of empires through force.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for peaceful annexations, voluntary unions, or alliances; in modern contexts "invade" or "occupy" may be more precise if long-term control is unclear.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - conquer a territory
   - conquer a kingdom
   - attempt to conquer
   - successfully / unsuccessfully conquer
   - armies that conquered new lands
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (completely) + quer / querre (to seek, gain) → conquer
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *conquerre* (to win, gain) → Latin *conquirere* (to seek out, obtain). In English it came to mean winning lands in war and establishing control.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The empire tried to conquer neighboring states to gain more resources.
   - They never fully conquered the region because local resistance was so strong.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - By 221 BCE, the Qin state had conquered all rival kingdoms and unified much of what is now China.
   - Nomadic armies conquered large parts of the empire within a few decades.

1. **Verb**: colonize
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T)* To establish settlements in a foreign territory and bring it under the control of a distant power.
   - *(In colonial history)* To impose political, economic, and cultural dominance over another land and its people, often through migration and exploitation.
   
   **Synonyms**: settle, occupy, annex, establish colonies in, subjugate
   
   **Antonyms**: decolonize, withdraw, grant independence, restore sovereignty
   
   **When to Use**: When describing European and other powers creating overseas colonies, or when analyzing long-term effects of foreign rule.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in neutral descriptions of migration without political control; use carefully in modern contexts where the metaphor of "colonizing" digital or cultural spaces can be controversial.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - colonize a region
   - attempt to colonize
   - colonize indigenous lands
   - powers that colonized much of the world
   - efforts to colonize new territories
   
   **Root Analysis**: colon- (settler, farm) + -ize (verb ending meaning "to make" or "to cause to be")
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *colonia* (settlement) → French *coloniser* → English "colonize". Widely used from the 16th century onward to describe overseas expansion.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - European countries raced to colonize parts of Africa and Asia.
   - The film shows how settlers colonized the area and pushed local people aside.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company played a central role in the effort to colonize the subcontinent.
   - Scholars analyze how language and education were used to colonize minds as well as territories.

1. **Verb**: revolt
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(I)* To rise up against a government or authority, often using force.
   - *(In history)* For groups of people, especially subjects or soldiers, to resist or overthrow those in power.
   
   **Synonyms**: rebel, rise up, stage an uprising, mutiny, resist
   
   **Antonyms**: submit, obey, comply, remain loyal
   
   **When to Use**: When describing organized resistance to authority, such as peasant revolts, slave revolts, or military mutinies.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for peaceful protests or legal opposition; "protest" or "campaign" may fit better when there is no attempt to overthrow power.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - revolt against the king
   - peasants revolted
   - troops revolted
   - a failed revolt
   - plan to revolt
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + volt / volter (to roll, turn) → revolt
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *revolter* (to rebel) based on Latin *revolvere* (to roll back). In English it came to refer specifically to political and social uprisings.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Heavy taxes pushed farmers to revolt against the government.
   - The soldiers eventually revolted after months of unpaid wages.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The revolt was brutally suppressed, but it exposed deep tensions in rural society.
   - Enslaved people repeatedly revolted, challenging the idea that they were passive victims.

1. **Verb**: emerge
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(I)* To come into view or become known after being hidden or unclear.
   - *(In historical writing)* To develop gradually as a distinct power, idea, or pattern over time.
   
   **Synonyms**: arise, appear, develop, come into being, surface
   
   **Antonyms**: disappear, fade, decline, vanish
   
   **When to Use**: When explaining how new states, social classes, ideologies, or historical interpretations slowly appear and become important.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for sudden, clearly dated events like declarations or battles; in those cases verbs like "begin" or "break out" are more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - a new power emerged
   - nationalism emerged
   - patterns emerged over time
   - an independent state emerged
   - a new consensus emerged among historians
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- / ex- (out) + mergere (to dip, plunge) → emerge
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *emergere* (to rise out of, appear) via French and Middle English. In historical writing it often describes gradual, long-term developments.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A new leader slowly emerged as the most popular choice.
   - Over the semester, a clearer picture of the past emerged for me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A distinct merchant class emerged in the coastal cities during this period.
   - After the archives were opened, a different interpretation of the event emerged.

1. **Verb**: commemorate
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T)* To honor and remember a person or event, especially with a ceremony, monument, or official act.
   - *(In memory studies)* To shape public understanding of the past through rituals, symbols, and places of remembrance.
   
   **Synonyms**: honor, mark, memorialize, pay tribute to, observe
   
   **Antonyms**: forget, ignore, erase, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: When describing ceremonies, anniversaries, monuments, and other forms of public remembrance of historical events.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in strictly analytical passages where neutral verbs like "record" or "note" are more suitable; do not use when the goal is to hide or deny the past.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - commemorate a victory
   - commemorate the victims
   - commemorate an anniversary
   - a monument built to commemorate
   - officially commemorate the event
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + memor- (mind, memory) + -ate (verb ending)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *commemorare* (to remember together) → Late Latin and Old French → English "commemorate". Closely linked to religious and civic ceremonies of remembrance.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Every year they commemorate the end of the war with a public holiday.
   - The museum was built to commemorate the people who died there.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The memorial park was created to commemorate those who lost their lives during the conflict.
   - The article examines how different groups commemorate the same event in conflicting ways.
