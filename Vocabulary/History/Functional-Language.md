1. **Functional Expression**: Historians argue that ... (function: expressing opinions/reporting views)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces a claim or interpretation commonly made by historians, showing that what follows is someone else's reasoned view rather than an established fact.
   - *Formality*: formal/neutral; common in academic writing.
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens the claim by attributing it to a group of experts).
   
   **Synonyms**: Many historians believe that ..., Scholars have suggested that ..., It has been argued that ..., Some researchers maintain that ...
   
   **Antonyms**: Historians reject the idea that ..., Few historians now accept that ..., It is no longer widely believed that ...
   
   **When to Use**: When summarizing or reporting interpretations in secondary sources, or when positioning your own view within an existing debate.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in informal conversation where a simpler 'People think that ...' is enough; do not use it to hide your own opinion without evidence.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Many historians argue that ...
   - Some historians argue that ...
   - Historians have long argued that ...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Highlights that knowledge is interpretive and contested; shows respect for scholarly work and frames claims as part of an ongoing discussion.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Supporting: Recent research supports historians who argue that ...
   - Challenging: However, other historians argue that ...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Historians argue that the revolution had deeper social causes, not just a single event.
   - Many historians argue that ordinary people played a bigger role than old textbooks suggest.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Historians argue that imperial expansion was driven as much by economic pressures as by ideology.
   - Social historians argue that changes in family life reshaped political participation.

1. **Functional Expression**: According to [source], ... (function: reporting/attributing information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces information or a viewpoint taken from a particular source, such as a historian, document, or organization.
   - *Formality*: formal/neutral.
   - *Directness*: indirect (credits the information to an external authority).
   
   **Synonyms**: As [author] notes, ..., In the words of [source], ..., [Source] reports that ...
   
   **Antonyms**: Contrary to [source], ..., In contrast to [source], ...
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to show where an idea or piece of information comes from and avoid sounding as if you are stating it as your own unquestioned view.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use it when you have multiple sources or general knowledge; in that case, cite them more broadly (for example, 'Recent studies suggest that ...').
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - According to one contemporary witness, ...
   - According to the official report, ...
   - According to recent scholarship, ...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Helps readers distinguish between primary voices and secondary analysis, and signals that you are handling evidence carefully.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Checking: We must ask how reliable this is, according to whom, and in what context.
   - Balancing: According to other sources, however, ...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to one letter, people in the village were terrified.
   - According to the textbook, the war lasted for four years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to census data, the urban population doubled in less than twenty years.
   - According to recent archival research, the treaty negotiations were more complex than previously assumed.

1. **Functional Expression**: In comparison with ..., ... (function: comparing/contrasting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Sets up a comparison between two periods, places, or groups to highlight similarities or differences.
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal; very common in analytical writing.
   - *Directness*: direct but analytical.
   
   **Synonyms**: Compared with ..., Relative to ..., By comparison with ...
   
   **Antonyms**: Instead of comparing, some structures simply describe one case without explicit comparison.
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to show how one historical case looks different when set next to another, for example, 'In comparison with the north, the south remained largely rural.'
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the cases are too different to compare meaningfully, or when you risk implying a simple ranking (better/worse) without argument.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - In comparison with earlier periods, ...
   - In comparison with neighboring states, ...
   - In comparison with other revolutions, ...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Encourages a broader, more relational view of history; can challenge assumptions based on a single case.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Extending: In comparison with yet another region, ...
   - Questioning: This looks different in comparison with evidence from local archives.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In comparison with the capital, life in the countryside changed slowly.
   - In comparison with earlier kings, this ruler was much weaker.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In comparison with other European empires, this one relied more heavily on indirect rule.
   - In comparison with previous decades, the 1930s saw a marked increase in state intervention.

1. **Functional Expression**: It is important to note that ... (function: hedging/highlighting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Signals that the writer is drawing attention to a detail or qualification that might otherwise be overlooked.
   - *Formality*: formal.
   - *Directness*: indirect but firm; softens the command while still emphasizing importance.
   
   **Synonyms**: It should be noted that ..., We should remember that ..., One should keep in mind that ...
   
   **Antonyms**: It is easy to forget that ..., Many accounts ignore that ... (indicate neglect rather than emphasis).
   
   **When to Use**: When pointing out limits of sources, exceptions to general trends, or key contextual information.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overusing it in every paragraph; too many reminders weaken the impact. Do not use it to introduce trivial points.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It is also important to note that ...
   - It is important to note, however, that ...
   - It is important to note that our evidence is incomplete.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Helps the reader focus on nuance and avoid simple cause-and-effect stories; signals responsible, cautious interpretation.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: It is indeed important to note that ...
   - Adding: It is important to note that ..., and also that ...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It is important to note that most people at the time could not read.
   - It is important to note that our sources only show the voices of the winners.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - It is important to note that tax records undercount women and children.
   - It is important to note that the term 'nation' had a different meaning in this period.
