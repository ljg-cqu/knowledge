1. **Expression**: declare war on (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make an official announcement that one state will begin armed conflict against another.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (the verb 'declare' and the noun 'war' regularly occur together in this political meaning).
   
   **Synonyms**: formally go to war with, announce hostilities against, issue a declaration of war on
   
   **Antonyms**: make peace with, sign a peace treaty with, normalize relations with
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the formal, legal start of a war, especially when a government issues an official statement.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual talk about personal conflict; do not use for conflicts that start without a formal declaration.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - declare war against
   - issue a declaration of war
   - formally declare war
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: 'declare' is commonly used with official statements (declare war, declare independence, declare an emergency), so it naturally pairs with 'war' in international politics.
   
   **Root Analysis**: declare (from Latin 'declarare', to make clear) + war (armed conflict)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The country declared war on its neighbor after the border attack.
   - Newspapers reported that the government was about to declare war.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In 1914, several European powers declared war on each other within a few weeks.
   - The article analyzes the reasons the parliament decided to declare war on the empire.

1. **Expression**: come to power (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To gain control of a government or state, usually by winning an election, leading a movement, or seizing power.
   - *Fixedness*: medium to strong (other verbs are possible, but 'come to power' is a very common idiom in political history).
   
   **Synonyms**: gain power, assume power, take office, rise to power
   
   **Antonyms**: lose power, fall from power, be removed from office, step down
   
   **When to Use**: When explaining how leaders, parties, or groups became the ruling authority in a state.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal legal descriptions that require more precise terms such as 'was elected president' or 'was appointed prime minister'.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - rise to power
   - come back to power
   - come to power after a coup
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: 'come' emphasizes movement toward a new position; combined with 'power', it highlights the process of becoming the ruling force.
   
   **Root Analysis**: come (move toward) + to power (political control or authority)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The party came to power after years in the opposition.
   - Many people were hopeful when the new leader came to power.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The movement came to power through a combination of electoral victory and elite negotiation.
   - The study traces how different factions helped him come to power.

1. **Expression**: spark an uprising (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To cause a group of people to suddenly resist or fight against those in power.
   - *Fixedness*: medium (other verbs and nouns are possible, but 'spark' often combines with 'uprising', 'revolt', or 'protest' to suggest a trigger).
   
   **Synonyms**: trigger an uprising, set off a revolt, ignite a rebellion
   
   **Antonyms**: suppress an uprising, prevent a revolt, put down a rebellion
   
   **When to Use**: When a specific action or event sets off wider resistance, such as a new tax sparking an uprising.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid if the movement developed slowly rather than from a single dramatic cause.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - spark a revolt
   - spark mass protests
   - spark a rebellion
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: 'spark' suggests a small flame that starts a bigger fire, which matches the idea of a single event leading to large-scale unrest.
   
   **Root Analysis**: spark (small flash of fire) + uprising (act of rising up against authority)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new tax law sparked an uprising in the countryside.
   - A single protest can sometimes spark a much larger uprising.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The execution of the reformer sparked an uprising that spread across the region.
   - Economic hardship alone did not spark the uprising; political repression also played a central role.

1. **Expression**: sign a treaty (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - For leaders or representatives to write their names on an official agreement between states, making it legally valid.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (the phrase 'sign a treaty' is the standard way to describe formally approving an agreement).
   
   **Synonyms**: conclude a treaty, sign an agreement, ratify a treaty (partly overlapping), enter into a treaty
   
   **Antonyms**: break a treaty, violate a treaty, withdraw from a treaty, refuse to sign a treaty
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the formal act that ends a conflict, creates an alliance, or sets new rules between states.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for informal promises or understandings; in those cases 'make a deal' or 'reach an agreement' might be better.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - sign a peace treaty
   - sign a trade treaty
   - sign a treaty of alliance
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: 'sign' is the standard verb used with legal documents, and 'treaty' is a formal written agreement, so the partnership is very strong in diplomatic language.
   
   **Root Analysis**: sign (write one's name) + treaty (formal agreement between states)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The two sides finally signed a peace treaty.
   - Several countries signed a treaty to set new borders.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The powers signed a treaty in Paris that redrew the map of Europe.
   - Smaller states were not invited to sign the treaty that determined their future.

1. **Expression**: go into exile (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To leave one's country, often for political reasons, and live abroad because it is unsafe or impossible to stay.
   - *Fixedness*: medium to strong (other verbs exist, but 'go into exile' is a common idiom in historical narratives).
   
   **Synonyms**: flee into exile, live in exile, be forced into exile, go abroad (for political reasons)
   
   **Antonyms**: return from exile, come back home, be allowed to return, remain in the country
   
   **When to Use**: When describing rulers, activists, or ordinary people who leave their homeland to escape persecution or defeat.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use it for people who move abroad for work or study without political pressure; in those cases 'emigrate' or 'move abroad' is better.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - live in exile
   - forced into exile
   - spend years in exile
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: The phrase captures both the act of leaving ('go') and the condition of being cut off from home ('exile'), making it a natural choice in political history.
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (move) + into (toward a state) + exile (forced absence from one's country)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the coup, the former president went into exile.
   - Many writers went into exile to escape censorship.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The royal family went into exile after the revolution and never returned.
   - The article examines how political leaders in exile continued to influence events at home.
