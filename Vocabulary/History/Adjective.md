1. **Adjective**: medieval
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Non-gradable, mainly attributive)* Relating to the Middle Ages in European history (roughly the 5th to the 15th centuries).
   - *(Figurative, often informal)* Very old-fashioned, cruel, or backward in a way that reminds people of the distant past.
   
   **Synonyms**: Middle Ages, feudal, early European, pre-modern, old-fashioned
   
   **Antonyms**: modern, contemporary, progressive, up-to-date
   
   **When to Use**: When describing people, institutions, architecture, or practices from the Middle Ages, or when carefully using a metaphor for something that feels extremely old or harsh.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it for any past period outside the Middle Ages (for example, ancient Egypt is not medieval); use it figuratively with care because it can sound judgmental or imprecise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - medieval castle
   - medieval Europe
   - medieval manuscript
   - medieval society
   - late medieval period
   
   **Root Analysis**: medi- (middle) + -eval (age, period)
   
   **Etymology**: From Medieval Latin 'medium aevum' (middle age), which became 'medievalis' (of the Middle Ages). Borrowed into English in the 19th century to refer specifically to the period between ancient and modern history.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We visited a medieval town with narrow streets and stone walls.
   - The film shows what daily life might have looked like in a medieval village.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The book examines how medieval institutions shaped later European legal traditions.
   - Archaeologists discovered a set of medieval coins beneath the church floor.

1. **Adjective**: revolutionary (comp: more revolutionary, sup: most revolutionary)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Gradable)* Causing or involving a complete or very great change, especially in politics, technology, or society.
   - *(Attributive)* Relating to a political revolution or those who support overthrowing an existing system.
   
   **Synonyms**: radical, transformative, groundbreaking, insurgent, progressive
   
   **Antonyms**: conservative, traditional, gradual, moderate
   
   **When to Use**: When describing events, ideas, or movements that significantly alter political power or social structures, such as the revolutionary government, revolutionary reforms, or revolutionary technology.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overusing it for small improvements or routine policy changes; in academic history, use it precisely and support the claim that change was truly far-reaching.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - revolutionary movement
   - revolutionary leader
   - revolutionary change
   - revolutionary ideas
   - revolutionary era
   
   **Root Analysis**: revolution (a radical change or overthrow) + -ary (adjectival suffix meaning 'relating to')
   
   **Etymology**: Based on 'revolution', from Latin 'revolutio' (a turning over). 'Revolutionary' developed in the 17th–18th centuries to describe those who supported or caused political revolutions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The printing press was revolutionary because it made books much cheaper.
   - Her book offered a revolutionary way of looking at everyday life in the past.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Revolutionary movements in the nineteenth century challenged long-standing monarchical rule.
   - The study argues that these administrative changes were evolutionary rather than truly revolutionary.

1. **Adjective**: imperial
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Non-gradable, mainly attributive)* Relating to an empire or an emperor, especially in the context of expansion, rule, and administration.
   - *(Historical)* Connected with the official styles, symbols, or institutions of an imperial court.
   
   **Synonyms**: colonial, imperialist, expansionist, domineering, hegemonic
   
   **Antonyms**: local, provincial, national (non-imperial), anti-imperial
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to policies, armies, institutions, or cultures belonging to an empire, such as imperial rule, imperial armies, or imperial administration.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it loosely for any big or powerful thing; in history writing, keep it tied to actual imperial structures or ideologies.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - imperial power
   - imperial capital
   - imperial court
   - imperial administration
   - imperial ideology
   
   **Root Analysis**: imper- (command, authority) + -ial (adjectival suffix meaning 'relating to')
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin 'imperialis' (of the empire, of the emperor) derived from 'imperium' (command, dominion). Spread through European languages to describe empires and their symbols.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The museum displays robes once worn at the imperial court.
   - Imperial armies marched across huge distances to control the frontier.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Imperial policy combined direct control with negotiated arrangements at the local level.
   - The article explores how imperial ideology justified expansion and hierarchy.

1. **Adjective**: colonial
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Mainly attributive)* Relating to a colony or the system by which one country rules other lands.
   - *(In architecture and culture)* Characteristic of styles and institutions that developed under colonial rule.
   
   **Synonyms**: imperial, settler, plantation-era, overseas, dependent
   
   **Antonyms**: postcolonial, independent, metropolitan, sovereign
   
   **When to Use**: When describing administrations, economies, societies, or cultural practices shaped by foreign rule, such as colonial government, colonial trade, or colonial education.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it as a neutral or nostalgic label without acknowledging power imbalances; do not confuse colonial with merely 'old' or 'traditional'.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - colonial rule
   - colonial administration
   - colonial economy
   - colonial society
   - colonial legacy
   
   **Root Analysis**: colon- (settler, farmer) + -ial (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin 'colonia' (settlement) → French 'colonial' → English 'colonial'. Became common in the 18th–19th centuries during European overseas expansion.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Many streets still carry names from the colonial period.
   - The film shows life in a colonial port city.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Colonial institutions continued to shape politics long after formal independence.
   - The article compares different forms of colonial rule in Asia and Africa.

1. **Adjective**: feudal
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Mainly attributive)* Relating to the social and economic system in medieval Europe and some other regions, where land was held in exchange for service and loyalty.
   - *(Figurative)* Extremely hierarchical or outdated, as if based on rigid duties and ranks.
   
   **Synonyms**: manorial, hierarchical, land-based, vassal-based, lordly
   
   **Antonyms**: egalitarian, modern, capitalist, democratic
   
   **When to Use**: When analyzing medieval societies organized around lords, vassals, and peasants, or when discussing similar systems of personal obligation in world history.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid stretching it to describe all pre-modern societies; historians debate when and where the term 'feudal' truly applies.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - feudal system
   - feudal lord
   - feudal obligations
   - feudal society
   - feudal privileges
   
   **Root Analysis**: feud- / feod- (fee, fief, piece of land held in return for service) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: From Medieval Latin 'feodalis' (relating to a fief) → Old French → English 'feudal'. The term became central in nineteenth-century debates about medieval social structures.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In the feudal system, peasants worked the land for powerful lords.
   - The story is set in a feudal kingdom with castles and knights.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The book questions whether the region ever developed a truly feudal order.
   - Feudal relationships tied military service to the control of land.

1. **Adjective**: industrial
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Attributive)* Relating to industry, factories, and large-scale production, especially after the Industrial Revolution.
   - *(Broader historical use)* Connected with societies whose economies are dominated by manufacturing and mechanized work.
   
   **Synonyms**: manufacturing, factory-based, mechanized, urbanized, industrialized
   
   **Antonyms**: agrarian, rural, agricultural, pre-industrial
   
   **When to Use**: When describing economies, cities, or working conditions shaped by factories, machines, and large-scale production.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid labeling early craft or home-based production as industrial; keep the term for contexts where mechanization and factory systems are central.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - industrial revolution
   - industrial city
   - industrial worker
   - industrial capitalism
   - industrial pollution
   
   **Root Analysis**: industri- (diligent, active, relating to productive work) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin 'industria' (diligence, activity) → French 'industrie' → English 'industry' and 'industrial'. Became widely used in the nineteenth century to describe new forms of work and society.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The industrial cities were crowded and noisy.
   - Many people left farms to work in industrial areas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Industrial growth transformed patterns of migration and family life.
   - The article analyzes how industrial labor laws developed during this period.
