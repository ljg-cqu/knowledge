# Token - Verbs

1. **Verb**: mint
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To create new tokens through smart contract execution or protocol mechanisms, increasing the circulating supply; applies to both fungible tokens and NFTs. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: create, generate, issue, produce, coin
   
   **Antonyms**: burn, destroy, eliminate, remove
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the creation of new tokens, whether through mining rewards, staking emissions, NFT creation, or protocol-authorized supply expansion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to purchasing existing tokens, transferring tokens, or receiving tokens from another party rather than creating new ones.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mint tokens
   - mint NFTs
   - minting rights
   - newly minted
   - mint and distribute
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "moneta" (money, mint) → Old English "mynet" → verb form
   
   **Etymology**: Latin moneta → Old English mynet → mint (coin factory) → to mint (create coins) → blockchain context for digital token creation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Anyone can mint their own NFT on OpenSea for just the gas fee.
   - The protocol mints new rewards tokens every block to pay validators.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smart contracts mint tokens according to predetermined emission schedules or in response to specific contract interactions.
   - Centralized stablecoins mint new units upon receiving equivalent fiat collateral deposits in reserve accounts.

1. **Verb**: burn
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To permanently remove tokens from circulation by sending them to addresses with no accessible private keys, effectively reducing total supply. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: destroy, eliminate, remove permanently, reduce supply
   
   **Antonyms**: mint, create, issue, generate
   
   **When to Use**: When describing deflationary mechanisms, fee destruction models, supply reduction strategies, or permanent token removal from circulation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When tokens are locked but retrievable (staking), temporarily held, or destroyed in non-permanent ways.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - burn tokens
   - burn mechanism
   - automatically burn
   - burn address
   - burn and reduce supply
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "byrnan" (to be on fire) → metaphor for destruction
   
   **Etymology**: Old English byrnan → Middle English brennen → modern burn → blockchain metaphor (2017+) for token destruction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The platform burns 10% of every transaction fee to make the token deflationary.
   - They burned half the total supply to increase scarcity and drive up value.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Protocols burn tokens through provably inaccessible address transfers or smart contract destruction functions to implement deflationary tokenomics.
   - Transaction fee burning mechanisms create permanent supply reduction proportional to network utilization levels.

1. **Verb**: stake
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To lock tokens in a protocol or smart contract to support network security, earn rewards, participate in governance, or provide liquidity, typically involving temporary loss of transfer rights. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: lock, bond, delegate, commit, pledge
   
   **Antonyms**: unstake, withdraw, unlock, release
   
   **When to Use**: When describing committing tokens to Proof-of-Stake validation, liquidity provision, governance participation, or any mechanism requiring temporary lockup for rewards.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When tokens remain freely transferable, when describing permanent burns, or simple token holding without lockup.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stake tokens
   - stake for rewards
   - stake and earn
   - stake with validators
   - delegate and stake
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "staca" (pointed stick) → "to stake" (to wager, to mark)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English staca → stake (noun) → to stake (verb, 16th century) → blockchain context (2012+) for Proof-of-Stake commitment
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can stake your ETH to earn about 5% annual rewards while helping secure the network.
   - I staked all my tokens in their liquidity pool to earn trading fees.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Validators stake substantial token quantities as collateral subject to slashing penalties for malicious behavior or protocol violations.
   - Liquid staking protocols enable users to stake tokens while maintaining liquidity through derivative token representations.

1. **Verb**: transfer
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To send tokens from one wallet address to another on a blockchain network through transaction execution. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: send, transmit, move, convey, remit
   
   **Antonyms**: receive, retain, hold, keep
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the action of sending tokens between addresses, whether for payments, trades, or moving assets between wallets.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When tokens remain in the same address, or when describing off-chain database updates rather than blockchain transactions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - transfer tokens
   - transfer ownership
   - transfer funds
   - unable to transfer
   - transfer between wallets
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "trans-" (across) + "ferre" (to carry)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin transferre → Old French transferer → English transfer (14th century) → blockchain token movement context
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just transfer the tokens to my wallet address and I'll receive them in a few seconds.
   - You can't transfer those tokens yet—they're still subject to the vesting schedule.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smart contracts execute token transfers atomically through function calls modifying state variables representing address balances.
   - Cross-chain transfers require bridge protocols to lock tokens on the source chain while minting equivalent representations on destination chains.

1. **Verb**: swap
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To exchange one token for another through decentralized or centralized trading platforms, typically using automated market makers or order books. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: exchange, trade, convert, switch, change
   
   **Antonyms**: hold, retain, keep unchanged
   
   **When to Use**: When describing token-for-token exchanges on DEXs (like Uniswap) or exchanges, emphasizing direct conversion rather than traditional trading terminology.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to traditional buy/sell order book trading where "trade" might be more appropriate, or when no actual exchange occurs.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - swap tokens
   - token swap
   - instant swap
   - swap on DEX
   - swap for another token
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English "swappen" (to strike, to exchange by striking hands)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English swappen → swap (informal exchange) → DeFi context (2018+) for automated token exchanges
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I swapped all my USDC for ETH on Uniswap before the price went up.
   - You can swap between any two tokens instantly using their automated market maker.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Automated market makers enable users to swap tokens through liquidity pools implementing constant product formulas for price determination.
   - Cross-chain atomic swaps execute trustlessly through hash time-locked contracts ensuring simultaneous token exchange without intermediaries.

1. **Verb**: bridge
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To transfer tokens from one blockchain network to another using cross-chain bridge protocols that lock, wrap, or convert assets for use on different chains. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: cross-chain transfer, port, move across chains, convert
   
   **Antonyms**: isolate, contain, single-chain hold
   
   **When to Use**: When describing moving tokens between different blockchain ecosystems using bridge infrastructure, creating wrapped versions or cross-chain representations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When transferring within the same blockchain, or when discussing metaphorical connections rather than actual cross-chain token movement.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bridge tokens
   - bridge to another chain
   - bridge assets
   - cross-chain bridge
   - bridge and use
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "brycg" (structure spanning divide) → verb form
   
   **Etymology**: Old English brycg → Middle English brigge → bridge (noun) → to bridge (verb) → blockchain context (2019+) for cross-chain transfers
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I bridged my USDC from Ethereum to Polygon to avoid high gas fees.
   - You can bridge tokens between chains in minutes using their official bridge.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Bridge protocols lock native assets in source-chain smart contracts while minting equivalent wrapped representations on destination chains.
   - Cross-chain bridges employ validator sets or relay networks to verify and execute token transfers across heterogeneous blockchain architectures.

1. **Verb**: vest
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Finance/Blockchain) To gradually become transferable or accessible according to a predetermined schedule, typically applying to team tokens, investor allocations, or employee compensation. Intransitive verb (tokens vest) or transitive (to vest tokens).
   
   **Synonyms**: unlock gradually, release on schedule, become available
   
   **Antonyms**: immediate release, instant availability, revoke
   
   **When to Use**: When describing time-locked token releases that gradually become transferable for founders, teams, investors, or advisors over defined periods.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When tokens are immediately transferable upon receipt, or when describing permanent locks without gradual release schedules.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tokens vest
   - vest over time
   - fully vested
   - vest according to schedule
   - gradually vest
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "vestire" (to clothe, to invest with authority)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin vestire → Old French vestir → English vest (17th century legal sense) → blockchain token release context
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My tokens vest 25% each year for four years, so I can't sell everything at once.
   - Team tokens don't vest immediately—there's usually a cliff period first.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Advisor allocations vest linearly over 24 months following a 6-month cliff period to align long-term incentives with project success.
   - Smart contracts enforce vesting schedules through time-locked transfer restrictions automatically releasing tokens according to predetermined intervals.

1. **Verb**: delegate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To assign voting power or staking rights to another party (validator, representative) while retaining token ownership, common in Proof-of-Stake and governance systems. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: assign, entrust, authorize, empower, appoint
   
   **Antonyms**: revoke, reclaim, self-stake, withdraw delegation
   
   **When to Use**: When describing assigning staking or voting rights to validators or delegates without transferring token ownership, maintaining rewards while outsourcing participation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When actually transferring token ownership, or when participating directly rather than through representatives.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - delegate stake
   - delegate voting power
   - delegate to validators
   - delegation mechanism
   - choose a delegate
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "delegare" (to send on a mission) from "de-" + "legare" (to send)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin delegare → Old French deleguer → English delegate (16th century) → blockchain staking/governance context
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can delegate your staking to professional validators and still earn rewards without running your own node.
   - I delegated my voting power to a community member who actively participates in governance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Token holders delegate staking rights to validator nodes while retaining custody and earning proportional consensus rewards minus validator commission.
   - Governance systems implement liquid democracy through delegation mechanisms enabling token holders to transitively assign voting authority to trusted representatives.

1. **Verb**: airdrop
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To distribute tokens freely to wallet addresses, typically for marketing, community rewards, or governance decentralization purposes. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: distribute freely, give away, allocate without cost, send gratis
   
   **Antonyms**: sell, charge for, require payment
   
   **When to Use**: When describing free token distributions to users, community members, or addresses meeting specific criteria for marketing or ecosystem building.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When tokens are sold, earned through work, or acquired through staking rather than freely distributed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - airdrop tokens
   - airdrop to users
   - airdrop campaign
   - retroactively airdrop
   - airdrop for promotion
   
   **Root Analysis**: "air" + "drop" (to distribute from above)
   
   **Etymology**: Military airdrop (1943) → marketing term (1990s) → cryptocurrency context (2014+) for free token distribution
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The project airdropped 1,000 tokens to every early user who tried their testnet.
   - Many protocols airdrop governance tokens retroactively to reward community members.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Projects airdrop tokens strategically to bootstrap network effects, incentivize adoption, and achieve governance decentralization without direct capital raises.
   - Retroactive airdrops distribute tokens to addresses demonstrating historical protocol usage verified through on-chain activity analysis.

1. **Verb**: lock
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To make tokens temporarily non-transferable by committing them to smart contracts, staking protocols, or time-lock mechanisms, restricting access for a defined period. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: freeze, immobilize, commit, restrict, secure
   
   **Antonyms**: unlock, release, free, unstake
   
   **When to Use**: When describing making tokens temporarily non-transferable through staking, liquidity provision, vesting, or security mechanisms.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When tokens remain freely transferable, or when referring to permanent destruction (burning) rather than temporary restriction.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lock tokens
   - lock liquidity
   - lock for period
   - time-lock tokens
   - lock and earn
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "loc" (fastening, barrier)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English loc → lock (noun/verb) → blockchain context for temporary token restriction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to lock your tokens for 30 days to get the highest staking rewards.
   - Developers locked liquidity for a year to prove they won't rug pull.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smart contracts lock tokens through time-based restrictions or conditional release mechanisms enforcing predetermined unlocking criteria.
   - Liquidity locking increases investor confidence by demonstrating project commitment through verifiable, time-restricted token pool commitments.

1. **Verb**: claim
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To retrieve or collect earned tokens, rewards, airdrops, or vested allocations by executing a smart contract function that transfers them to your wallet. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: collect, retrieve, withdraw, receive, obtain
   
   **Antonyms**: forfeit, abandon, leave unclaimed
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the action of collecting available rewards, airdrops, or vested tokens through active user interaction with smart contracts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When rewards are automatically distributed without user action, or when referring to initial purchases rather than claiming existing entitlements.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - claim rewards
   - claim airdrop
   - claim tokens
   - available to claim
   - claim vested amount
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "clamare" (to call out, to assert)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin clamare → Old French clamer → English claim (13th century) → blockchain context for collecting earned tokens
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't forget to claim your staking rewards every week or you'll miss out on compound interest.
   - The airdrop is available now—you just need to connect your wallet and claim it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Users must actively claim accrued rewards through smart contract interactions, as automatic distributions would incur unnecessary gas costs for the protocol.
   - Claiming mechanisms enable eligible addresses to retrieve allocated tokens while allowing protocols to defer distribution costs until user-initiated transactions.

1. **Verb**: trade
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Finance/Blockchain) To buy and sell tokens on exchanges for profit or portfolio rebalancing, involving active market participation and price speculation. Transitive or intransitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: buy and sell, exchange, deal in, speculate, transact
   
   **Antonyms**: hold, HODL, keep, retain
   
   **When to Use**: When describing active buying and selling of tokens on exchanges for profit, arbitrage, or portfolio management purposes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to long-term holding strategies, simple one-time swaps, or non-market token transfers.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - trade tokens
   - actively trade
   - trade on exchanges
   - day trade
   - trade for profit
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle Low German "trade" (track, course) → trading route → commercial exchange
   
   **Etymology**: Middle Low German trade → English trade (14th century) → modern financial markets → cryptocurrency exchanges
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I trade tokens on Binance, trying to catch short-term price movements.
   - Most people lose money when they try to trade actively—better to just hold long-term.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Professional traders utilize algorithmic strategies to trade tokens across multiple exchanges, exploiting arbitrage opportunities and market inefficiencies.
   - High-frequency trading bots execute microsecond-precision token trades responding to order book dynamics and liquidity pool price movements.

1. **Verb**: approve
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To grant a smart contract permission to spend or transfer tokens on your behalf by signing an approval transaction, required before interacting with DeFi protocols. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: authorize, permit, allow, grant access, enable
   
   **Antonyms**: revoke, deny, prohibit, restrict
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the prerequisite transaction that grants smart contracts permission to interact with your tokens for swaps, staking, or other DeFi operations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to general agreement or manual actions rather than blockchain-specific approval transactions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approve tokens
   - approve spending
   - token approval
   - approve contract access
   - unlimited approval
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "approbare" (to assent to) from "ad-" + "probare" (to test, prove)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin approbare → Old French aprover → English approve (14th century) → blockchain smart contract permission context
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to approve the token first before you can swap it on Uniswap—it's a separate transaction.
   - Be careful with unlimited approvals—only approve the amount you need for security.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - ERC-20 approval mechanisms enable token holders to authorize smart contracts to execute transfers on their behalf within specified allowance limits.
   - Security best practices recommend limited approvals rather than infinite allowances to minimize potential exploit exposure from compromised smart contracts.

1. **Verb**: wrap
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain) To convert native tokens into token-standard compatible versions (like WETH from ETH) or to create cross-chain representations backed 1:1 by locked collateral. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: convert, encapsulate, tokenize, represent, bridge
   
   **Antonyms**: unwrap, redeem, convert back, native form
   
   **When to Use**: When describing converting native assets to ERC-20 compatible versions, or creating cross-chain representations through bridge protocols.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing native token usage, synthetic assets without backing, or non-conversion token operations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wrap tokens
   - wrap ETH
   - wrapped version
   - wrap for compatibility
   - wrap and use
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "wrappan" (to wind around, to cover)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wrappan → Middle English wrappen → modern wrap → blockchain context (2018+) for token encapsulation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to wrap your ETH into WETH before you can use it in some DeFi protocols.
   - Wrapped Bitcoin (WBTC) lets you use Bitcoin on Ethereum by wrapping it through custodians.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Wrapping mechanisms convert native blockchain assets into token-standard representations maintaining 1:1 parity through collateral locking protocols.
   - Cross-chain wrapped tokens enable multi-chain liquidity aggregation while requiring trust in bridge validators or decentralized custodial mechanisms.

1. **Verb**: liquidate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Finance/Blockchain) To forcibly sell collateralized tokens when their value falls below required thresholds in lending protocols, or to voluntarily sell positions to exit markets. Transitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: force-sell, close position, sell off, convert to cash
   
   **Antonyms**: accumulate, hold, maintain position, add collateral
   
   **When to Use**: When describing forced selling of collateral in DeFi lending when loan-to-value ratios exceed protocol limits, or voluntary position closure.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When positions remain healthy, when adding collateral prevents liquidation, or when describing simple voluntary trades without urgency.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - liquidate position
   - get liquidated
   - liquidation event
   - liquidate collateral
   - avoid liquidation
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "liquidare" (to melt, to make liquid/clear)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin liquidare → Medieval Latin → English liquidate (16th century) → financial markets → DeFi lending protocols
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I got liquidated when ETH crashed—the protocol sold my collateral to cover my loan.
   - If your collateral ratio drops too low, bots will liquidate your position automatically.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Lending protocols liquidate under-collateralized positions through incentivized liquidator participation, maintaining protocol solvency during market volatility.
   - Automated liquidation mechanisms execute penalty-inclusive collateral sales when loan-to-value ratios breach predetermined safety thresholds.

1. **Verb**: farm
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Blockchain/DeFi) To earn tokens by providing liquidity, staking, or participating in yield-generating protocols, typically involving moving capital between highest-APY opportunities. Transitive or intransitive verb.
   
   **Synonyms**: yield-farm, mine yields, earn rewards, maximize returns
   
   **Antonyms**: hold idle, avoid yields, simple holding
   
   **When to Use**: When describing active pursuit of token rewards through liquidity provision, staking, or protocol participation aimed at maximizing yield returns.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to simple holding, traditional mining, or non-reward-seeking token activities.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - farm tokens
   - yield farming
   - farm rewards
   - farm for APY
   - actively farm
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "feorm" (food, provisions) → farming (cultivation) → metaphor for harvesting yields
   
   **Etymology**: Old English feorm → farm (agriculture) → yield farming metaphor (2020 DeFi Summer) for earning token rewards
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm farming on three different protocols right now, chasing the highest APY.
   - Yield farmers move their capital constantly to farm the newest, highest-paying liquidity pools.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Yield farming strategies allocate capital across multiple protocols to optimize risk-adjusted returns through automated vault rebalancing mechanisms.
   - Liquidity mining programs incentivize protocol adoption through token farming rewards distributed proportionally to participant capital commitments.
