1. **Expression**: take a nap (type: collocation - verb + noun)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To sleep for a short period during the day; to rest briefly by sleeping
   - *Fixedness*: strong (this is the standard collocation; "have a nap" also possible but "take" is most common)
   
   **Synonyms**: have a nap, catch some sleep, get some rest, have a sleep, rest briefly
   
   **Antonyms**: stay awake, keep alert, remain active
   
   **When to Use**: Standard expression for describing the action of napping; all registers and contexts; most natural collocation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "make a nap" would be incorrect (common learner error); "do a nap" is also incorrect
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - have a nap (British English more common)
   - grab a nap (informal, quick)
   - get a nap (less common, but acceptable)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Take" commonly collocates with actions involving time/breaks (take a break, take time, take a rest); conventionally established; high frequency of co-occurrence makes this the natural choice
   
   **Root Analysis**: take (Old English "tacan") + nap (Middle English "nappen")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to take a quick nap before dinner.
   - She takes a nap every afternoon at 2 PM.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were instructed to take a 20-minute nap between testing sessions.
   - Employees are encouraged to take brief naps during extended shifts to maintain alertness.

1. **Expression**: power nap (type: compound noun)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A short nap, typically 10-20 minutes, taken during the day to restore energy and mental alertness quickly and efficiently
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed compound; always "power nap" not "energy nap" or "strong nap")
   
   **Synonyms**: quick nap, energy nap, strategic rest, brief nap, efficiency nap
   
   **Antonyms**: long sleep, deep sleep, extended rest, overnight sleep
   
   **When to Use**: Modern productivity contexts, workplace environments, emphasizing efficiency and quick energy restoration; business/corporate settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May sound like jargon or buzzword; not appropriate for relaxation-focused or traditional contexts; some may find term pretentious
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - strategic nap
   - efficiency nap
   - quick recharge (metaphorical equivalent)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Coined in late 20th century corporate culture; "power" suggests efficiency, strength, energy restoration; became popular with productivity optimization movement; emphasizes quick, effective energy boost
   
   **Root Analysis**: power (Old French "poeir" = ability) + nap (compound)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I just need a power nap—15 minutes and I'll be good to go.
   - Athletes swear by power naps for quick recovery.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research demonstrates that power naps of 10-20 minutes optimize alertness without inducing sleep inertia.
   - Corporate wellness programs increasingly incorporate power nap facilities to enhance employee productivity.

1. **Expression**: catch some Z's (type: idiom - fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: The letter Z represents sleep in cartoons/comics (Z-Z-Z)
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: To sleep or nap; to get some sleep
   - *Fixedness*: strong (must be "Z's" not other letters; "catch" is standard verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: get some sleep, take a nap, grab some shut-eye, get some rest, snooze
   
   **Antonyms**: stay awake, keep alert, stay up
   
   **When to Use**: Very informal, casual contexts; playful tone; primarily North American English; friendly conversations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal or professional contexts; may not be understood by non-native speakers or outside North America; written professional communication
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - catch some sleep
   - grab some Z's
   - get some Z's
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Origin from cartoon convention where "Z-Z-Z" represents snoring/sleeping sounds; became colloquial expression in mid-20th century; "catch" suggests briefly capturing something; playful, visual imagery
   
   **Root Analysis**: catch (Old French "cachier") + Z's (letter representing sleep sound)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm gonna catch some Z's before we head out tonight.
   - Let me catch a few Z's and I'll be ready to go.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts* ("obtain rest" or "sleep briefly" more appropriate)

1. **Expression**: fall asleep (type: phrasal verb - verb + adverb/preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To transition from wakefulness to sleep; to begin sleeping
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard phrasal verb; "fall asleep" not "fall sleep")
   
   **Synonyms**: drift off, drop off, nod off, go to sleep, doze off
   
   **Antonyms**: wake up, stay awake, remain conscious, come to
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the process of entering sleep; all registers; neutral and natural expression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing sleep duration rather than onset; not interchangeable with "sleep" (ongoing state)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fall into sleep (less common)
   - fall into a deep sleep (with descriptor)
   - quickly fall asleep (with manner adverb)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Fall" metaphorically suggests dropping from conscious state to unconscious; conventionally established phrasal verb; high frequency partnership; natural semantic fit (sleep as "lower" state)
   
   **Root Analysis**: fall (Old English "feallan") + asleep (a- + sleep)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I fell asleep on the couch watching TV.
   - She falls asleep the second her head hits the pillow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Subjects fell asleep within 15 minutes of the relaxation protocol initiation.
   - The ability to fall asleep quickly is associated with good sleep hygiene.

1. **Expression**: out like a light (type: idiom - fixed simile)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: Like a light that has been switched off
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: Falling asleep very quickly and deeply; unconscious immediately
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed simile; must be "like a light" not other comparisons)
   
   **Synonyms**: fell asleep immediately, passed out, crashed hard, went out cold, spark out
   
   **Antonyms**: wide awake, alert, took forever to fall asleep
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts; emphasizing sudden, complete loss of consciousness; often implies exhaustion; conversational
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal writing or professional contexts; may be confused with actual loss of consciousness/fainting; medical contexts (ambiguous)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - out cold (similar meaning)
   - out in seconds
   - lights out (related but different)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Modern idiom (20th century) comparing sudden sleep to instant darkness when light switches off; vivid imagery of immediate state change; emphasizes totality and suddenness; relates to electric lights becoming common
   
   **Root Analysis**: out (loss of consciousness/wakefulness) + like a light (simile for instant off-switch)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was so exhausted, I was out like a light the second I lay down.
   - The kids were out like a light after the busy day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; use "fell asleep immediately" in formal contexts*

1. **Expression**: hit the hay / hit the sack (type: idiom - fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: Strike hay/sack (old mattress materials)
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: To go to bed; to lie down to sleep
   - *Fixedness*: strong (must be "hay" or "sack"; "hit" is the standard verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: go to bed, turn in, call it a night, go to sleep, retire for the night
   
   **Antonyms**: get up, wake up, rise, stay up
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts; nighttime sleep primarily (less common for naps); casual, friendly tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal or professional contexts; may sound dated or rural; primarily for nighttime sleep not daytime naps
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - hit the sack (more common modern usage)
   - hit the pillow
   - crash (modern slang equivalent)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Historical origin from when mattresses were hay-filled sacks; "hit" suggests forceful contact, implying exhaustion; dates from early 20th century American slang; originally rural/working-class expression
   
   **Root Analysis**: hit (Old English "hyttan") + hay/sack (mattress materials)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm exhausted—time to hit the hay.
   - We've got an early start, so let's hit the sack now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; use "retire for the evening" or "go to sleep" in formal contexts*

   **Common Variations**: 
   - completely dead to the world
   - out to the world
   - dead asleep
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Dead" metaphorically represents complete unresponsiveness; "to the world" indicates no awareness of external environment; emphasizes totality of unconsciousness; established usage from 19th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: dead (Old English "dēad" = lifeless) + to the world (external environment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:

   - *Typically informal; use "sleeping deeply" or "unresponsive to stimuli" in formal/medical contexts*

1. **Expression**: sleep like a baby / sleep like a log (type: idiom - simile)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Sleep very deeply, peacefully, and without interruption
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed similes; "baby" and "log" are the standard comparisons)
   
   **Synonyms**: sleep soundly, sleep deeply, sleep peacefully, dead to the world
   
   **Antonyms**: toss and turn, sleep fitfully, sleep restlessly, barely sleep
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts; describing quality of sleep; emphasizing peacefulness and depth; conversational
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Like a baby" is ironic (babies often wake frequently); formal contexts; when precision matters
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - sleep like a rock
   - sleep like the dead
   - out like a log
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Baby" suggests innocent, peaceful sleep (though ironically inaccurate); "log" suggests complete immobility and heaviness; both emphasize depth and quality; established idioms from 19th century; vivid imagery
   
   **Root Analysis**: sleep + like (simile) + baby/log (comparison objects)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I slept like a baby last night—didn't wake up once.
   - After that exhausting day, I slept like a log.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Subjects reported sleeping soundly throughout the night following the intervention.
   - Participants experienced consolidated sleep with minimal awakenings.
