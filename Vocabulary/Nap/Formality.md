1. **Original Expression**: I'm going to take a nap  
   **Context**: Informing someone about your intention to rest/sleep briefly
   
   **Core Intent**: Announce your intention to sleep briefly during the day
   
   **Casual**: I'm gonna crash for a bit  
   *Characteristics*: Slang verb "crash," contracted form "gonna," informal phrase "for a bit"  
   *Use when*: Close friends, family, very relaxed settings, young people
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'm going to take a short nap  
   *Characteristics*: Complete verb form, neutral vocabulary, added descriptor "short"  
   *Use when*: Colleagues, acquaintances, general workplace, polite conversation
   
   **Formal**: I will rest briefly  
   *Characteristics*: Formal auxiliary "will," abstract noun "rest," adverb "briefly," no contractions  
   *Use when*: Professional settings, written communication, authority figures, medical contexts
   
   **Humorous**: I'm gonna grab some Z's  
   *Characteristics*: Playful idiom "grab some Z's," casual grammar  
   *Use when*: Friendly contexts, lighthearted mood, rapport-building  
   *Avoid when*: Professional settings, first meetings, serious situations
   
   **Lively**: Time for me to recharge my batteries!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic metaphor, exclamation point, enthusiastic framing  
   *Use when*: Expressing self-care positively, motivational contexts, upbeat conversations
   
   **Empathetic**: I need to rest and take care of myself for a bit  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges self-care need, gentle explanation, validation of rest  
   *Use when*: Discussing self-care, when justification is needed, supportive contexts
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher authority requires "I will rest briefly"; peers allow "gonna crash"
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/professional = formal; close relationships = casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Work context = semi-formal/formal; weekend with friends = casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures view daytime napping differently; adjust accordingly
   - *Medium*: Email/written = more formal; face-to-face = can be more casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "crash" in professional emails - too informal and may confuse non-native speakers
   - Over-explaining nap need to authority figures - brief statement sufficient
   - Using formal "I will rest" with close friends - sounds distant or pretentious
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Mediterranean and Latin cultures have siesta traditions where napping is culturally normalized; Anglo cultures may require more justification for daytime naps
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Texting your best friend → **Appropriate Variant**: "Gonna crash for a bit" (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Informing your manager → **Appropriate Variant**: "I'm going to take a short break to rest" (Semi-Formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Hospital patient to nurse → **Appropriate Variant**: "I will rest now" (Formal)

   *Characteristics*: Formal adverb "quite," standard adjective "tired," polite hedging  
   *Use when*: Workplace, general professional settings, acquaintances, neutral contexts
   
   **Formal**: I am experiencing significant fatigue  
   *Characteristics*: No contractions, clinical noun "fatigue," formal verb "experiencing," quantifier "significant"  
   *Use when*: Medical contexts, formal reports, professional documentation, authority figures
   
   **Humorous**: I'm running on fumes here  
   *Characteristics*: Metaphorical idiom, playful exaggeration, conversational tone  
   *Use when*: Light-hearted contexts, building rapport, sympathetic audience  
   *Avoid when*: Serious discussions, first impressions, may minimize genuine exhaustion
   
   **Lively**: My energy tank is completely empty!  
   *Characteristics*: Vivid metaphor, exclamation, dramatic presentation  
   *Use when*: Expressive conversations, storytelling, emphasizing exhaustion dramatically
   
   **Empathetic**: I'm feeling really exhausted and need to listen to my body  
   *Characteristics*: Validates physical needs, self-awareness language, gentle explanation  
   *Use when*: Self-care discussions, when explaining boundaries, supportive environments
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Boss/supervisor requires formal "experiencing fatigue"; peers allow "beat"
   - *Social Distance*: Professional = semi-formal/formal; intimate = casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Medical concern = formal; everyday tiredness = casual/semi-formal
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures view admitting tiredness as weakness; adjust sensitivity
   - *Medium*: Written professional = formal; verbal casual = informal okay
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "wiped out" in professional health contexts - too casual for medical discussions
   - Being overly dramatic with lively variants when situation is serious
   - Not acknowledging fatigue appropriately in cultures where rest is stigmatized
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Work cultures vary - some normalize tiredness discussions, others view it as weakness; gauge your environment
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Explaining to roommate why you're napping → **Appropriate Variant**: "I'm so beat" (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Declining meeting invitation at work → **Appropriate Variant**: "I'm feeling quite tired today" (Semi-Formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Discussing symptoms with doctor → **Appropriate Variant**: "I am experiencing significant fatigue" (Formal)

1. **Original Expression**: Don't wake me up  
   **Context**: Instructing someone not to disturb your nap
   
   **Core Intent**: Request not to be disturbed during sleep
   
   **Casual**: Let me sleep / Don't bug me  
   *Characteristics*: Imperative form, informal verb "bug," direct statement  
   *Use when*: Close relationships, family members, people who know you well
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Please don't disturb me while I'm resting  
   *Characteristics*: Polite "please," formal verb "disturb," complete explanation  
   *Use when*: Roommates, colleagues, general social situations, setting boundaries politely
   
   **Formal**: I request not to be disturbed during my rest period  
   *Characteristics*: Formal request structure, clinical phrasing "rest period," impersonal construction  
   *Use when*: Hotels, professional facilities, written notices, formal service contexts
   
   **Humorous**: I'm off to dreamland - entry forbidden!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor "dreamland," mock-serious tone "entry forbidden"  
   *Use when*: Lighthearted family/friend contexts, with children, playful mood  
   *Avoid when*: When genuinely need uninterrupted rest, serious situations
   
   **Lively**: I'm going into deep sleep mode - mission critical!  
   *Characteristics*: Dramatic framing, tech metaphor, enthusiastic intensity  
   *Use when*: Emphasizing importance playfully, younger audiences, casual but emphatic
   
   **Empathetic**: I really need this rest time for myself - I'd appreciate not being disturbed  
   *Characteristics*: Explains personal need, appreciation language, boundary-setting with kindness  
   *Use when*: Setting boundaries with family, when past interruptions occurred, assertive but kind
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Can't command authority figures; request politely. Can be direct with subordinates/children
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/service staff = formal request; family = can be direct
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Genuine need for rest = semi-formal; casual nap = casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Directness varies by culture; some require softer approaches
   - *Medium*: Written sign/note = semi-formal; verbal = can vary
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being too harsh with "don't bug me" to people being considerate - damages relationships
   - Not being clear enough about need, then being upset when disturbed
   - Using humorous variants when genuinely exhausted and needing respect
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Directness in requests varies; Asian cultures often prefer indirect requests, Western cultures accept more directness
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Teenager to sibling → **Appropriate Variant**: "Let me sleep!" (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Roommate leaving note → **Appropriate Variant**: "Please don't disturb me while I'm resting" (Semi-Formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Hotel door sign → **Appropriate Variant**: "Please do not disturb" (Formal)

1. **Original Expression**: I fell asleep

   **Core Intent**: Acknowledge unintentional sleep occurrence, often with apologetic or explanatory tone
   
   **Casual**: I totally crashed / I passed out  
   *Characteristics*: Intensifier "totally," slang verbs "crashed/passed out," informal  
   *Use when*: Friends, explaining to peers, casual conversation, emphasizing unexpectedness
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I accidentally dozed off  
   *Characteristics*: Adverb "accidentally," neutral verb "dozed off," acknowledges unintentional  
   *Use when*: Workplace, explaining missed call, professional contexts, polite explanation
   
   **Formal**: I unintentionally fell into a light sleep  
   *Characteristics*: Adverb "unintentionally," formal construction, descriptive "light sleep"  
   *Use when*: Formal apologies, professional correspondence, medical contexts, formal explanations
   
   **Humorous**: I took an unscheduled trip to dreamland  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor "dreamland," ironic "unscheduled trip"  
   *Use when*: Self-deprecating humor, friendly contexts, lightening mood  
   *Avoid when*: Serious situations where falling asleep was problematic
   
   **Lively**: My body hit the override button and knocked me out!  
   *Characteristics*: Dramatic metaphor, vivid imagery, energetic explanation  
   *Use when*: Storytelling, emphasizing exhaustion dramatically, sympathetic audience
   
   **Empathetic**: My body needed rest and I couldn't fight it anymore  
   *Characteristics*: Validates physical needs, explains exhaustion, self-compassionate  
   *Use when*: When apologizing but asserting boundaries, explaining genuine fatigue, seeking understanding
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Superior/client = formal apology; peers = casual explanation
   - *Social Distance*: Professional = semi-formal with apology; personal = casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Missed important event = formal apology; casual setting = lighthearted
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures view sleeping as lack of discipline; frame carefully
   - *Medium*: Written apology = formal; verbal explanation = can be casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "passed out" in professional contexts - implies intoxication or medical issue
   - Not apologizing when falling asleep was inappropriate or caused problems
   - Being too casual when genuine apology is warranted
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Falling asleep in meetings/social contexts interpreted differently across cultures; some see as disrespectful, others understand fatigue
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Explaining to friend who called → **Appropriate Variant**: "I totally crashed" (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Apologizing to colleague for missed call → **Appropriate Variant**: "I accidentally dozed off" (Semi-Formal)
