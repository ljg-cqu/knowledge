1. **Functional Expression**: Would you mind if I took a nap? (function: requesting permission)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request permission to sleep briefly in a polite, indirect manner
   - *Formality*: formal/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Could I possibly take a nap?, Is it okay if I rest for a bit?, May I take a short rest?, Do you mind if I nap?
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm taking a nap (direct statement), I need to sleep now (assertive)
   
   **When to Use**: When in someone else's space, workplace contexts, when permission is genuinely needed, polite social situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With very close friends/family (too formal), when you have clear right to rest, when assertion is more appropriate than permission
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would it be okay if I took a nap? (slightly less formal)
   - Do you mind if I rest for a bit? (more casual)
   - May I take a brief rest? (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses negative politeness strategy (minimizing imposition); "would you mind" construction softens request; seeks explicit permission
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Of course!" / "Go ahead!" / "Not at all, please do"
   - Declining: "Actually, I was hoping we could..." / "Could you wait until...?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would you mind if I took a quick nap? I'm really beat.
   - Do you mind if I crash on your couch for like 20 minutes?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would you mind if I took a brief rest before we continue the meeting?
   - I wonder if it would be possible for me to rest for a short period before we proceed.

1. **Functional Expression**: Why don't you take a nap? (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggest that someone should rest/sleep as a solution to tiredness or problem
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (question form softens suggestion)
   
   **Synonyms**: You should get some rest, How about taking a nap?, Maybe you should sleep for a bit, You might want to rest
   
   **Antonyms**: Don't sleep now (advising against), Stay awake (instruction), You need to keep going (pushing through)
   
   **When to Use**: When someone appears tired, offering advice or solution, caring/concerned contexts, casual helping relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When person cannot rest (work obligations), when might sound dismissive of their concerns, with authority figures (unless very close)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How about taking a quick nap? (gentler)
   - You should probably get some rest (more direct)
   - Maybe a nap would help? (tentative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Question form reduces face-threat of directive; shows concern and care; implies speaker has identified problem (tiredness)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "You know what, I think I will" / "Good idea"
   - Declining: "I wish I could, but..." / "I don't have time"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You look exhausted. Why don't you take a nap before dinner?
   - Why don't you just crash for an hour? You'll feel better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - You appear fatigued. Perhaps you should rest before the next session.
   - I would suggest taking a brief rest period to restore your energy.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd love to, but I can't right now (function: refusing/declining)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely decline invitation or suggestion to nap; express genuine desire but cite constraints
   - *Formality*: semi-formal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (softened refusal)
   
   **Synonyms**: I wish I could, but..., That sounds great, but I have to..., I really need to, but I can't, Unfortunately I can't
   
   **Antonyms**: Sure, I will (accepting), Yes, let's do it (agreeing), I don't want to (direct refusal)
   
   **When to Use**: Declining well-meaning suggestions, when work/obligations prevent rest, maintaining relationship while refusing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple "no" is sufficient, when over-explaining creates problems, when genuine consideration isn't warranted
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I wish I could, but I have work to do (more explicit reason)
   - I really want to, but not right now (timing issue)
   - Maybe later? I can't right now (counter-offer)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I'd love to" validates the suggestion before declining; "but" signals constraint, not unwillingness; maintains positive face
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Understanding: "I get it, maybe later" / "No worries"
   - Insisting: "You really should though" / "Just a quick one?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: Why don't you take a nap? B: I'd love to, but I've got this deadline.
   - I wish I could crash, but the kids will be home any minute.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I appreciate the suggestion, however I must complete this task first.
   - While I would benefit from rest, current obligations prevent me from doing so.

1. **Functional Expression**: Just to clarify, how long are you planning to nap? (function: clarifying/checking understanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request specific information about nap duration; ensure mutual understanding of plans
   - *Formality*: neutral/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct question, softened by "just to clarify"
   
   **Synonyms**: How long will you sleep?, Can you tell me when you'll be up?, What time should I expect you back?, How much time do you need?
   
   **Antonyms**: (No clear opposite; different function)
   
   **When to Use**: When timing matters for plans, coordinating schedules, managing expectations, ensuring clarity for logistics
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When duration doesn't matter, might sound controlling or mistrusting, overly detailed for casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How long do you think you'll sleep? (more casual)
   - What time will you be available? (more formal/indirect)
   - Should I wake you at a specific time? (helpful offer)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Just to clarify" frames as collaborative understanding, not interrogation; acknowledges need while seeking coordination
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Specific: "About 20 minutes" / "I'll set an alarm for 3 PM"
   - Vague: "Not long" / "Just a quick one"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just checking - how long are you gonna sleep? Should I start dinner without you?
   - How long will you be out? I need to know when to expect you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you specify the expected duration of your rest period for scheduling purposes?
   - To coordinate our activities, may I inquire about your anticipated rest duration?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm afraid I need some quiet time to rest (function: asserting boundary/need)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Assert need for rest and quiet in a polite but firm manner; set boundary
   - *Formality*: semi-formal
   - *Directness*: moderately direct (clear need, polite delivery)
   
   **Synonyms**: I need to rest now, I require some peace and quiet, I must have some uninterrupted rest, I need time to myself
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm available (offering accessibility), Keep me company (inviting interaction)
   
   **When to Use**: When boundary-setting is needed, previous interruptions occurred, when assertion is necessary but politeness important
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With very close intimates (may sound distant), when request sufficient, overly formal for casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I really need to rest now (more direct, less apologetic)
   - I'm going to need some quiet time (firm statement)
   - I need some space to sleep (spatial boundary)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I'm afraid" softens assertion; "need" emphasizes necessity; specifies both action (rest) and condition (quiet)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Of course, I understand" / "I'll make sure it's quiet"
   - Resistant: "But I just need to..." / "Can't it wait?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm afraid I need some quiet time to rest - can we talk after my nap?
   - I really need to sleep now without interruptions, okay?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I must respectfully request uninterrupted rest time for health reasons.
   - I require a period of undisturbed rest to maintain optimal functioning.

1. **Functional Expression**: If I may add, a short nap would really help your productivity (function: suggesting with hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offer suggestion tentatively with emphasis on benefits; hedge to reduce face-threat
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (hedged suggestion)
   
   **Synonyms**: I would suggest that..., Perhaps it would be beneficial to..., You might find it helpful to..., May I suggest...
   
   **Antonyms**: You must rest now (directive), Rest! (command)
   
   **When to Use**: Advising someone in professional context, when relationship requires deference, offering expert/experienced advice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Close friends (too formal), when directness is preferred, may sound condescending to equals
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - In my experience, a nap really helps (personal testimony)
   - Have you considered resting briefly? (question form)
   - You might want to think about napping (softer)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "If I may" requests permission to speak; "would help" frames benefit; focuses on positive outcome not person's deficiency
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That's a good point" / "You're probably right"
   - Declining: "I appreciate that, but..." / "I don't think so"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If I can make a suggestion, maybe grab a quick nap? You'll be way more productive.
   - You know what helps? A power nap. Just saying.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If I may suggest, a brief rest period would enhance your cognitive performance significantly.
   - Research indicates that strategic rest intervals optimize productivity; perhaps this would be beneficial.

1. **Functional Expression**: It seems to me that you need some rest (function: expressing opinion/observation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - State observation about someone's apparent tiredness; soften opinion with hedging phrase
   - *Formality*: semi-formal/neutral
   - *Directness*: moderately indirect (hedged opinion)
   
   **Synonyms**: I think you need rest, You look like you need sleep, In my view you should rest, You seem tired
   
   **Antonyms**: You look energetic (opposite observation), You're fine (dismissing concern)
   
   **When to Use**: Showing concern, when observation might help person recognize own needs, caring contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When might offend (implying they look bad), when unsolicited advice unwelcome, overly presumptuous
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You seem really tired (more casual, direct observation)
   - I get the sense you could use some rest (gentler)
   - You look like you need a nap (casual, direct)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "It seems to me" hedges certainty, reduces imposition; focuses on observable evidence; shows concern without commanding
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: "You're right, I do" / "Yeah, I'm exhausted"
   - Disagreeing: "I'm okay, really" / "I'm fine"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It seems to me that you could really use a nap right about now.
   - You look beat - I think you need some sleep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - It appears that you may benefit from a period of rest at this time.
   - Your current state suggests that recuperative rest would be advisable.

1. **Functional Expression**: Sorry to interrupt, but I need to lie down for a bit (function: interrupting/excusing oneself)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely interrupt activity/conversation to announce need to rest; apologize for disruption
   - *Formality*: neutral/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: moderately direct (clear need, apologetic delivery)
   
   **Synonyms**: Excuse me, but I need to rest, I hate to interrupt, but..., Sorry to cut this short, but...
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's keep going (continuing), I'm fine to continue (staying engaged)
   
   **When to Use**: Mid-conversation/activity when rest becomes necessary, when interruption is unavoidable, maintaining politeness while asserting need
   
   **When NOT to Use**: At natural conversation break (no interruption needed), when "excuse me" is sufficient, overly apologetic tone undermines legitimacy
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Excuse me, I need to take a break (more formal)
   - Sorry, but I have to lie down (more casual)
   - I need to interrupt - I must rest (more direct)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Sorry to interrupt" acknowledges social transgression; "but" signals override necessity; "need" emphasizes non-optional nature
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Understanding: "Of course, go ahead" / "No problem"
   - Concerned: "Are you okay?" / "Do you need anything?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry to interrupt, but I'm feeling really dizzy. I need to lie down.
   - Hate to cut you off, but I've gotta crash for a bit.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I apologize for the interruption, however I must rest immediately.
   - Excuse me, but I need to excuse myself to rest for medical reasons.

1. **Functional Expression**: Would 20 minutes be enough, or do you need longer? (function: offering/accommodating)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offer time for rest while checking adequacy; show flexibility and accommodation
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct question, collaborative tone
   
   **Synonyms**: How much time do you need?, Is 20 minutes sufficient?, What would work for you?, How long should I give you?
   
   **Antonyms**: You have 20 minutes only (dictating), Hurry up (rushing)
   
   **When to Use**: Coordinating schedules, showing flexibility, helping someone plan, accommodation while managing time
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When time is genuinely fixed (false choice), may sound passive-aggressive if constraints exist but unstated
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How long do you need? (open question)
   - Will 20 minutes work? (yes/no question)
   - Take as long as you need (maximum flexibility)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Offers specific option while inviting negotiation; shows consideration; collaborative problem-solving approach
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting offer: "20 minutes is perfect" / "That should work"
   - Negotiating: "Could I have 30?" / "Maybe a bit longer?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would 20 minutes be enough, or do you need longer? I can wait.
   - How much time do you need to nap? Half an hour okay?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would a 20-minute rest period be sufficient, or would you require additional time?
   - Please indicate the rest duration you require so we may accommodate appropriately.

1. **Functional Expression**: I suppose I could rest for a bit (function: agreeing tentatively/hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Agree to rest suggestion with hedging; shows some reluctance or consideration
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (hedged agreement)
   
   **Synonyms**: I guess I could nap, Maybe I should rest, Perhaps I will sleep briefly, I might take a quick rest
   
   **Antonyms**: Yes, definitely (enthusiastic agreement), No, I won't (refusal)
   
   **When to Use**: When agreeing but not fully convinced, showing consideration of advice, when authority/social pressure influences decision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When genuine agreement exists (sounds reluctant unnecessarily), may communicate unwillingness when compliance expected
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I guess I should rest (slightly more convinced)
   - Maybe you're right, I'll nap (acknowledging advisor)
   - Alright, I'll try to rest (more cooperative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I suppose" hedges commitment; shows consideration rather than immediate acceptance; may indicate social pressure or uncertainty
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Encouraging: "I think it will help" / "Good idea"
   - Accepting: "Okay, get some rest"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I suppose I could rest for a bit, since you're all insisting.
   - Yeah, I guess I should probably nap. I am pretty tired.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Upon consideration, I believe a brief rest may be advisable.
   - I concede that rest would be beneficial at this juncture.
