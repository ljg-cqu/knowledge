1. **Expression**: can’t do without (someone/something) (type: idiom/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be unable to manage well or be happy if a particular person or thing is not present; to see them as indispensable in daily life or work.
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning vs. Figurative/Idiomatic meaning → Literally, "not able to act in the absence of"; figuratively, "treat as essential or non-negotiable."
   - *Fixedness*: strong (the wording is quite fixed; small variations like "couldn’t do without" are common but the core pattern remains).
   
   **Synonyms**: be unable to live without, depend heavily on, rely on completely, be lost without, be essential to
   
   **Antonyms**: be able to manage without, do fine without, not need, be optional
   
   **When to Use**: Use this expression in everyday conversations to emphasize the importance of something or someone. It is suitable for informal to neutral registers and can be used in various contexts, such as discussing habits, tools, or people that feel indispensable.
   - Everyday speech about tools, habits, or people that feel indispensable ("I can’t do without my morning coffee").
   - Light, friendly emphasis on dependence in teams or families.
   - Works in informal to neutral registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using this expression in very formal writing or when you need emotionally neutral language about system requirements.
   - Very formal writing or technical reports; there, use "cannot operate without" or "is essential to."
   - Contexts where you want to avoid sounding emotionally dependent.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - couldn’t do without
   - can’t live without
   - really can’t do without
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: N/A (idiom)
   - *For idioms*: Combines the modal "can’t" with "do without" to stress impossibility of functioning in the absence of something; very frequent in everyday English.
   
   **Root Analysis**: can (ability) + not + do (act, manage) + without (in the absence of)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can’t do without this note-taking app anymore; it’s become my second brain.
   - They can’t do without her on the team—she keeps everything running.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In practice, we cannot do without a reliable audit trail in a regulated environment.
   - Modern operations can hardly do without some form of automated monitoring.

1. **Expression**: make-or-break factor (type: collocation/idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A factor that is so important it will either ensure success or cause failure; a decisive, indispensable element.
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning vs. Figurative/Idiomatic meaning → Literally, "to make something succeed or break it"; figuratively, "a single high-impact condition that determines the outcome."
   - *Fixedness*: medium/strong ("make-or-break" is a set phrase, but the noun after it can vary: factor, moment, decision, deadline).
   
   **Synonyms**: decisive factor, critical factor, key determinant, turning point
   
   **Antonyms**: minor detail, side issue, background factor, nice-to-have
   
   **When to Use**: Use this expression when discussing strategies, negotiations, or projects where one condition is truly indispensable. It is suitable for neutral to informal business English, presentations, and analysis.
   - Discussing strategies, negotiations, or projects where one condition is truly indispensable.
   - Neutral to informal business English, presentations, and analysis.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using this expression in very formal academic writing where "decisive factor" or "determinant" is preferred.
   - Very formal academic writing where "decisive factor" or "determinant" is preferred.
   - Overstating small influences as "make-or-break" can reduce credibility.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - make-or-break moment
   - make-or-break decision
   - make-or-break deadline
   - make-or-break test
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: The hyphenated pair "make-or-break" functions as a fixed modifier that signals extreme importance; pairing it with nouns like "factor" or "moment" is conventional and frequent.
   - *For idioms*: It condenses the idea that one thing both holds the potential to create success and to destroy it.
   
   **Root Analysis**: make (create, achieve success) + or + break (destroy, cause failure)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Good onboarding can be a make-or-break factor for keeping new hires.
   - For some students, the final exam is the make-or-break moment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Trust in leadership often becomes the make-or-break factor during organizational change.
   - Robust key management is a make-or-break factor in any cryptographic deployment.

1. **Expression**: non-negotiable requirement (type: collocation/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A condition or standard that must be met and will not be changed or traded away; something treated as indispensable.
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning vs. Figurative/Idiomatic meaning → Literally, "a requirement that cannot be negotiated"; figuratively, "a rule or value seen as untouchable."
   - *Fixedness*: medium ("non-negotiable" and "requirement" occur together often; the noun can vary: condition, rule, boundary).
   
   **Synonyms**: mandatory requirement, fixed condition, hard requirement, absolute must
   
   **Antonyms**: flexible guideline, optional preference, negotiable term
   
   **When to Use**: [Context description - domains, situations, register levels, cultural contexts]
   - In policies, agreements, and team norms where you need to mark indispensable items clearly.
   - In professional, semi-formal, and formal settings to express strong boundaries.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: [Inappropriate contexts - unnatural combinations, wrong register, cultural misunderstandings, commonly broken by learners]
   - When you are actually open to compromise; calling something "non-negotiable" and then negotiating it can damage trust.
   - In very casual chat where simpler phrases like "must-have" or "not optional" may feel more natural.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - non-negotiable condition
   - non-negotiable rule
   - non-negotiable boundary
   - make this non-negotiable
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "non-negotiable" is commonly used with nouns that express conditions (requirement, condition, boundary), and "requirement" makes the indispensability explicit.
   - *For idioms*: It signals that certain elements are not up for trade in any deal or plan.
   
   **Root Analysis**: non- (not) + negotiable (able to be discussed or changed) + requirement (something demanded or needed)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - For me, mutual respect is a non-negotiable requirement in any relationship.
   - A working backup is a non-negotiable requirement before we touch production.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Data integrity is a non-negotiable requirement in regulated environments.
   - For this role, ethical conduct is explicitly defined as a non-negotiable requirement.
