1. **Functional Expression**: Sorry, I’m running a bit late this morning. (function: apologizing/explaining delay)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to apologize for not arriving on time and briefly explain that the reason is a busy or delayed morning.
   - *Formality*: neutral/informal
   - *Directness*: indirect apology + explanation
   
   **Synonyms**: I’m afraid I’m running late this morning., I’m a little behind schedule this morning., I might be a few minutes late.
   
   **Antonyms**: I’m right on time., I arrived early., I’m ahead of schedule.
   
   **When to Use**: Informing colleagues, classmates, or friends that you will not arrive exactly on time for a morning class, meeting, or appointment.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal emails to clients or senior executives (use a more formal apology), or when you will be significantly late and need to propose a reschedule.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I’m running a little late this morning.
   - Sorry, I’m a bit behind this morning.
   - I’ll be a few minutes late—morning’s been hectic.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Politeness strategy combining apology + brief reason; softens the impact of lateness and shows respect for others’ time.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That’s okay, thanks for letting us know.
   - No worries, we’ll start when you arrive.
   - Thanks for the heads-up—see you soon.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry, I’m running a bit late this morning—traffic is worse than usual.
   - Hey, I’m a little behind this morning; I’ll join the call in about ten minutes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I’m afraid I’m running slightly behind schedule this morning but will join the meeting as soon as possible.
   - Due to unexpected delays, I may be a few minutes late this morning; thank you for your understanding.

1. **Functional Expression**: Could we start a little later this morning? (function: making a polite request)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A soft, polite way to ask for a later start time for a morning activity or meeting.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect request using modal "could"
   
   **Synonyms**: Would it be possible to start a bit later this morning?, Do you mind if we begin a little later?, Could we push the start time back slightly?
   
   **Antonyms**: Let’s start earlier., We need to start on time., We have to begin promptly at 8.
   
   **When to Use**: Asking teammates or a teacher/manager (if relationship allows) to delay a morning start due to scheduling or energy reasons.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For formal events with fixed times (conferences, exams), or when you don’t have the authority and the impact would be large.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could we push the meeting back by 15 minutes?
   - Would it be okay to start a bit later this morning?
   - Is it possible to begin at nine instead of eight-thirty?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses indirectness and tentative language ("could," "a little") to reduce imposition and show respect for others’ schedules.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, that’s fine—let’s start at nine.
   - I’m afraid we have to keep the original time.
   - We can move it a little, but not too much.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could we start a little later this morning? My train only gets in at nine.
   - Do you mind if we push our coffee chat back by half an hour?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would it be possible to begin the morning session at 9:30 a.m. instead of 9:00 a.m.?
   - If everyone agrees, could we start tomorrow’s briefing a little later to accommodate commuting times?

1. **Functional Expression**: How did your morning go? (function: checking in/maintaining relationships)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A friendly way to ask someone about the first part of their day and how they are feeling now.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but gentle question
   
   **Synonyms**: How’s your morning been?, Did you have a busy morning?, How has your day started?
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (opposite function would be ignoring or not asking)
   
   **When to Use**: Morning or late-morning small talk, team check-ins, casual relationship-building.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When time is extremely limited and you need to go straight to business, or when the person looks clearly distressed and a more empathetic opening is needed.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How’s your morning going so far?
   - Busy morning?
   - Has your day started okay?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Functions as a social lubricant; invites short sharing and signals interest in the person, not just the work.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Pretty good, thanks. How about yours?
   - It’s been hectic, to be honest.
   - Not bad—just a typical Monday morning.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Morning! How’s your morning going so far?
   - Hey, did you have a crazy morning or was it okay?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I hope your morning has been going well so far.
   - Before we begin, how has everyone’s morning been?

1. **Functional Expression**: Let’s quickly go over today’s plan. (function: organizing/leading a brief review)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A way to introduce a short, structured overview of tasks or schedule at the start of the day.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct invitation with inclusive "let’s"
   
   **Synonyms**: Let’s take a quick look at today’s agenda., Let’s briefly review what we’ve got on this morning., Shall we run through today’s priorities?
   
   **Antonyms**: Let’s just get started without a plan., We don’t need to review anything.
   
   **When to Use**: Beginning of a team meeting, morning stand-up, or one-on-one check-in to align on tasks.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When participants already clearly know the plan and time is extremely limited, or in very hierarchical cultures where only the leader should initiate.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let’s quickly run through today’s agenda.
   - Let’s go over what we need to do this morning.
   - Shall we take a minute to review the plan for today?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines leadership and collaboration; "let’s" includes everyone, "quickly" reassures about time.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, that sounds good.
   - Yes, that would help.
   - We already have the agenda—can we jump straight in?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Okay, before we dive in, let’s quickly go over today’s plan.
   - Let’s run through what we’ve got on this morning so nothing gets missed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before we begin the main discussion, let’s briefly review today’s agenda.
   - To ensure we’re aligned, let’s quickly go over the plan for this morning’s session.

1. **Functional Expression**: I’ll grab a coffee and be right back. (function: announcing a brief absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Informing others that you will step away for a short time in the morning to get coffee and then return.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct statement of intention
   
   **Synonyms**: I’m just going to get a coffee., I’ll step out for a minute to grab some coffee., I’ll be back in a moment—I’m getting a drink.
   
   **Antonyms**: I’m leaving for the day., I won’t be back., I need to step out for a long time.
   
   **When to Use**: During a break in a morning meeting, before starting a call, or when leaving your desk briefly.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal, high-stakes meetings (ask permission more formally), or when your absence will significantly disrupt progress.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I’ll be right back—just grabbing a coffee.
   - I’m going to get some coffee; back in a minute.
   - I’ll step away for a quick coffee.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals both reason and duration; helps manage expectations so people know you are not gone for long.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, no problem.
   - Okay, we’ll wait for you.
   - Grab one for me too? (joking/request)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’ll grab a coffee and be right back before the stand-up starts.
   - I’m just going to get some coffee—back in two minutes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If you’ll excuse me for a moment, I’d like to get some coffee before we continue.
   - I’ll step out briefly to get some coffee and return before the next item on the agenda.
