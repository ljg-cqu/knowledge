1. **Noun**: morning (C/U, pl: mornings)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The early part of the day, from the time you wake up or from sunrise until around noon.
   - *(Extended)* The period when daily activities begin, especially work or school.
   
   **Synonyms**: early day, first part of the day, a.m. hours (informal)
   
   **Antonyms**: evening, night, afternoon
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about daily routines, times of day, schedules, and how you feel or what you do at the start of the day.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When talking specifically about late afternoon or night (use "afternoon" or "evening"), or about a single moment (use a clock time instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - early morning
   - in the morning
   - tomorrow morning
   - Monday morning
   - morning routine
   
   **Root Analysis**: morn (early day, dawn) + -ing (time or action noun)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English *morgen* meaning "morning, the early part of the day." Related to Old High German *morgen* and Old Norse *morgunn*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’m useless in the morning until I’ve had my coffee.
   - Let’s go for a run tomorrow morning before it gets too hot.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The meeting is scheduled for tomorrow morning at 9:00 a.m.
   - Morning productivity tends to be higher for individuals who follow a consistent sleep schedule.

1. **Noun**: dawn (C/U, pl: dawns)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The first light of day, when the sun is just starting to appear but is not yet fully above the horizon.
   - *(Figurative)* The beginning of a period or development.
   
   **Synonyms**: daybreak, sunrise (near-synonym), first light, crack of dawn (informal)
   
   **Antonyms**: dusk, sunset, nightfall
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the very start of the day, poetic or descriptive language about light, or the beginning of a new phase.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For the whole morning (use "morning"), or for the period when the sun is already high (use "mid-morning" or "daytime").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at dawn
   - before dawn
   - the dawn of a new day
   - the dawn of a new era
   - from dawn till dusk
   
   **Root Analysis**: Plain root noun; historical connection to verbs meaning "to become day" or "to grow light"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English *dagian* (to become day) and *dawn* as a noun meaning "daybreak." Related to German *tagen*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We left at dawn to avoid the morning traffic.
   - The sky looks beautiful at dawn when the colors start to change.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many farmers begin work at dawn to take advantage of the cooler temperatures.
   - At dawn, light levels increase rapidly, triggering biological rhythms in many species.

1. **Noun**: sunrise (C, pl: sunrises)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The time in the morning when the sun first appears over the horizon.
   - The view or event of the sun appearing in the sky at that time.
   
   **Synonyms**: daybreak, sunup, dawn (near-synonym)
   
   **Antonyms**: sunset, sundown
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about specific times of day, photography, scenery, or early-morning activities tied to the sun appearing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For the general early part of the day (use "morning"), or when light appears but the sun is not yet visible (some speakers prefer "dawn").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at sunrise
   - before sunrise
   - watch the sunrise
   - sunrise yoga
   - sunrise view
   
   **Root Analysis**: sun (the star) + rise (to go up)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → literal compound of "sun" + "rise." Widely used in navigation, weather reports, and daily schedules.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We got up early to watch the sunrise at the beach.
   - From my window I get a beautiful sunrise every morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The experiment began precisely at sunrise to control for light conditions.
   - Sunrise times shift gradually throughout the year due to the tilt of the Earth’s axis.

1. **Noun**: alarm clock (C, pl: alarm clocks)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A clock that you can set to make a sound at a particular time to wake you up.
   - *(Extended)* Any device or app that plays a sound to wake someone at a chosen time.
   
   **Synonyms**: alarm, bedside clock, phone alarm (informal)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (no direct lexical opposite; conceptually "silent clock")
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about waking up, morning routines, punctuality, or oversleeping.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring only to the alarm sound inside a phone or watch (you can just say "alarm"), or when talking about general time-keeping without a wake-up function (use "clock").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - set the alarm clock
   - turn off the alarm clock
   - hit the snooze button on the alarm clock
   - wake up to the alarm clock
   - alarm clock goes off
   
   **Root Analysis**: alarm (warning sound) + clock (time-keeping device)
   
   **Etymology**: "Alarm" from Italian *all’arma* (to arms), later generalized to "warning," plus "clock" from Middle Dutch *clokke* (bell). The compound "alarm clock" became common in the 19th–20th centuries.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I forgot to set my alarm clock and almost missed my morning class.
   - My alarm clock goes off at 6:30 every weekday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Reliance on an alarm clock can disrupt natural sleep cycles if wake-up times vary significantly.
   - Many people now use smartphone alarms instead of a traditional alarm clock.

1. **Noun**: routine (C/U, pl: routines)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A fixed way of doing something, usually repeated regularly.
   - A set of usual actions you do in the same order each day, especially in the morning or evening.
   
   **Synonyms**: habit, schedule, pattern, daily ritual, set of habits
   
   **Antonyms**: spontaneity, randomness, chaos, one-off event
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about regular morning activities such as washing, eating breakfast, commuting, or getting children ready for school.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For a single event or special occasion (use "event" or "plan"), or for a performance in entertainment contexts (clarify "comedy routine," "dance routine").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - morning routine
   - daily routine
   - break your routine
   - get into a routine
   - stick to a routine
   
   **Root Analysis**: rout(e) (way, path) + -ine (forming nouns/adjectives about type or pattern)
   
   **Etymology**: French → from French *routine* meaning "usual course, way," from *route* (road, way). Adopted into English in the 17th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My morning routine includes stretching, coffee, and checking my email.
   - Once you have a routine, getting up early feels much easier.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A consistent morning routine is associated with better time management and reduced stress.
   - The study examined how small changes to participants’ morning routines affected their mood throughout the day.

1. **Noun**: commute (C, pl: commutes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The journey you make regularly between your home and your workplace or school, especially in the morning and evening.
   - *(Extended)* The experience or time spent traveling to and from work.
   
   **Synonyms**: journey to work, trip to the office, daily travel
   
   **Antonyms**: remote work (conceptual opposite), work-from-home arrangement
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about travel time at the start and end of the workday, transportation choices, and morning traffic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For travel during holidays or irregular trips (use "trip" or "journey"), or when distance is very short and not regular (use "walk" or "drive" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - morning commute
   - daily commute
   - long commute
   - commute time
   - stuck in the commute
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + mute (change) → originally "to change, exchange" (as in swapping a penalty)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin *commutare* (to change, exchange). The noun "commute" in the sense of "journey to work" developed in American English in the early 20th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My morning commute takes about forty minutes by subway.
   - Since I moved closer to the office, my commute is much easier.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The average urban morning commute has increased in length over the past decade.
   - Flexible work policies can significantly reduce the environmental impact of daily commutes.

1. **Noun**: breakfast (C/U, pl: breakfasts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The first meal of the day, usually eaten in the morning.
   - *(Extended)* A morning meal event, often shared socially or used for business meetings.
   
   **Synonyms**: first meal, morning meal, brunch (if later and heavier)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (conceptual opposite would be "skip breakfast")
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about eating in the morning, nutrition, daily habits, or meetings that happen over the first meal of the day.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For meals eaten at noon or later (use "lunch" or "brunch"), or when someone eats their first meal in the evening (specify "first meal" if needed).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have breakfast
   - skip breakfast
   - make breakfast
   - a quick breakfast
   - breakfast meeting
   
   **Root Analysis**: break (stop, interrupt) + fast (period without eating)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → "to break one’s fast" after sleeping. Over time, the phrase fused into the single word "breakfast."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I usually have a light breakfast and a big lunch.
   - We had a long chat over breakfast this morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regular breakfast consumption is associated with improved concentration in the morning.
   - Many organizations use informal breakfast meetings to discuss priorities at the start of the day.

1. **Noun**: rush hour (C/U, pl: rush hours)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The busy time of day when roads and public transport are very crowded because many people are traveling to or from work or school.
   - Often refers specifically to the morning and evening periods of peak traffic.
   
   **Synonyms**: peak hour, peak time, morning traffic, commuting time
   
   **Antonyms**: off-peak hours, quiet period, midday lull
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about heavy traffic, crowded trains or buses, and delays during the morning commute.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For quiet or flexible travel times, or in small towns with little difference between busy and quiet periods.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - morning rush hour
   - beat the rush hour
   - stuck in rush hour
   - avoid rush hour traffic
   - rush-hour congestion
   
   **Root Analysis**: rush (sudden or busy movement) + hour (unit of time)
   
   **Etymology**: Modern English → developed in the late 19th and early 20th centuries with urbanization and public transport systems.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If we leave after morning rush hour, the drive will be much easier.
   - The train is packed during rush hour, especially on Monday mornings.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Public transport systems experience their highest load during morning and evening rush hours.
   - Flexible starting times can help reduce rush-hour congestion in large cities.
