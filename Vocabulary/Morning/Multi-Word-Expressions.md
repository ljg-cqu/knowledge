1. **Expression**: morning person (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Someone who feels more energetic, alert, and productive in the morning than later in the day.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very common and natural expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: early riser, lark (metaphor), early bird (near-synonym)
   
   **Antonyms**: night owl, evening person, late-night person
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about personal preferences for time of day, discussing productivity patterns, describing yourself or others.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal writing (use "morning-type person" or "individual with a morning chronotype"), or when talking about one unusual day (use "I woke up early today" instead).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I’m a morning person.
   - She’s definitely not a morning person.
   - morning-person type
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Morning" + "person" combine to describe someone whose natural energy peak is early in the day, contrasting with "night owl."
   
   **Root Analysis**: morning (time of day) + person (human individual)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’m such a morning person—I get my best work done before 10 a.m.
   - He’s not a morning person at all; don’t ask him anything difficult before coffee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Morning people often schedule their most demanding tasks for the first part of the day.
   - Self-reported "morning person" status often correlates with earlier sleep and wake times.

1. **Expression**: early bird (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Someone who wakes up early or arrives early.
   - *Fixedness*: strong; also appears in the proverb "The early bird catches the worm."
   
   **Synonyms**: early riser, morning person (near-synonym)
   
   **Antonyms**: night owl, latecomer
   
   **When to Use**: Informal descriptions of people who like early mornings or arrive before others.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal reports (use "early riser"), or when no time reference is involved.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I’m an early bird.
   - early-bird discount (extended marketing use)
   - early birds in the office
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - Metaphor from birds that look for food early in the day, suggesting advantage in being early.
   
   **Root Analysis**: early (time) + bird (animal associated with dawn)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’m an early bird, so morning meetings work well for me.
   - The trains are full of early birds commuting before rush hour.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - So-called "early birds" may benefit from quieter conditions during the first work hours.
   - Early-bird attendance patterns can reduce crowding during the morning rush.

1. **Expression**: sleep in (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To stay in bed and wake up later than usual, especially on weekends or holidays.
   - *Fixedness*: strong as a phrasal verb; sometimes written "sleep-in" as noun.
   
   **Synonyms**: have a lie-in (BrE), stay in bed late, get up late
   
   **Antonyms**: get up early, rise early, wake up at the crack of dawn
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about relaxed mornings, days off, or catching up on sleep.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When lateness causes problems (use "oversleep"), or in very formal writing.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - sleep in on weekends
   - have a sleep-in
   - plan to sleep in tomorrow
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Sleep" + particle "in" emphasizes staying inside bed or inside the usual sleep period, extending it.
   
   **Root Analysis**: sleep (verb) + in (particle indicating inside/longer)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I usually sleep in on Sunday mornings if I don’t have plans.
   - We finally had a chance to sleep in after a busy week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many participants reported "sleeping in" on days without fixed morning commitments.
   - Allowing individuals to sleep in occasionally may help compensate for chronic weekday sleep loss.

1. **Expression**: hit the snooze (button) (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To press the snooze button on an alarm clock or phone to delay waking up for a few more minutes.
   - *Fixedness*: strong; very common in everyday speech.
   
   **Synonyms**: snooze the alarm, delay the alarm, put the alarm on snooze
   
   **Antonyms**: get up immediately, turn the alarm off and get up
   
   **When to Use**: Describing small extra periods of sleep in the morning; talking about difficulty waking up.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal reports (say "use the snooze function"), or when there's no alarm involved.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - hit the snooze button
   - keep hitting snooze
   - hit snooze three times
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Hit" (press quickly) + "snooze" (short light sleep) describe the quick, repeated action of delaying an alarm.
   
   **Root Analysis**: hit (strike/press) + snooze (short sleep)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I hit the snooze button so many times that I missed my morning bus.
   - If I hit snooze once, I’ll hit it three times.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Frequent use of the "snooze" function suggests difficulty transitioning from sleep to wakefulness in the morning.
   - Participants who regularly hit snooze reported feeling more tired during the first hour of the day.

1. **Expression**: bright and early (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very early in the morning, often with a positive or energetic feeling.
   - *Fixedness*: strong; set phrase.
   
   **Synonyms**: first thing in the morning, at the crack of dawn, very early
   
   **Antonyms**: late in the morning, at the last minute, late at night
   
   **When to Use**: Talking cheerfully about starting something early, promising to arrive early, or describing a fresh start.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In serious or technical writing (use specific times instead), or when the early start feels negative.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - up bright and early
   - be there bright and early
   - start bright and early
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Bright" evokes morning light and energetic mood; "early" specifies time. Together they suggest a fresh, optimistic start.
   
   **Root Analysis**: bright (full of light) + early (near the start of the day)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’ll be up bright and early tomorrow for our morning hike.
   - I like to get to the office bright and early on Mondays.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Better to avoid; use a precise time instead in formal writing*
   - The workshop begins at 8:00 a.m., an intentionally early start.

1. **Expression**: first thing in the morning (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At the very beginning of the day, soon after waking.
   - *Fixedness*: strong; very common in spoken English.
   
   **Synonyms**: as soon as I wake up, right after waking, at the start of the day
   
   **Antonyms**: last thing at night, later in the day, toward the evening
   
   **When to Use**: Describing priority tasks, habits, or checks done immediately after waking.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the timing is vague (use a specific time), or in very formal research writing (use "immediately after waking" instead).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - check email first thing in the morning
   - drink water first thing in the morning
   - do this first thing tomorrow morning
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "First thing" refers to the first action; adding "in the morning" anchors it to the time of day.
   
   **Root Analysis**: first (earliest) + thing (action/task) + in the morning (time phrase)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I stretch first thing in the morning to wake my body up.
   - Please call me first thing in the morning so we can fix this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants completed a short survey first thing in the morning on each study day.
   - The maintenance team checks critical systems first thing in the morning.

1. **Expression**: at the crack of dawn (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very early in the morning, at or just before sunrise.
   - *Fixedness*: strong; colorful, slightly literary.
   
   **Synonyms**: at daybreak, at first light, very early in the morning
   
   **Antonyms**: late in the day, around noon, late at night
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing how early something happens, often with a dramatic or humorous tone.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral technical writing (use "at sunrise" or a time), or if the time is not actually very early.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - up at the crack of dawn
   - leave at the crack of dawn
   - start work at the crack of dawn
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Crack" suggests the first sudden line of light breaking through darkness; "dawn" is sunrise.
   
   **Root Analysis**: crack (sudden split/opening) + of + dawn (first light of day)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We left at the crack of dawn to avoid the morning traffic.
   - My parents like to get up at the crack of dawn even on holidays.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Avoid in formal contexts; use "at sunrise" or "early in the morning" instead*
   - Field observations began at sunrise each morning.

1. **Expression**: morning rush hour (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The busy time in the morning when roads and public transport are very crowded because many people are traveling to work or school.
   - *Fixedness*: strong; standard traffic/transport phrase.
   
   **Synonyms**: morning peak, peak hour traffic, rush-hour period
   
   **Antonyms**: off-peak time, quiet period, midday lull
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about commuting, scheduling, travel times, or traffic conditions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For evenings (use "evening rush hour"), or in very rural contexts with no real traffic differences.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - during morning rush hour
   - avoid the morning rush hour
   - stuck in the morning rush hour
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Morning" specifies time of day; "rush hour" names the peak traffic period. Together they form a precise label for the busiest commuting time.
   
   **Root Analysis**: morning (time) + rush (sudden busy movement) + hour (time unit)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If we leave after morning rush hour, the drive will be much easier.
   - The subway is packed during the morning rush hour.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Travel times are longest during morning rush hour due to congestion.
   - Many companies shift start times to reduce pressure on morning rush-hour traffic.
