1. Q: What is the page-level essence (purpose, scope, and organizing structure) of Morning vocabulary?
   A:
   - **Purpose**: Provide a compact set of core morning-related concepts and terms that let you describe daily routines, time of day, feelings, and typical morning activities clearly in English.
   - **Scope**: Focus on high-leverage vocabulary for talking about everyday mornings (waking up, getting ready, commuting, early work or study) and how people feel and behave at that time. Excludes detailed sleep science, late-night activities, and specialized professional routines (for example, medical or shift-work contexts).
   - **Central Questions**:
     - How do you describe what typically happens from waking up until late morning in simple, precise English?
     - Which few words let you talk about time, light, energy level, and basic morning habits without long explanations?
     - How can you explain differences between calm vs rushed mornings and their impact on the rest of the day?
     - How do you talk about planning, goals, and mood at the start of the day?
   - **Organizing Dimensions**:
     - Time & rhythm (early / late morning, weekday vs weekend, routine vs exception).
     - Activities & habits (wake-up, hygiene, breakfast, commute, exercise, planning).
     - Energy & mood (sleepy, refreshed, rushed, relaxed, grumpy, motivated).
     - Context & environment (home, street, transport, workplace/school; quiet vs busy).
   - **Minimal Glossary**:
     - Morning: The part of the day from when you wake up until around midday.
     - Routine: A set of actions you do in the same order regularly, often every day.
     - Alarm clock: A device or phone function that wakes you up at a set time.
     - Commute: The regular journey you make between home and work or school.
     - Rush hour: The busy time when many people travel to or from work or school.
     - Refreshed: Feeling awake and full of new energy after rest or sleep.
     - Sleepy: Feeling tired and wanting to sleep, not fully awake yet.
   - **Wiki Neighborhood**:
     - **Upstream / Parent**: Time-of-day vocabulary; Daily routine; Sleep & rest.
     - **Siblings**: Evening vocabulary; Weekend & holiday routines; Workday schedule; Breakfast & coffee vocabulary.
     - **Downstream**: Productivity and habit formation; Well-being and mental health in daily life; Describing a typical day essays.
   - **Decision-Critical Metadata**:
     - **Decision Criticality**: Blocks, Roles.
     - **Primary Stakeholders / Roles**: English learners, teachers, textbook writers, productivity coaches, content creators.
     - **Time Sensitivity**: Evergreen.

1. Q: Essence card – Time & daily rhythm
   A:
   - **Label**: Time & daily rhythm – defining the morning window
   - **Core Idea**: A few time expressions like early morning, late morning, weekday, and weekend morning let you quickly place events on the daily timeline and compare different kinds of mornings.
   - **Why It Matters**: Without clear time phrases, you cannot explain when things happen, plan schedules, or describe contrasts such as "busy weekday mornings" vs "slow weekend mornings," which appear often in conversations and writing tasks.
   - **Type**: concept
   - **Dimensions**: Time & rhythm; Context = weekday vs weekend.
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - early morning, late morning, weekday, weekend, schedule, routine

1. Q: Essence card – Wake-up & getting ready
   A:
   - **Label**: Wake-up & getting ready – from alarm to out the door
   - **Core Idea**: Words and phrases for waking up (alarm goes off, hit snooze, get up), basic hygiene (brush your teeth, take a shower, get dressed), and preparing to leave form the backbone of any morning description.
   - **Why It Matters**: These terms are needed to tell simple stories about your day, explain why you are late or on time, and follow or give instructions; without them, you can only say vague things like "I do some things and then go out."
   - **Type**: workflow
   - **Dimensions**: Activities & habits; Context = home.
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - wake up, get up, alarm clock, hit snooze, get dressed, brush your teeth, take a shower

1. Q: Essence card – Breakfast & first energy
   A:
   - **Label**: Breakfast & first energy
   - **Core Idea**: Terms like breakfast, grab a quick bite, skip breakfast, and have coffee or tea capture how people get their first energy in the morning and how this affects mood and performance.
   - **Why It Matters**: Many daily conversations, health tips, and productivity discussions mention breakfast habits; being able to say whether you eat properly, rush with only coffee, or often skip breakfast shapes how others understand your lifestyle.
   - **Type**: pattern
   - **Dimensions**: Activities & habits; Energy & mood.
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - breakfast, grab a quick bite, skip breakfast, coffee, tea, hungry, full

1. Q: Essence card – Commute & environment
   A:
   - **Label**: Commute & morning environment
   - **Core Idea**: Commute-related words like rush hour, traffic jam, crowded, platform, bus stop, and quiet street let you describe the outside world you move through between home and work or school.
   - **Why It Matters**: These terms are crucial for explaining stress, delays, and mood during the morning; they appear in news, small talk, and practical instructions (for example, "Leave early to avoid rush hour").
   - **Type**: concept
   - **Dimensions**: Context & environment; Time & rhythm.
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - commute, rush hour, traffic jam, crowded, platform, bus stop, quiet street

1. Q: Essence card – Energy, mood & start-of-day mindset
   A:
   - **Label**: Energy, mood & start-of-day mindset
   - **Core Idea**: A small set of adjectives and phrases (sleepy, refreshed, in a rush, calm, grumpy, in a good mood, set an intention, plan your day) lets you describe how people feel and think at the start of the day.
   - **Why It Matters**: Morning mood strongly shapes the rest of the day and often appears in diaries, coaching, and well-being discussions; without precise words for energy and emotion, you can only say "good" or "bad," which hides useful detail.
   - **Type**: concept
   - **Dimensions**: Energy & mood; Activities & habits.
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - sleepy, refreshed, in a rush, calm, grumpy, in a good mood, set an intention, plan your day
