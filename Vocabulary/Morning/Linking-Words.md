1. **Linking Word**: before (type: subordinating conjunction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce an action or time that happens earlier than another action or time.
   - *Function*: Marks time sequence in morning routines, showing what happens earlier.
   
   **Synonyms**: prior to, earlier than, ahead of (time), in advance of
   
   **Antonyms**: after, once, when (for later or same time)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing what you do earlier in the morning compared with something else ("Before I leave, I have coffee").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For causes or reasons (use "because"), or for conditions (use "if").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: Before + [clause], [main clause]  
     [Main clause] before + [clause]
   - **Position**: Sentence-initial or mid-sentence
   - **Punctuation**: Comma after "before" clause at the start of the sentence
   - **Typical Morning Uses**: before sunrise, before work, before breakfast, before I get up
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + fore (in front of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *beforan* meaning "in front of, earlier than."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Before I check my phone, I like to drink a glass of water in the morning.
   - I always stretch before I have breakfast.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before employees open their inbox in the morning, they are encouraged to review their priorities.
   - Participants completed a short alertness test before starting the morning task.

1. **Linking Word**: after (type: subordinating conjunction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce an action or time that happens later than another action or time.
   - *Function*: Shows what comes next in a morning sequence.
   
   **Synonyms**: once, when (in some contexts), following, later than
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, earlier than
   
   **When to Use**: Describing what you do next in the morning—after waking up, after breakfast, after arriving at work.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For reasons (use "because"), or for contrast (use "although" or "but").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: After + [clause], [main clause]  
     [Main clause] after + [clause or noun phrase]
   - **Position**: Sentence-initial or mid-sentence
   - **Punctuation**: Comma after initial "after" clause
   - **Typical Morning Uses**: after I wake up, after breakfast, after the morning meeting
   
   **Root Analysis**: aft (behind) + -er (comparative/relational ending)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *æfter* meaning "behind, later than."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After I wake up, I usually lie in bed for a few minutes.
   - I answer messages after my morning coffee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After the morning briefing, staff begin work on their individual tasks.
   - Alertness levels increased significantly after participants had breakfast.

1. **Linking Word**: when (type: subordinating conjunction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce the time at which something happens, often linking a routine trigger and action.
   - *Function*: Connects a specific morning moment with what happens at that time.
   
   **Synonyms**: at the time (that), once, whenever (for repeated times)
   
   **Antonyms**: while (for overlap/contrast), before, after
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about habitual morning patterns triggered by a time or event ("When the alarm rings, I..." ).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For strict cause (use "because"), or for non-time conditions (use "if").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: When + [clause], [main clause]  
     [Main clause] when + [clause]
   - **Position**: Sentence-initial or mid-sentence
   - **Punctuation**: Comma after initial "when" clause
   - **Typical Morning Uses**: when I wake up, when the sun comes up, when the kids get up
   
   **Root Analysis**: Interrogative/relative adverb used as a conjunction
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *hwænne* meaning "at what time." Later extended to a conjunction.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When my alarm goes off, I usually hit snooze once.
   - I feel better when I drink water first thing in the morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When participants woke up naturally, they reported higher morning satisfaction.
   - Productivity often increases when people protect the first hour of the morning for focused work.

1. **Linking Word**: while (type: subordinating conjunction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to show that two actions happen at the same time, or to introduce a mild contrast.
   - *Function*: Describes overlapping morning actions or contrasts in habits.
   
   **Synonyms**: as (temporal), when (temporal), whereas (contrast), although (concession)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, after, then (for sequence)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing multitasking in the morning ("I eat breakfast while I check the news"), or gentle contrasts between people’s routines.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For strong contrast in formal writing (prefer "whereas"), or strict sequence (use "before"/"after").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Action 1] while [simultaneous action 2]  
     While [action 1], [action 2]
   - **Position**: Mid-sentence or sentence-initial
   - **Punctuation**: Comma after introductory "while" clause
   - **Typical Morning Uses**: while I’m getting ready, while the coffee is brewing, while everyone is still asleep
   
   **Root Analysis**: Noun "while" (period of time) used as conjunction
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *hwīl* meaning "a period of time." Later used as "during the time that."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I listen to podcasts while I commute in the morning.
   - While the kids are sleeping, I enjoy a quiet cup of tea.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - While some employees prefer to start work immediately, others need a short quiet period to adjust to the morning.
   - Participants were asked not to use their phones while completing the early-morning questionnaire.

1. **Linking Word**: because (type: subordinating conjunction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - For the reason that; introduces a cause or explanation.
   - *Function*: Explains why something happens in the morning.
   
   **Synonyms**: since, as, due to the fact that, for the reason that
   
   **Antonyms**: although, even though, despite (concession)
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining morning habits, giving reasons for being late or tired, justifying schedule choices.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For results (use "so" or "therefore"), or for contrasts (use "although").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Result] because [cause]  
     Because [cause], [result]
   - **Position**: Mid-sentence or sentence-initial
   - **Punctuation**: Comma after initial "because" clause in longer sentences
   - **Typical Morning Uses**: because I stayed up late, because of the traffic, because mornings are quieter
   
   **Root Analysis**: by + cause
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from phrase "by cause" meaning "for the reason."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’m slow this morning because I went to bed too late.
   - She takes the earlier train because the later one is always crowded.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many workers skip breakfast because they feel pressed for time in the morning.
   - Because traffic peaks around eight, some commuters choose to start work earlier.

1. **Linking Word**: so (type: coordinating/result connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Therefore; for that reason; introduces a result or logical consequence.
   - *Function*: Connects morning causes and their outcomes.
   
   **Synonyms**: therefore, as a result, consequently, thus (more formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: but, however, yet (contrast)
   
   **When to Use**: Showing the outcome of morning decisions or events ("I overslept, so I skipped breakfast").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal writing (prefer "therefore" or "as a result"), or when you need a contrast, not a result.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Cause], so [result]  
     [Cause]. So [result] (more conversational)
   - **Position**: Usually mid-sentence between two main clauses
   - **Punctuation**: Comma before "so" when it links two full clauses
   - **Typical Morning Uses**: woke up late, so..., traffic was light, so..., felt tired, so...
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple adverb/conjunction
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *swā* meaning "in this way, therefore." 
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I forgot to set my alarm, so my whole morning was rushed.
   - The streets were empty, so I got to work much earlier than usual.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants arrived later than planned, so the first morning session was shortened.
   - Many offices are quieter early in the day, so some employees reserve that time for focused work.
