1. **Preposition**: in (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* During a period such as the morning, week, or month.
   - *(Abstract)* Within a part of the day or phase of a routine.
   
   **Synonyms**: during (temporal), over the course of
   
   **Antonyms**: before, after, outside (of that period)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking generally about what happens during the morning, not at an exact clock time.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For precise times (use "at"), or deadlines (use "by").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in the morning, in the early morning, in the late morning
   - **Verb + prep**: work in the morning, study in the morning, exercise in the morning
   - **Adjective + prep**: busy in the morning, free in the mornings
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple preposition from Old English
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *in* meaning "in, into, during."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I prefer to do my hardest tasks in the morning.
   - The buses are crowded in the morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many people report higher concentration in the morning than in the afternoon.
   - Most deliveries to the office arrive in the morning.

1. **Preposition**: at (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* At a specific point in time.
   - *(Abstract)* At a particular stage or moment of a routine.
   
   **Synonyms**: N/A for exact time; closest is "on" for dates
   
   **Antonyms**: around (approximate), before, after
   
   **When to Use**: Mentioning precise morning times (clock times or named points like sunrise).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general periods (use "in the morning"), or durations (use "for"/"during").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at 7 a.m., at sunrise, at breakfast, at the start of the day
   - **Verb + prep**: wake up at, leave at, arrive at
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple preposition from Old English
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *æt* meaning "near, at, to."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I get up at six and leave the house at seven.
   - The meeting starts at nine in the morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Sessions begin promptly at 8:30 a.m. each morning.
   - Participants were instructed to arrive at the lab by 7:45 a.m.

1. **Preposition**: by (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Not later than a particular time or point in the morning.
   - *(Abstract)* As a deadline or latest acceptable moment.
   
   **Synonyms**: no later than, before, up to (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: after (too late), past (beyond the time)
   
   **When to Use**: Setting morning deadlines or goals ("finish this by 10"), or describing completion before a time.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For starting times (use "at" or "from").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by nine, by mid-morning, by the time I leave
   - **Verb + prep**: finish by, arrive by, submit by
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple preposition from Old English
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *bi* meaning "near, by, about." Extended to temporal deadlines.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like to clear my inbox by mid-morning.
   - Try to get to the office by nine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All reports must be submitted by 10:00 a.m. each morning.
   - Breakfast service ends by 9:30 a.m. on weekdays.

1. **Preposition**: before (type: simple/temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Earlier than a particular time or event.
   - *(Prepositional use)* Followed by a noun, noun phrase, or -ing form.
   
   **Synonyms**: prior to, earlier than, ahead of
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about tasks or events that must happen earlier in the morning than something else.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With a full clause in very formal writing without a subject (prefer "before I"/"before we" etc.).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: before work, before breakfast, before sunrise, before my morning meeting
   - **Verb + prep**: shower before, eat before, read before
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + fore (in front of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *beforan* meaning "in front of, earlier than."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like to meditate before breakfast.
   - Please call me before your morning class starts.
   
   **Examples [Formal**]: 
   - Participants were instructed to avoid caffeine before the morning measurements.
   - Employees must complete safety checks before opening the office each morning.

1. **Preposition**: after (type: simple/temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Later than a particular time or event; following something in time.
   
   **Synonyms**: following, later than, once (in some structures)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, ahead of
   
   **When to Use**: Describing what happens later in the morning or the sequence of morning activities.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For causes (use "because of"), or for very precise immediate sequence where "immediately after" is needed.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: after breakfast, after the morning meeting, after sunrise
   - **Verb + prep**: leave after, start after, call after
   
   **Root Analysis**: aft (behind) + -er
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *æfter* meaning "behind, later than."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I usually exercise after work, not in the morning.
   - After my morning class, I grab a quick coffee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Surveys were completed after participants’ usual morning routines.
   - Blood samples were taken after breakfast each morning.

1. **Preposition**: during (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Throughout the duration of a period; at some point in the course of it.
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, in the course of, over
   
   **Antonyms**: before, after, outside (of that time)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing activities or states that occur within the morning period, especially over some time.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For single specific moments (use "at"), or very general habits ("in the morning" is more natural).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: during the morning, during the morning rush, during breakfast
   - **Verb + prep**: happen during, occur during, be busy during
   
   **Root Analysis**: dur- (last, endure) + -ing
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *durer* (to last), via Middle English, used for time periods.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don’t answer personal calls during the morning.
   - The trains are very full during the morning rush.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Noise levels increased significantly during the late-morning period.
   - Most customer inquiries arrive during the first two hours of the morning.

1. **Preposition**: around (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Approximately at a time, not exact.
   - *(Spatial)* In the area near something, often the house or office in the morning.
   
   **Synonyms**: about (for time), roughly, approximately
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly at, precisely at
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about approximate morning times or loose routines.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact timing matters (use "at" and a specific time).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: around six, around sunrise, around mid-morning
   - **Verb + prep**: wake up around, leave around, get in around
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + round (circular)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from phrase "on round," extended to approximate times.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I usually wake up around seven.
   - We left the house around sunrise.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Most employees arrive around 9:00 a.m., creating a short morning peak.
   - Energy levels tend to dip slightly around late morning.

1. **Preposition**: until (type: simple/temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Up to the time of; before and continuing to a particular point.
   
   **Synonyms**: till, up to, up until
   
   **Antonyms**: from (starting point), after (beyond that time)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing how long something continues in the morning ("sleep until nine").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For starting times (use "from"), or for vague periods (use "during").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: until noon, until lunchtime, until my morning break
   - **Verb + prep**: sleep until, work until, wait until
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (up to) + till (to)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from *un-til* meaning "up to," combining earlier directional elements.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I could sleep until noon if nobody woke me up.
   - She works non-stop until her late-morning break.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reception desk is not staffed until 8:30 a.m. each morning.
   - Morning symptoms usually persist until mid-morning and then gradually decrease.
