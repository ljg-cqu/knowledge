1. **Verb**: wake up
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To stop sleeping and become conscious.
   - (Transitive) To cause someone to stop sleeping.
   
   **Synonyms**: awaken, get up (near-synonym for start of day), rouse, stir
   
   **Antonyms**: fall asleep, doze off, drift off
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about the moment sleep ends, especially in the morning, or when describing how someone is brought out of sleep.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simply getting out of bed physically (use "get up" if the person is already awake), or for staying alert during the day (use "stay awake").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wake up early
   - wake up late
   - wake up to an alarm
   - wake up naturally
   - wake someone up
   
   **Root Analysis**: wake (become or cause to become awake) + up (particle intensifying completion)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English *wacan* (to wake) and phrasal pattern with "up" becoming common in Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I usually wake up around six, but I don’t get out of bed until half past.
   - Please don’t wake me up before seven tomorrow morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many people report that they wake up more easily when they maintain a consistent bedtime.
   - Participants were instructed to wake up without an alarm on the final study day.

1. **Verb**: get up
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive, phrasal) To rise from bed after sleeping.
   - *(Extended)* To stand up from a lying or sitting position.
   
   **Synonyms**: rise, get out of bed, stand up (for posture change)
   
   **Antonyms**: lie down, go to bed, sit down, turn in (informal)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about the physical action of leaving bed in the morning, or giving instructions about starting the day.
   
   **When NOT TO Use**: When the focus is specifically on the moment of becoming conscious (use "wake up"), or in very formal writing (use "rise").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get up early
   - get up late
   - get up at six
   - get up for work
   - can’t get up in the morning
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (obtain, move, reach) + up (particle indicating movement to higher position)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → phrasal pattern "get up" established in early Modern English with the meaning "rise from bed."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have to get up at 6:30 to catch the 7 a.m. train.
   - On weekends I don’t get up until almost noon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Individuals who get up at a consistent time each morning often report better sleep quality.
   - The schedule required participants to get up no later than 7:00 a.m. on each test day.

1. **Verb**: snooze
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To sleep lightly, especially for a short time.
   - (Intransitive/Transitive, informal) To delay an alarm temporarily by using a snooze function.
   
   **Synonyms**: doze, nap briefly, drift off, half-sleep
   
   **Antonyms**: wake fully, stay alert, get up immediately
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about short extra sleep in the morning, especially after the alarm goes off; describing lazy or slow starts to the day.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal writing (prefer "doze" or "sleep lightly"), or for long periods of deep sleep.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hit the snooze (button)
   - snooze for ten more minutes
   - keep snoozing the alarm
   - morning snooze
   - snooze on the bus
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple root verb; possible sound-symbolic origin suggesting gentle sleep
   
   **Etymology**: 18th–19th century English → originally meaning "to doze," later extended in the 20th century to the "snooze button" on alarm clocks.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I hit the snooze three times and almost missed my morning meeting.
   - Let me snooze for just five more minutes, please.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Excessive use of the snooze function may fragment morning sleep and reduce alertness.
   - Participants were asked not to snooze their alarms on experimental days.

1. **Verb**: oversleep
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To sleep longer than you intended, especially so that you wake up late.
   
   **Synonyms**: sleep in (often positive/neutral), sleep too long, miss your alarm
   
   **Antonyms**: wake on time, get up early, rise promptly
   
   **When to Use**: Describing being late in the morning because you did not wake up when planned.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When someone deliberately stays in bed late and it is not a problem (use "sleep in"), or for going to bed too late (use "stay up late").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - oversleep and be late
   - accidentally oversleep
   - oversleep your alarm
   - oversleep on a workday
   - tend to oversleep
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (too much, beyond) + sleep (to rest in sleep)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English/Modern English → pattern of "over-" + verb to indicate excess (as in "overeat," "overwork").
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I overslept this morning and missed the first half of class.
   - If I don’t set two alarms, I always oversleep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Chronic fatigue increases the likelihood of oversleeping and missing morning commitments.
   - Participants who overslept were excluded from the first morning session.

1. **Verb**: brew
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To make tea, coffee, or another hot drink by soaking it in hot water.
   - *(Extended)* To prepare a pot or cup of coffee or tea, especially as part of a morning routine.
   
   **Synonyms**: make (coffee/tea), prepare, steep (for tea)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A for direct opposite; conceptually "drink" or "finish" (later stage)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about making coffee or tea in the morning, café work, or home routines.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For instant drinks where no steeping is involved (people usually still say "make coffee," but "brew" suggests a process), or for cold drinks (use "mix" or "prepare").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - brew coffee
   - brew a fresh pot
   - brew tea
   - let it brew
   - freshly brewed coffee
   
   **Root Analysis**: Root verb; historically used for making beer, extended metaphorically to coffee/tea
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English *breowan* (to brew, make a drink by boiling or steeping). Use with coffee and tea became common in modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I brew a pot of coffee every morning before work.
   - Give the tea a few minutes to brew before you drink it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many workers report that they brew coffee as the first step in their morning routine.
   - Brewing time and water temperature significantly affect the flavor profile of coffee.

1. **Verb**: shower
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To wash yourself in a shower.
   - (Transitive) To wash (your body) using water from a shower.
   
   **Synonyms**: take a shower, wash, freshen up, rinse off
   
   **Antonyms**: stay unwashed, skip a shower (informal)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about personal hygiene, especially morning routines before work or school.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For baths (use "bathe" or "take a bath"), or for washing only your face or hands.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - shower in the morning
   - quick shower
   - cold shower
   - hot morning shower
   - shower before work
   
   **Root Analysis**: Derived from the noun "shower" (fall of water) used as a verb
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English *scur* (shower of rain). The sense of washing under flowing water developed with indoor plumbing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I always shower in the morning—it helps me wake up.
   - Let me shower quickly before breakfast.
   
   **Examples [Formal**]: 
   - Many participants reported that they showered immediately after waking as part of their morning routine.
   - Morning showering habits are influenced by cultural norms and work start times.

1. **Verb**: commute
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To travel regularly between your home and your place of work or study.
   - (Transitive, less common) To travel regularly a particular distance or route to and from work.
   
   **Synonyms**: travel to work, go to the office, ride in to work
   
   **Antonyms**: work from home, stay in
   
   **When to Use**: Describing daily travel in the morning and evening, especially in cities.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For one-time trips (use "travel" or "go"), or for non-work journeys like holidays.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - commute by train
   - commute by car
   - commute to the city
   - commute every morning
   - have a long commute
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + mute (change) in original legal sense "exchange, substitute"; travel sense developed later
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *commutare* (to change, exchange). The travel sense emerged in the early 20th century with suburban rail travel.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I commute by subway every morning—it’s faster than driving.
   - He used to commute two hours each way to get to work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees who commute long distances often experience higher stress levels.
   - The study compares productivity between workers who commute daily and those who work remotely.

1. **Verb**: rush
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To move or act quickly because you are late or in a hurry.
   - (Transitive) To do something too quickly, often with less care than usual.
   
   **Synonyms**: hurry, dash, race, scramble, sprint (for running)
   
   **Antonyms**: take your time, slow down, stroll
   
   **When to Use**: Describing stressful mornings when you are late for work or school, or when many tasks must be done quickly.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the movement is calm and relaxed (use "walk" or "go"), or in very formal scientific descriptions (prefer "hasten" or "move rapidly").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rush to work
   - rush through breakfast
   - rush out the door
   - rush to catch the train
   - morning rush
   
   **Root Analysis**: Root verb; possibly from French influences meaning "to move or drive forcefully"
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from Anglo-French *ruser* (to drive back, charge). Modern meaning of moving quickly and in a hurry became dominant in everyday English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I had to rush this morning because I woke up late.
   - Don’t rush—let’s leave a few minutes earlier tomorrow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When individuals rush through their morning tasks, they are more likely to forget essential items.
   - The data suggest that students who do not have to rush in the morning report lower stress levels.
