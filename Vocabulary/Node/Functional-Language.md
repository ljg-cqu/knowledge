# Node Vocabulary: Functional Language

1. **Functional Expression**: Could you...? / Could you please...? (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Polite way to ask someone to do something or provide assistance
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Would you mind...?, Can you...?, Please..., I'd appreciate if you could...
   
   **Antonyms**: You must..., Do this... (direct commands)
   
   **When to Use**: When asking for help, code reviews, clarifications, or contributions. Polite team communication.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse with very familiar teammates. Avoid in urgent situations requiring direct commands.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you help me...?
   - Could you take a look at...?
   - Could you explain...?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness strategy—minimizes imposition. More polite than "can you" due to hypothetical form.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, I'd be happy to.
   - Of course, give me a moment.
   - I'm sorry, I can't right now.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could you review my pull request when you have time?
   - Could you help me debug this callback issue?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you please provide feedback on the proposed API design?
   - Could you clarify the expected behavior for this edge case?

1. **Functional Expression**: I was wondering if... (function: requesting/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very polite, indirect way to make requests or suggestions
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: very indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Would it be possible to...?, Might you...?, Could we...?, Do you think we could...?
   
   **Antonyms**: We should..., Let's... (direct suggestions)
   
   **When to Use**: When making suggestions to seniors, sensitive requests, or proposing changes to established code.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in urgent situations. Don't use with very familiar team members (too formal).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I was wondering if we could...
   - I was wondering if you might...
   - I'm wondering whether...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Highly tentative expression. Past continuous tense adds extra politeness. Minimizes face threat.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's a good idea. Let's try it.
   - I see what you mean. What do you suggest?
   - Hmm, I'm not sure that would work because...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was wondering if we could use promises instead of callbacks here?
   - I was wondering if you'd considered using async/await?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I was wondering if we might refactor this module for improved maintainability.
   - I was wondering if you would be available to discuss the architecture proposal.

1. **Functional Expression**: Why don't we...? / How about...? (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Friendly way to make suggestions or propose alternatives
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: What if we...?, Perhaps we could...?, Let's..., We could...
   
   **Antonyms**: We must..., You should... (stronger directives)
   
   **When to Use**: When proposing solutions, architectural decisions, or alternative approaches. Collaborative tone.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when giving direct instructions. Avoid in very formal documentation.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Why don't we try...?
   - How about using...?
   - What about...?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Question form softens suggestion. Implies collaboration and equal partnership. Invites discussion.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Good idea! Let's do that.
   - That could work. Let me think about it.
   - I prefer... because...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why don't we add error handling here?
   - How about using middleware for authentication?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - How about implementing a caching layer to improve performance?
   - Why don't we schedule a meeting to discuss the deployment strategy?

1. **Functional Expression**: I think/I believe... (function: expressing opinions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Softened way to express personal opinions or judgments
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: In my opinion..., It seems to me..., I would say..., My view is...
   
   **Antonyms**: It is..., Clearly..., Obviously... (stating as fact)
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing technical opinions, suggesting approaches, or disagreeing politely. Common in discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse (sounds uncertain). Avoid for stating facts or when confidence is needed.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I think that...
   - I believe we should...
   - I'd say...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedging device. Presents opinion as subjective, leaving room for disagreement. Face-saving for both parties.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I see your point. However...
   - I agree. Let's go with that.
   - Interesting. I was thinking...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think promises are cleaner than callbacks here.
   - I believe we should add validation to this endpoint.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I believe the microservices architecture would better serve our scalability requirements.
   - I think we should prioritize security considerations in the implementation.

1. **Functional Expression**: It might be better to... / We might want to... (function: suggesting/hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Tentative suggestion that something should be done differently
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: very indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Perhaps we should..., We could consider..., It may be worth...
   
   **Antonyms**: We must..., You should... (stronger directives)
   
   **When to Use**: When suggesting improvements to others' code, pointing out issues gently, or proposing alternatives.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for critical bugs (be direct). Don't use when strong recommendation is needed.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It might be worth...
   - We might consider...
   - It may be better to...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Might" adds tentativeness. Softens criticism. Preserves face by not directly saying "this is wrong."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're right. I'll change that.
   - Good point. Let me update it.
   - I see. Why do you think that?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It might be better to use const instead of var here.
   - We might want to add error logging to this function.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - It might be advisable to implement rate limiting for this endpoint.
   - We might consider adopting TypeScript for improved type safety.

1. **Functional Expression**: Have you considered...? / Did you think about...? (function: suggesting/questioning)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Polite way to suggest alternatives or point out potential issues
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: What about...?, Have you thought about...?, Did you look at...?
   
   **Antonyms**: You should..., You forgot... (direct criticism)
   
   **When to Use**: In code reviews, when pointing out alternatives, or questioning design decisions. Respectful inquiry.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use condescendingly. Avoid when the person clearly has considered it.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Have you considered using...?
   - Did you think about adding...?
   - Have you looked into...?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Question form avoids direct criticism. Implies the person may have valid reasons. Opens discussion.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Oh, good point! I hadn't thought of that.
   - Yes, but I decided against it because...
   - No, what do you suggest?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Have you considered using async/await instead?
   - Did you think about edge cases for null values?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Have you considered the security implications of this approach?
   - Did you evaluate alternative authentication mechanisms?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm not sure, but... / Correct me if I'm wrong, but... (function: hedging/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing uncertainty while making a statement or suggestion
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: If I understand correctly..., I could be wrong, but..., As far as I know...
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm certain..., Definitely..., Clearly... (confidence)
   
   **When to Use**: When uncertain, making tentative suggestions, or disagreeing with someone more experienced.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse (undermines credibility). Avoid when you're actually certain.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm not entirely sure, but...
   - Correct me if I'm mistaken, but...
   - If I'm not wrong...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedging reduces commitment. Invites correction. Face-saving device—leaves room to be wrong without embarrassment.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Actually, that's correct!
   - You're on the right track, but...
   - Let me clarify...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not sure, but this might cause a memory leak.
   - Correct me if I'm wrong, but doesn't this need error handling?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If I understand correctly, this approach may introduce performance overhead.
   - Correct me if I'm mistaken, but this pattern appears to violate separation of concerns.

1. **Functional Expression**: That makes sense, but... / I see your point, but... (function: polite disagreeing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Acknowledging someone's view before presenting alternative perspective
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I understand, however..., That's true, although..., Good point, but...
   
   **Antonyms**: No, that's wrong... (direct disagreement), I disagree (blunt)
   
   **When to Use**: When disagreeing in discussions, code reviews, or architectural debates. Maintains positive relationship.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use insincerely. Avoid in written form where tone is unclear.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - That's a fair point, but...
   - I see what you mean, but...
   - I understand your concern, however...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness—acknowledges other's face. "But" signals disagreement coming. Softens opposition.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What do you suggest instead?
   - Fair enough. Let's discuss alternatives.
   - Can you explain your reasoning?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That makes sense, but we might hit performance issues.
   - I see your point, but callbacks are getting nested pretty deep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I understand your reasoning, but the proposed approach may compromise maintainability.
   - That's a valid consideration, however we must also account for scalability requirements.

1. **Functional Expression**: Would it make sense to...? / Does it make sense to...? (function: suggesting/proposing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposing an idea by framing it as a logical question
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Should we...?, Would it be better to...?, Is it reasonable to...?
   
   **Antonyms**: We should..., Let's... (direct proposals)
   
   **When to Use**: When proposing architectural decisions, refactoring, or questioning approaches. Invites evaluation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you need to be decisive. Don't overuse in implementation discussions.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would it make sense to refactor...?
   - Does it make sense to add...?
   - Wouldn't it make more sense to...?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Frames suggestion as logical evaluation rather than personal preference. Collaborative problem-solving tone.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, that would be logical.
   - Hmm, maybe. Let's think through the implications.
   - I'm not sure. What's the benefit?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would it make sense to split this into separate modules?
   - Does it make sense to cache this response?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would it make sense to implement a microservices architecture given our scaling requirements?
   - Does it make sense to introduce TypeScript at this stage of the project?

   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: To make sure I understand..., Let me confirm..., So you're saying...?
   
   **Antonyms**: Obviously..., Clearly... (assuming understanding)
   
   **When to Use**: When confirming requirements, verifying assumptions, or ensuring correct interpretation.

   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just to make sure...

   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-saving for both parties. Allows correction without assigning blame. Shows active listening.
   
   **Response Patterns**:

   **Examples [Casual]**:

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd recommend... / I'd suggest... (function: advising/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Giving advice or recommendations based on experience
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: moderately indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I suggest..., My recommendation is..., I'd advise..., It would be good to...
   
   **Antonyms**: You must..., You have to... (strong directives)
   
   **When to Use**: When giving technical advice, best practices, or experienced guidance. Authority with politeness.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use without expertise. Avoid when collaborative suggestion is better.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd strongly recommend...
   - I'd suggest trying...
   - My recommendation would be...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conditional "would" softens directive. Presents as suggestion while implying expertise. Balance of authority and politeness.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks for the suggestion!
   - That's good advice. I'll do that.
   - Why do you recommend that?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd recommend using async/await for cleaner code.
   - I'd suggest adding error handling here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'd recommend implementing comprehensive logging for production environments.
   - I'd suggest conducting a security audit before deployment.

1. **Functional Expression**: It depends on... / That depends (function: conditional response)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Indicating that the answer varies based on circumstances
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: It varies..., It's situational..., Well, it's conditional...
   
   **Antonyms**: Always..., Never..., Definitely... (absolute answers)
   
   **When to Use**: When answering "should I" questions, discussing trade-offs, or providing nuanced answers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse to avoid decisions. Avoid when clear answer exists.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It depends on your use case
   - That depends on what you're trying to do
   - Well, it depends
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Acknowledges complexity. Avoids oversimplification. Opens discussion about context and requirements.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What factors should I consider?
   - What does it depend on?
   - In my case...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Should I use callbacks or promises? It depends on your codebase.
   - That depends on whether performance or readability is more important.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The optimal architecture depends on your scalability requirements and team expertise.
   - The choice of database technology depends on data structure and query patterns.

1. **Functional Expression**: In my experience... / From what I've seen... (function: sharing experience)

   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Based on my experience..., I've found that..., I've noticed...
   
   **Antonyms**: It's proven..., Research shows... (objective facts)
   
   **When to Use**: When sharing lessons learned, giving advice based on experience, or providing context.

   **Common Variations**: 
   - In my experience, ...
   - From what I've seen, ...
   - I've found that...

   **Response Patterns**:

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In my experience, microservices introduce significant operational complexity.
