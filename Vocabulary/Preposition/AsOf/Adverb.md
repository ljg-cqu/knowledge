1. **Adverb**: currently (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At the present time; now; at this moment
   - During the current period being discussed
   - Used to indicate that something is happening or true at the specific time mentioned (often used with "as of")
   
   **Synonyms**: presently, now, at present, at this time, at the moment
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, formerly, then, once, in the past
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing the present state or situation, particularly in formal contexts; frequently paired with "as of" to specify exact timing of current status; common in business, academic, and news reporting
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in past tense narratives; redundant when present tense already indicates current time; can sound overly formal in casual conversation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - currently available as of
   - currently in effect as of
   - currently employed as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: current (running, present) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "currere" (to run) → "current" (belonging to present time) + "-ly" → "currently" (in the present time) (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm currently working there as of last Monday.
   - She's currently living in Boston as of this year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy is currently under review as of the date of this memorandum.
   - As of December 31, 2023, the company currently employs 1,247 full-time staff.

1. **Adverb**: immediately (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Without delay; instantly; at once
   - Directly or very closely in time or space
   - (With "as of") Indicating something takes effect right away from the specified date
   
   **Synonyms**: instantly, at once, right away, straightaway, forthwith
   
   **Antonyms**: gradually, later, eventually, subsequently, belatedly
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing urgency or lack of delay; common with "as of" to indicate instant effectiveness from a specified date; used across all registers but particularly important in business and legal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when gradual implementation is intended; not appropriate when flexibility in timing exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - effective immediately as of
   - immediately following [date]
   - immediately available as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (in, not) + medi (middle) + -ate + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "immediatus" (without anything between) → "im-" (not) + "medius" (middle) → "immediately" (without intervening time) (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The changes start immediately as of Monday.
   - You can use it immediately as of today.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This directive is effective immediately as of the publication date.
   - All employees are immediately subject to the revised policy as of January 1, 2024.

1. **Adverb**: retroactively (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With effect from a date in the past; applying to a period before the present
   - Taking effect or applying to a time before the action was taken
   - (With "as of") Indicating that something applies back to an earlier specified date
   
   **Synonyms**: retrospectively, backdated, with retroactive effect
   
   **Antonyms**: prospectively, going forward, from now on, henceforth
   
   **When to Use**: In legal, business, and policy contexts when changes or decisions apply to past dates; commonly used with "as of" to specify the earlier effective date; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts; not used for future applications; be careful of legal implications when used
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - retroactively effective as of
   - applied retroactively to
   - retroactively as of [past date]
   
   **Root Analysis**: retro- (backward) + act + -ive + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "retro" (backward) + "agere" (to do, drive) → "retroactive" (operative on past matters) + "-ly" (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The pay raise applies retroactively as of last July.
   - They're crediting us retroactively as of when we signed up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The amendment shall be applied retroactively as of the fiscal year beginning July 1, 2023.
   - Compensation adjustments will be implemented retroactively as of the employee's hire date.

1. **Adverb**: officially (type: manner)

   - According to the formal record or announcement
   - (With "as of") Indicating formal recognition or documentation from a specified date
   
   **Synonyms**: formally, authoritatively, on the record, legitimately
   
   **Antonyms**: unofficially, informally, casually, privately
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing formal status or authorized recognition; often used with "as of" to indicate when something becomes formally recognized or documented; appropriate in business, legal, and institutional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where formality is inappropriate; not needed when informal acknowledgment is sufficient
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - officially announced as of
   - officially effective as of
   - officially recognized as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: offici (duty, office) + -al + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "officialis" (relating to duty or office) → "official" (authorized, formal) + "-ly" → "officially" (in an authorized manner) (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're officially moving as of next Friday.
   - It's officially summer as of tomorrow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The merger is officially completed as of the closing date specified herein.
   - Dr. Chen officially assumed the role as of September 1, 2024.

1. **Adverb**: previously (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At an earlier time; before the time mentioned or implied
   - In the past; formerly; before now
   - (With "as of") Used to reference an earlier date for comparison
   
   **Synonyms**: formerly, earlier, before, beforehand, in the past
   
   **Antonyms**: subsequently, later, afterward, currently, now
   
   **When to Use**: When comparing past states to current states; often used in contexts discussing changes or updates relative to "as of" dates; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing only current or future states without past comparison; can create confusion if timeline isn't clear
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - previously reported as of
   - as compared to previously
   - previously announced as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: previ (before) + -ous + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praevius" (going before) → "previous" (coming before in time) + "-ly" → "previously" (at an earlier time) (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The price was previously $50 as of last month.
   - I previously worked there as of 2020.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The figure differs from that previously reported as of June 30, 2023.
   - As of the revision date, previously disclosed estimates have been updated.

1. **Adverb**: accurately (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a correct and precise manner; without error
   - Conforming exactly to truth or to a standard
   - (With "as of") Modifying verbs to emphasize precision of information at a specified date
   
   **Synonyms**: precisely, exactly, correctly, faithfully, truly
   
   **Antonyms**: inaccurately, incorrectly, wrongly, imprecisely
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing precision and correctness of actions or statements; commonly used with "as of" in contexts requiring data accuracy; formal and professional registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when approximate information is acceptable; redundant when accuracy is already assumed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accurately reported as of
   - accurately reflects as of
   - cannot accurately determine as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: accur (with care) + -ate + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "accuratus" (done with care) → "accurate" (correct, precise) + "-ly" → "accurately" (in a precise manner) (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't accurately say what the balance is as of today.
   - The app accurately tracks steps as of the latest update.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The financial statements accurately represent the company's position as of year-end.
   - Data has been accurately recorded as of the date indicated in each entry.

1. **Adverb**: presently (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At the present time; now; currently (American English)
   - After a short time; soon (British English)
   - (With "as of") Indicating current status at a specified date
   
   **Synonyms**: currently, now, at present, at this time (American); soon, shortly (British)
   
   **Antonyms**: formerly, previously, in the past, never
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts to indicate current status (American usage); often paired with "as of" in business and academic writing; note regional differences in meaning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in British English if meaning "currently" (use "currently" instead); can be ambiguous; less common in casual American speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - presently employed as of
   - presently available as of
   - presently in effect as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: present (being here now) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "present" (present, at hand) → "presently" originally meant "immediately" → evolved to "currently" in American English (14th-20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's presently living in Seattle as of this spring.
   - I'm presently working from home as of March.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The applicant is presently employed as of the application submission date.
   - As of December 31, 2023, the facility is presently operating at 85% capacity.

1. **Adverb**: recently (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a time not long ago; in the recent past
   - Not long before the present or before a specified time
   - (With "as of") Often used to indicate information that was updated or changed shortly before the reference date
   
   **Synonyms**: lately, not long ago, just, of late, freshly
   
   **Antonyms**: long ago, anciently, formerly, distantly
   
   **When to Use**: When indicating events or states from the near past; can be used with "as of" to show timing of recent changes; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when precise dates are required; "recently" is vague and subjective; not appropriate in legal or technical documents requiring exactness
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - recently updated as of
   - recently announced as of
   - most recently as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: recent (fresh, new) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "recens" (fresh, new) → French "recent" → "recently" (in the near past) (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I recently moved here as of last month.
   - The site was recently updated as of last week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy was recently revised as of Q3 2024.
   - As of the most recently available data, enrollment has increased by 12%.

1. **Adverb**: temporarily (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - For a limited period of time; not permanently
   - During a short or interim period
   - (With "as of") Indicating a non-permanent status that applies from a specified date
   
   **Synonyms**: provisionally, for now, for the time being, for the moment, interim
   
   **Antonyms**: permanently, indefinitely, always, forever, continuously
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing the non-permanent nature of a situation; often used with "as of" to indicate when temporary status began; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when permanence is intended or implied; not appropriate when duration is known to be very brief or very long
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - temporarily suspended as of
   - temporarily unavailable as of
   - temporarily assigned as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: tempor (time) + -ary + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "temporarius" (lasting for a time) → "temporary" (not permanent) + "-ly" → "temporarily" (for a limited time) (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The store is temporarily closed as of today.
   - I'm temporarily staying there as of last week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Services are temporarily suspended as of March 15, 2024, pending further review.
   - As of the date hereof, operations are temporarily relocated to the auxiliary facility.

1. **Adverb**: accordingly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is appropriate to the particular circumstances
   - As a result; consequently; therefore
   - (With "as of") Used to indicate actions taken in response to information current at a specified date
   
   **Synonyms**: consequently, therefore, thus, hence, as a result
   
   **Antonyms**: regardless, nevertheless, notwithstanding, despite
   
   **When to Use**: In formal writing to show logical consequence or appropriate response; can follow "as of" statements to indicate resulting actions; primarily formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; avoid when simple "so" would suffice in informal contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - and accordingly, as of [date]
   - adjusted accordingly as of
   - planned accordingly as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: accord (agreement, harmony) + -ing + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "acorder" (bring to agreement) → "according" → "accordingly" (in agreement with, consequently) (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The rules changed, so plan accordingly as of next week.
   - Prices went up, so budget accordingly as of January.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As of the effective date, all departments should adjust their procedures accordingly.
   - The regulations have been revised as of July 1, 2024; compliance programs must be modified accordingly.
