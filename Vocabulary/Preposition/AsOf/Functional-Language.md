1. **Functional Expression**: "As of [date], [statement]..." (function: stating/specifying temporal validity)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Specifies the exact point in time from which information is accurate or valid
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Effective [date], From [date] onward, Starting on [date], Beginning [date]
   
   **Antonyms**: Prior to [date], Before [date], Up until [date], Until [date]
   
   **When to Use**: In business reports, financial statements, official documents, data presentations, and any context requiring precise temporal reference; essential for disclaimers about data currency; used across professional and academic settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where precise dates aren't critical; when approximate timing is sufficient; in contexts where the date is obvious from context
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As of [specific date] (formal/precise)
   - As of now/today (semi-formal)
   - As of this writing (written reports)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression serves as a legal/professional hedge, protecting the speaker from accountability for changes after the stated date. It's a face-saving device that manages expectations about information currency. Essential in financial and legal contexts for liability management.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acknowledgment: "Understood, thank you for clarifying the date"
   - Clarification: "Has anything changed since then?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "How much do we have in the account?" B: "As of yesterday, about five thousand."
   - As of now, I'm still planning to go.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As of December 31, 2023, total assets amounted to $15.7 million.
   - As of the date of this report, all requirements have been fulfilled.

1. **Functional Expression**: "Effective as of [date]..." (function: announcing/implementing changes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Announces when a change, policy, or new rule begins to apply
   - Indicates the start date of validity or enforcement
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Taking effect from [date], Valid from [date], Operative as of [date], Commencing [date]
   
   **Antonyms**: Terminating as of [date], Ceasing as of [date], Expiring [date]
   
   **When to Use**: In policy announcements, contract modifications, pricing changes, regulatory updates, and official notices; particularly important in employment, legal, and financial contexts where timing of changes matters
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When changes are tentative or subject to approval; in contexts where the effective date is still being determined
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Effective immediately as of [date] (emphasis on urgency)
   - Shall be effective as of [date] (legal/mandatory tone)
   - Becomes effective as of [date] (neutral professional tone)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression carries authoritative weight and implies mandatory compliance. It's used to establish clear accountability and eliminate ambiguity about when obligations begin. Common in power-down communication (organization to employees, business to customers).
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Compliance: "Acknowledged, we will comply as of the stated date"
   - Clarification: "Does this apply retroactively?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new schedule is effective as of Monday.
   - Your discount starts effective as of today.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This amendment is effective as of January 1, 2024.
   - The revised compensation structure shall be effective as of the commencement of the next fiscal year.

1. **Functional Expression**: "I was wondering if, as of [date], [question]..." (function: politely requesting information with temporal specificity)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely inquires about status or information at a specific point in time
   - Combines indirect request with temporal precision
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Could you tell me if, as of [date]..., I'd like to know whether, as of [date]..., Might you confirm if, as of [date]...
   
   **Antonyms**: Tell me as of [date] (direct command)
   
   **When to Use**: When requesting information politely in professional contexts; when the temporal reference is important but you want to soften the request; in email inquiries and formal conversations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In urgent situations requiring direct questions; with close colleagues where politeness formulas are unnecessary; when you have authority to make direct requests
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you confirm whether, as of [date]... (more direct but still polite)
   - I'm curious to know if, as of [date]... (softer, more casual)
   - Would it be possible to find out if, as of [date]... (very indirect, very polite)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses negative politeness strategy (respecting recipient's autonomy) combined with precise information need. The temporal clause adds specificity that justifies the request and demonstrates preparation.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Direct answer: "As of that date, the status was..."
   - Redirect: "I'll need to check our records for that specific date"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was wondering if, as of today, you'd made a decision?
   - Do you know if, as of last week, they'd finished the project?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I was wondering if, as of the close of business yesterday, the application had been processed.
   - Could you please confirm whether, as of December 31, 2023, all outstanding invoices were settled?

1. **Functional Expression**: "Please note that, as of [date], [change/situation]..." (function: directing attention/giving notice)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Formally alerts recipients to important information with temporal specificity
   - Signals that what follows requires attention or action
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct but polite
   
   **Synonyms**: Please be advised that, as of [date]..., Kindly note that, as of [date]..., Be aware that, as of [date]...
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (directive function has no opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: In official notifications, policy announcements, formal emails, customer communications, and any context requiring clear documentation that information was conveyed; creates record of notice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual contexts where "please note" sounds overly formal; when addressing superiors (may sound presumptuous); with close colleagues in routine matters
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Please be aware that, as of [date]... (slightly more emphatic)
   - Kindly note that, as of [date]... (more polite, British English)
   - Note that, as of [date]... (more direct, less polite)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression creates accountability by formally directing attention. Often used in communications where the sender needs documentation that information was provided. The temporal specificity protects both parties by establishing clear timelines.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acknowledgment: "Noted, thank you"
   - Clarification: "Could you provide more details about the change?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just so you know, as of Monday, we're on the new system.
   - FYI, as of today, parking is in the west lot.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please note that, as of January 1, 2024, all submissions must use the revised template.
   - We wish to advise that, as of the effective date, the previous agreement is null and void.

1. **Functional Expression**: "Has [something] been [done] as of [date]?" (function: checking completion/status)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Inquires whether an action or status has been achieved by a specific date
   - Checks on progress or completion with temporal reference
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: By [date], had [something] been [done]?, Was [something] [done] as of [date]?, Did [something] get [done] by [date]?
   
   **Antonyms**: Will [something] be [done] as of [date]? (future rather than checking past/present)
   
   **When to Use**: In project management, deadline tracking, status check-ins, and accountability conversations; when you need to establish what was true at a specific past or present moment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to avoid sounding like you're checking up on someone (may sound mistrustful); when general timing is sufficient
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Can you confirm whether [something] was [done] as of [date]? (more polite)
   - Was [something] completed as of [date]? (emphasizes completion)
   - As of [date], had you [done something]? (personal accountability focus)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This question form can carry accountability implications and may be face-threatening if it suggests the listener should have completed something. The temporal specificity makes it a reasonable request for information but can feel like auditing.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Affirmative: "Yes, it was completed on [date]"
   - Negative: "Not as of that date, but it's done now"
   - Defensive: "I wasn't aware that was due by then"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Has the report been sent as of today?
   - Were the invitations mailed as of last Friday?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Has the compliance audit been completed as of the end of Q3?
   - Can you confirm whether all deliverables were submitted as of the contractual deadline?

1. **Functional Expression**: "This information is accurate as of [date] and subject to change" (function: disclaiming/hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Provides information while explicitly limiting liability for future changes
   - Establishes temporal boundaries of accuracy
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: This data is current as of [date] and may be revised, Information valid as of [date] but may change, Correct as of [date]; updates possible
   
   **Antonyms**: This information is permanent and unchanging
   
   **When to Use**: In reports, presentations, websites, and any published material where information currency is limited; particularly important in volatile contexts (markets, regulations, pricing); protects against liability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When information is stable and unlikely to change; when the disclaimer might undermine confidence unnecessarily; in contexts where certainty is more important than hedging
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Current as of [date]; subject to modification (more formal)
   - Accurate as of [date] but check for updates (more casual, actionable)
   - Valid as of [date]; terms subject to change without notice (legal disclaimer)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This is a standard liability-limiting device in professional communications. It manages recipient expectations while protecting the information provider from accountability for future changes. Essential in legal, financial, and regulatory contexts.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acknowledgment: "Understood, I'll verify if I need the latest"
   - Inquiry: "Where can I find updated information?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These prices are good as of today but might change next week.
   - This schedule is accurate as of now but could shift.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The financial information contained in this document is accurate as of December 31, 2023, and is subject to change without notice.
   - All product specifications are accurate as of the publication date and are subject to modification.

1. **Functional Expression**: "Could you provide [information] as of [date]?" (function: requesting date-specific information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely requests information with specific temporal parameters
   - Combines polite request form with precision about timing needed
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite request)
   
   **Synonyms**: Would you be able to supply [information] as of [date]?, Can you give me [information] for [date]?, Please provide [information] as of [date]
   
   **Antonyms**: Give me [information] as of [date] (direct command)
   
   **When to Use**: When requesting reports, data, status updates, or any information where the date matters; in professional emails, formal requests, and business communications; when politeness is appropriate
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In urgent situations requiring direct requests; with subordinates where "could you" might sound tentative; when you have clear authority to command
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would it be possible to get [information] as of [date]? (more tentative)
   - I need [information] as of [date] (more direct)
   - Please send [information] as of [date] (polite but clearer command)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses modal verb "could" for negative politeness (not imposing). The temporal specificity demonstrates that the request is reasonable and well-defined, making compliance easier. Common in hierarchical work environments and external business communications.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Certainly, I'll prepare that for you"
   - Negotiation: "I can provide data as of [different date], would that work?"
   - Refusal: "I'm afraid that information isn't available for that date"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could you send me the headcount as of last Friday?
   - Can you tell me what the status was as of yesterday?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you please provide a complete financial statement as of the end of Q3 2024?
   - Would you be able to furnish a status report as of the close of the reporting period?

1. **Functional Expression**: "As of when/what date is this information valid?" (function: clarifying temporal scope)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asks for clarification about the date to which information applies
   - Checks the currency or validity period of data or statements
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: What date is this information current to?, When was this information accurate?, This applies as of which date?, What's the date of this data?
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (clarification question has no direct opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: When receiving information without clear temporal reference; in situations where data currency is critical (financial, legal, medical); when you need to assess reliability of information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the date is clearly stated in the material; in contexts where approximate timing is sufficient
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What's the effective date for this information? (more formal)
   - When is this info from? (casual)
   - As of what date does this apply? (formal, precise)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This question demonstrates due diligence and critical thinking. It's a professional practice in fields where data currency matters. Asking this question positions you as thorough and careful, though excessive use might suggest mistrust.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Direct answer: "This information is as of December 31, 2023"
   - Uncertain: "I'm not sure, I'd need to check the source"
   - Defensive: "It should be current, I just pulled it"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As of when is this price list valid?
   - What date is this roster from?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you please clarify as of what date these financial projections are calculated?
   - As of what date are these regulatory requirements effective?
