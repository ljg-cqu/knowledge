1. **Preposition**: due to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; caused by; as a result of; attributable to
   - Used to introduce the cause or reason for something, typically following a linking verb
   
   **Synonyms**: because of, owing to, on account of, as a result of, thanks to (positive contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "due to" after linking verbs (be, seem, appear) to indicate causation; common in formal and semi-formal contexts; particularly frequent in academic, technical, and business writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Traditional grammar prescribes not using "due to" to start sentences or replace "because of" in non-linking verb contexts (though modern usage is more flexible); avoid in very informal conversation where "because of" is more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: due to circumstances, due to factors, due to the fact that, due to illness, due to weather
   - **Verb + prep**: is due to, was due to, seems due to, appears due to
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A (due to is itself prepositional)
   - **Noun + prep**: delay due to, cancellation due to, failure due to, success due to
   
   **Root Analysis**: due (owed, attributable) + to (preposition indicating direction/relation)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin debere (to owe) → Old French deu (owed) → Middle English due (owed, proper, appropriate) → "due to" emerged as causal expression meaning "attributable to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The game was called off due to rain.
   - She's staying home due to a cold.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project delay was due to insufficient resource allocation.
   - Market volatility is largely due to geopolitical tensions.

1. **Preposition**: owing to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; as a result of; due to
   - Used to introduce the cause or reason, often interchangeable with "due to" but can appear more freely in sentence positions
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, because of, on account of, as a result of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "owing to" in formal contexts to express causation; more flexible in sentence position than "due to"; particularly common in British English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very informal contexts; less common in American English where "because of" or "due to" are preferred
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: owing to circumstances, owing to the fact that
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (typically used at beginning or after comma)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: closure owing to, postponement owing to
   
   **Root Analysis**: owing (present participle of owe) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Old English agan (to own, possess) → Middle English owen (to owe) → "owing to" developed as causal expression
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The shop's closed owing to renovations.
   - We're late owing to traffic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Owing to unforeseen circumstances, the conference has been rescheduled.
   - The decline in enrollment is owing to demographic shifts.

1. **Preposition**: because of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* By reason of; as a result of; on account of
   - Used to introduce the cause or reason for an action, event, or state; more flexible than "due to"
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, owing to, on account of, as a result of, thanks to
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "because of" to introduce reasons in all positions; works after both linking and action verbs; appropriate for all registers from informal to formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal academic writing where "owing to" or "due to" might be preferred for stylistic variation
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: because of this, because of that, because of you, because of the weather
   - **Verb + prep**: happened because of, changed because of, left because of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: problem because of, success because of, delay because of
   
   **Root Analysis**: because (by + cause) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English bi cause (by reason) → "because of" as compound preposition expressing causation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm tired because of the long drive.
   - We stayed inside because of the storm.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative failed because of inadequate funding.
   - Productivity increased significantly because of the new system.

1. **Preposition**: on account of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; by reason of; due to
   - Formal expression introducing the cause or reason for something
   
   **Synonyms**: because of, due to, owing to, as a result of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "on account of" in formal writing and speech for stylistic variation; common in business and legal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; can sound old-fashioned or overly formal in everyday speech
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on account of circumstances, on account of health reasons
   - **Verb + prep**: cancelled on account of, postponed on account of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: absence on account of, refusal on account of
   
   **Root Analysis**: on + account (reckoning, consideration) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Latin computare → Old French acont → Middle English acount → "on account of" meaning "by reason of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The party was cancelled on account of bad weather.
   - He quit on account of the long commute.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hearing was adjourned on account of the defendant's illness.
   - Exemptions may be granted on account of extenuating circumstances.

1. **Preposition**: as a result of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; consequently from; due to
   - Explicitly emphasizes the cause-effect relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, because of, owing to, consequent upon
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "as a result of" when emphasizing the causal relationship and outcome; common in academic, scientific, and analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be wordy in casual conversation where "because of" is more natural and concise
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as a result of this, as a result of which
   - **Verb + prep**: occurred as a result of, changed as a result of, emerged as a result of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: outcome as a result of, change as a result of
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the manner of) + a + result (outcome) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Latin resultare (to rebound) → "as a result of" emphasizing consequential relationship
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He lost his job as a result of the layoffs.
   - The road is closed as a result of the accident.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Significant improvements were observed as a result of the intervention.
   - The decline occurred as a result of multiple contributing factors.

1. **Preposition**: thanks to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal - Positive)* Because of; as a result of (typically for positive outcomes)
   - *(Causal - Ironic)* Because of (used ironically for negative outcomes)
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, because of, owing to (when positive)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "thanks to" primarily for positive outcomes or achievements; also used ironically for negative results; common in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for neutral or clearly negative causation unless being deliberately ironic; may sound inappropriate in very serious negative contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: thanks to you, thanks to technology, thanks to efforts
   - **Verb + prep**: succeeded thanks to, improved thanks to, achieved thanks to
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: success thanks to, improvement thanks to
   
   **Root Analysis**: thanks (expression of gratitude) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Old English þanc (thought, gratitude) → "thanks to" developed as expression attributing positive results
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We made it on time thanks to your help.
   - Thanks to the rain, the picnic was ruined. (ironic)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project succeeded thanks to collaborative efforts.
   - Mortality rates declined thanks to improved healthcare access.
