1. **Noun**: cause (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person, thing, or event that makes something happen; the reason for an action or condition
   - A principle or aim that a group supports or fights for
   
   **Synonyms**: reason, factor, source, origin, root
   
   **Antonyms**: effect, result, consequence, outcome
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when discussing reasons or origins; appropriate for both formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing consequences rather than origins (use "effect" or "result" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - root cause
   - cause and effect
   - due to the cause
   - underlying cause
   - primary cause
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin causa (reason, motive)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin causa (cause, reason, lawsuit) → Old French cause → meaning "that which produces an effect"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the cause of the problem?
   - The cause of the fire was electrical.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers identified multiple causal factors.
   - The report examines the underlying causes of poverty.

1. **Noun**: reason (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A cause, explanation, or justification for an action or event
   - The power of the mind to think and make judgments
   
   **Synonyms**: cause, grounds, motive, rationale, explanation
   
   **Antonyms**: irrationality, randomness
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when explaining why something happened or why someone acted; appropriate for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "cause" would be more scientifically precise in technical contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - for this reason
   - the reason for
   - due to reasons
   - main reason
   - good reason
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ratio (reckoning, calculation)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin ratio (reckoning, understanding) → Old French raison → meaning "explanation, justification"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the reason for the delay?
   - That's the reason I'm here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reasons for the decline are multifaceted.
   - There is reason to believe the hypothesis is correct.

1. **Noun**: factor (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - One of several things that cause or influence something
   - A circumstance, fact, or influence that contributes to a result
   
   **Synonyms**: element, component, variable, aspect, contributor
   
   **Antonyms**: whole, entirety
   
   **When to Use**: Analytical and academic contexts when discussing multiple contributing causes; common in research and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing a single definitive cause (use "cause" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contributing factor
   - key factor
   - risk factor
   - due to factors
   - multiple factors
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin factor (maker, doer)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin factor (maker) → facere (to make, do) → mathematical and causal sense developed later
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Cost is a major factor in my decision.
   - Several factors led to the problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Socioeconomic factors significantly influence health outcomes.
   - The study identified three principal factors.

1. **Noun**: consequence (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A result or effect of an action or condition
   - The importance or relevance of something
   
   **Synonyms**: result, outcome, effect, upshot, ramification
   
   **Antonyms**: cause, origin, source
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when discussing results or implications; common in analytical and academic writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing causes rather than effects; casual contexts where "result" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - as a consequence
   - consequence of something
   - serious consequences
   - unforeseen consequences
   - suffer the consequences
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (with) + sequence (following)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin consequentia (following) → con- (together) + sequi (to follow) → meaning "that which follows"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What are the consequences of this decision?
   - He faced serious consequences.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy had far-reaching consequences.
   - Climate change consequences are becoming increasingly evident.

1. **Noun**: result (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that happens or exists because of something else; an outcome or consequence
   - A final score, mark, or achievement
   
   **Synonyms**: outcome, consequence, effect, upshot, product
   
   **Antonyms**: cause, origin, beginning
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when discussing outcomes; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing causes rather than effects (use "cause" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - as a result of
   - result of something
   - end result
   - direct result
   - desired result
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + -sult (jump)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin resultare (to spring back, rebound) → meaning "consequence, outcome"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The result was unexpected.
   - This is the result of hard work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results demonstrate a significant correlation.
   - Implementation yielded positive results.

1. **Noun**: outcome (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The way a situation or event ends; the final result
   - A consequence or result
   
   **Synonyms**: result, consequence, upshot, effect, end result
   
   **Antonyms**: beginning, cause, origin
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts when discussing final results or conclusions; common in research and policy contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation where "result" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - outcome of something
   - desired outcome
   - positive outcome
   - ultimate outcome
   - influence the outcome
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + come (compound showing emergence)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English out + come → meaning "what comes out, result"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What was the outcome of the meeting?
   - We're waiting to see the outcome.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patient outcomes improved significantly.
   - The study examined educational outcomes across demographics.

1. **Noun**: circumstance (C, usually plural)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A fact or condition connected with an event or action
   - The conditions that affect a situation
   
   **Synonyms**: situation, condition, factor, state of affairs, context
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (no direct antonym)
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when discussing conditions or factors affecting a situation; common in legal and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation where "situation" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - under the circumstances
   - due to circumstances
   - extenuating circumstances
   - unforeseen circumstances
   - in these circumstances
   
   **Root Analysis**: circum- (around) + -stance (standing)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin circumstantia (surrounding condition) → circum- (around) + stare (to stand) → meaning "surrounding conditions"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Under the circumstances, we did our best.
   - The circumstances were beyond our control.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The decision was justified given the circumstances.
   - Mitigating circumstances were considered in the judgment.

1. **Noun**: impact (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The effect or influence of one thing on another
   - A marked effect or influence
   
   **Synonyms**: effect, influence, consequence, repercussion, impression
   
   **Antonyms**: insignificance, irrelevance
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts when discussing significant effects; common in academic, business, and policy writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing minor or routine effects (use "effect" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - impact of something
   - impact on something
   - significant impact
   - environmental impact
   - long-term impact
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (in) + -pact (push, drive)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin impactus (driven in) → im- (in) + pangere (to fix, drive) → meaning "strong effect"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The news had a big impact on us.
   - What's the impact of this change?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention had a measurable impact on outcomes.
   - Economic impacts were assessed through multiple metrics.

1. **Noun**: effect (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A change that is a result or consequence of an action or cause
   - The power to produce an outcome or result
   
   **Synonyms**: result, consequence, outcome, impact, upshot
   
   **Antonyms**: cause, origin, source
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when discussing results or consequences; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing causes rather than results (use "cause" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cause and effect
   - side effect
   - have an effect on
   - take effect
   - in effect
   
   **Root Analysis**: ef- (out) + -fect (make, do)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin effectus (accomplishment) → ex- (out) + facere (to make) → meaning "result, consequence"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The medicine had no effect.
   - What effect will this have?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy produced several unintended effects.
   - Therapeutic effects were observed within weeks.

1. **Noun**: attribution (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of regarding something as being caused by someone or something
   - The action of ascribing a work or quality to a particular person or thing
   
   **Synonyms**: ascription, assignment, credit, imputation
   
   **Antonyms**: dissociation, denial
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts when discussing causation or credit; common in research and analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where simpler terms like "blaming" or "crediting" are more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - attribution of something
   - causal attribution
   - attribution error
   - attribution theory
   - correct attribution
   
   **Root Analysis**: at- (to) + tribute (assign) + -ion (action)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin attributio (assignment) → attribuere (to assign to) → meaning "act of ascribing"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The attribution of blame was unfair.
   - There's some dispute about the attribution.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Causal attribution requires careful analysis.
   - The study examined attribution patterns in decision-making.

1. **Noun**: explanation (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A statement or account that makes something clear; a reason given for something
   - The action of explaining the meaning of something
   
   **Synonyms**: reason, justification, clarification, account, interpretation
   
   **Antonyms**: confusion, mystery, obfuscation
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when providing or requesting reasons; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When a more technical term like "hypothesis" or "theory" is required in scientific contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - explanation for something
   - give an explanation
   - due to explanation
   - plausible explanation
   - demand an explanation
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + -plan (flat, clear) + -ation (action)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin explanare (to make clear) → ex- (out) + planus (flat) → meaning "act of making clear"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's your explanation for this?
   - I owe you an explanation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theory provides a coherent explanation for the phenomenon.
   - Multiple explanations have been proposed.

1. **Noun**: source (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A place, person, or thing from which something comes or originates
   - The origin or cause of something
   
   **Synonyms**: origin, cause, root, beginning, wellspring
   
   **Antonyms**: destination, end result, outcome
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when identifying origins; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing end results rather than origins (use "destination" or "outcome")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - source of something
   - primary source
   - reliable source
   - trace to the source
   - identify the source
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin surgere (to rise, spring up)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin surgere (to rise) → Old French sourse → meaning "origin, starting point"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the source of the problem?
   - We need to find the source.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The source of contamination was identified.
   - Multiple data sources were consulted.
