1. **Adjective**: causal (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to or acting as a cause; expressing or indicating cause
   - Of, relating to, or constituting a cause
   
   **Synonyms**: causative, causational, etiological
   
   **Antonyms**: consequential, resultant, effectual
   
   **When to Use**: Technical, academic, and scientific contexts when describing cause-effect relationships; formal analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where simpler terms like "causing" are more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - causal relationship
   - causal factor
   - causal link
   - causal analysis
   - causal connection
   
   **Root Analysis**: caus(e) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin causa (cause) + -al → meaning "relating to cause"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a causal link between smoking and cancer.
   - We need to find the causal factors.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study established causal relationships between variables.
   - Causal inference requires rigorous methodology.

1. **Adjective**: consequent (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Following as a result or effect; resulting from something
   - Logically following from premises or evidence
   
   **Synonyms**: resulting, ensuing, subsequent, following, attendant
   
   **Antonyms**: causal, prior, antecedent, preceding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when describing results or effects; common in academic and analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "resulting" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - consequent upon something
   - consequent changes
   - consequent damage
   - consequent effects
   - consequent developments
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + -sequ (follow) + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin consequent- (following closely) → con- + sequi (to follow) → meaning "resulting from"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The consequent problems were serious.
   - Deal with the consequent issues.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Economic recession and consequent unemployment increased substantially.
   - Policy changes and consequent reforms transformed the sector.

1. **Adjective**: attributable (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Able to be regarded as caused by; able to be ascribed to
   - Capable of being attributed to a particular cause or source
   
   **Synonyms**: ascribable, assignable, traceable, imputable
   
   **Antonyms**: inexplicable, unaccountable, mysterious
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts when discussing causation; common in research and analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "caused by" or "because of" are more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - attributable to something
   - directly attributable
   - largely attributable
   - solely attributable
   - clearly attributable
   
   **Root Analysis**: attribute + -able (able to be)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin attribuere (to assign to) + -able → meaning "able to be assigned to a cause"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The success is attributable to hard work.
   - Most accidents are attributable to human error.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The decline is attributable to multiple systemic factors.
   - Mortality reductions are attributable to improved healthcare access.

1. **Adjective**: resultant (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Occurring or produced as a result or consequence of something
   - Following as an effect or consequence
   
   **Synonyms**: resulting, consequent, ensuing, following
   
   **Antonyms**: causal, causative, antecedent
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and technical contexts when describing effects or outcomes; common in scientific and analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "resulting" is more common
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - resultant effect
   - resultant damage
   - resultant change
   - resultant force (physics)
   - resultant problems
   
   **Root Analysis**: result + -ant (causing or performing)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin resultare (to rebound) + -ant → meaning "arising as a result"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The resultant mess took hours to clean.
   - Deal with the resultant issues.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The resultant pressure differential drives the process.
   - Resultant changes were monitored continuously.

1. **Adjective**: underlying (non-gradable, attributive)

   **Synonyms**: fundamental, basic, root, core, hidden
   
   **Antonyms**: superficial, apparent, surface, obvious
   
   **When to Use**: Analytical contexts when discussing deep or hidden causes; common in academic and professional writing

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - underlying cause
   - underlying factor
   - underlying reason
   - underlying problem
   - underlying condition
   
   **Root Analysis**: under + lie + -ing
   
   **Etymology**: under (beneath) + lie (be positioned) + -ing → meaning "fundamental, basic"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the underlying reason for this?
   - There's an underlying problem here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research identified underlying mechanisms.

1. **Adjective**: primary (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Of chief importance; principal; main
   - First in order of time or development; earliest
   
   **Synonyms**: main, principal, chief, foremost, leading
   
   **Antonyms**: secondary, subordinate, minor, auxiliary
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when indicating the most important or main cause/factor; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing multiple equal causes without hierarchy
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - primary cause
   - primary reason
   - primary factor
   - primary concern
   - primary objective
   
   **Root Analysis**: prim(e) (first) + -ary (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin primarius (of the first rank) → primus (first) → meaning "first in importance"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's the primary reason I'm here.
   - The primary cause was human error.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The primary objective is to reduce costs.
   - Primary factors were identified through regression analysis.

1. **Adjective**: contributing (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Helping to cause or bring about a result; playing a part in producing something
   - Being one of several causes
   
   **Synonyms**: contributory, causative, influential, conducive
   
   **Antonyms**: hindering, obstructive, counterproductive
   
   **When to Use**: Analytical contexts when discussing partial or multiple causation; common in research and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing sole or definitive causes (use "sole" or "primary")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contributing factor
   - contributing cause
   - contributing element
   - contributing variable
   - contributing circumstance
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + tribute (give) + -ing
   
   **Etymology**: Latin contribuere (to bring together) + -ing → meaning "helping to cause"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stress was a contributing factor.
   - Several contributing causes were identified.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Multiple contributing variables were controlled for.
   - The analysis identified key contributing factors.

1. **Adjective**: direct (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(In causation)* Without intervening factors or intermediaries; immediate
   - Having a clear and immediate connection
   
   **Synonyms**: immediate, straightforward, unmediated, proximate
   
   **Antonyms**: indirect, mediated, remote, distant
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when emphasizing immediate or clear causation; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing complex or mediated causation (use "indirect")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - direct cause
   - direct result
   - direct consequence
   - direct effect
   - direct link
   
   **Root Analysis**: di- (apart) + rect (straight, right)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin directus (straight) → dis- (apart) + regere (to guide) → meaning "straight, immediate"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That was a direct result of his actions.
   - There's a direct link between them.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention had a direct effect on outcomes.
   - Direct causation was established through controlled experiments.

1. **Adjective**: indirect (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(In causation)* Not directly caused by or resulting from something; mediated through other factors
   - Having intervening steps or factors
   
   **Synonyms**: mediated, secondary, circuitous, roundabout
   
   **Antonyms**: direct, immediate, straightforward, proximate
   
   **When to Use**: Analytical contexts when describing complex or mediated causation; common in research and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing clear, immediate causation (use "direct")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - indirect cause
   - indirect effect
   - indirect consequence
   - indirect impact
   - indirect relationship
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (not) + direct (straight)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin in- (not) + directus (straight) → meaning "not straight, mediated"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's an indirect connection between them.
   - It had an indirect effect on us.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Indirect effects were assessed through mediation analysis.
   - The policy had significant indirect consequences.

1. **Adjective**: inevitable (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Certain to happen; unavoidable
   - Unable to be prevented or avoided due to causal factors
   
   **Synonyms**: unavoidable, certain, inescapable, inexorable, fated
   
   **Antonyms**: avoidable, preventable, uncertain, contingent
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when describing certainty or unavoidability; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing preventable or uncertain outcomes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inevitable result
   - inevitable consequence
   - inevitable outcome
   - seem inevitable
   - make inevitable
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (not) + evit(able) (avoidable)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inevitabilis (unavoidable) → in- (not) + evitare (to avoid) → meaning "cannot be avoided"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Failure was inevitable given the circumstances.
   - The outcome seemed inevitable.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The collapse was an inevitable consequence of policy failures.
   - Given these conditions, conflict was inevitable.

1. **Adjective**: proximate (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(In causation)* Nearest in time or relation; immediately preceding or following
   - The immediate or direct cause
   
   **Synonyms**: immediate, direct, closest, nearest
   
   **Antonyms**: remote, distant, ultimate, underlying
   
   **When to Use**: Legal, philosophical, and analytical contexts when distinguishing immediate from remote causes; formal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where it sounds overly technical
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proximate cause
   - proximate factor
   - proximate reason
   - proximate trigger
   - proximate event
   
   **Root Analysis**: proxim(ate) (nearest)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin proximus (nearest) → prope (near) + -imus (superlative) → meaning "nearest, immediate"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What was the proximate cause?
   - Find the proximate trigger.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Legal liability depends on proximate causation.
   - The proximate cause was identified as equipment failure.

1. **Adjective**: unforeseen (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not anticipated or predicted; unexpected
   - Not expected or able to be predicted due to lack of prior indication
   
   **Synonyms**: unexpected, unanticipated, unpredicted, surprise
   
   **Antonyms**: foreseen, expected, anticipated, predicted
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when describing unexpected causes or outcomes; appropriate for formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing predictable or anticipated causes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unforeseen circumstances
   - unforeseen consequences
   - unforeseen events
   - unforeseen problems
   - due to unforeseen
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + fore (before) + see + -n
   
   **Etymology**: un- (not) + foresee (see beforehand) + -n (past participle) → meaning "not predicted"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We ran into unforeseen problems.
   - It was an unforeseen complication.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project was delayed due to unforeseen circumstances.
   - Unforeseen consequences emerged during implementation.
