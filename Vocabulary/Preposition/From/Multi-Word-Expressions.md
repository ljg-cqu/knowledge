# Multi-Word Expressions with "From"

1. **Expression**: from scratch (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Starting from the very beginning with no prior preparation or existing foundation; starting with basic raw materials or ingredients
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning refers to starting line ("scratch" in sports); Figurative meaning is starting completely anew without any existing work
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: from the ground up, from zero, from nothing, from the beginning, from square one
   
   **Antonyms**: with a head start, building on existing work, continuing from where left off
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing complete new beginnings, original creation, absence of shortcuts or existing foundation; used across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when partial work already exists or when building on others' contributions (would undervalue existing work)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - start from scratch
   - build from scratch
   - create from scratch
   - made from scratch
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originates from sporting events where "scratch" was the starting line drawn on the ground; competitors starting "from scratch" had no handicap or advantage, beginning at the literal zero point
   
   **Root Analysis**: "from" (origin point) + "scratch" (starting line in racing)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I lost all my files, so I had to start the project from scratch.
   - She learned to code from scratch with no prior experience.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization was built from scratch without external funding.
   - Developing the framework from scratch required significant research investment.

2. **Expression**: from time to time (type: idiom/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Occasionally; sometimes but not regularly or frequently
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning refers to time intervals; Figurative meaning is irregular, occasional occurrence
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: occasionally, sometimes, now and then, every so often, once in a while, periodically
   
   **Antonyms**: constantly, always, never, regularly, continuously
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing irregular or infrequent occurrence; neutral register suitable for all contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to regular schedules or predictable patterns (use "regularly," "periodically")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - from time to time (most common)
   - time to time (less common, often with "from" implied)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: The repetition of "time" with the connector "from...to" creates emphasis on intervals between time points, suggesting intermittent occurrence across temporal span
   
   **Root Analysis**: Correlative "from...to" structure with repeated "time"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I check in with my parents from time to time to see how they're doing.
   - From time to time, I like to treat myself to something nice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee convenes from time to time to review policy implementations.
   - From time to time, exceptions to the standard protocol may be warranted.

3. **Expression**: from now on (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Starting at the present moment and continuing into the future; henceforth
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: henceforth, from this point forward, starting now, going forward, in the future
   
   **Antonyms**: from now until now, in the past, previously
   
   **When to Use**: Marking decisive change, new resolution, or policy that begins immediately; used in announcements, resolutions, decisions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when change started in the past or will start in future (use "from then on" or "from tomorrow on")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - from now on (most common)
   - from here on (out)
   - from this moment on
   - from today on
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "now" as temporal anchor + "on" indicating continuation creates clear demarcation between past and future states
   
   **Root Analysis**: "from" (starting point) + "now" (present moment) + "on" (continuation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - From now on, I'm eating healthy—no more junk food!
   - Let's make sure to communicate better from now on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - From now on, all submissions must be received by the specified deadline.
   - The revised policy will be in effect from now on.

4. **Expression**: from the bottom of one's heart (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With complete sincerity and genuine emotion; with the deepest feelings
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning refers to the physical heart; Figurative meaning is deep emotional sincerity and authenticity
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: sincerely, genuinely, wholeheartedly, with all one's heart, truly, earnestly
   
   **Antonyms**: insincerely, half-heartedly, superficially, without feeling
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing gratitude, apologies, wishes, or emotions with maximum sincerity; tends toward informal/emotional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly sentimental or melodramatic in professional contexts; avoid in objective or technical writing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - from the bottom of my/your/his/her heart
   - from the heart (shorter version)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: The heart is culturally metaphor for emotions; "bottom" suggests the deepest, most fundamental level, creating intensification of sincerity
   
   **Root Analysis**: "from" (source) + "the bottom of" (deepest part) + "heart" (emotion center)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I want to thank you from the bottom of my heart for everything you've done.
   - She apologized from the bottom of her heart and really meant it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board extends its gratitude from the bottom of their hearts to all volunteers.
   - (*Note: Less common in formal contexts; typically reserved for speeches or personal letters*)

5. **Expression**: from A to Z (type: idiom/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely; thoroughly; covering everything from beginning to end
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning refers to first and last letters of alphabet; Figurative meaning is comprehensive coverage
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: comprehensively, thoroughly, completely, in every detail, from start to finish, from beginning to end
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, incompletely, superficially
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing completeness, thoroughness, or comprehensive coverage; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound clichéd in formal academic writing; consider more specific alternatives
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - from A to Z (most common)
   - A to Z (used as adjective: "an A to Z guide")
   - from alpha to omega (more formal/literary variant)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: A and Z are first and last letters of English alphabet, symbolizing complete span from beginning to end
   
   **Root Analysis**: "from" + "A" (first letter) + "to" + "Z" (last letter)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He knows that car from A to Z—he can fix anything on it.
   - She explained the whole process from A to Z.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manual covers project management from A to Z.
   - Our comprehensive guide addresses system administration from A to Z.

6. **Expression**: suffer from (type: collocation - strong)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To experience pain, illness, or negative conditions; to be afflicted with
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: be afflicted with, experience, endure, be affected by, have
   
   **Antonyms**: benefit from, enjoy, thrive with
   
   **When to Use**: Describing ongoing medical conditions, psychological states, or negative situations; medical and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for temporary minor discomforts (use "have"); "suffer" carries weight of serious or prolonged difficulty
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - suffer from illness/disease/condition
   - suffering from
   - has suffered from
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "suffer" requires "from" (not "of" or "with") to indicate the source/cause of suffering; this is a strong dependent preposition pattern
   
   **Root Analysis**: "suffer" (Latin "sufferre" = to bear) + "from" (source)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's been suffering from terrible headaches lately.
   - A lot of people suffer from anxiety these days.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients suffering from chronic conditions require ongoing care.
   - The economy continues to suffer from structural inefficiencies.

7. **Expression**: benefit from (type: collocation - strong)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To gain advantage, improvement, or positive results from something
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: gain from, profit from, derive advantage from, be helped by
   
   **Antonyms**: suffer from, be harmed by, lose from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing positive outcomes, advantages gained, or improvements resulting from actions/conditions; formal and academic contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in very casual speech (people often say "get something out of")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - benefit from something
   - would benefit from
   - could benefit from
   - benefiting from
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "benefit" requires "from" (not "of" or "by" typically) to indicate source of advantage; dependent preposition
   
   **Root Analysis**: "benefit" (Latin "benefactum" = good deed) + "from" (source)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You'd really benefit from getting more sleep.
   - Students benefit from hands-on experience.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations benefit from implementing systematic quality control measures.
   - Research participants benefited from access to experimental treatments.

8. **Expression**: derive from (type: collocation - strong)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To originate from; to obtain or receive from a source; to trace the origin to
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: originate from, stem from, come from, be obtained from, trace back to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (source-based verb doesn't have direct opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts discussing etymology, sources, origins, or obtaining things from sources
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation (use "come from" or "get from")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - derive from something
   - derived from
   - is derived from
   - derives from
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "derive" requires "from" to indicate source/origin; this is a dependent preposition relationship
   
   **Root Analysis**: "derive" (Latin "derivare" = to draw off water) + "from" (source)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The word "salary" derives from the Latin word for salt.
   - Most of my income derives from freelance work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theoretical framework derives from post-structuralist philosophy.
   - Significant economic benefits derive from technological innovation.

9. **Expression**: differ from (type: collocation - strong)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be unlike or dissimilar to; to show differences when compared to
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: be different from, vary from, contrast with, diverge from
   
   **Antonyms**: resemble, be similar to, match, correspond to
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic comparison contexts; note: "different from" (not "different than") is standard in formal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal tone; casual contexts often use "be different from" or simply "not like"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - differ from something
   - differs from
   - differing from
   - different from (adjective form)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "differ" requires "from" (not "with" or "to") to indicate the comparison point; important for learners as this differs from some other languages
   
   **Root Analysis**: "differ" (Latin "differre" = to carry apart) + "from" (comparison point)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How does this model differ from the old one?
   - My opinion differs from yours on this issue.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Contemporary approaches differ significantly from traditional methodologies.
   - The results differ from our initial hypotheses in several key respects.

10. **Expression**: prevent from (type: collocation - strong)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To stop someone or something from doing something; to make impossible or unable to happen
    - *Fixedness*: strong
    
    **Synonyms**: stop from, keep from, hinder from, prohibit from, block from
    
    **Antonyms**: allow to, enable to, permit to, facilitate
    
    **When to Use**: Describing obstacles, barriers, or active stopping of actions; formal and informal contexts
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Note structure: "prevent someone FROM doing" (not "prevent someone TO do")
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - prevent someone from doing something
    - prevented from
    - preventing from
    - to prevent from
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "prevent" requires "from" + gerund structure; common learner error is using "to" infinitive instead
    
    **Root Analysis**: "prevent" (Latin "praevenire" = to come before) + "from"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - The rain prevented us from going to the beach.
    - Nothing's going to prevent me from achieving my goals.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Technical limitations prevented researchers from completing the analysis.
    - Legal restrictions prevent organizations from sharing confidential data.

11. **Expression**: refrain from (type: collocation - strong)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To hold oneself back from; to deliberately avoid doing something; to abstain
    - *Fixedness*: strong
    
    **Synonyms**: abstain from, avoid, hold back from, resist, forbear from
    
    **Antonyms**: indulge in, engage in, proceed with
    
    **When to Use**: Formal contexts describing deliberate self-control or avoidance; often in rules, requests, or polite instructions
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Very formal; casual contexts use "avoid" or "not do"
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - refrain from doing something
    - please refrain from
    - must refrain from
    - refrained from
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "refrain" requires "from" + gerund; dependent preposition relationship
    
    **Root Analysis**: "refrain" (Latin "refrenare" = to hold back with reins) + "from"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Could you refrain from smoking in here?
    - I'm trying to refrain from checking my phone so much.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Participants are requested to refrain from discussing the study with others.
    - Please refrain from making any comments until the presentation concludes.

12. **Expression**: result from (type: collocation - strong)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To occur or exist as a consequence of; to be caused by
    - *Fixedness*: strong
    
    **Synonyms**: arise from, stem from, be caused by, follow from, occur because of
    
    **Antonyms**: result in (different direction of causality), cause, lead to
    
    **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts describing cause-effect relationships with focus on the consequence
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Note distinction: "result from" (consequence ← cause) vs. "result in" (cause → consequence)
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - result from something
    - results from
    - resulting from
    - resulted from
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "result" with "from" indicates backward causal direction (effect from cause); dependent preposition
    
    **Root Analysis**: "result" (Latin "resultare" = to spring back) + "from" (source)
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - His success resulted from years of hard work.
    - Most accidents result from driver error.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The economic downturn resulted from multiple systemic failures.
    - Disparities in educational outcomes result from socioeconomic inequalities.

13. **Expression**: protect from / shield from (type: collocation - strong)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To keep safe from harm, danger, or negative effects; to guard against
    - *Fixedness*: strong
    
    **Synonyms**: guard from, defend from, safeguard from, shelter from, preserve from
    
    **Antonyms**: expose to, subject to, endanger with
    
    **When to Use**: Describing defensive actions, safety measures, or prevention of harm; all registers
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Note: "protect from" (harm/danger) vs. "protect against" (future threats); subtle distinction
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - protect someone/something from
    - protection from
    - protecting from
    - shield from (stronger version)
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "protect/shield" requires "from" (or sometimes "against") to indicate the threat source
    
    **Root Analysis**: "protect" (Latin "protegere" = to cover in front) + "from"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Sunscreen protects your skin from UV damage.
    - I'm just trying to protect you from getting hurt.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Regulations protect consumers from fraudulent practices.
    - Encryption shields sensitive data from unauthorized access.

14. **Expression**: distinguish from (type: collocation - strong)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To recognize or identify differences between; to see or show as different
    - *Fixedness*: strong
    
    **Synonyms**: differentiate from, tell apart from, discriminate from, separate from
    
    **Antonyms**: confuse with, mix up with, conflate with
    
    **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts involving comparison, classification, or identification of differences
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can be too formal for casual speech (use "tell apart" or "tell the difference")
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - distinguish something from something else
    - distinguish between X and Y (different pattern)
    - distinguished from
    - distinguishing from
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "distinguish" uses "from" when marking one thing as different from another; dependent preposition
    
    **Root Analysis**: "distinguish" (Latin "distinguere" = to separate) + "from"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Can you distinguish the original from the fake?
    - It's hard to distinguish his voice from his brother's.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The methodology distinguishes this research from previous studies.
    - Critical thinking skills enable students to distinguish fact from opinion.

15. **Expression**: ranging from...to (type: collocation - strong correlative)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Extending or varying between two extremes; covering a spectrum or variety
    - *Fixedness*: strong
    
    **Synonyms**: varying from...to, extending from...to, spanning from...to
    
    **Antonyms**: limited to, restricted to, confined to
    
    **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts describing diversity, variety, or spectrum of options/values/items
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Requires two endpoints; cannot use with single value
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - ranging from X to Y
    - range from...to
    - ranges from...to
    - ranged from...to
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "range" naturally pairs with correlative "from...to" structure to indicate span between limits
    
    **Root Analysis**: "range" (from Old French "reng" = row) + correlative "from...to"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - The class has students ranging from beginners to advanced level.
    - Prices range from $10 to $100.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Participants included individuals ranging from 18 to 75 years of age.
    - The collection encompasses artifacts ranging from ancient pottery to modern installations.
