# Adjectives Commonly Used with "From"

1. **Adjective**: different from (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not the same as; unlike; distinct or separate when compared to something else
   - *Standard usage: "different from" is preferred in formal writing; "different than" is considered non-standard by many style guides*
   
   **Synonyms**: distinct from, separate from, unlike, dissimilar to, divergent from
   
   **Antonyms**: similar to, identical to, the same as, alike, comparable to
   
   **When to Use**: Making comparisons, highlighting distinctions, emphasizing uniqueness; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Important style note: use "different from" (not "different than") in formal writing; British English strongly prefers "different from"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very different from
   - quite different from
   - completely different from
   - significantly different from
   - not so different from
   
   **Root Analysis**: different (Latin "dis-" [apart] + "ferre" [to carry]) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "differentem" (setting apart) → Old French "different" → English "different"; paired with "from" to indicate comparison point
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This version is totally different from the original.
   - My experience was really different from yours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Contemporary methodologies are fundamentally different from traditional approaches.
   - The observed results are statistically different from predicted outcomes.

2. **Adjective**: absent from (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not present at a place or event; missing from; not existing in
   
   **Synonyms**: missing from, away from, not present at, lacking in
   
   **Antonyms**: present at, attending, existing in, available at
   
   **When to Use**: Describing non-presence at specific locations, events, or in compositions; formal and administrative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Absent" can stand alone or pair with "from"; context determines usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - absent from work
   - absent from school
   - conspicuously absent from
   - notably absent from
   - absent from the discussion
   
   **Root Analysis**: absent (Latin "ab-" [away] + "esse" [to be]) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "absentem" (being away) → Old French → English "absent"; "from" specifies the location of absence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's been absent from class all week.
   - Your name is absent from the list.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Key stakeholders were notably absent from the decision-making process.
   - These considerations are conspicuously absent from the current policy framework.

3. **Adjective**: far from (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) At a great distance from (literal spatial meaning)
   - (2) Very different from; not at all; opposite of (figurative meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: distant from, remote from, nowhere near (informal), not at all, contrary to
   
   **Antonyms**: close to, near to, similar to, exactly like
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing distance (literal or figurative), contradiction, or negation; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be intensifying before adjectives ("far from perfect"); functions differently in that construction
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - far from perfect
   - far from home
   - far from over
   - far from clear
   - far from ideal
   
   **Root Analysis**: far (Old English "feor" = at a distance) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "feor" (at a distance) combined with "from" for emphasis
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're still far from finishing this project.
   - That's far from the truth!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results are far from conclusive and warrant further investigation.
   - Current policies are far from adequate in addressing systemic inequalities.

4. **Adjective**: free from (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not containing or affected by something undesirable; exempt from; unencumbered by
   
   **Synonyms**: exempt from, clear of, without, devoid of, unaffected by
   
   **Antonyms**: affected by, burdened with, subject to, containing
   
   **When to Use**: Describing absence of negative qualities, substances, or conditions; formal and commercial contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Free from" emphasizes absence of negatives; "free to" emphasizes liberty to act
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - free from harm
   - free from bias
   - free from defects
   - free from charge
   - free from worry
   
   **Root Analysis**: free (Old English "frēo" = not in bondage) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "frēo" (free, not in bondage) + "from" specifying what is absent
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This product is free from artificial colors.
   - I want to be free from all this stress.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis is free from methodological bias.
   - Participants were screened to ensure they were free from pre-existing conditions.

5. **Adjective**: safe from (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Protected from harm, danger, or negative consequences; not likely to be harmed by
   
   **Synonyms**: protected from, secure from, shielded from, immune to
   
   **Antonyms**: exposed to, vulnerable to, endangered by, at risk from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing protection or security from specific threats; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Safe" can also pair with other prepositions depending on context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - safe from harm
   - safe from danger
   - safe from attack
   - safe from prosecution
   - keep safe from
   
   **Root Analysis**: safe (Latin "salvus" = uninjured, healthy) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "salvus" → Old French "sauf" → English "safe"; "from" specifies the threat
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - At least here we're safe from the storm.
   - Your secret is safe from me—I won't tell anyone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Whistleblowers must be safe from retaliation to encourage transparency.
   - Data encryption keeps information safe from unauthorized access.

6. **Adjective**: distinct from (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Recognizably different in nature from something else; separate and not to be confused with
   
   **Synonyms**: separate from, different from, distinguishable from, unlike
   
   **Antonyms**: similar to, indistinguishable from, identical to, the same as
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts emphasizing clear differences or separate identities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More formal than "different from"; casual contexts prefer simpler alternatives
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - clearly distinct from
   - quite distinct from
   - entirely distinct from
   - distinct from other
   - wholly distinct from
   
   **Root Analysis**: distinct (Latin "dis-" [apart] + "-stinguere" [to prick]) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "distinctus" (separated) → English "distinct"; "from" marks comparison point
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This style is pretty distinct from what we usually see.
   - Her approach is distinct from everyone else's.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This methodology is conceptually distinct from previous research paradigms.
   - The observed phenomenon is distinct from previously documented cases.

7. **Adjective**: separate from (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not joined or connected to; existing as an independent entity; apart from
   
   **Synonyms**: apart from, detached from, disconnected from, independent of
   
   **Antonyms**: connected to, joined to, attached to, part of
   
   **When to Use**: Describing physical or conceptual separation; emphasizing independence or distinction
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Separate from" emphasizes independence; context determines if physical or abstract
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - completely separate from
   - entirely separate from
   - keep separate from
   - remain separate from
   - separate from others
   
   **Root Analysis**: separate (Latin "se-" [apart] + "parare" [prepare]) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "separatus" (divided) → English "separate"; "from" indicates what it's divided from
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Keep your work stuff separate from your personal life.
   - This issue is completely separate from what we were discussing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These findings are entirely separate from the primary research questions.
   - The administrative division operates separate from the academic departments.

8. **Adjective**: immune from (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Protected or exempt from something, especially an obligation or harmful effect; not affected by
   
   **Synonyms**: exempt from, protected from, resistant to, shielded from
   
   **Antonyms**: susceptible to, vulnerable to, subject to, exposed to
   
   **When to Use**: Legal, medical, and formal contexts describing exemption or protection
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Note: "immune from" (legal/abstract protection) vs. "immune to" (medical/physical resistance); distinction is important
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immune from prosecution
   - immune from criticism
   - immune from liability
   - not immune from
   - immune from consequences
   
   **Root Analysis**: immune (Latin "im-" [not] + "munis" [serving, obligated]) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "immunis" (exempt from public service) → English "immune"; "from" specifies what is avoided
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Nobody's immune from making mistakes.
   - Even celebrities aren't immune from criticism.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Diplomatic personnel are immune from local prosecution under international law.
   - No organization is immune from the effects of economic recession.

9. **Adjective**: removed from (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Distant or detached from; having little connection with; not involved in
   
   **Synonyms**: detached from, distant from, disconnected from, isolated from
   
   **Antonyms**: connected to, involved in, close to, engaged with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts describing emotional, social, or physical distance or detachment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can have passive voice sense ("was removed from"); context clarifies usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - far removed from
   - quite removed from
   - completely removed from
   - increasingly removed from
   - removed from reality
   
   **Root Analysis**: removed (Latin "re-" [back] + "movere" [to move]) + from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "removere" (to move back) → English "remove"; adjective form with "from" indicates separation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He seems pretty removed from what's actually going on.
   - This plan is far removed from reality.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Policy decisions appear increasingly removed from stakeholder concerns.
   - The theoretical models are far removed from practical implementation contexts.

10. **Adjective**: exempt from (non-gradable)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Free from an obligation, duty, or liability to which others are subject; not required to comply with
    
    **Synonyms**: excused from, released from, free from, not subject to
    
    **Antonyms**: subject to, liable for, obligated to, required to
    
    **When to Use**: Legal, administrative, and regulatory contexts describing exceptions to rules or requirements
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Formal term; casual contexts use "don't have to" or "not required to"
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - exempt from taxes
    - exempt from requirements
    - exempt from duty
    - exempt from liability
    - exempt from service
    
    **Root Analysis**: exempt (Latin "ex-" [out] + "emere" [to take]) + from
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "exemptus" (taken out, removed) → English "exempt"; "from" indicates what is avoided
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Are students exempt from paying this fee?
    - Nobody should be exempt from following the rules.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Certain organizations are exempt from federal taxation under applicable statutes.
    - No employee is exempt from adherence to ethical guidelines.

11. **Adjective**: independent from (non-gradable)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Not depending on or controlled by another; free from external control or support; self-governing
    
    **Synonyms**: autonomous from, separate from, free from, self-governing from
    
    **Antonyms**: dependent on, reliant on, controlled by, subject to
    
    **When to Use**: Formal contexts describing autonomy, self-governance, or freedom from control
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Note: "independent from" (emphasizes separation) vs. "independent of" (emphasizes lack of dependence); subtle difference
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - independent from control
    - independent from influence
    - politically independent from
    - financially independent from
    - independent from others
    
    **Root Analysis**: independent (Latin "in-" [not] + "de-" [from] + "pendere" [to hang]) + from
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "independentem" (not hanging from) → English "independent"; "from" reinforces separation
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - She's completely independent from her family now.
    - The group operates independent from the main organization.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The oversight body must remain independent from political influence.
    - Research findings should be independent from funding source biases.

12. **Adjective**: isolated from (non-gradable)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Separated from others; alone or apart; having minimal contact or connection with
    
    **Synonyms**: separated from, cut off from, detached from, removed from
    
    **Antonyms**: connected to, integrated with, linked to, in contact with
    
    **When to Use**: Describing physical, social, or conceptual separation; medical, social, and scientific contexts
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can be passive ("was isolated from"); context clarifies adjective vs. passive verb
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - isolated from society
    - isolated from others
    - completely isolated from
    - geographically isolated from
    - socially isolated from
    
    **Root Analysis**: isolated (Latin "insula" = island) + from
    
    **Etymology**: Italian "isolato" (made into an island) → French "isolé" → English "isolated"; "from" indicates separation source
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - He feels totally isolated from his coworkers.
    - The village is pretty isolated from the rest of the country.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The community remains geographically isolated from urban centers.
    - Participants were socially isolated from external influences during the study period.

13. **Adjective**: insulated from (non-gradable)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Protected from unpleasant influences or experiences; shielded from direct effects
    
    **Synonyms**: protected from, shielded from, sheltered from, buffered from
    
    **Antonyms**: exposed to, vulnerable to, subject to, affected by
    
    **When to Use**: Formal contexts describing protection from negative effects; can be literal (physical) or figurative (social/economic)
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Implies protective barrier; not used for active threats (prefer "protected")
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - insulated from competition
    - insulated from consequences
    - insulated from reality
    - well-insulated from
    - insulated from effects
    
    **Root Analysis**: insulated (Latin "insula" = island) + from
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "insula" (island) → "insulate" (make into an island, isolate) → English "insulated"; "from" indicates what is kept away
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Rich people are often insulated from economic problems.
    - He's been insulated from criticism his whole life.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Tenured faculty are relatively insulated from institutional pressures.
    - The sector remained partially insulated from broader market volatility.

14. **Adjective**: derived from (non-gradable)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Obtained or developed from a specified source; originating from
    
    **Synonyms**: obtained from, originating from, coming from, stemming from
    
    **Antonyms**: original, primary, underived, independent
    
    **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts describing origins, sources, or development; etymology and scientific contexts
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can be passive ("was derived from"); context clarifies adjective vs. passive verb
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - derived from Latin
    - derived from research
    - derived from experience
    - derived from data
    - originally derived from
    
    **Root Analysis**: derived (Latin "de-" [from] + "rivus" [stream]) + from
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "derivare" (to draw off from a stream) → English "derive"; "from" specifies source
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - This word is derived from Greek.
    - The recipe is derived from an old family tradition.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The theoretical framework is derived from post-structuralist philosophy.
    - Conclusions are derived from rigorous statistical analysis of empirical data.

15. **Adjective**: banned from (non-gradable)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Officially prohibited or excluded from a place, activity, or privilege
    
    **Synonyms**: prohibited from, barred from, excluded from, forbidden from
    
    **Antonyms**: permitted to, allowed to, welcome to, authorized for
    
    **When to Use**: Legal, administrative, and social contexts describing official exclusion or prohibition
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can be passive ("was banned from"); context clarifies usage
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - banned from entry
    - banned from competition
    - permanently banned from
    - banned from using
    - banned from the premises
    
    **Root Analysis**: banned (Germanic origin related to "summon, proclaim") + from
    
    **Etymology**: Old English "bannan" (to summon, command) → Middle English "ban" (prohibition) → English "ban"; "from" indicates what is prohibited
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - He got banned from the forum for spamming.
    - She's banned from driving for six months.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The substance is banned from use in competitive athletics.
    - Participants found in violation are permanently banned from future studies.
