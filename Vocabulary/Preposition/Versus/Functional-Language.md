1. **Functional Expression**: How does X compare to/with Y? (function: requesting comparison)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asking for information about similarities and differences between two entities
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: How does X stack up against Y?, What's the difference between X and Y?, How is X different from Y?, How does X measure up to Y?
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct functional opposite; different question types)
   
   **When to Use**: Seeking evaluative information, consumer decisions, research contexts; appropriate when comparison is informative
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When comparison is offensive or inappropriate; when items are incomparable
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How does it compare?
   - How would you compare X and Y?
   - Can you compare X to Y?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Neutral request; implies items are comparable; suggests speaker values comparison for decision-making
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - X is better/worse than Y because...
   - They're similar in... but different in...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How does the new iPhone compare to Samsung?
   - How does living here compare with your hometown?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - How does the proposed methodology compare to established approaches?
   - How do these results compare with previous findings?

1. **Functional Expression**: X is better/worse than Y (function: expressing opinion/comparison)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Making a comparative judgment about quality or value between two things
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: X surpasses Y, X is superior/inferior to Y, X outperforms Y, X is preferable to Y
   
   **Antonyms**: X and Y are equal, X is the same as Y, X is comparable to Y
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing preferences, making evaluative statements, recommendations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When objectivity is required; when diplomatic neutrality is needed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - X is much better than Y
   - X is far superior to Y
   - X beats Y
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Subjective evaluation; can be face-threatening if interlocutor prefers Y; hedge with "in my opinion" for politeness
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I agree/disagree because...
   - That depends on what you value
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Coffee is better than tea, in my opinion.
   - This restaurant is way better than that one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Method A demonstrates superior efficacy compared to Method B.
   - The intervention produced better outcomes than conventional treatment.

1. **Functional Expression**: On the one hand... on the other hand (function: presenting balanced argument)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Presenting two contrasting perspectives or considerations systematically
   - *Formality*: formal to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (shows balanced consideration)
   
   **Synonyms**: From one perspective... from another..., One argument is... another is..., While X is true... Y is also true
   
   **Antonyms**: Simply put..., The only consideration is..., There's only one way to see this
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, balanced arguments, complex decision-making; showing multiple perspectives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When strong position is needed; casual conversation (sounds formal)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - On the one hand [argument], but on the other hand [counter-argument]
   - On one hand... on the other
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedging strategy; demonstrates critical thinking; creates expectation of resolution or synthesis
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Which do you think is more important?
   - How do you weigh those considerations?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - On the one hand, the job pays well. On the other hand, I'd have to relocate.
   - On one hand I agree, but on the other hand I see their point.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - On the one hand, deregulation promotes efficiency. On the other hand, it may compromise safety standards.
   - On the one hand, the methodology ensures rigor. On the other hand, it limits ecological validity.

1. **Functional Expression**: Compared to X, Y is... (function: making comparison)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Establishing a basis for comparison and stating relative characteristics
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: In comparison with X, Y is..., Relative to X, Y is..., When measured against X, Y is...
   
   **Antonyms**: Independent of X, Y is..., Regardless of X, Y is...
   
   **When to Use**: Analysis, evaluation, establishing relative qualities or quantities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When absolute rather than relative statements are needed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Compared with X
   - When compared to X
   - If we compare X and Y
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Frames evaluation as relative; implies comparison is meaningful and fair
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's true, but consider...
   - How significant is that difference?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Compared to last year, this year is much better.
   - Compared to what I expected, the movie was disappointing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Compared to baseline measurements, post-intervention scores improved significantly.
   - Compared to conventional approaches, this method demonstrates superior precision.

1. **Functional Expression**: What are the advantages/disadvantages of X versus Y? (function: requesting evaluation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asking for systematic evaluation of positive and negative aspects of alternatives
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: What are the pros and cons of X vs Y?, How do X and Y compare?, What's better: X or Y?, What are the trade-offs?
   
   **Antonyms**: Tell me only about X, Should I choose X?, What's good about X?
   
   **When to Use**: Decision-making, research, consumer choices; seeking comprehensive evaluation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When quick yes/no answer is needed; when only one option is viable
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What are the pros and cons?
   - What are the trade-offs between X and Y?
   - What are the benefits and drawbacks?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies both options are viable; requests balanced analysis; suggests rational decision-making process
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - The main advantages of X are... while Y offers...
   - It depends on your priorities
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What are the advantages of buying versus leasing a car?
   - What are the pros and cons of each approach?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - What are the relative advantages and disadvantages of quantitative versus qualitative methodologies?
   - What are the comparative benefits of centralized versus decentralized governance?

1. **Functional Expression**: While X is true, Y is also the case (function: conceding and contrasting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Acknowledging one point while introducing a contrasting or qualifying point
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens disagreement)
   
   **Synonyms**: Although X is true, Y..., X may be accurate, but Y..., I grant that X, however Y...
   
   **Antonyms**: X is true, therefore..., Only X matters, X is the complete picture
   
   **When to Use**: Diplomatic disagreement, balanced arguments, complex analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When full agreement or complete disagreement is appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - While I agree that X, I also think Y
   - While X has merit, Y is also important
   - While that's true, consider also...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness strategy; acknowledges interlocutor's point before contrasting; maintains face
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I see your point about Y
   - Fair enough
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - While that's a good point, I still think we should wait.
   - While I like the idea, I'm worried about the cost.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - While the methodology ensures rigor, ecological validity remains limited.
   - While previous research supports this view, recent evidence suggests reconsideration.

1. **Functional Expression**: In contrast to..., In comparison with... (function: introducing comparison/contrast)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Formally introducing comparative or contrastive information
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Unlike..., As opposed to..., Differently from..., Compared to...
   
   **Antonyms**: Similar to..., Like..., In accordance with..., Consistent with...
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, formal analysis, research papers; introducing systematic comparison
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; when simpler "unlike" or "compared to" works
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - By contrast...
   - As contrasted with...
   - When compared with...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals formal analytical discourse; sets up expectation of detailed comparison
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - How significant is that difference?
   - What accounts for that contrast?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In contrast to what I expected, the food was great.
   - In comparison with other restaurants, this one is expensive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In contrast to conventional wisdom, data suggest alternative interpretations.
   - In comparison with control groups, treatment groups exhibited significant improvement.

1. **Functional Expression**: If we're comparing X and Y... (function: framing comparison)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Establishing a comparative framework before making specific points
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: When we compare X and Y..., In comparing X to Y..., Looking at X versus Y...
   
   **Antonyms**: Looking at X alone..., Focusing only on X..., Independent of Y...
   
   **When to Use**: Introducing comparative analysis; setting up evaluation; discussion contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When comparison is already established; when brevity is important
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - If we compare X with Y
   - When comparing X to Y
   - In a comparison of X and Y
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conditional framing suggests tentative or exploratory comparison; invites dialogue
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, and in that comparison...
   - That's a fair comparison
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If we're comparing the two phones, the newer one is clearly better.
   - If we're comparing costs, Option A wins.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If we're comparing methodological rigor, quantitative approaches offer distinct advantages.
   - If we're comparing outcomes, the intervention demonstrates superior efficacy.

1. **Functional Expression**: Which is better/worse: X or Y? (function: requesting preference/evaluation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asking for evaluative judgment between two alternatives
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: What's better, X or Y?, Should I choose X or Y?, Do you prefer X or Y?, X or Y?
   
   **Antonyms**: Are both good?, Are they the same?, Does it matter?
   
   **When to Use**: Seeking advice, consumer decisions, informal evaluations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When both options may be offensive; formal academic contexts (use more formal phrasing)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Which do you prefer?
   - Which would you choose?
   - What's the better option?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Binary choice question; implies mutual exclusivity; puts pressure on respondent to choose
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I'd say X because...
   - It depends on what you want
   - They're both good for different reasons
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Which is better: iPhone or Android?
   - Which is worse: being late or canceling?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Which approach offers superior outcomes: qualitative or quantitative analysis?
   - Which policy alternative presents fewer risks: A or B?

1. **Functional Expression**: There's no comparison (between X and Y) (function: asserting superiority)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Stating that one option is so superior that comparison is meaningless
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: X is far superior, X is in a different league, Y doesn't come close, X wins hands down
   
   **Antonyms**: They're very close, They're comparable, It's neck and neck
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing strong preference, emphasizing quality differences; informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When diplomacy required; when other option has supporters present
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - There's simply no comparison
   - No comparison whatsoever
   - They don't compare
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Strong assertion; potentially face-threatening to supporters of inferior option; emphatic
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I agree completely
   - Really? I think they're closer than you think
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's no comparison—homemade is way better.
   - Between these two cars, there's no comparison.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In terms of efficacy, there is no comparison between the interventions.
   - Regarding precision, there exists no comparison between the methodologies.

1. **Functional Expression**: It depends on what you value/prioritize (function: hedging/qualifying comparison)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggesting that evaluation depends on subjective preferences or priorities
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (avoids definitive answer)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's a matter of preference, That depends on your priorities, Different people value different things
   
   **Antonyms**: X is objectively better, There's a clear winner, The answer is obvious
   
   **When to Use**: Avoiding commitment, acknowledging multiple valid perspectives, diplomatic response
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When objective answer exists; when decisiveness is valued
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It depends on what matters to you
   - That's subjective
   - Different strokes for different folks
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-saving hedge; acknowledges subjectivity; avoids potential conflict from definitive answer
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Fair point
   - But if you had to choose...
   - What do YOU value?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - iPhone vs Android? It depends on what you value—simplicity or customization.
   - Which is better? It depends on what you prioritize.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The optimal methodology depends upon research objectives and epistemological commitments.
   - Selection depends on organizational priorities and resource constraints.
