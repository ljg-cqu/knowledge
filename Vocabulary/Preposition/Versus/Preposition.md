1. **Preposition**: versus (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Against; in contrast to or as an alternative to; used to indicate competition, opposition, or comparison
   - *(Legal/Sports)* Used to indicate parties in a legal case or teams in a competition
   
   **Synonyms**: against (in competition), compared to, as opposed to, contrary to, vis-à-vis
   
   **Antonyms**: with, alongside, together with, in cooperation with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts, legal proceedings, sports matches, comparisons, contrasts; often abbreviated as "vs." or "v."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cooperative contexts; when "with" or "and" would indicate partnership
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: X versus Y, quality versus quantity, speed versus accuracy
   - **Verb + prep**: compete versus, argue versus (rare)
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not common)
   - **Noun + prep**: case versus, match versus, debate versus
   
   **Root Analysis**: versus (Latin past participle of vertere "to turn")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin versus (turned toward/against) → English legal/formal usage (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's quality versus quantity, you know?
   - Tonight's game: Tigers versus Lions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The case of Brown versus Board of Education transformed educational policy.
   - The analysis examined efficiency versus effectiveness in organizational performance.

1. **Preposition**: against (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In opposition to; in competition with; contrary to
   - *(Spatial)* In contact with or in the direction of
   - *(Abstract)* As protection from or in anticipation of
   
   **Synonyms**: versus, opposing, counter to, contrary to, in opposition to
   
   **Antonyms**: for, with, supporting, in favor of, pro
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing opposition, competition, contrast, or physical contact
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When indicating cooperation or support
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: against the odds, against expectations, against the rules
   - **Verb + prep**: compete against, argue against, vote against, fight against
   - **Adjective + prep**: opposed against (less common)
   - **Noun + prep**: case against, argument against, protection against
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (toward) + gain (opposite)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English againes (in opposition to) → "against" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're playing against the champions tomorrow.
   - I'm against that idea completely.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Arguments against the proposition were systematically refuted.
   - The study examined outcomes in treatment versus control conditions.

1. **Preposition**: compared to/with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In relation to; when measured against; in contrast with
   - Used to draw comparisons or show relative positions
   
   **Synonyms**: relative to, in relation to, versus, as opposed to, against
   
   **Antonyms**: independent of, separate from, isolated from
   
   **When to Use**: Making comparisons, showing relative measurements or qualities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When absolute rather than relative statements are needed
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: compared to last year, compared with other methods, compared to expectations
   - **Verb + prep**: (compare naturally takes this preposition)
   - **Adjective + prep**: superior compared to, inferior compared to
   - **Noun + prep**: performance compared to, results compared with
   
   **Root Analysis**: compare (com- + par "equal") + to/with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin comparare + to/with → phrasal preposition usage (Modern English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is cheap compared to what I paid last time.
   - It's nothing compared with what he went through.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Outcomes improved significantly compared with baseline measurements.
   - Compared to conventional approaches, the method demonstrated superior efficacy.

1. **Preposition**: in contrast to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Showing striking difference when compared with; unlike
   - Used to highlight differences or opposite characteristics
   
   **Synonyms**: unlike, as opposed to, contrary to, differently from, versus
   
   **Antonyms**: similar to, like, in accordance with, consistent with, in harmony with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, academic contexts; emphasizing differences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds formal); when emphasizing similarities
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in contrast to previous findings, in contrast to expectations
   - **Verb + prep**: stand in contrast to, appear in contrast to
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not common)
   - **Noun + prep**: (used as transition phrase)
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + contrast (contra- "against" + -st "stand") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal construction from Latin contrast + English prepositions (Modern English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In contrast to yesterday, today is beautiful.
   - He's quiet, in contrast to his brother.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In contrast to conventional wisdom, data suggest alternative conclusions.
   - In contrast to Group A, Group B exhibited significant improvement.

1. **Preposition**: as opposed to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Rather than; instead of; in contradistinction to
   - Used to present an alternative or contrast
   
   **Synonyms**: rather than, instead of, in contrast to, versus, unlike
   
   **Antonyms**: similar to, along with, together with, in addition to
   
   **When to Use**: Making distinctions, clarifying choices, presenting alternatives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When adding rather than contrasting; cooperative contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: quality as opposed to quantity, theory as opposed to practice
   - **Verb + prep**: choose X as opposed to Y, prefer X as opposed to Y
   - **Adjective + prep**: different as opposed to similar
   - **Noun + prep**: (used to distinguish alternatives)
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + oppose (op- "against" + pose "place") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal construction (17th-18th century English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I want coffee as opposed to tea.
   - We're talking about practice, as opposed to theory.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined qualitative as opposed to quantitative methodologies.
   - Focus on preventive as opposed to reactive measures may prove more efficacious.

1. **Preposition**: over (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating preference or superiority in comparison; more than
   - *(Spatial)* Above in position; across
   
   **Synonyms**: above, more than, in preference to, rather than, instead of
   
   **Antonyms**: under, below, beneath, less than
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing preference, superiority, comparison, or spatial relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When equality rather than preference is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: preference over, advantage over, victory over, superiority over
   - **Verb + prep**: choose over, prefer over, favor over, select over
   - **Adjective + prep**: superior over (though "superior to" is more common)
   - **Noun + prep**: advantage over, preference over, dominance over
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English ofer (above, beyond)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ofer → Middle English over → English "over" (continuous usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I prefer this over that one.
   - We won the game over them last week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The advantages of Method A over Method B merit consideration.
   - The intervention demonstrated clear superiority over conventional treatment.

1. **Preposition**: than (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Used to introduce the second element in a comparison; following comparative adjectives and adverbs
   - Indicating the exception or different case
   
   **Synonyms**: compared to, as opposed to, rather than, versus
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; structural element)
   
   **When to Use**: All comparative constructions; essential for expressing relative degree
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With superlatives (use "of" or "in"); after positive adjectives
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: better than, more than, less than, different than/from
   - **Verb + prep**: (used with comparative structures)
   - **Adjective + prep**: bigger than, faster than, more complex than
   - **Noun + prep**: (follows comparative nouns)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English þanne (then, when)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English þanne (then) → comparative use → English "than" (12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is better than I expected.
   - She's taller than her brother.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Results proved more significant than anticipated.
   - The effect size was larger than that observed in previous studies.

1. **Preposition**: contrary to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In opposition to; in conflict with; opposite to what is stated
   - Against the direction or nature of
   
   **Synonyms**: against, opposed to, in opposition to, counter to, unlike
   
   **Antonyms**: in accordance with, consistent with, in line with, agreeing with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; expressing contradiction or opposition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds formal); neutral comparisons
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: contrary to expectations, contrary to belief, contrary to evidence
   - **Verb + prep**: run contrary to, act contrary to, go contrary to
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not common)
   - **Noun + prep**: (used to indicate opposition)
   
   **Root Analysis**: contrary (contra- "against") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin contrarius + to → phrasal preposition (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Contrary to what you think, I do care.
   - It went contrary to our plans.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Contrary to conventional assumptions, data indicated inverse correlation.
   - The findings run contrary to established theoretical frameworks.

1. **Preposition**: instead of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* As an alternative or substitute for; in place of; rather than
   - Used to indicate replacement or preference
   
   **Synonyms**: rather than, in place of, as an alternative to, as opposed to, in lieu of
   
   **Antonyms**: in addition to, along with, together with, as well as
   
   **When to Use**: Presenting alternatives, indicating substitution or replacement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When addition rather than replacement is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: instead of that, instead of going, instead of X, do Y
   - **Verb + prep**: use instead of, choose instead of, opt instead of
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not common)
   - **Noun + prep**: alternative instead of original, substitute instead of
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + stead (place) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English in stede of (in place of) → "instead of" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's stay home instead of going out.
   - Use this instead of that one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee adopted Plan B instead of the original proposal.
   - Researchers employed qualitative methods instead of quantitative approaches.

1. **Preposition**: relative to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In comparison or proportion to; with regard to; concerning
   - Considered in relation to something else
   
   **Synonyms**: compared to, in relation to, with respect to, concerning, regarding
   
   **Antonyms**: independent of, separate from, irrespective of, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal analysis, comparisons, showing relationships or proportions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Absolute statements; casual contexts where simpler prepositions work
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: relative to baseline, relative to others, relative to expectations
   - **Verb + prep**: measure relative to, assess relative to, position relative to
   - **Adjective + prep**: high relative to, low relative to, significant relative to
   - **Noun + prep**: performance relative to, position relative to, value relative to
   
   **Root Analysis**: relative (relat- "refer back") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin relativus + to → phrasal preposition (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The cost is low relative to other options.
   - His performance is good relative to his experience.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Outcomes were assessed relative to predetermined benchmarks.
   - The effect proved significant relative to control conditions.
