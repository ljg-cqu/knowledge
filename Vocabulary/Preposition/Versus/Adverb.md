1. **Adverb**: comparatively (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In relation to or in comparison with something else; relatively or to a moderate degree
   - When measured against other similar things or standards
   
   **Synonyms**: relatively, fairly, rather, moderately, somewhat
   
   **Antonyms**: absolutely, extremely, completely, totally, independently
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, analysis, when qualifying statements to show relative rather than absolute measurement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When absolute statements are needed; can weaken strong claims unnecessarily
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - comparatively speaking
   - comparatively small
   - comparatively few
   - comparatively high
   - comparatively easy
   
   **Root Analysis**: comparative + -ly (forming adverbs)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin comparativus + -ly → English "comparatively" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It was comparatively easy compared to last time.
   - Comparatively speaking, we're doing pretty well.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention proved comparatively effective in reducing symptoms.
   - Investment returns were comparatively modest during this period.

1. **Adverb**: conversely (type: sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an opposite or contrary manner; introducing a statement that reverses or contrasts with what has been said
   - From the opposite perspective or direction
   
   **Synonyms**: inversely, oppositely, contrarily, alternatively, on the contrary
   
   **Antonyms**: similarly, likewise, correspondingly, equally, identically
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, academic discourse, presenting opposing viewpoints or reverse relationships; sentence-initial position
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds formal); when simple "but" would suffice
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - conversely, one could argue
   - conversely, if we consider
   - conversely, the opposite is true
   - or conversely
   - conversely speaking
   
   **Root Analysis**: converse (Latin conversus) + -ly (forming adverbs)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin conversus (turned around) + -ly → English "conversely" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like the heat. Conversely, my wife prefers the cold.
   - You could increase speed or, conversely, improve quality.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Higher education correlates with income; conversely, poverty often limits educational access.
   - Conversely, decreased regulation may stimulate innovation while increasing systemic risk.

1. **Adverb**: alternatively (type: sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - As another option or possibility; used to introduce a different choice or course of action
   - Instead; as a substitute
   
   **Synonyms**: instead, otherwise, or else, as an alternative, optionally
   
   **Antonyms**: necessarily, exclusively, solely, definitively, invariably
   
   **When to Use**: Presenting options, suggestions, or different approaches; formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When there's no real choice; when commitment to one option is expected
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - alternatively, you could
   - or alternatively
   - alternatively known as
   - alternatively, we might
   - alternatively, consider
   
   **Root Analysis**: alternative + -ly (forming adverbs)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin alternare (do one after another) + -ly → English "alternatively" (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We could go to the movies. Alternatively, we could just stay home.
   - Alternatively, you could try the other method.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee may approve funding; alternatively, it may request revisions.
   - Alternatively, the data may be interpreted as indicating systemic failure.

1. **Adverb**: likewise (type: sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the same way; also; similarly
   - In addition; moreover; used to add a similar or related statement
   
   **Synonyms**: similarly, equally, correspondingly, also, too
   
   **Antonyms**: conversely, differently, contrarily, oppositely, alternatively
   
   **When to Use**: Showing agreement or similarity; adding parallel information; formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When introducing contrasts; when "also" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - likewise for
   - do likewise
   - likewise in
   - likewise true
   - and likewise
   
   **Root Analysis**: like + -wise (in the manner of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English gelicwise (in like manner) → "likewise" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I enjoyed the movie. My friend felt likewise.
   - She agreed, and I did likewise.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Productivity increased in manufacturing; likewise, service sector output improved.
   - The policy applies to full-time employees; part-time staff are likewise affected.
