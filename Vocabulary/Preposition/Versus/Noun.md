1. **Noun**: comparison (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act or process of comparing; consideration of similarities and differences
   - A statement in which two or more things are compared; an analogy or simile
   
   **Synonyms**: contrast, juxtaposition, evaluation, assessment, analogy
   
   **Antonyms**: isolation, separation, distinction, independence
   
   **When to Use**: Analysis, evaluation contexts; academic writing; when systematically examining similarities and differences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When contrast without similarity is meant; when simple observation suffices
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - by comparison
   - in comparison with
   - make a comparison
   - comparison between
   - beyond comparison
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + par (equal) + -ison (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin comparationem (placing together) → Old French comparaison → English "comparison" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's do a quick comparison of the prices.
   - By comparison, this one seems much better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A systematic comparison of methodologies revealed significant disparities.
   - The comparison yielded insights into structural differences.

1. **Noun**: contrast (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being strikingly different from something else when juxtaposed or compared
   - The degree of difference between tones in a visual image or between sounds
   
   **Synonyms**: difference, disparity, distinction, divergence, dissimilarity
   
   **Antonyms**: similarity, uniformity, consistency, harmony, agreement
   
   **When to Use**: Highlighting differences; visual, analytical, or descriptive contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing similarities; when "difference" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in contrast to
   - by contrast
   - sharp contrast
   - stark contrast
   - striking contrast
   
   **Root Analysis**: contra- (against) + -st (stand)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin contra (against) + stare (to stand) → Italian contrasto → English "contrast" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The contrast between the two is pretty obvious.
   - In contrast to yesterday, today is much warmer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data present a stark contrast to previous findings.
   - In contrast to conventional approaches, this methodology emphasizes qualitative analysis.

1. **Noun**: opposition (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Resistance or dissent expressed in action or argument; the state of being opposed
   - A group of adversaries or competitors; the party contesting those in power
   
   **Synonyms**: resistance, hostility, antagonism, defiance, dissent
   
   **Antonyms**: support, cooperation, agreement, alliance, compliance
   
   **When to Use**: Political, competitive, or adversarial contexts; describing resistance or contrary positions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Neutral disagreement; when "difference" or "alternative" would be less confrontational
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in opposition to
   - fierce opposition
   - face opposition
   - opposition party
   - opposition forces
   
   **Root Analysis**: op- (against) + pos (place) + -ition (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin oppositionem (placing against) → Old French oposition → English "opposition" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a lot of opposition to that plan.
   - The opposition won the debate.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal encountered significant opposition from stakeholders.
   - Opposition parties challenged the legislation in parliament.

1. **Noun**: dichotomy (C, pl: dichotomies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A division or contrast between two things that are represented as being opposed or entirely different
   - A separation into two mutually exclusive or contradictory groups or entities
   
   **Synonyms**: division, split, separation, polarization, duality
   
   **Antonyms**: unity, continuity, synthesis, integration, harmony
   
   **When to Use**: Academic, philosophical contexts; describing fundamental divisions or binary oppositions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple differences; when "difference" or "contrast" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - false dichotomy
   - dichotomy between
   - create a dichotomy
   - dichotomy exists
   - clear dichotomy
   
   **Root Analysis**: dicho- (in two) + -tomy (cutting)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek dichotomia (division into two) → English "dichotomy" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a real dichotomy between what they say and do.
   - It's not a simple dichotomy—there are shades of gray.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The dichotomy between theory and practice requires examination.
   - A false dichotomy obscures the complexity of policy options.

1. **Noun**: juxtaposition (U/C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The fact of two things being seen or placed close together with contrasting effect
   - The act of positioning things side by side for comparison or contrast
   
   **Synonyms**: comparison, contrast, proximity, apposition, collocation
   
   **Antonyms**: separation, isolation, distance, segregation
   
   **When to Use**: Formal, artistic, literary contexts; emphasizing deliberate placement for effect
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple comparisons; casual contexts (sounds formal)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - juxtaposition of
   - in juxtaposition
   - juxtaposition between
   - stark juxtaposition
   - create a juxtaposition
   
   **Root Analysis**: juxta- (near) + position (place)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin juxta (near) + position → French juxtaposition → English (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The juxtaposition of old and new architecture is striking.
   - There's an interesting juxtaposition in that scene.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The juxtaposition of conflicting data requires methodological scrutiny.
   - This juxtaposition reveals underlying thematic tensions.
