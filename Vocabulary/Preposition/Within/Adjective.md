1. **Adjective**: reasonable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Fair, practical, and sensible; based on good judgment; not excessive or extreme
   - Within the limits of what is acceptable, fair, or appropriate; moderate
   
   **Synonyms**: sensible, rational, fair, moderate, practical
   
   **Antonyms**: unreasonable, excessive, extreme, irrational, absurd
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing limits, expectations, prices, or behaviors that are fair and not extreme; common in professional, legal, and everyday contexts; appropriate when emphasizing moderation and practicality
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing something that is merely adequate but not particularly good; not appropriate when precision is needed (use specific measurements instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within reasonable limits
   - reasonable timeframe
   - reasonable price
   - reasonable doubt
   - reasonable expectations
   
   **Root Analysis**: reason (from Latin "ratio" meaning "calculation, reason") + -able (suffix meaning "capable of") → "capable of reason, sensible"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ratio" (reckoning, understanding, reason) → Old French "raison" → "reasonable" (capable of reasoning) → Middle English → Modern English, meaning "endowed with reason" or "having sound judgment"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Keep your requests within reasonable limits.
   - That's a reasonable price for what you're getting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All conclusions must be supported by evidence beyond reasonable doubt.
   - The defendant acted within reasonable boundaries given the circumstances.

1. **Adjective**: acceptable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Satisfactory or adequate; good enough to be allowed or approved; meeting minimum standards
   - Able to be tolerated or allowed; within the bounds of what is considered appropriate
   
   **Synonyms**: satisfactory, adequate, tolerable, permissible, suitable
   
   **Antonyms**: unacceptable, inadequate, intolerable, inappropriate, substandard
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing standards, behaviors, or outcomes that meet minimum requirements; common in professional, academic, and regulatory contexts; appropriate when discussing compliance or approval
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when something is excellent (acceptable implies "good enough" not "great"); not appropriate when stricter standards need emphasis
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within acceptable limits
   - acceptable range
   - acceptable standards
   - acceptable behavior
   - acceptable level
   
   **Root Analysis**: accept (from Latin "acceptare" meaning "to receive willingly") + -able (suffix meaning "capable of") → "capable of being accepted"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "acceptare" (to take, receive) → Old French "acceptable" → Middle English → Modern English. Originally meant "worthy of acceptance", now often implies meeting minimum standards
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your behavior needs to stay within acceptable limits.
   - Is this quality acceptable, or should we redo it?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All measurements must fall within acceptable tolerances as specified in the protocol.
   - The proposed solution represents an acceptable compromise within budgetary constraints.

1. **Adjective**: specified
   **Meaning**: 
   - Clearly stated, identified, or defined in detail; explicitly mentioned or designated
   - (non-gradable) Precisely determined or designated; not vague or general
   
   **Synonyms**: designated, stated, defined, prescribed, stipulated
   
   **Antonyms**: unspecified, vague, general, undefined, arbitrary
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, technical, and legal contexts when referring to explicitly stated conditions, limits, or requirements; appropriate when emphasizing precision and clarity; common in contracts, regulations, and technical documentation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual speech where "stated" or "mentioned" would be simpler; not appropriate when discussing general or flexible conditions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within specified limits
   - specified timeframe
   - specified parameters
   - specified range
   - specified requirements
   
   **Root Analysis**: specify (from Latin "species" meaning "particular kind") + -ed (past participle suffix) → "made into a particular kind"
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin "specificare" (to mention specifically) from "specificus" (specific) from "species" (kind, sort) → Old French "specifier" → English "specify" → "specified" (past participle used as adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure you finish within the specified time.
   - Did you stay within the specified budget?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All procedures must be completed within the specified timeframe outlined in Section 3.2.
   - Deviations from specified parameters require prior authorization from the supervising authority.

1. **Adjective**: legal
   **Meaning**: 
   - Allowed or permitted by law; conforming to or recognized by law; not illegal or forbidden
   - Relating to law or the administration of justice
   
   **Synonyms**: lawful, legitimate, permissible, authorized, licit
   
   **Antonyms**: illegal, unlawful, illicit, forbidden, prohibited
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing actions, rights, or situations that conform to law; appropriate in legal, business, and regulatory contexts; common when emphasizing compliance with legal standards
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "moral" or "ethical" would be more appropriate (legal doesn't necessarily mean moral); not suitable when discussing matters unrelated to law
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within legal limits
   - legal boundaries
   - legal rights
   - legal framework
   - legal requirements
   
   **Root Analysis**: leg- (from Latin "lex, leg-" meaning "law") + -al (suffix meaning "relating to") → "relating to law"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "legalis" (relating to law) from "lex, legis" (law) → Old French "legal" → Middle English → Modern English "legal"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is what they're doing within legal limits?
   - Make sure everything stays legal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization operates within legal boundaries established by federal and state regulations.
   - All contractual obligations must remain within the legal framework of applicable jurisdiction.

1. **Adjective**: normal
   **Meaning**: 
   - Conforming to a standard; typical, usual, or expected; not abnormal or exceptional
   - Within the usual or expected range; not significantly different from the average
   
   **Synonyms**: typical, usual, standard, ordinary, regular
   
   **Antonyms**: abnormal, unusual, atypical, exceptional, extraordinary
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing what falls within expected or typical ranges; common in medical, statistical, scientific, and everyday contexts; appropriate when distinguishing between ordinary and exceptional cases
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "healthy" would be more precise (in medical contexts); not appropriate when value judgments about what "should" be normal are contested; can be insensitive when discussing human variation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within normal limits
   - normal range
   - normal parameters
   - normal bounds
   - normal variation
   
   **Root Analysis**: norm (from Latin "norma" meaning "carpenter's square, rule") + -al (suffix meaning "relating to") → "relating to the standard"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "normalis" (made according to a square, according to rule) from "norma" (carpenter's square, rule, pattern) → English "normal" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your results are within normal limits—nothing to worry about.
   - That's pretty normal for this time of year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patient vital signs remained within normal parameters throughout the observation period.
   - Statistical analysis revealed that all data points fell within normal distribution ranges.

1. **Adjective**: designated
   **Meaning**: 
   - Officially assigned, appointed, or specified for a particular purpose or role; marked or indicated for a specific use
   - (non-gradable) Chosen or selected officially; set apart for a particular function
   
   **Synonyms**: assigned, appointed, specified, allocated, earmarked
   
   **Antonyms**: unassigned, random, unspecified, general-purpose
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and official contexts when referring to officially assigned areas, roles, times, or resources; common in organizational, legal, and administrative settings; appropriate when emphasizing official status
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "assigned" or "chosen" would be simpler; not appropriate for informal or unofficial arrangements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within designated areas
   - designated timeframe
   - designated zone
   - designated boundaries
   - designated limits
   
   **Root Analysis**: design (from Latin "designare" meaning "to mark out") + -ate (verb suffix) + -ed (past participle) → "officially marked out"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "designatus" (past participle of "designare": to mark out, specify) from "de-" (out) + "signare" (to mark) → English "designate" → "designated" (past participle used as adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stay within the designated parking areas.
   - We need to finish within the designated time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All activities must occur within designated operational boundaries as specified in the permit.
   - Vehicles must remain within designated zones throughout the duration of the event.

1. **Adjective**: prescribed
   **Meaning**: 
   - Officially laid down, specified, or ordered by authority; required by rule or regulation
   - (non-gradable) Set down as a rule or direction; established by custom or authority
   
   **Synonyms**: stipulated, mandated, specified, ordained, required
   
   **Antonyms**: voluntary, optional, discretionary, flexible, unregulated
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, legal, medical, and regulatory contexts when referring to officially required standards, limits, or procedures; appropriate when emphasizing authoritative specification or mandate
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "required" would be simpler; not appropriate when discussing suggestions or recommendations rather than requirements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within prescribed limits
   - prescribed boundaries
   - prescribed timeframe
   - prescribed parameters
   - prescribed guidelines
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (Latin "before") + scribe (Latin "scribere" meaning "to write") + -ed → "written down beforehand"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praescribere" (to write before, dictate, order) from "prae-" (before) + "scribere" (to write) → "prescribe" → "prescribed" (past participle used as adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure you stay within the prescribed limits.
   - Did you take it within the prescribed timeframe?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All experimental procedures must remain within the prescribed ethical guidelines.
   - Dosages must be administered within the prescribed limits specified by the FDA approval.

1. **Adjective**: established
   **Meaning**: 
   - Officially recognized, accepted, or set up; having been in existence for a long time and recognized as valid
   - Set up on a firm or permanent basis; widely accepted or recognized
   
   **Synonyms**: recognized, accepted, confirmed, settled, instituted
   
   **Antonyms**: new, unestablished, disputed, provisional, experimental
   
   **When to Use**: Use when referring to long-standing, officially recognized, or widely accepted systems, rules, or boundaries; appropriate in formal, academic, and professional contexts; implies stability and official recognition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing new or provisional arrangements; not appropriate when emphasizing change or innovation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within established limits
   - established boundaries
   - established parameters
   - established guidelines
   - established framework
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (from Latin "ex-" meaning "out") + stablish (from Latin "stabilire" meaning "to make firm") + -ed → "made firm, set up"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "stabilire" (to make firm) → Old French "establir" → Middle English "establissen" → "establish" → "established" (past participle used as adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's work within the established rules we already have.
   - Stay within established boundaries.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research methodology operates within established protocols validated by previous studies.
   - All recommendations must fall within the established framework of institutional policy.
