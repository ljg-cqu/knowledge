1. **Noun**: accompaniment (C/U, pl: accompaniments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that is provided, eaten, or experienced along with something else; a complement to a main item
   - Music or sound that is played along with a singer or main instrument
   
   **Synonyms**: side dish, complement, addition, accessory, garnish
   
   **Antonyms**: main course, centerpiece, core, standalone item
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about things that naturally come along with a main element—food with accompaniments, text with musical accompaniment, slides with spoken commentary
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the item is central rather than supporting; avoid outside contexts of food, music, or supportive additions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - musical accompaniment
   - as an accompaniment to
   - perfect accompaniment
   - accompaniment to the main dish
   - accompaniment to the text
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + company (to go with) + -ment (result of an action)
   
   **Etymology**: French accompagnement → English accompaniment (something that goes along with something else, 18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We ordered a salad as an accompaniment along with the main course.
   - Soft piano music played as a gentle accompaniment along with the speaker’s talk.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The figures are presented with commentary as an accompaniment to the main report.
   - The piece is performed with orchestral accompaniment along with a solo vocalist.

1. **Noun**: add-on (C, pl: add-ons)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An extra feature or service that is added to something standard
   - Something optional that comes along with a basic product or plan for an additional cost
   
   **Synonyms**: extra, extension, supplement, plug-in, optional feature
   
   **Antonyms**: core feature, default option, built-in component
   
   **When to Use**: In technology, services, and commerce to describe options that customers can choose along with a base product (software add-ons, data add-ons)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing; when the feature is mandatory or built in rather than optional
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - software add-on
   - premium add-on
   - optional add-on
   - add-on service
   - add-on package
   
   **Root Analysis**: add (to increase) + on (attached to the main thing)
   
   **Etymology**: From phrasal verb "add on" (to attach extra) → noun "add-on" (20th-century commercial usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I bought extra storage as an add-on along with my phone plan.
   - The app offers a few paid add-ons along with the free version.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Advanced analytics are available as an add-on along with the standard subscription.
   - Optional training modules can be purchased as add-ons along with the core course.

1. **Noun**: bundle (C, pl: bundles)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A set of products, services, or features that are sold or provided together
   - A group of things tied or packaged together along with each other
   
   **Synonyms**: package, set, kit, collection, combo
   
   **Antonyms**: individual item, standalone product, single unit
   
   **When to Use**: When describing groups of items that come along with each other as one offer (subscription bundles, product bundles)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When items are chosen or sold one by one; avoid metaphorical use where clarity is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - service bundle
   - product bundle
   - bundle deal
   - bundle of services
   - bundle pricing
   
   **Root Analysis**: bundle (to tie together)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English bondel (package) → bundle; later extended to digital and service bundles
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I got a bundle of channels along with my internet plan.
   - The game comes in a bundle along with extra content and skins.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The provider offers a discounted bundle along with optional upgrades.
   - Several components are delivered as a bundle along with implementation support.

1. **Noun**: side effect (C, pl: side effects)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An unwanted or secondary result of a drug, action, or decision that happens along with the main effect
   - Any extra consequence that comes along with something, often unintentionally
   
   **Synonyms**: secondary effect, unintended consequence, by-product, collateral effect
   
   **Antonyms**: primary effect, intended outcome, main result
   
   **When to Use**: When describing outcomes that appear along with a main outcome but are not the main goal (medicine, policy, technology)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For neutral or positive additions that are clearly intended; avoid dramatizing small inconveniences as "side effects" unless context supports it
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - potential side effects
   - experience side effects
   - side effects include
   - unintended side effect
   - side effect of
   
   **Root Analysis**: side (secondary, off to one side) + effect (result)
   
   **Etymology**: Medical usage from early 20th century, then extended metaphorically to other domains
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The medicine works, but it has some side effects along with the benefits.
   - Working late every night has the side effect of eating dinner way too late.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention produced several side effects along with the desired outcomes.
   - Policy changes can generate social side effects along with economic benefits.

1. **Noun**: attachment (C, pl: attachments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A file or document that is sent along with an email or message
   - Something fastened or connected to something else; a physical or digital add-on
   
   **Synonyms**: add-on, appendix, accessory, enclosure, extra file
   
   **Antonyms**: main body, standalone text, core file
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to files, tools, or components that come along with a main message or device (email attachment, vacuum attachment)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For emotional attachment (different sense); avoid confusion by context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - email attachment
   - see attachment
   - as an attachment
   - attachment to this message
   - equipment attachment
   
   **Root Analysis**: at- (to) + tach (fasten) + -ment (result of an action)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French attacher (to fasten) → attach → attachment (15th century, "something attached")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I sent the contract as an attachment along with the email.
   - The mixer comes with several attachments along with the base unit.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please review the attachment provided along with this message.
   - Additional specifications are included as attachments along with the main proposal.

1. **Noun**: accessory (C, pl: accessories)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An extra item that is added to something else to make it more useful, attractive, or complete
   - A supporting item that comes along with main clothing, devices, or equipment
   
   **Synonyms**: add-on, extra, supplement, enhancement, complement
   
   **Antonyms**: essential, necessity, core item, basic component
   
   **When to Use**: For clothing, gadgets, and tools when describing smaller items that go along with a main piece (phone accessories, fashion accessories)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the item is essential for function rather than optional
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - phone accessories
   - fashion accessories
   - kitchen accessories
   - accessories included
   - accessory kit
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + cedere (go) + -ory (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin accessorius (additional) → French accessoire → English accessory (something added, 15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The console came with a few accessories along with the main controller.
   - She bought some matching accessories along with the dress.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The device is supplied with standard accessories along with an optional expansion kit.
   - Accessories are listed along with core components in the inventory.

1. **Noun**: by-product (C, pl: by-products)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that is produced as a result of another process, often unintentionally
   - A secondary outcome that comes along with a primary process or activity
   
   **Synonyms**: side product, spin-off, secondary result, offshoot, side effect
   
   **Antonyms**: main product, primary outcome, intended result
   
   **When to Use**: When describing results, materials, or effects that arise along with a main process (industrial by-products, social by-products)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the outcome is planned or primary; avoid negative connotations when describing positive main results
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - industrial by-product
   - by-product of
   - unintended by-product
   - useful by-product
   - environmental by-products
   
   **Root Analysis**: by (secondary, off to the side) + product (something produced)
   
   **Etymology**: 19th century industrial term for secondary output of a process
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The stress is kind of a by-product along with the extra responsibility.
   - Fame can be a by-product of doing good work along with recognition.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Increased collaboration is a by-product that emerges along with process redesign.
   - Waste materials are generated as by-products along with primary outputs.

1. **Noun**: supplement (C/U, pl: supplements)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that is added to something else to improve it or complete it
   - Additional material published or distributed along with a main text, issue, or report
   
   **Synonyms**: addition, extra, complement, appendix, add-on
   
   **Antonyms**: core content, main text, primary section
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about material or items that come along with a main body to add more information or value (dietary supplements, Sunday supplement)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the item is integral rather than optional; avoid confusion with "replacement" or "alternative"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - dietary supplement
   - weekend supplement
   - supplement to the report
   - supplement along with the main text
   - provide a supplement
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (under/up from below) + plere (fill) + -ment (result)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplementum (that which fills up) → English supplement (something added, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The magazine comes with a fashion supplement along with the main issue.
   - I take a vitamin supplement along with my meals.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A technical supplement is provided along with the main publication.
   - The survey data are presented in a digital supplement along with the printed report.
