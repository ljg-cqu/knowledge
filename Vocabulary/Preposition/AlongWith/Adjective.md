1. **Adjective**: supplementary (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Added to something else to make it more complete, effective, or sufficient; extra but supportive rather than central
   - Providing additional help, explanation, or material alongside a main element
   
   **Synonyms**: additional, extra, auxiliary, complementary, supporting
   
   **Antonyms**: primary, main, central, essential
   
   **When to Use**: When describing material, resources, or roles that come along with a main item to enhance or complete it (supplementary notes, supplementary income)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the thing is core or indispensable (use "main" or "primary" instead); avoid in very casual speech where "extra" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supplementary material
   - supplementary notes
   - supplementary income
   - supplementary information
   - supplementary exercises
   
   **Root Analysis**: supple- (from Latin supplere, fill up) + -ment (result of an action) + -ary (having the nature of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplere (to fill up) → supplement (something added) → supplementary (serving as a supplement, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The teacher uploaded some supplementary videos along with the slides.
   - I do some freelance work as supplementary income along with my main job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Supplementary material is provided along with the main report for interested readers.
   - The policy includes supplementary guidelines along with the core regulatory text.

1. **Adjective**: additional (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Extra; more than what already exists or has been mentioned
   - Added on top of a basic amount, requirement, or list
   
   **Synonyms**: extra, further, supplementary, added, incremental
   
   **Antonyms**: fewer, reduced, limited, minimal
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about extra items, costs, or tasks that come along with what is already there (additional fees, additional responsibilities)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the item is integral or built-in rather than added later; avoid overuse in casual speech where "more" may sound more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - additional costs
   - additional responsibilities
   - additional benefits
   - additional support
   - no additional charge
   
   **Root Analysis**: add- (to) + -ition (act of) + -al (related to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin addere (to add) → addition → additional (15th century, "added, further")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There’s an additional service fee along with the ticket price.
   - I’ve got some additional questions along with what we discussed earlier.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Additional documentation is required along with the application form.
   - The intervention offers additional benefits along with the primary health outcomes.

1. **Adjective**: accompanying (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Going together with or happening at the same time as something else
   - Existing or provided along with a main thing as a natural or intended part
   
   **Synonyms**: attendant, associated, related, concurrent, connected
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, independent, unrelated, standalone
   
   **When to Use**: When describing documents, symptoms, effects, or people that appear or are provided along with a main element (accompanying documents, accompanying symptoms)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the relationship is weak, accidental, or only loosely connected; avoid when you simply mean "near" or "next to"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accompanying documentation
   - accompanying symptoms
   - accompanying materials
   - accompanying risks
   - accompanying person
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + company (companion, association) + -ing (participial adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French acompaigner (to accompany) → accompany → accompanying (as adjective referring to things that go along with something else)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Please bring the accompanying receipts along with the reimbursement form.
   - The flu came with the usual accompanying symptoms of fever and fatigue.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report should be read together with the accompanying technical appendix.
   - The treatment has several accompanying side effects that must be monitored closely.

1. **Adjective**: incidental (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Happening as a minor result of something else; secondary or not the main focus
   - Occurring along with something but not planned as a primary purpose
   
   **Synonyms**: secondary, minor, peripheral, side, unintended
   
   **Antonyms**: primary, central, deliberate, intentional
   
   **When to Use**: When describing costs, benefits, or effects that come along with a main activity but are not its main goal (incidental expenses, incidental benefits)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the feature is deliberately planned or central; avoid when the effect is major rather than minor
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - incidental expenses
   - incidental benefits
   - incidental damage
   - incidental findings
   - incidental contact
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (on) + cid (fall) + -ent (being) + -al (related to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin incidere (to fall upon) → incidental (falling upon something, occurring by chance, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had a few incidental costs along with the main travel expenses.
   - You may notice some incidental side effects along with the treatment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study discusses incidental benefits that arise along with the primary outcomes.
   - Incidental expenses incurred along with the project will be reimbursed in accordance with policy.

1. **Adjective**: ancillary (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Providing necessary support to the primary activities or operation of something; subordinate but helpful
   - Serving as a supplementary or additional component alongside a central element
   
   **Synonyms**: supporting, auxiliary, secondary, subordinate, supplementary
   
   **Antonyms**: primary, principal, core, central
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and technical contexts (business, medicine, education) when describing services, staff, or features that work along with primary functions (ancillary services, ancillary staff)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (sounds formal); when the item is actually central or main
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ancillary services
   - ancillary staff
   - ancillary benefits
   - ancillary features
   - ancillary support
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ancilla (maid, helper) + -ary (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin ancillaris (serving) → English ancillary (supporting, subordinate, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The streaming service comes with some nice ancillary features along with the main shows.
   - We hired ancillary staff along with the core team to handle logistics.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Ancillary services are offered along with the core treatment program.
   - The contract includes ancillary benefits along with the primary compensation package.

1. **Adjective**: collateral (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Additional but secondary; occurring as a side effect or by-product of something else
   - In finance, pledged as security for a loan (separate technical sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: secondary, indirect, side, accompanying, incidental
   
   **Antonyms**: direct, primary, intentional, central
   
   **When to Use**: When describing damage, benefits, or effects that happen along with the main action but are not its direct aim (collateral damage, collateral benefits)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral or positive everyday contexts where "additional" or "extra" is clearer; be careful with strong negative connotations of "collateral damage"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - collateral damage
   - collateral effects
   - collateral benefits
   - collateral impact
   - collateral consequences
   
   **Root Analysis**: co- (together) + later- (side) + -al (related to)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin collateralis (side by side) → English collateral (15th century, "parallel, additional")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We got some nice collateral benefits along with the main outcome.
   - The update fixed the bug but caused some collateral issues along with it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention produced collateral benefits along with the targeted improvements.
   - Policymakers must consider collateral consequences that arise along with intended effects.

1. **Adjective**: bundled (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Sold, offered, or provided together with other items as a package
   - Grouped along with related elements rather than offered separately
   
   **Synonyms**: packaged, combined, grouped, integrated, consolidated
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, standalone, unbundled, individual
   
   **When to Use**: When describing products, services, or features that come along with each other in a single package (bundled services, bundled pricing)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When items are clearly sold or provided individually; avoid outside commercial or technical contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bundled services
   - bundled package
   - bundled offer
   - bundled pricing
   - bundled features
   
   **Root Analysis**: bundle (to tie or wrap together) + -ed (past participle used adjectivally)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English bondel (package) → bundle → bundled (provided together, 20th-century commercial usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The phone comes bundled with a case and charger along with a screen protector.
   - We got internet bundled with TV along with a discount.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The subscription includes bundled services along with priority support.
   - Bundled pricing is offered along with flexible standalone options.

1. **Adjective**: secondary (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Less important than something else but still related or connected; not primary
   - Occurring as a result of or along with a main condition or process
   
   **Synonyms**: subordinate, minor, peripheral, incidental, derivative
   
   **Antonyms**: primary, main, principal, leading
   
   **When to Use**: When distinguishing main vs. additional roles, effects, or goals (secondary effects, secondary aim) that come along with primary ones
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When something is equally important or central; avoid using as a value judgment about people (can imply they are less important)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - secondary effects
   - secondary goal
   - secondary role
   - secondary benefit
   - secondary objective
   
   **Root Analysis**: second (following first) + -ary (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin secundarius (second-class, following) → Old French secondaire → English secondary (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The extra perks are secondary benefits along with the main salary.
   - There were some secondary issues along with the main problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Secondary outcomes are reported along with the primary endpoint.
   - Secondary objectives, considered along with the main aim, provide a fuller picture of impact.
