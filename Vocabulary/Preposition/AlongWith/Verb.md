1. **Verb**: accompany
   **Meaning**: 
   - To go somewhere with someone; to be present along with another person or thing
   - To exist or occur together with something else as an addition or result
   
   **Synonyms**: go with, escort, attend, come with, be coupled with
   
   **Antonyms**: leave, depart from, separate from, abandon
   
   **When to Use**: When describing people, sounds, or effects that come or appear along with something else (music accompanies, symptoms accompany, staff accompany)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts where "go with" or "come with" sounds more natural; avoid when there is no real sense of "going along with" or co-occurring
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accompany someone
   - be accompanied by
   - symptoms accompany
   - services that accompany
   - document accompanying
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + company (companion) → accompany
   
   **Etymology**: Old French acompaigner (to go with) → English accompany (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’ll accompany you along with the rest of the team to the meeting.
   - The dish is accompanied by a salad along with fresh bread.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report is accompanied by detailed appendices along with a summary brief.
   - The policy changes accompany, and are implemented along with, organizational restructuring.

1. **Verb**: co-occur
   **Meaning**: 
   - To happen at the same time as something else; to occur along with another event or condition
   - To appear together repeatedly in data, language, or phenomena
   
   **Synonyms**: occur together, coincide, appear alongside, overlap, coincide with
   
   **Antonyms**: occur separately, occur independently, diverge
   
   **When to Use**: In technical, scientific, or analytic contexts when describing events, symptoms, or features that show up along with each other (co-occurring disorders, co-occurring patterns)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Everyday conversation ("happen together" is simpler); avoid when order or cause is more important than simple overlap
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - co-occur with
   - frequently co-occur
   - tend to co-occur
   - co-occur in data
   - co-occurring conditions
   
   **Root Analysis**: co- (together) + occur (happen)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin concurrere (run together) and occurrere (run to meet) → occur → co-occur (20th century scientific usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These problems often co-occur along with stress and lack of sleep.
   - Certain habits seem to co-occur along with specific personality types.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Anxiety symptoms frequently co-occur along with depressive features.
   - Several risk factors co-occur along with socioeconomic disadvantage in the dataset.

1. **Verb**: supplement
   **Meaning**: 
   - To add something to something else in order to improve it or complete it
   - To provide extra information or resources along with what already exists
   
   **Synonyms**: add to, reinforce, top up, enrich, bolster
   
   **Antonyms**: reduce, diminish, remove, deplete
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the act of bringing additional material, resources, or explanation along with existing content (supplement income, supplement a report)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the addition replaces rather than supports; avoid in very casual speech where "add" is sufficient
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supplement income
   - supplement a report
   - supplement with examples
   - supplement existing resources
   - supplement along with
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (from below, up) + plere (fill) → supplement (fill up)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplementum (that which fills up) → English supplement (verb, "to complete," 15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I supplement my salary along with some freelance work.
   - We supplemented the slides along with a short video.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manual is supplemented with visual aids along with detailed checklists.
   - Existing datasets were supplemented along with newly collected qualitative interviews.

1. **Verb**: complement
   **Meaning**: 
   - To add to something in a way that improves it or makes it more complete
   - To go well with something else, working along with it to form a balanced whole
   
   **Synonyms**: enhance, complete, go well with, balance, enrich
   
   **Antonyms**: clash with, undermine, detract from, weaken
   
   **When to Use**: When describing roles, skills, or features that work well along with others (skills that complement, flavors that complement)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you mean "praise" (that is "compliment" with an i); avoid in contexts where things compete or conflict
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - complement each other
   - complement the main course
   - complement existing skills
   - complement a strategy
   - perfectly complement
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + plere (fill) → complement (that which completes)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin complementum (that which completes) → English complement (verb, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her design skills complement his coding skills along with his project experience.
   - The side dishes really complement the main meal along with the sauce.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The qualitative findings complement, and are presented along with, the quantitative results.
   - Additional modules complement the core curriculum along with optional workshops.

1. **Verb**: bundle
   **Meaning**: 
   - To group things together and offer them as a single unit
   - To include several items along with each other in one package or price
   
   **Synonyms**: package, group, combine, consolidate, wrap together
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, unbundle, split, isolate
   
   **When to Use**: In business, marketing, or technology when you put services, products, or features together along with each other (bundle products, bundle services)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When items must remain clearly separate; avoid figurative uses that confuse readers
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bundle services
   - bundle features
   - bundle together
   - bundled with
   - bundle up (physical sense)
   
   **Root Analysis**: bundle (noun: group of items) → bundle (verb: group together)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English bondel (package) → bundle; verb sense (to tie together) developed later
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They bundle internet along with TV and phone service.
   - The course bundles videos along with downloadable notes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The provider bundles several features along with standard support.
   - Training materials are bundled along with licensing for institutional clients.

1. **Verb**: attach
   **Meaning**: 
   - To fasten, join, or connect one thing to another
   - To include a file or document along with an email or other message
   
   **Synonyms**: fasten, connect, join, affix, append
   
   **Antonyms**: detach, remove, disconnect, separate
   
   **When to Use**: When physically or digitally joining something along with something else (attach a file, attach a label)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When there is no real notion of joining or including; avoid confusion with emotional "attach" unless context supports it
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - attach a file
   - attach a document
   - attach to an email
   - attach a note
   - attached along with
   
   **Root Analysis**: at- (to) + tach (fasten)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French atachier (to fasten) → English attach (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’ll attach the photos along with the message.
   - Don’t forget to attach your CV along with the application.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please attach the required forms along with your submission.
   - Supporting evidence is attached along with the main report.

1. **Verb**: incorporate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To include something as part of a whole; to mix in or integrate
   - To take ideas, features, or elements and bring them in along with existing ones
   
   **Synonyms**: integrate, include, combine, merge, fold in
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, separate, isolate
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and technical contexts when new elements are brought in along with existing structures (incorporate feedback, incorporate data)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual speech where "include" or "add" is simpler; avoid in contexts where elements remain clearly separate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - incorporate into
   - incorporate along with
   - incorporate feedback
   - incorporate features
   - incorporate data
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + corpus (body) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin incorporare (to unite into one body) → English incorporate (to include as part of a whole, 15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We tried to incorporate everyone’s ideas along with the original plan.
   - The new design incorporates user feedback along with expert advice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The framework incorporates qualitative insights along with quantitative indicators.
   - The updated policy incorporates stakeholder feedback along with regulatory requirements.

1. **Verb**: tag along
   **Meaning**: 
   - To go somewhere with someone, often when you were not originally invited or central to the plan
   - To follow or join an activity along with others in a casual or secondary way
   
   **Synonyms**: come along, follow along, join in, trail along, go along
   
   **Antonyms**: stay behind, go alone, lead, separate
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts when someone comes along with a group in a minor or last-minute role (tag along with friends, kids tagging along)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal contexts; when the person is central or officially part of the plan (use "join" or "accompany")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tag along with
   - let someone tag along
   - kids tagging along
   - just tag along
   
   **Root Analysis**: tag (to follow, touch lightly) + along (in the same direction)
   
   **Etymology**: From children’s game "tag" (to touch and chase) + along; figurative sense of following a group (19th–20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Mind if I tag along with you guys to the conference?
   - The kids tagged along with us to the store.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; in formal contexts use "accompany" or "join" instead.*
