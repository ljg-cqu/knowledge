1. **Functional Expression**: Would you like to come along with us to [place/event]? (function: inviting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite invitation asking someone to join a group for an activity or event
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (question form with modal "would")
   
   **Synonyms**: Would you like to join us at [place]?, Would you like to come with us?, Are you interested in coming along?, Do you want to come along with us?
   
   **Antonyms**: Please don’t come this time, This is a closed meeting, You’re not invited (strongly contrasting communicative intent)
   
   **When to Use**: When inviting someone to join along with a group in a friendly but polite way—social events, team activities, informal work gatherings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Highly formal invitations (use written invitations or more formal wording); when the person has already declined or when the event is restricted
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would you like to come along with us for dinner?
   - Would you like to come along with the team to the conference?
   - Would you like to come along with us on a quick walk?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses modal "would" to soften the invitation and signal respect for the other person’s choice; "come along with" highlights inclusion in a group rather than a one-to-one outing.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "I’d love to, thanks!", "Yes, that sounds great.", "Sure, I’d like to come along."
   - Declining: "Thanks, but I already have plans.", "I’d like to, but I can’t today.", "Maybe another time."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would you like to come along with us to grab coffee after work?
   - We’re going hiking this weekend—would you like to come along with us?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would you like to come along with the delegation to the site visit tomorrow?
   - If your schedule allows, would you like to come along with us to the stakeholder briefing?

1. **Functional Expression**: I’ll go along with that (idea/plan). (function: agreeing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A mild, sometimes reluctant-sounding agreement to accept a suggestion or decision
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly states acceptance)
   
   **Synonyms**: I can accept that., I’m okay with that., I’ll agree to that., I’ll support that.
   
   **Antonyms**: I can’t go along with that., I’m not comfortable with that., I have to disagree., I can’t support that.
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to signal that you will accept or support a decision along with others, especially when it isn’t your first choice but is still acceptable
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Strong, enthusiastic agreement (use "I fully support this" or "That’s a great idea"); very formal contexts like official votes or legal approvals
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I’ll go along with that for now.
   - I’m willing to go along with that.
   - I’ll go along with your proposal.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Suggests alignment along with the group while quietly preserving some distance; can imply compromise or limited enthusiasm depending on tone.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Follow-up: "Great, thanks.", "I appreciate you going along with this.", "Let’s document that decision."
   - Partial pushback: "I’ll go along with it, but I still have concerns about…"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’m not thrilled, but I’ll go along with that plan.
   - Everyone wants pizza, so I’ll go along with that.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given the constraints, I’m prepared to go along with the revised schedule.
   - I can go along with this recommendation provided we review the risks.

1. **Functional Expression**: Thanks for coming along with me to [activity/place]. (function: expressing gratitude)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing appreciation to someone who joined you for support, company, or help
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit thanks)
   
   **Synonyms**: Thanks for coming with me., I appreciate you coming along., Thanks for being there with me., Thanks for joining me.
   
   **Antonyms**: I wish you’d been there., It’s a shame you didn’t come., You should have come.
   
   **When to Use**: After someone has come along with you to an event (meeting, appointment, difficult situation) and you want to acknowledge their presence and support
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal acknowledgements in writing (use more formal thanks); when the person did not actually attend
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Thanks for coming along with me today.
   - Thanks for coming along with me to the doctor.
   - Thanks for coming along with me to the presentation.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Frames presence "along with" the speaker as supportive companionship; strengthens relationships by recognizing time and emotional effort.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Typical: "Of course.", "No problem.", "Anytime.", "I’m glad I could come along."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for coming along with me to the interview—it really calmed my nerves.
   - I really appreciate you coming along with me to the hospital.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Thank you for coming along with me to the meeting; your presence made a difference.
   - I would like to thank my colleague for coming along with me to the stakeholder session.

1. **Functional Expression**: If you’d like, you can bring [person/thing] along with you. (function: offering/inviting inclusion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offering someone the option to include another person or extra items
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (conditional "if you’d like" softens the offer)
   
   **Synonyms**: Feel free to bring [person/thing]., You’re welcome to bring [person/thing]., It’s okay to bring [person/thing] along., You can also bring [person/thing].
   
   **Antonyms**: Please don’t bring anyone else., Extra guests are not allowed., Additional items are not permitted.
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to show flexibility and hospitality by allowing someone to bring a guest or extra materials along with them (family member, friend, child, laptop)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Events with strict capacity or security limits; when extra people or things could create problems or break rules
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You can bring a friend along with you if you like.
   - You’re welcome to bring your partner along with you.
   - You can bring your laptop along with any printed notes.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines conditional politeness ("if you’d like") with inclusive "along with" to reduce pressure and make the offer optional.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Thanks, I might bring my sister along.", "Great, I’ll bring my team along with me."
   - Declining: "Thanks, but I’ll just come by myself.", "I’ll come alone this time."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If you’d like, you can bring your kids along with you.
   - You can bring some snacks along with you if you want.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If you’d like, you may bring a colleague along with you to the consultation.
   - Participants may bring supporting documents along with them if they wish.
