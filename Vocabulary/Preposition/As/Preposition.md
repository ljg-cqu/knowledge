1. **Preposition**: as (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Role/Function)* In the capacity of; in the role of; functioning as
   - *(Temporal)* At the same time that; when; while
   - *(Comparison)* In the same way or manner that; like
   - *(Causal)* Because; since; for the reason that
   
   **Synonyms**: like (comparison), while (temporal), because (causal), in the capacity of (role)
   
   **Antonyms**: unlike (when used for comparison)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "as" to indicate someone's role, simultaneous actions, comparisons, or reasons; extremely versatile in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "like" in formal comparisons (though increasingly accepted); avoid overuse in a single sentence as it can create ambiguity about which meaning is intended
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as a result, as usual, as always, as expected, as well as, as far as, as long as
   - **Verb + prep**: work as, serve as, act as, regard as, view as, define as, describe as
   - **Adjective + prep**: known as, recognized as, identified as, classified as
   - **Noun + prep**: role as, position as, job as, career as, duty as
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English eallswā (all so)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English eallswā → Middle English als, also → Modern English "as" (simplified form); originally meant "equally so" or "in like manner"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm working as a consultant these days.
   - As I was saying earlier, we need to leave soon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee appointed her as chairperson for the next term.
   - As outlined in the previous section, the methodology requires careful consideration.

1. **Preposition**: as for (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Topic Shift)* Used to introduce a new topic or person into the discussion; concerning; regarding
   - *(Dismissive)* Often implies a contrast or dismissal of something previously mentioned
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with regard to, speaking of
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Use to shift conversational focus or introduce a contrasting point; works in both casual and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound dismissive if tone isn't managed carefully; avoid when you want to show equal importance to all topics
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as for me, as for the rest, as for now, as for that matter
   - **Sentence patterns**: As for [topic], [statement about it]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + for
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in the manner of) + "for" (with respect to)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As for me, I'll have the pasta.
   - You can go if you want. As for me, I'm staying here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As for the budget allocation, further discussion is required.
   - The first objective has been met. As for the remaining goals, progress continues.

1. **Preposition**: as of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal Reference Point)* Starting from a particular time or date; from; beginning on
   
   **Synonyms**: from, starting from, beginning on, effective from
   
   **Antonyms**: until, before
   
   **When to Use**: Use when specifying an exact time or date from which something is true or valid; common in formal, business, and legal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for vague time references; requires a specific date or time
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as of now, as of today, as of January 1st, as of this moment, as of the date hereof
   - **Verb patterns**: effective as of, valid as of, applicable as of, current as of
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + of
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in the state of) + "of" (from)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As of today, I'm officially done with school!
   - The new rules take effect as of Monday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As of December 31, 2024, the contract will be terminated.
   - All changes are effective as of the date of publication.

1. **Preposition**: as to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Concerning)* About; regarding; concerning; with respect to
   - *(Purpose)* With the purpose or intention of
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, about, with respect to, with regard to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or academic contexts to introduce a topic of inquiry or discussion; less common in casual speech
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sounds overly formal in casual conversation; can often be replaced with simpler "about"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as to whether, as to why, as to how, questions as to, doubts as to
   - **Sentence patterns**: [statement] as to [topic], There is [noun] as to [question]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + to
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in relation) + "to" (toward/regarding)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not sure as to why she left early.
   - There's some confusion as to what time we're meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - There remains considerable debate as to whether the policy will be effective.
   - The committee requested clarification as to the proposed timeline.

1. **Preposition**: as per (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Accordance)* In accordance with; according to; following
   
   **Synonyms**: according to, in accordance with, following, per
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, despite
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal business, legal, or technical writing to indicate compliance with instructions or agreements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation; often considered bureaucratic or overly formal; "according to" is usually preferred
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as per your request, as per instructions, as per agreement, as per usual, as per the contract
   - **Sentence patterns**: [action taken] as per [authority/document]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + per (Latin)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in the manner of) + "per" (through/according to, from Latin)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I did it as per your instructions.
   - Everything's running as per usual.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project will proceed as per the guidelines established in Section 3.
   - Payment shall be made as per the terms specified in Clause 5.2.

1. **Preposition**: as from (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal Starting Point)* Starting from; beginning from a specific date or time
   
   **Synonyms**: from, starting from, as of, beginning from
   
   **Antonyms**: until, up to
   
   **When to Use**: More common in British English; use to specify when something begins; similar to "as of"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in American English (prefer "as of" or "from"); can sound formal or bureaucratic
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as from today, as from next Monday, as from the date mentioned
   - **Sentence patterns**: [change/status] as from [date/time]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + from
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in the state of) + "from" (starting point)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new schedule starts as from next week.
   - You're officially hired as from today!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The revised policy will be in effect as from 1 January 2025.
   - All employees must comply with the new regulations as from the implementation date.

1. **Preposition**: as against (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Comparison/Contrast)* In comparison with; in contrast to; as opposed to
   
   **Synonyms**: compared to, in contrast to, as opposed to, versus
   
   **Antonyms**: along with, together with
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to make explicit comparisons or contrasts, especially with statistics or data
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sounds formal; in casual contexts, use simpler alternatives like "compared to"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as against last year, as against expectations, as against the average
   - **Sentence patterns**: [figure/data] as against [comparative figure/data]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + against
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in comparison) + "against" (in opposition to)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We sold 500 units this month as against 300 last month.
   - That's pretty good as against what we usually do.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Revenue increased by 15% as against the previous fiscal year.
   - The results show improvement as against the control group.

1. **Preposition**: as with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Similarity)* In the same way as; similarly to; like
   
   **Synonyms**: like, similarly to, in the same way as, just as with
   
   **Antonyms**: unlike, in contrast to
   
   **When to Use**: Use to draw parallels or show that something applies in similar ways to different things
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse in a single text; vary with "like," "similarly," or other comparison phrases
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as with many things, as with any..., as with most..., as with other...
   - **Sentence patterns**: As with [thing A], [thing B] also [characteristic]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + with
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in the manner of) + "with" (in association with)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As with most apps, you need to create an account first.
   - It's complicated, as with anything worth doing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As with previous studies, the results indicate a significant correlation.
   - As with any intervention, careful monitoring is essential.

1. **Preposition**: as regards (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Concerning)* Concerning; regarding; with respect to; about
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, as for, about
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to introduce a topic or aspect being discussed; more formal than "about"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "about" or "regarding" instead
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as regards the matter, as regards payment, as regards your question
   - **Sentence patterns**: As regards [topic], [statement]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + regards (verb form used as preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "as" (in relation to) + "regards" (plural of "regard," meaning consideration)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As regards your question, I'll need to check and get back to you.
   - Everything looks good as regards the basics.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As regards the implementation timeline, further consultation is required.
   - The proposal is satisfactory as regards technical specifications.

1. **Preposition**: as well as (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Addition)* In addition to; and also; together with
   
   **Synonyms**: in addition to, along with, together with, and, plus
   
   **Antonyms**: instead of, rather than, excluding
   
   **When to Use**: Use to add information while emphasizing the first item; note that grammatically, the subject doesn't change (unlike "and")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "and" grammatically—"as well as" doesn't change verb agreement (the first subject determines it)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as well as others, as well as that, as well as being
   - **Sentence patterns**: [A] as well as [B], Not only [A] but [B] as well as [C]
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + well + as
   
   **Etymology**: Compound phrase combining "as" (equally) + "well" (satisfactorily) + "as" (as)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She speaks French as well as English.
   - I need to buy milk as well as bread.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The program benefits students as well as faculty members.
   - The research examines qualitative as well as quantitative data.
