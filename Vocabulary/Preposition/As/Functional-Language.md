1. **Functional Expression**: As you know... (function: referencing shared knowledge)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to refer to information the speaker assumes the listener already knows; appeals to common ground
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens assertions by framing as shared knowledge)
   
   **Synonyms**: As you're aware..., As you may recall..., You know that..., As I'm sure you know...
   
   **Antonyms**: You may not know this, but...; Let me tell you something new...
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and professional communication to reference shared information; builds rapport by acknowledging common ground
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound condescending if the information isn't actually shared knowledge; avoid with new information
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As you may know... (more tentative)
   - As you probably know... (acknowledges possible gaps)
   - As you'll know... (British, more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Functions as positive politeness strategy by emphasizing shared knowledge and community; can also hedge assertions by attributing knowledge to listener
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, I do./Yes, exactly.
   - Actually, I didn't know that.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As you know, I've been working on this project for months.
   - So, as you know, we're meeting at 3pm.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As you know, the deadline for submissions is December 31st.
   - As you are aware, the policy requires prior approval for such expenditures.

1. **Functional Expression**: As I was saying... (function: resuming interrupted discourse)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to return to a previous topic after interruption or digression
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: semi-direct (asserts right to continue speaking)
   
   **Synonyms**: As I mentioned..., To continue..., Getting back to..., Anyway, as I said...
   
   **Antonyms**: But moving on to something else...; Let me change the subject...
   
   **When to Use**: When resuming after interruption or digression; manages conversational flow
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound impatient or dismissive of the interruption; use carefully to avoid sounding irritated
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As I was saying (most common)
   - Like I was saying (more casual)
   - As I said earlier
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Discourse management device; can be face-threatening if tone suggests annoyance at interruption; works to re-establish speaker's floor
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Oh, sorry, please continue.
   - Right, go on.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - So, as I was saying before we got sidetracked, the deadline is tomorrow.
   - As I was saying, I think we should leave early.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As I was saying, the methodology requires significant revision.
   - As I was saying before the interruption, the data support our hypothesis.

1. **Functional Expression**: As I see it... (function: expressing opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce one's perspective or interpretation; hedges opinion as personal view
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens opinion by framing as personal perspective)
   
   **Synonyms**: In my view..., From my perspective..., The way I see it..., In my opinion...
   
   **Antonyms**: Objectively speaking...; It's a fact that...; Indisputably...
   
   **When to Use**: When offering subjective opinions or interpretations; shows awareness that others may see differently
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When stating objective facts; when authority rather than perspective needs emphasis
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As I see it (standard)
   - The way I see it (more casual)
   - As I view it (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedging device that performs negative politeness by not imposing view on others; acknowledges multiple valid perspectives
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I see your point, but...
   - That's one way to look at it.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As I see it, we've got two options here.
   - The way I see it, he made the right call.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As I see it, the evidence suggests a causal relationship.
   - As I see it, further investigation is warranted.

1. **Functional Expression**: As far as I'm concerned... (function: expressing firm opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express one's personal opinion strongly; indicates the speaker's definite position
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: semi-direct (clear personal stance but limited to speaker's domain)
   
   **Synonyms**: In my opinion..., From my point of view..., To my mind..., For me...
   
   **Antonyms**: Objectively...; Everyone agrees that...; It's universally accepted that...
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing firm personal positions while acknowledging others may differ; limits scope to speaker's concern
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound dismissive of other views; avoid when consensus or objective truth is being discussed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As far as I'm concerned
   - So far as I'm concerned (more formal/British)
   - For all I'm concerned
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Stronger than "as I see it"â€”indicates speaker's firm commitment to position while limiting scope to their domain of concern; can signal end of discussion from speaker's side
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Well, I disagree.
   - That's your view, but...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As far as I'm concerned, we're done here.
   - She's the best candidate, as far as I'm concerned.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As far as I'm concerned, the proposal meets all requirements.
   - The matter is settled, as far as I am concerned.

1. **Functional Expression**: As you might expect... (function: setting expectations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce unsurprising or predictable information; manages expectations
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (frames information as predictable)
   
   **Synonyms**: Unsurprisingly..., Predictably..., As expected..., As you can imagine...
   
   **Antonyms**: Surprisingly...; Unexpectedly...; Against all odds...
   
   **When to Use**: When presenting outcomes that follow logically from previous information; academic and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When information is genuinely surprising; overuse can make writing boring
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As you might expect
   - As one might expect (more formal)
   - As you would expect
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Helps maintain coherent discourse by signaling logical connections; can also soften face-threatening information by framing as expected
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, that makes sense.
   - Naturally.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As you might expect, the place was packed.
   - He was upset, as you'd expect.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As one might expect, the results varied by demographic group.
   - As you might expect, costs increased proportionally.

1. **Functional Expression**: As it happens... (function: introducing coincidental information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce information that coincidentally relates to what's being discussed; by chance
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (frames information as coincidental)
   
   **Synonyms**: Actually..., Coincidentally..., Interestingly enough..., By chance...
   
   **Antonyms**: Predictably...; By design...; Intentionally...
   
   **When to Use**: When introducing information that coincidentally connects to current topic; conversational and narrative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the connection is intentional or planned rather than coincidental
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As it happens
   - As it turned out (past tense version)
   - As luck would have it
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Discourse marker signaling topic connection while downplaying intentionality; creates conversational flow by framing associations as natural/coincidental
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Oh really? That's interesting.
   - What a coincidence!
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As it happens, I'm going that way myself.
   - I have that book. As it happens, I just finished reading it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As it happens, relevant data had been collected in a previous study.
   - As it happens, similar findings emerged independently in parallel research.

1. **Functional Expression**: As I understand it... (function: hedging/checking understanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to preface one's understanding while inviting correction; epistemic hedge
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (explicitly tentative, invites correction)
   
   **Synonyms**: From what I understand..., If I understand correctly..., As far as I can tell..., My understanding is that...
   
   **Antonyms**: I know for certain that...; Definitively...; Without doubt...
   
   **When to Use**: When stating understanding tentatively, especially when uncertain or when inviting correction/clarification
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When claiming expertise or certain knowledge; when confidence is rhetorically necessary
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As I understand it
   - If I understand correctly
   - As I understand the situation
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Strong hedging device showing epistemic humility; negative politeness strategy that avoids imposing potentially incorrect understanding; invites interlocutor to correct
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's correct./Yes, exactly.
   - Actually, it's more like...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As I understand it, the meeting's been moved to Friday.
   - So as I understand it, you're saying we should wait?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As I understand it, the regulation applies to all entities.
   - As I understand the proposal, implementation would begin in Q2.

1. **Functional Expression**: As requested... (function: referencing prior request)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate compliance with a previous request; formal acknowledgment
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly states compliance)
   
   **Synonyms**: As you requested..., Per your request..., In response to your request...
   
   **Antonyms**: Without being asked...; Unsolicited...; Proactively...
   
   **When to Use**: In professional and business correspondence to reference and fulfill requests
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual contexts where it sounds overly formal; when initiating rather than responding
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As requested
   - As you requested
   - As per your request
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Compliance marker in professional discourse; satisfies request while establishing clear accountability (shows requester initiated action)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you for following up.
   - Perfect, thank you.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Here's the file, as requested.
   - I've made the changes as you requested.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please find attached the revised proposal, as requested.
   - As requested, I have prepared a comprehensive analysis of the data.

1. **Functional Expression**: As I mentioned earlier... (function: back-referencing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to refer back to previously stated information; maintains discourse coherence
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly references prior statement)
   
   **Synonyms**: As I said before..., As previously mentioned..., As stated earlier..., As noted above...
   
   **Antonyms**: To introduce something new...; For the first time...
   
   **When to Use**: To maintain coherence by linking to earlier statements; common in presentations and formal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound repetitive if overused; avoid if audience clearly remembers the earlier mention
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As I mentioned (earlier/before/previously)
   - As mentioned earlier
   - As I said earlier
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Cohesion device that guides audience through argument structure; can also subtly remind audience of previous points they may have missed or forgotten
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, I remember.
   - Right, okay.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - So, as I mentioned earlier, we need to leave by five.
   - As I said before, I'm not interested.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As I mentioned earlier, the methodology follows established protocols.
   - As previously mentioned, funding constraints limit the scope of implementation.

1. **Functional Expression**: As you can see... (function: directing attention)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to direct attention to visual or evident information; assumes shared perception
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly directs attention)
   
   **Synonyms**: As is evident..., As shown..., As demonstrated..., Clearly...
   
   **Antonyms**: What you might not notice is...; The hidden aspect is...
   
   **When to Use**: When presenting visual data, demonstrations, or obvious evidence; presentations and explanatory contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When information isn't actually visible or evident to audience; assumes shared access to information
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As you can see (from this chart/graph)
   - As is evident
   - As can be seen
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Attention-directing device in presentations and demonstrations; creates shared focus; assumes audience has access to same information
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, I see that.
   - Indeed.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As you can see, it's pretty straightforward.
   - The damage is extensive, as you can see.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As you can see from Figure 3, the correlation is significant.
   - As can be seen in the data, outcomes varied considerably.

1. **Functional Expression**: As luck would have it... (function: introducing fortunate/unfortunate coincidence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce coincidental outcomes, often with ironic tone for unfortunate events
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (attributes outcomes to luck/chance)
   
   **Synonyms**: Fortunately/Unfortunately..., By chance..., It so happened that..., Coincidentally...
   
   **Antonyms**: By design...; Predictably...; According to plan...
   
   **When to Use**: In narrative contexts to introduce coincidental events, often with ironic or humorous tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal academic or business writing; when outcomes were actually planned
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As luck would have it
   - As fate would have it (more dramatic)
   - As chance would have it
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often used ironically when "luck" is bad; narrative device that frames events as beyond control; can minimize agency or responsibility
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Of course that happened!
   - What were the odds?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As luck would have it, it started raining right when we left.
   - As luck would have it, I ran into him at the store.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As luck would have it, relevant archival materials became available during the research period.
   - As fortune would have it, the timing proved advantageous.

1. **Functional Expression**: As things stand... (function: describing current situation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to describe the current state of affairs, often implying potential for change
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly describes current state)
   
   **Synonyms**: Currently..., At present..., As it stands..., In the current situation...
   
   **Antonyms**: Ideally...; In the future...; After changes...
   
   **When to Use**: When describing current circumstances while leaving open possibility of change; updates and status reports
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing permanent or unchangeable states
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As things stand (now/currently)
   - As matters stand
   - As the situation stands
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Temporal framing device that emphasizes current state's potential impermanence; often used when current state is unsatisfactory and change is anticipated
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - So what needs to change?
   - For now, at least.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As things stand, we're on track to finish on time.
   - I'm not planning to go, as things stand.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As things stand, the proposal requires substantial revision before approval.
   - As matters stand, funding remains uncertain.

1. **Functional Expression**: As a last resort... (function: indicating final option)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce the final option when all others have failed or been exhausted
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly marks option as final)
   
   **Synonyms**: If all else fails..., As a final option..., In the worst case..., Failing everything else...
   
   **Antonyms**: As a first choice...; Ideally...; Preferably...
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing contingency plans or undesirable but necessary options
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For preferred or desirable options; when multiple good alternatives remain
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As a last resort
   - As a final resort
   - Only as a last resort
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Frames option as undesirable but potentially necessary; manages expectations by signaling this is not the preferred choice; justifies potentially extreme measures
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Let's hope it doesn't come to that.
   - If necessary, I suppose.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As a last resort, we could always cancel the trip.
   - You could ask him for help as a last resort.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As a last resort, the committee may seek external arbitration.
   - Legal action will be pursued only as a last resort.
