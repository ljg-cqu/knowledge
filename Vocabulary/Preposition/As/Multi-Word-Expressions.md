1. **Expression**: as a matter of fact (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to emphasize the truth of a statement, often introducing a correction or surprising information
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase; variations uncommon)
   
   **Synonyms**: in fact, actually, in reality, truth be told, to tell the truth
   
   **Antonyms**: supposedly, allegedly, theoretically
   
   **When to Use**: To emphasize truth or introduce contrasting factual information; works in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound defensive or contrary if overused; avoid in writing where simpler "in fact" suffices
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as a matter of fact (most common)
   - in point of fact (more formal)
   - the fact of the matter is
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrases*: Traditional emphatic construction using "matter" to stress factuality; conventionalized as discourse marker
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + a + matter (from Latin materia) + of + fact
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not tired. As a matter of fact, I'm wide awake.
   - As a matter of fact, I've been there before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As a matter of fact, the data contradict the initial hypothesis.
   - The intervention was, as a matter of fact, less costly than anticipated.

1. **Expression**: as a result (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Consequently; therefore; because of what has been mentioned
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very common collocation for causation)
   
   **Synonyms**: consequently, therefore, thus, accordingly, hence
   
   **Antonyms**: nevertheless, despite this, however
   
   **When to Use**: To show causal relationships between events or states; formal and semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech where "so" is more natural; avoid overuse in writing (vary with synonyms)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as a result of (followed by noun phrase)
   - as a direct result
   - as a result of this
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Result" naturally collocates with "as a" to form causal connector; high frequency of co-occurrence in formal writing
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + a + result (from Latin resultare, to rebound)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I missed my train. As a result, I was late for work.
   - The roads were icy, and as a result, school was canceled.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As a result of these findings, the methodology was revised.
   - Funding was reduced. As a result, the project timeline was extended.

1. **Expression**: as far as I know (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Based on the information I have; to the best of my knowledge
   - *Fixedness*: strong (common epistemic hedge)
   
   **Synonyms**: to the best of my knowledge, from what I understand, as I understand it
   
   **Antonyms**: I know for certain, definitely, I'm sure
   
   **When to Use**: To qualify statements with limited certainty; hedging device in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When certainty is required or desired; overuse suggests lack of confidence
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as far as I know
   - as far as I can tell
   - as far as I'm aware
   - as far as I'm concerned (different meaning: "in my opinion")
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrases*: Conventionalized epistemic hedge using "as far as" to limit knowledge claim; standard discourse formula
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + far + as + I + know
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As far as I know, she's still living in Boston.
   - The meeting is tomorrow, as far as I know.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As far as I know, no such study has been conducted.
   - The data are accurate as far as I know.

1. **Expression**: as well (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Also; in addition; too
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed collocation for addition)
   
   **Synonyms**: too, also, in addition, additionally, furthermore
   
   **Antonyms**: only, exclusively, solely
   
   **When to Use**: To add information at end of clause; works in all registers; more common in British English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal academic writing where "also" or "in addition" may be preferred; position is typically clause-final
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as well (clause-final)
   - as well as (conjunction)
   - might as well (different meaning: suggestion)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "As well" functions as unit meaning "equally well" → "also"; conventional partnership for addition
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + well
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm coming to the party. John's coming as well.
   - She speaks French and German as well.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines quantitative data. Qualitative findings are considered as well.
   - Economic factors were significant. Political considerations mattered as well.

1. **Expression**: as usual (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the normal or typical way; as is customary
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase for habitual action)
   
   **Synonyms**: as always, typically, normally, characteristically
   
   **Antonyms**: unusually, atypically, for once
   
   **When to Use**: To indicate something is happening in the expected, habitual manner; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound critical or sarcastic in some contexts; tone matters
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as usual (most common)
   - as per usual (slightly informal)
   - business as usual
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Usual" naturally combines with "as" to indicate conformity to habit or norm; very high frequency partnership
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + usual (from Latin usualis, customary)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He was late, as usual.
   - Everything's going smoothly, as usual.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee met as usual on the first Monday of the month.
   - Operations proceeded as usual despite external circumstances.

1. **Expression**: as such (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the exact sense of the word; in that capacity
   - *(Sense 2)* Therefore; consequently (less common)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (conventionalized for precise categorization)
   
   **Synonyms**: per se, in itself, in that capacity, strictly speaking
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: To clarify or emphasize precise categorization or capacity; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal or pedantic in casual speech; meaning can be ambiguous (sense 1 vs sense 2)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as such (standalone)
   - and as such (connecting to previous statement)
   - is not...as such
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrases*: "Such" (meaning "of that kind") combines with "as" to emphasize precise categorization; formal discourse marker
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + such (from Old English swelc)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's no rule as such, but people usually do it that way.
   - He's not a doctor as such, but he knows a lot about medicine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy is not legally binding as such, but carries significant weight.
   - These are recommendations and, as such, should be carefully considered.

1. **Expression**: as expected (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the way that was predicted or anticipated; unsurprisingly
   - *Fixedness*: strong (common collocation for confirmation)
   
   **Synonyms**: as anticipated, predictably, as predicted, unsurprisingly
   
   **Antonyms**: unexpectedly, surprisingly, against expectations
   
   **When to Use**: When outcomes match predictions; neutral tone in formal contexts, can be sarcastic casually
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing surprise or deviation from expectations
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as expected
   - as might be expected
   - just as expected
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Expected" naturally partners with "as" to indicate conformity to prediction; conventional in reporting results
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + expected (from Latin expectare, to look out for)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The movie was boring, as expected.
   - He won the game, as expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Results conformed to the hypothesis, as expected.
   - As expected, the intervention yielded positive outcomes.

1. **Expression**: as follows (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the following manner; what comes next is
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed introductory formula)
   
   **Synonyms**: the following, in this way, in the manner described below
   
   **Antonyms**: as above, as previously stated
   
   **When to Use**: To introduce lists, explanations, or detailed information; formal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual speech where simpler phrases work better; always singular ("follows" not "follow")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as follows (standard)
   - is as follows
   - are as follows (some usage guides accept this)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrases*: Grammatically frozen phrase (verb doesn't agree with subject); conventional introduction to lists or explanations
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + follows (from Old English folgian)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used in casual speech)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The procedure is as follows: first, calibrate the instrument; second, record baseline measurements.
   - The main findings are as follows.

1. **Expression**: as opposed to (type: phrasal preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In contrast with; rather than; instead of
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed contrast marker)
   
   **Synonyms**: rather than, instead of, in contrast to, unlike
   
   **Antonyms**: similar to, like, along with
   
   **When to Use**: To draw explicit contrasts between alternatives; formal and semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be wordy; "rather than" or "instead of" often simpler
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as opposed to
   - as directly opposed to
   - as sharply opposed to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Opposed to" naturally combines with "as" to create contrastive construction; conventional in comparative discourse
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + opposed (from Latin opponere, to set against) + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I prefer coffee as opposed to tea.
   - He works evenings as opposed to mornings.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Qualitative methods were employed as opposed to quantitative approaches.
   - The study examines outcomes in adults as opposed to pediatric populations.

1. **Expression**: as it were (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - So to speak; in a manner of speaking; one might say
   - *Fixedness*: strong (archaic-sounding hedge/qualifier)
   
   **Synonyms**: so to speak, in a manner of speaking, if you will, one might say
   
   **Antonyms**: literally, exactly
   
   **When to Use**: To qualify or soften statements, especially when using figurative or approximate language; formal/literary
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual modern speech where it sounds archaic; can seem affected or pretentious
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as it were (standard)
   - so to speak (more common modern alternative)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Historical subjunctive construction meaning "as it would be"; fossilized phrase used as hedge marker
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + it + were (subjunctive of "be")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's the captain of the ship, as it were.
   - She became, as it were, the unofficial leader.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theory provides, as it were, a lens through which to examine the phenomenon.
   - This represents a turning point, as it were, in the development of the field.

1. **Expression**: as of now (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At this present moment; currently; from now on
   - *Fixedness*: medium (common temporal marker)
   
   **Synonyms**: currently, at present, right now, from this point forward
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, formerly, in the past
   
   **When to Use**: To specify the current time as a reference point; works in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specific dates would be clearer; can be vague about whether it means "currently" or "starting now"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as of now
   - as of right now
   - as of this moment
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "As of" (temporal marker) + "now" (present moment) creates precise temporal reference; common in updates and announcements
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + of + now
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As of now, I haven't heard anything back.
   - The plan is still on as of now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As of now, enrollment remains open.
   - No decision has been reached as of now.

1. **Expression**: as it stands (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the current state or situation; as things are now
   - *Fixedness*: strong (conventionalized for current state)
   
   **Synonyms**: as things are, currently, at present, in its current form
   
   **Antonyms**: as it will be, potentially, after changes
   
   **When to Use**: To refer to current state, often implying possible future change; formal and semi-formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to permanent or unchangeable states
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as it stands
   - as things stand
   - as matters stand
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrases*: "Stand" meaning "remain in state" combines with "as it" to create snapshot of current condition; conventional phrase
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + it + stands
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As it stands, we're going ahead with the plan.
   - The schedule, as it stands, has us leaving at noon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As it stands, the proposal lacks sufficient detail.
   - The policy, as it stands, requires amendment.

1. **Expression**: as a rule (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Generally; usually; normally; as a general principle
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase for generalization)
   
   **Synonyms**: generally, usually, typically, normally, ordinarily
   
   **Antonyms**: exceptionally, rarely, unusually
   
   **When to Use**: To express general patterns or principles; works in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to specific rules (use "according to the rule" instead)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as a rule
   - as a general rule
   - as a rule of thumb (different: rough principle)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrases*: "Rule" meaning "regular practice" combines with "as a" to indicate general pattern; conventionalized generalization marker
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + a + rule (from Latin regula)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As a rule, I don't eat dessert.
   - He's late, as a rule.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As a rule, the committee meets monthly.
   - Participants, as a rule, showed high levels of engagement.

1. **Expression**: as good as (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Practically; virtually; almost
   - *(Sense 2)* Equally good when compared to
   - *Fixedness*: strong (multiple meanings depending on context)
   
   **Synonyms**: virtually, practically, almost, nearly (sense 1); equally good, just as good (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: not quite, far from, inferior to
   
   **When to Use**: For comparisons (sense 2) or to indicate near-completion (sense 1); all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous—context must clarify which meaning is intended
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as good as (done/finished/dead)
   - as good as new
   - just as good as
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Good" with "as...as" creates comparison frame; when followed by past participle, means "practically"
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + good + as
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The project's as good as finished.
   - This phone is as good as the expensive one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The alternative approach is as good as the standard method.
   - The agreement is as good as finalized.
