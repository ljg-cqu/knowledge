1. **Noun**: comparison (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of comparing two or more things to show similarities or differences
   - *Often used with "as" in phrases about the nature or quality of comparison*
   
   **Synonyms**: contrast, analogy, parallel, juxtaposition, collation
   
   **Antonyms**: distinction, differentiation, separation
   
   **When to Use**: In analytical, academic, and formal contexts when discussing similarities and differences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple identification without comparison is meant; in very casual contexts, "comparing" may sound more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - by comparison as
   - in comparison as
   - make a comparison as
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + par (equal) + -ison (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French comparaison → Latin comparationem (a comparing) → English "comparison" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's really no comparison as far as quality goes.
   - In comparison as a value proposition, this one's better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - By comparison as a methodological approach, the alternative offers greater validity.
   - The data permits comparison as a baseline for future research.

1. **Noun**: role (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The function or position that someone or something has in a particular situation
   - *Very commonly used with "as" to indicate function: "role as"*
   
   **Synonyms**: function, position, capacity, part, duty
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: When describing someone's function, position, or responsibility; works in all contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing dramatic/theatrical roles exclusively, "part" may be more specific
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - role as leader
   - serve in a role as
   - fulfilling the role as
   
   **Root Analysis**: French rôle (roll of paper with actor's part)
   
   **Etymology**: French rôle (part an actor plays) → from roll of paper → English "role" (1600s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's doing great in her role as team captain.
   - His role as mediator was crucial.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution's role as regulator requires careful consideration.
   - She assumed the role as principal investigator for the project.

1. **Noun**: capacity (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The position, function, or role in which someone operates
   - *(Sense 2)* The maximum amount that something can contain or produce
   - *Used with "as" to indicate function: "in one's capacity as"*
   
   **Synonyms**: role, function, position, capability, ability
   
   **Antonyms**: incapacity, inability (for sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when describing official positions or functional roles
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where "as" or "role" might sound more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in one's capacity as
   - acting in the capacity as
   - capacity as advisor
   
   **Root Analysis**: capac- (able to hold) + -ity (quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin capacitas (breadth, capacity) → Old French capacité → English "capacity" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm speaking in my capacity as a friend, not as your boss.
   - He's serving in his capacity as treasurer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee acted in its capacity as oversight body.
   - She participated in her official capacity as department chair.

1. **Noun**: function (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The purpose or intended use of something; the role something is designed to perform
   - *Used with "as" to indicate purpose: "function as"*
   
   **Synonyms**: purpose, role, duty, task, operation
   
   **Antonyms**: malfunction, dysfunction
   
   **When to Use**: In technical, formal, or analytical contexts when describing purpose or operation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing people's social roles (use "role" instead); in very casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - function as a tool
   - serves a function as
   - primary function as
   
   **Root Analysis**: funct- (perform) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin functio (performance, execution) → Old French fonction → English "function" (1530s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This app's main function as I see it is tracking expenses.
   - It basically serves a function as a backup.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The mechanism operates in its function as a regulatory control.
   - The committee fulfills its function as advisory body.

1. **Noun**: example (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thing characteristic of its kind or illustrating a general rule
   - *Used with "as" to introduce instances: "as an example"*
   
   **Synonyms**: instance, illustration, specimen, case, sample
   
   **Antonyms**: exception, anomaly
   
   **When to Use**: When providing specific instances to illustrate general points; works in all contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the thing is unique rather than representative
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - as an example
   - serve as example
   - example as illustration
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + empl- (take) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin exemplum (sample, model) → Old French example → English "example" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Take this as an example of what I mean.
   - She's a great example as someone who succeeded against odds.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This case serves as an example of effective policy implementation.
   - The phenomenon functions as an example of broader systemic issues.

1. **Noun**: instance (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An example or single occurrence of something; a particular case
   - *Used with "as" in formal writing: "as an instance of"*
   
   **Synonyms**: example, case, occurrence, occasion, illustration
   
   **Antonyms**: generality, rule
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and academic writing when citing specific cases or examples
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where "example" is more common; when discussing generalities
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - as an instance of
   - for instance (as)
   - single instance as
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + sta- (stand) + -nce (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin instantia (presence, urgency) → Old French instance → English "instance" (example, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is just one instance as far as I know.
   - Take for instance as an example what happened yesterday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This serves as an instance of the theoretical framework in practice.
   - The case represents a clear instance as defined by the criteria.

1. **Noun**: equivalent (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that is equal to or corresponds with another in value, function, or meaning
   - *Used with "as" to indicate equivalence: "equivalent as"*
   
   **Synonyms**: equal, counterpart, parallel, match, correspondent
   
   **Antonyms**: opposite, contrary, unequal
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when discussing things of equal value or function
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When things are merely similar but not truly equivalent
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - serve as equivalent
   - functional equivalent as
   - equivalent as substitute
   
   **Root Analysis**: equi- (equal) + val- (worth) + -ent (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin aequivalent- → Old French equivalent → English "equivalent" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A GED is basically the equivalent as a high school diploma.
   - This works as an equivalent as far as I'm concerned.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The qualification is recognized as the equivalent of the national standard.
   - This metric serves as an adequate equivalent for comparative purposes.

1. **Noun**: measure (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A standard unit used to express size, amount, or degree
   - *(Sense 2)* An action taken to achieve a particular purpose
   - *Used with "as" to indicate function: "as a measure of"*
   
   **Synonyms**: gauge, standard, criterion, indicator, metric
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: In analytical, technical, or formal contexts when discussing quantification or assessment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact measurements are meant (specify the unit); in very casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - as a measure of
   - serve as measure
   - measure as indicator
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin mensura (a measuring)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin mensura → Old French mesure → English "measure" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We use sales as a measure of success.
   - This serves as a measure of how well it's working.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The index functions as a measure of economic stability.
   - Response time serves as an appropriate measure of system performance.

1. **Noun**: means (singular and plural same form)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A method, course of action, or instrument by which something is achieved
   - *Used with "as" to indicate method: "as a means of"*
   
   **Synonyms**: method, way, manner, instrument, mechanism
   
   **Antonyms**: end, goal, purpose
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when discussing methods or instruments for achieving goals
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal; "way" or "method" often clearer in casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - as a means of
   - means as method
   - serves as means
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English mene (middle, intermediate)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French meien (means, middle) → Latin medianus (in the middle) → English "means"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're using social media as a means of reaching people.
   - This works as a means of getting things done faster.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention serves as a means of addressing the underlying issues.
   - Technology functions as a means of enhancing operational efficiency.

1. **Noun**: extent (U, singular)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The degree to which something is the case; the scope or range of something
   - *Used with "as" in phrases like "to the same extent as"*
   
   **Synonyms**: degree, level, scope, range, magnitude
   
   **Antonyms**: limitation, restriction
   
   **When to Use**: In formal or analytical contexts when discussing degree or scope
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specific measurements are needed; in very casual conversation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - to the same extent as
   - to a large extent as
   - extent as measure
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + tent- (stretch) + noun suffix
   
   **Etymology**: Latin extentus (stretched out) → Anglo-Norman extent → English "extent" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I agree to some extent as far as the basics go.
   - It's true to the same extent as what you said.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings apply to the same extent as previous research.
   - The policy succeeded to a limited extent as measured by outcomes.
