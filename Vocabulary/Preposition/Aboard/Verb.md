1. **Verb**: board
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To get onto or allow someone to get onto a ship, aircraft, train, or other vehicle
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To provide someone with meals and accommodation for payment
   
   **Synonyms**: embark, get on, enter, mount, enplane (for aircraft)
   
   **Antonyms**: disembark, alight, get off, deboard, deplane
   
   **When to Use**: Use "board" when referring to entering or getting onto vehicles, especially in formal or transportation contexts. Also used when discussing providing accommodation with meals.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "board" for entering buildings or rooms (use "enter"). Avoid using it for small personal vehicles like bicycles or motorcycles in everyday speech.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - board a plane/train/ship
   - board the bus
   - now boarding
   - ready to board
   - board with someone
   
   **Root Analysis**: board (from Old English "bord" = ship's side) → verb form meaning "to go over the side onto a ship"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bord" (ship's side) → verb "to board" (to go onto a ship by crossing over its side), 16th century; accommodation sense from "bed and board" (lodging and meals)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to board the train in five minutes or we'll miss it.
   - I boarded with my aunt when I was in college.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Passengers are requested to board the aircraft through Gate 7.
   - The students board at the academy during the academic term.

2. **Verb**: embark
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To go onto a ship or aircraft; to begin a journey
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To begin an activity or enterprise, especially one that is important or demanding
   
   **Synonyms**: board, set sail, depart, commence, begin, undertake
   
   **Antonyms**: disembark, conclude, finish, abandon, end
   
   **When to Use**: Use "embark" for formal contexts involving boarding ships or aircraft, or metaphorically for beginning important journeys, projects, or life phases. Common in travel and business contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "embark" for casual, everyday boarding of buses or trains. Avoid using it for trivial activities that don't warrant the formality.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - embark on a journey
   - embark on a project
   - embark on a career
   - embark from (port/location)
   - embark upon an adventure
   
   **Root Analysis**: em- (in, into) + bark (boat, from French "barque") = to go into a boat
   
   **Etymology**: French "embarquer" → from "em-" (in) + "barque" (boat), originally meant "to put or go on board a ship," later extended metaphorically to any beginning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're embarking on a road trip across the country next week.
   - She's embarking on a new career in tech.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegation will embark from Southampton on Thursday morning.
   - The university has embarked upon an ambitious research initiative to address climate change.

3. **Verb**: disembark
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To leave a ship, aircraft, or other vehicle; to put ashore
   
   **Synonyms**: alight, get off, deboard, deplane, land
   
   **Antonyms**: board, embark, get on, enter
   
   **When to Use**: Use "disembark" in formal or transportation contexts when referring to leaving ships, aircraft, or large vehicles. Common in travel, tourism, and transportation announcements.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "disembark" for casual, everyday situations with cars, bicycles, or small vehicles (use "get off" or "get out"). Avoid using it when "leave" would sound more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disembark from the ship
   - disembark at the port
   - passengers disembark
   - disembark the plane
   - safely disembark
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (away from, off) + embark (go on board) = to go off board
   
   **Etymology**: French "désembarquer" → from "dés-" (opposite of) + "embarquer" (to board), literally "to un-board"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We'll disembark in Miami and spend a couple days there.
   - Wait until everyone disembarks before you try to get on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Passengers will disembark at Terminal 3 upon arrival.
   - The crew disembarked the cargo at precisely 0600 hours.

4. **Verb**: sail
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive/Transitive) To travel in a boat with sails, especially as a sport or recreation; to control a boat with sails
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To travel on water in a ship or boat; to begin a voyage
   - *(Sense 3)* (Intransitive) To move quickly, smoothly, or effortlessly
   
   **Synonyms**: voyage, cruise, navigate, glide, breeze through
   
   **Antonyms**: dock, anchor, moor, land, struggle
   
   **When to Use**: Use "sail" for water travel, especially involving sailboats, ships departing on voyages, or metaphorically for moving smoothly through something. Common in nautical, travel, and figurative contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "sail" for motorized boat travel when the sails aren't the primary means of propulsion (though it's acceptable in general speech). Avoid using metaphorical "sail" in formal technical writing.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - set sail
   - sail the seas
   - sail through (something)
   - smooth sailing
   - sail away
   
   **Root Analysis**: sail (from Old English "segl" = piece of fabric used to catch wind on a boat)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "segl" (the cloth) → "seglian" (to travel by sail) → "sail," originally nautical, later extended to any ship travel and metaphorical smooth movement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're sailing to the Caribbean next month for vacation.
   - She just sailed through that exam without studying much.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vessel is scheduled to sail at dawn on the fifteenth of March.
   - The project sailed through the approval process without significant opposition.

5. **Verb**: depart
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To leave, especially to start a journey
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To deviate or differ from a standard, tradition, or plan
   
   **Synonyms**: leave, set off, go, exit, withdraw
   
   **Antonyms**: arrive, come, stay, remain, enter
   
   **When to Use**: Use "depart" in formal or transportation contexts for leaving places or beginning journeys. Also used metaphorically for deviating from norms or plans.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "depart" in very casual everyday conversation where "leave" or "go" would be more natural. Avoid overusing it in informal writing.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - depart from (location)
   - depart for (destination)
   - depart on time
   - depart from tradition
   - depart this life (euphemism for die)
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, from) + part (separate, divide) = to separate away from
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "departire" (to divide, separate) → Old French "departir" → English "depart," originally "to separate," later specialized to mean "to go away"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What time does your flight depart?
   - They're departing from the usual menu tonight and serving Thai food.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The train will depart from Platform 9 at 14:45 precisely.
   - This approach departs significantly from established methodological protocols.
