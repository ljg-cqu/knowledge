1. **Noun**: board (C, pl: boards)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A flat piece of wood or other hard material used for a particular purpose
   - *(Sense 2)* A group of people who have responsibility for managing or directing a company or organization; the deck or side of a ship
   
   **Synonyms**: plank, panel, committee, council, directors
   
   **Antonyms**: individual, sole authority (in organizational context)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "board" when referring to flat surfaces, organizational governing bodies, or nautical contexts (ship's deck). Common in construction, corporate governance, and maritime settings.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "board" as a verb substitute when you mean to enter a vehicle; avoid confusion between "board" (noun) and "aboard" (preposition/adverb).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - board of directors
   - on board
   - across the board
   - go by the board
   - above board
   
   **Root Analysis**: board (Old English bord = plank, table, ship's side)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bord" → originally meant "plank, flat surface" and "ship's side," later extended to mean "table" and then "group meeting at a table"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to replace that old board in the fence.
   - The board meets every Tuesday to discuss company stuff.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board of directors has approved the merger proposal.
   - All decisions must be submitted to the advisory board for review.

2. **Noun**: passenger (C, pl: passengers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who is traveling in a vehicle but is not driving it or working on it
   
   **Synonyms**: traveler, rider, commuter, fare, voyager
   
   **Antonyms**: driver, pilot, crew member, operator
   
   **When to Use**: Use "passenger" for anyone traveling in a vehicle (car, plane, train, ship, bus) who is not operating it or part of the crew. Essential in transportation contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "passenger" for crew members, drivers, or pilots. Avoid using it for people who are merely present but not traveling.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - passenger seat
   - passenger list
   - fellow passenger
   - carry passengers
   - passenger safety
   
   **Root Analysis**: pass (go through) + -enger (agent suffix) = one who passes through
   
   **Etymology**: French "passager" → from "passage" (act of passing) + agent suffix, originally meaning "one who passes by/through"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was just a passenger, so don't blame me for the traffic ticket!
   - The flight had about 200 passengers on it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All passengers are required to present valid identification before boarding.
   - The airline is committed to ensuring passenger comfort and safety throughout the journey.

3. **Noun**: crew (C, pl: crews)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A group of people who work together, especially all those who work on and operate a ship, aircraft, or spacecraft
   - *(Sense 2)* A group of people who work together on a particular task or project
   
   **Synonyms**: team, staff, personnel, workforce, company
   
   **Antonyms**: passengers, individuals, solo operator
   
   **When to Use**: Use "crew" for groups working on vehicles (ships, planes, spacecraft) or for teams working on specific projects (film crew, construction crew). Common in transportation, film, and construction industries.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "crew" for passengers or for formal organizational hierarchies. Avoid using it when "team" or "staff" would be more appropriate for professional office settings.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - crew member
   - ground crew
   - flight crew
   - crew chief
   - skeleton crew
   
   **Root Analysis**: crew (from French "creue" = reinforcement, recruit)
   
   **Etymology**: French "creue" (military reinforcement) → English "crew" (group of sailors), later extended to any working group
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The whole crew is going out for pizza after the show.
   - Our film crew worked twelve hours straight yesterday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The cabin crew will demonstrate safety procedures before takeoff.
   - A crew of five technicians has been assigned to maintain the equipment.

4. **Noun**: vessel (C, pl: vessels)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A ship or large boat
   - *(Sense 2)* A container, especially one used to hold liquid; a tube or duct carrying blood or other fluid in the body
   
   **Synonyms**: ship, boat, craft, container, receptacle
   
   **Antonyms**: cargo, contents, passenger (in nautical context)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "vessel" for ships/boats in formal or technical contexts, for containers in scientific settings, or for blood vessels in medical contexts. Common in maritime law, medicine, and formal writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "vessel" in casual conversation when "ship" or "boat" would sound more natural. Avoid using it for small recreational boats in informal contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - blood vessel
   - merchant vessel
   - naval vessel
   - pressure vessel
   - empty vessel
   
   **Root Analysis**: vessel (from Latin "vascellum" = small container, diminutive of "vas" = container)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "vas" (container) → "vascellum" (small container) → Old French "vaissel" → English "vessel," extended from containers to ships (which contain cargo/people)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's a pretty big vessel for just weekend sailing.
   - The doctor said I have a blocked blood vessel.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vessel was registered under Panamanian maritime law.
   - Foreign vessels must obtain clearance before entering territorial waters.

5. **Noun**: boarding (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of getting onto a ship, aircraft, train, or other vehicle
   - *(Sense 2)* Long flat pieces of wood used to cover floors, walls, or other surfaces
   
   **Synonyms**: embarkation, entraining, emplaning (for aircraft), planking (for wood sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: disembarkation, deboarding, alighting, departure
   
   **When to Use**: Use "boarding" for the process of entering vehicles, especially in transportation contexts (boarding passes, boarding time), or for wooden panels in construction contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "boarding" when referring to staying somewhere (that's "lodging" or "accommodation"). Avoid confusion with "board" (noun) or "bored" (adjective).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - boarding pass
   - boarding time
   - boarding gate
   - now boarding
   - priority boarding
   
   **Root Analysis**: board (enter a ship) + -ing (noun suffix) = act of entering a vehicle
   
   **Etymology**: From "board" (to go on board a ship) → "boarding" (the action of going aboard), dating to nautical usage in the 16th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Boarding starts in about twenty minutes, so let's grab coffee.
   - They're boarding families with small kids first.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Boarding will commence at Gate 12 at 14:30 hours.
   - Passengers holding boarding passes for rows 20-35 may now proceed to the aircraft.
