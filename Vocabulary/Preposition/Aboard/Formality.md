1. **Original Expression**: Welcome aboard  
   **Context**: Greeting someone joining a vehicle, team, or organization
   
   **Core Intent**: To greet and express pleasure at someone joining
   
   **Casual**: Hey, welcome! Glad you're here!  
   *Characteristics*: Informal greeting, contracted forms, friendly tone  
   *Use when*: With friends, close colleagues, informal team settings, relaxed environments
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Welcome aboard. We're pleased to have you with us.  
   *Characteristics*: Standard business greeting, complete sentences, professional but warm  
   *Use when*: New employee orientations, standard business meetings, professional team introductions
   
   **Formal**: We are delighted to welcome you aboard and look forward to your contributions.  
   *Characteristics*: Complete formal sentence, sophisticated vocabulary, ceremonial tone  
   *Use when*: Executive announcements, official ceremonies, formal business communications
   
   **Humorous**: Welcome aboard the crazy train! Hope you're ready for the ride!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor, exclamation marks, self-deprecating humor  
   *Use when*: Casual team environments, creative industries, when building rapport with new members  
   *Avoid when*: First day of formal jobs, serious business settings, with senior executives
   
   **Lively**: So excited to have you aboard! This team just got even better!  
   *Characteristics*: High enthusiasm, positive emphasis, exclamations  
   *Use when*: Startup cultures, young teams, when building energy and excitement
   
   **Empathetic**: Welcome aboard. I know starting something new can feel overwhelming, but we're here to support you every step of the way.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of feelings, reassurance language, supportive tone  
   *Use when*: Anxious new members, career transitions, major life changes
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status (CEO to employee) requires formal; peer-to-peer allows casual
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers and new professional relationships require semi-formal minimum
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Important positions require formal; routine additions allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect formal welcomes regardless of hierarchy
   - *Medium*: Written (email) typically more formal than face-to-face spoken
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "Hey, welcome aboard!" in formal business email to senior executives—too casual for authority figures
   - Using overly formal "We are honored by your presence" for routine team member—sounds insincere or sarcastic
   - Mixing registers: "Hey, we are delighted to welcome you"—inconsistent tone confuses the message
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In hierarchical cultures (East Asia, Latin America), welcoming aboard someone requires more formality regardless of personal relationship. Anglo cultures allow more casual welcomes between peers.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Welcoming new intern to tech startup → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively/Casual ("So pumped to have you aboard!")
   - **Situation 2**: CEO welcoming new board member → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("We are honored to welcome you aboard")
   - **Situation 3**: Manager welcoming nervous first-time employee → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("Welcome aboard. We know it's your first role, and we'll support you")

2. **Original Expression**: Get everyone aboard with the plan  
   **Context**: Ensuring all stakeholders support or understand a proposal
   
   **Core Intent**: To gain agreement or buy-in from all relevant parties
   
   **Casual**: Let's make sure everyone's cool with this.  
   *Characteristics*: Slang ("cool with"), relaxed phrasing, informal verb choice  
   *Use when*: Friends planning activities, casual team discussions, low-stakes decisions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We need to get everyone on board with the plan before we proceed.  
   *Characteristics*: "On board" (two words), professional vocabulary, clear action-oriented language  
   *Use when*: Standard business meetings, project planning sessions, manager to team communications
   
   **Formal**: It is essential that we secure unanimous stakeholder approval before implementation.  
   *Characteristics*: Passive construction possible, technical vocabulary ("stakeholder," "implementation"), serious tone  
   *Use when*: Board meetings, official proposals, executive communications, legal contexts
   
   **Humorous**: Let's make sure nobody's planning to jump ship on this one!  
   *Characteristics*: Maritime metaphor continued playfully, idiomatic expression, light tone  
   *Use when*: Teams with good rapport, creative brainstorming, when reducing tension  
   *Avoid when*: Serious projects with history of disagreement, formal presentations, crisis situations
   
   **Lively**: We've got to rally everyone behind this—it's going to be amazing!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic verb choice ("rally"), optimistic framing, enthusiasm markers  
   *Use when*: Motivational contexts, exciting new projects, when building momentum
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand there may be concerns, so let's make sure everyone feels heard and comfortable with the direction before we move forward.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of feelings ("concerns," "comfortable"), inclusive language, patient tone  
   *Use when*: Controversial decisions, changes affecting team members, sensitive topics
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Seeking buy-in from superiors requires formal; from subordinates allows semi-formal
   - *Social Distance*: Cross-departmental or external stakeholders require semi-formal minimum
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-budget or high-risk projects require formal; routine matters allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Consensus-oriented cultures require more careful, formal buy-in processes
   - *Medium*: Written proposals and presentations typically require semi-formal to formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "Let's make sure everyone's cool" in written proposal to executives—too casual for formal documentation
   - Using "unanimous stakeholder approval" in quick team chat—unnecessarily stiff and bureaucratic
   - Rushing buy-in without empathy in change management—ignores emotional aspects of agreement
   
   **Cultural Notes**: North American business culture often uses direct buy-in language. Asian cultures may require more indirect approaches with "unanimous approval" implying harmony rather than explicit agreement.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Small team deciding on lunch venue → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("Everyone cool with Thai food?")
   - **Situation 2**: Manager presenting project plan to team → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("Let's get everyone on board before we launch")
   - **Situation 3**: Presenting merger to board of directors → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("We must secure unanimous board approval")

3. **Original Expression**: Come aboard the ship  
   **Context**: Inviting someone to enter a vessel, vehicle, or join an endeavor
   
   **Core Intent**: To invite or instruct someone to board or join
   
   **Casual**: Hop on! / Get in!  
   *Characteristics*: Short imperative, informal verb ("hop"), friendly and direct  
   *Use when*: Friends, family, casual rides, everyday transportation
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Please board the vessel. / Please come aboard.  
   *Characteristics*: Polite imperative with "please," standard vocabulary, clear instruction  
   *Use when*: Cruise ships, tour guides, standard transportation services, professional contexts
   
   **Formal**: Passengers are cordially invited to board the vessel at their earliest convenience.  
   *Characteristics*: Passive construction, formal vocabulary ("cordially invited," "at their earliest convenience")  
   *Use when*: Luxury cruises, diplomatic vessels, ceremonial occasions, VIP transportation
   
   **Humorous**: All aboard who's coming aboard! Next stop: adventure!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful repetition, traditional announcement parody, exclamatory enthusiasm  
   *Use when*: Tour groups with families, fun excursions, theme park transportation  
   *Avoid when*: Emergency situations, serious business transport, military vessels
   
   **Lively**: Let's go, everyone! Time to board and start this amazing journey!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic call to action, positive framing ("amazing journey"), group motivation  
   *Use when*: Tour departures, group trips, when building excitement for the journey
   
   **Empathetic**: Please take your time boarding. We know it can be overwhelming, and crew members are here to help if you need assistance.  
   *Characteristics*: Patient language ("take your time"), acknowledgment of difficulties, offer of support  
   *Use when*: Passengers with mobility issues, first-time travelers, anxious passengers
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: VIP guests require formal invitations; general passengers receive semi-formal
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers on commercial vessels get semi-formal; friends get casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Ceremonial or luxury contexts require formal; routine transport allows casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Maritime tradition often maintains semi-formal register even in casual contexts
   - *Medium*: Written invitations more formal than verbal announcements
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "Hop on!" to elderly passengers or VIPs—too casual and potentially disrespectful
   - Using overly formal language for routine ferry service—creates unnecessary distance
   - Failing to provide accessible language for passengers with special needs—lacks empathy
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Maritime culture traditionally maintains formality around boarding procedures across many cultures. However, modern casual travel has relaxed this in some contexts.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend picking you up in their car → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("Hop in!")
   - **Situation 2**: Tour boat boarding announcement → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("Please come aboard when ready")
   - **Situation 3**: Royal yacht invitation to dignitaries → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Your Excellencies are cordially invited to board")
