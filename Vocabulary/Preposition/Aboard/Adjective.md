1. **Adjective**: onboard
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Attributive/Predicative, non-gradable) Located on or within a ship, aircraft, vehicle, or spacecraft
   - *(Sense 2)* (Predicative) Participating in or supporting a plan, project, or organization
   
   **Synonyms**: aboard, installed, integrated, participating, committed
   
   **Antonyms**: external, offboard, separate, opposed, disconnected
   
   **When to Use**: Use "onboard" (one word) as an adjective to describe equipment, systems, or people located on a vehicle, or metaphorically for people who support a project or idea. Common in technical, transportation, and business contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse "onboard" (adjective, one word) with "on board" (prepositional phrase, two words). Avoid using "onboard" when describing stationary locations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - onboard computer
   - onboard equipment
   - onboard entertainment
   - get someone onboard
   - bring everyone onboard
   
   **Root Analysis**: on + board = positioned on the board/deck/vehicle
   
   **Etymology**: From "on board" (prepositional phrase) → "onboard" (adjective), nautical origin referring to things present on a ship's deck
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The onboard WiFi is pretty slow on this flight.
   - Are you onboard with the plan for Saturday?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The aircraft is equipped with sophisticated onboard navigation systems.
   - We need all stakeholders onboard before proceeding with implementation.

2. **Adjective**: maritime
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Attributive, non-gradable) Connected with the sea, especially in relation to seafaring commercial or military activity
   
   **Synonyms**: nautical, naval, marine, seafaring, oceanic
   
   **Antonyms**: terrestrial, landlocked, inland, land-based
   
   **When to Use**: Use "maritime" for formal or technical references to sea-related activities, especially commerce, law, and military operations. Common in legal, historical, and professional contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "maritime" in casual conversation where "sea" or "ocean" would be more natural. Avoid using it for freshwater contexts (use "aquatic" or "naval" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - maritime law
   - maritime trade
   - maritime industry
   - maritime nation
   - maritime history
   
   **Root Analysis**: marit- (sea, from Latin "mare") + -ime (relating to) = relating to the sea
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "maritimus" (of the sea) → from "mare" (sea) + "-timus" (superlative suffix), meaning "by or near the sea"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His family has a long maritime tradition going back generations.
   - The museum has a great maritime exhibit with old ship models.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The dispute falls under international maritime jurisdiction.
   - Maritime trade routes have been crucial to economic development throughout history.

3. **Adjective**: nautical
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Attributive, non-gradable) Relating to ships, sailing, or navigation
   
   **Synonyms**: maritime, naval, marine, seafaring, sailing
   
   **Antonyms**: terrestrial, land-based, landlocked
   
   **When to Use**: Use "nautical" for references to sailing, navigation, and general ship-related matters. Common in sailing, navigation, and marine contexts, often more technical than "sea-related."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "nautical" when discussing marine biology or ocean science (use "marine"). Avoid using it for military naval forces (use "naval").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nautical mile
   - nautical chart
   - nautical terms
   - nautical theme
   - nautical experience
   
   **Root Analysis**: naut- (sailor, from Greek "nautes") + -ical (relating to) = relating to sailors/sailing
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "nautes" (sailor) → Latin "nauticus" → English "nautical," specifically relating to sailing and navigation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She decorated her beach house with a nautical theme.
   - I don't understand all these nautical terms they're using.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The distance is measured in nautical miles, which differ from statute miles.
   - Proficiency in nautical navigation requires extensive training and certification.

4. **Adjective**: naval
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Attributive, non-gradable) Relating to a navy or navies; concerning military ships
   
   **Synonyms**: maritime (in military context), military marine, fleet-related
   
   **Antonyms**: civilian, commercial, merchant, land-based
   
   **When to Use**: Use "naval" specifically for military maritime forces, operations, and installations. Essential in military and defense contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "naval" for commercial shipping or civilian boating (use "maritime" or "nautical"). Avoid confusing with "navel" (belly button).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - naval officer
   - naval base
   - naval warfare
   - naval academy
   - naval forces
   
   **Root Analysis**: nav- (ship, from Latin "navis") + -al (relating to) = relating to ships
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "navis" (ship) → "navalis" (relating to ships) → Old French "naval" → English "naval," specifically military ships
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My brother graduated from the naval academy last year.
   - There's a big naval base near San Diego.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The naval commander directed the fleet to maintain defensive positions.
   - Naval architecture requires expertise in both engineering and hydrodynamics.

5. **Adjective**: seaborne
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Attributive, non-gradable) Transported or traveling by sea; carried out from the sea
   
   **Synonyms**: maritime, oceanic, shipborne, waterborne
   
   **Antonyms**: airborne, land-based, overland, terrestrial
   
   **When to Use**: Use "seaborne" when describing trade, cargo, military operations, or travel that occurs by sea. Common in logistics, military, and trade contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "seaborne" for things that simply exist near the sea but aren't transported by it. Avoid using it when "shipped" or "by sea" would be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - seaborne trade
   - seaborne invasion
   - seaborne cargo
   - seaborne transport
   - seaborne attack
   
   **Root Analysis**: sea (ocean) + borne (carried, past participle of "bear") = carried by sea
   
   **Etymology**: "Sea" (Old English "sæ") + "borne" (carried, from "bear") → compound adjective meaning "carried by sea," similar to "airborne"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Most of the world's goods are still seaborne, not flown.
   - The seaborne invasion was depicted in the movie really dramatically.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Seaborne commerce accounts for approximately 90% of global trade volume.
   - The seaborne assault commenced at dawn under heavy artillery cover.
