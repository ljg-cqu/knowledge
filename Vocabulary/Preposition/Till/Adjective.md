1. **Adjective**: tilled
   **Meaning**: 
   - (of land or soil) prepared for growing crops by plowing, turning, and breaking up; cultivated (attributive and predicative, non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: plowed, cultivated, worked, farmed, prepared
   
   **Antonyms**: untilled, uncultivated, fallow, wild, unworked
   
   **When to Use**: Use "tilled" in agricultural, farming, or gardening contexts to describe land that has been prepared for planting. Appropriate in both technical agricultural writing and general descriptions of farmland. Often used to contrast with natural or uncultivated land.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for describing mental or intellectual preparation (use "cultivated" instead). Not appropriate for urban or non-agricultural contexts. Avoid when discussing land that is simply cleared but not prepared for crops.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tilled land
   - tilled soil
   - tilled field
   - tilled ground
   - tilled earth
   - freshly tilled
   - well-tilled
   
   **Root Analysis**: "till" (verb root) + "-ed" (past participle/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English "tilian" + past participle ending → Middle English "tilled" - used as adjective from at least the 14th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The freshly tilled field looked ready for planting.
   - You can see the difference between tilled and untilled sections of the farm.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Tilled agricultural land comprises approximately 40% of the region's total area.
   - The study compared carbon sequestration rates in tilled versus no-till farming systems.

1. **Adjective**: untilled
   **Meaning**: 
   - (of land or soil) not prepared or cultivated for growing crops; not plowed or worked (attributive and predicative, non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: uncultivated, unplowed, unworked, fallow, wild, virgin (land)
   
   **Antonyms**: tilled, cultivated, plowed, worked, farmed
   
   **When to Use**: Use "untilled" to describe land that remains in its natural state or has not been prepared for agriculture. Common in environmental discussions, land use planning, and agricultural contexts. Often used when contrasting natural vs. agricultural landscapes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for abandoned farmland that was previously tilled (use "fallow" or "abandoned" instead). Not appropriate for land that is unsuitable for agriculture. Avoid in contexts unrelated to agriculture or land use.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - untilled land
   - untilled soil
   - untilled field
   - untilled ground
   - remain untilled
   - leave untilled
   
   **Root Analysis**: "un-" (negative prefix) + "till" (verb root) + "-ed" (past participle/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: "un-" (Old English negative prefix) + "tilled" → formed by standard English negation pattern, in use since Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The back forty acres have been left untilled for years now.
   - Wildlife loves untilled areas where native plants can grow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Untilled land in the conservation area provides critical habitat for native species.
   - The environmental impact assessment recommended leaving steep slopes untilled to prevent erosion.

1. **Adjective**: tillable
   **Meaning**: 
   - (of land) suitable or able to be tilled or cultivated; capable of being prepared for growing crops (attributive and predicative, non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: arable, cultivable, farmable, workable, plowable
   
   **Antonyms**: untillable, unsuitable (for farming), non-arable, rocky, barren
   
   **When to Use**: Use "tillable" in agricultural planning, land assessment, and farming discussions to describe land that has the potential for cultivation. Common in agricultural economics, land surveys, and farm management contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for land that is already being farmed (use "tilled" or "cultivated"). Not appropriate for land with crops currently growing. Avoid in non-agricultural contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tillable land
   - tillable soil
   - tillable acres
   - tillable area
   - potentially tillable
   - economically tillable
   
   **Root Analysis**: "till" (verb root) + "-able" (adjective suffix meaning "capable of being")
   
   **Etymology**: "till" + "-able" (suffix from Latin "-abilis" via French) → formed by standard English derivation pattern, documented from 17th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We have about 200 acres of tillable land on this property.
   - Not all the land here is tillable - some parts are too rocky.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agricultural survey identified 15,000 hectares of tillable land currently unused.
   - Climate change may reduce the total area of tillable land available for food production.
