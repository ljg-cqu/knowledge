1. **Preposition**: past (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving by and beyond something without stopping
   - *(Temporal)* Beyond a particular time or age; after
   - *(Abstract)* Beyond the limits or scope of something
   
   **Synonyms**: beyond (in spatial contexts), after (in temporal contexts), by (in movement contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, until, up to
   
   **When to Use**: Use "past" to express movement beyond a point in space, progression beyond a time point, or exceeding limits or capabilities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse with "passed" (past tense of "pass"). Common error: "I past the test" (incorrect) vs. "I passed the test" (correct). Do not use for future time references
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: past midnight, past the deadline, past the building, past belief
   - **Verb + prep**: walk past, run past, drive past, go past
   - **Adjective + prep**: way past (informal)
   - **Noun + prep**: five minutes past three
   
   **Root Analysis**: past (from Middle English "passed", past participle of "passen")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from "passed" (past participle of pass) → Latin "passus" (step, pace)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We drove past your house yesterday but you weren't home.
   - It's already ten past nine—we're going to be late!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project deadline is now past, and we must submit a formal extension request.
   - The vehicle proceeded past the checkpoint without incident.

1. **Preposition**: in the past (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* During a previous time or period; formerly; in earlier times
   - *(Abstract)* Referring to historical events or previous experiences
   
   **Synonyms**: previously, formerly, in former times, in earlier days, historically
   
   **Antonyms**: in the future, currently, presently, nowadays
   
   **When to Use**: Use to reference events, states, or situations that occurred at an unspecified earlier time; appropriate for both formal and informal contexts when discussing history or personal experience
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when specific dates or times are needed (use "on [date]" or "in [year]" instead). Not appropriate for immediate past (use "just now," "recently" instead)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in the distant past, in the recent past, buried in the past
   - **Verb + prep**: happened in the past, lived in the past, stuck in the past
   - **Adjective + prep**: different in the past
   - **Noun + prep**: mistakes in the past, experiences in the past
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (preposition) + the (article) + past (noun from verb "pass")
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal construction combining Old English "in" + Middle English "past" (from Latin "passus")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In the past, we used to meet every weekend, but now everyone's so busy.
   - I made mistakes in the past, but I've learned from them.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the past, this institution maintained strict admission criteria.
   - Economic policies in the past have demonstrated limited effectiveness in addressing inequality.

1. **Preposition**: since (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* From a particular time in the past until now; continuously from a past point to the present
   - *(Abstract)* Because; for the reason that (as conjunction)
   
   **Synonyms**: from (in temporal contexts), ever since, from the time that
   
   **Antonyms**: until, before, up to
   
   **When to Use**: Use "since" with present perfect tense to indicate duration from a past point to now; appropriate for specific time points (since Monday, since 2020, since childhood)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use with past simple for completed actions (use "ago" instead). Common error: "I saw him since yesterday" (incorrect) vs. "I haven't seen him since yesterday" (correct) or "I saw him yesterday" (correct)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: since Monday, since last year, since childhood, since then
   - **Verb + prep**: has been since, have lived since, changed since
   - **Adjective + prep**: different since, improved since
   - **Noun + prep**: changes since, progress since
   
   **Root Analysis**: since (from Middle English "sithenes", "sins")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "sithenes" → Old English "siþþan" (afterward, from that time) → Proto-Germanic
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been waiting here since noon and nobody's shown up yet.
   - Since we moved to this neighborhood, we've made so many friends.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has undergone significant restructuring since its establishment in 1995.
   - Research findings have evolved considerably since the publication of the original study.

1. **Preposition**: from (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Starting at a particular point in past time; indicating the beginning point
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating the point of origin or departure
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating source, cause, or origin
   
   **Synonyms**: starting from, beginning at, as of (in temporal contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: to, until, toward
   
   **When to Use**: Use "from" to indicate the starting point of a time period, often paired with "to" or "until" for time ranges; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use alone for duration without an endpoint (use "since" instead). Common error: "I work here from 2015" (incomplete) vs. "I've worked here since 2015" (correct) or "I worked here from 2015 to 2020" (correct)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from the beginning, from that time, from the past, from early childhood
   - **Verb + prep**: date from, stem from, come from, originate from
   - **Adjective + prep**: different from (when comparing past and present)
   - **Noun + prep**: lessons from the past, echoes from the past
   
   **Root Analysis**: from (Old English "fram")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "fram" (forward, from, away) → Proto-Germanic "*fram" → Proto-Indo-European "*pro-mo"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I remember everything from my childhood—the good and the bad.
   - She's been different from the day we first met until now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legislative framework has evolved significantly from its original conception.
   - Data collected from the previous decade provides crucial insights.

1. **Preposition**: ago (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Going back in time by a specified amount from now; in the past by a specified duration
   
   **Synonyms**: back, in the past, previously (though not exact equivalents)
   
   **Antonyms**: from now, in the future, hence
   
   **When to Use**: Use "ago" after time expressions with past simple tense to indicate how far back something occurred (three days ago, years ago); always follows the time expression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use with present perfect tense (use "for" or "since" instead). Common error: "I have seen him three days ago" (incorrect) vs. "I saw him three days ago" (correct) or "I haven't seen him for three days" (correct). Never place "ago" before the time expression
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: years ago, months ago, a while ago, long ago, not long ago
   - **Verb + prep**: happened ago, occurred ago, was ago (Note: "ago" follows the noun phrase)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A (ago follows time nouns)
   - **Noun + prep**: [time period] + ago (two weeks ago, several years ago)
   
   **Root Analysis**: ago (from Middle English "ago", "agon" - gone by, past)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "ago(n)" (gone by, past) → Old English "āgān" (to pass away) → "ā-" (away) + "gān" (to go)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I bumped into Sarah a few weeks ago at the mall.
   - That happened ages ago—I can barely remember it now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulation was enacted two decades ago to address emerging concerns.
   - Initial observations were recorded approximately three months ago.

1. **Preposition**: before (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* At an earlier time than; prior to a specified moment or event
   - *(Spatial)* In front of; ahead of in position
   - *(Abstract)* In preference to; rather than
   
   **Synonyms**: prior to, earlier than, ahead of, previously to
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following, subsequently, later than
   
   **When to Use**: Use "before" to establish temporal sequence in both past and future contexts; works with all tenses; appropriate for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse with "ago" (which measures duration backward from now) or "since" (which indicates starting point continuing to present)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: before now, before that time, before the war, before long
   - **Verb + prep**: happened before, occurred before, come before, existed before
   - **Adjective + prep**: different before, better before
   - **Noun + prep**: the day before, the year before, time before
   
   **Root Analysis**: before (Old English "beforan")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "beforan" → "be-" (by) + "foran" (in front of, in the presence of) → Proto-Germanic "*bi-" + "*fura"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've heard this story before—can we talk about something else?
   - Everything was so much simpler before smartphones existed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before the implementation of these measures, compliance rates were significantly lower.
   - The institution maintained traditional practices before the comprehensive reform.

1. **Preposition**: after (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Following in time; later than a specified moment or event
   - *(Spatial)* Behind in position or order
   - *(Abstract)* In pursuit of; in imitation of
   
   **Synonyms**: following, subsequent to, later than, behind
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, ahead of, preceding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "after" to establish temporal sequence for events following other events; works with all tenses; can connect clauses or introduce prepositional phrases
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid ambiguity in complex sentences with multiple events; be clear about which event "after" references
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: after that, after the event, after years, shortly after
   - **Verb + prep**: came after, happened after, followed after, occurred after
   - **Adjective + prep**: different after, changed after
   - **Noun + prep**: the day after, the morning after, years after
   
   **Root Analysis**: after (Old English "æfter")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "æfter" (behind, following in time) → Proto-Germanic "*after" → Proto-Indo-European "*apo-tero" (more away)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After high school, I traveled around Europe for six months.
   - Things got weird after that conversation we had.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After the dissolution of the committee, responsibility transferred to the central authority.
   - The findings were published after extensive peer review and verification.

1. **Preposition**: throughout (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* During the entire period of time; from beginning to end
   - *(Spatial)* In every part of; all through a space or area
   
   **Synonyms**: during, all through, all over, everywhere in (spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: intermittently, sporadically, partially
   
   **When to Use**: Use "throughout" to emphasize continuity or completeness across time or space; implies no gaps or interruptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing intermittent or partial coverage; not appropriate for specific points in time
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: throughout history, throughout the years, throughout the past, throughout the decade
   - **Verb + prep**: remained throughout, continued throughout, persisted throughout
   - **Adjective + prep**: consistent throughout, evident throughout
   - **Noun + prep**: changes throughout, consistency throughout
   
   **Root Analysis**: through (Old English "þurh") + out (Old English "ūt")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English compound from "through" + "out" → Old English "þurhūt" (from beginning to end)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My grandparents stayed together throughout their entire lives—65 years of marriage!
   - Throughout my childhood, we moved around constantly because of Dad's job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Throughout the twentieth century, technological advancement accelerated exponentially.
   - The principle has remained consistent throughout the organization's operational history.

1. **Preposition**: during (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the course of; at some point in the period of
   
   **Synonyms**: in the course of, in, while, through
   
   **Antonyms**: outside of, beyond, before, after
   
   **When to Use**: Use "during" to indicate when something happens within a time period; can mean throughout the entire period or at some point within it
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use with time duration amounts (use "for" instead). Common error: "during three hours" (incorrect) vs. "for three hours" (correct) or "during the three-hour meeting" (correct)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: during the past, during that time, during the war, during the 1990s
   - **Verb + prep**: happened during, occurred during, changed during
   - **Adjective + prep**: different during, active during
   - **Noun + prep**: events during, changes during, developments during
   
   **Root Analysis**: during (from Old French "durant", present participle of "durer" - to last)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → Old French "durant" (lasting) → Latin "durare" (to harden, last, endure)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - During the summer of 2019, I worked at that café downtown.
   - I wasn't really paying attention during the meeting—my mind was elsewhere.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - During the previous fiscal year, revenues increased by twelve percent.
   - Significant policy changes were implemented during the transitional period.

1. **Preposition**: until (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Up to the point in time or the event mentioned; continuing to a specified time and then stopping
   
   **Synonyms**: till, up to, before, up until
   
   **Antonyms**: from, since, after
   
   **When to Use**: Use "until" to mark the endpoint of a situation or action that began earlier; indicates continuation up to a boundary
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse with "by" (deadline) which means "not later than." "Until" emphasizes continuation, "by" emphasizes completion before a deadline
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: until now, until then, until recently, until that point
   - **Verb + prep**: lasted until, continued until, remained until, persisted until
   - **Adjective + prep**: unchanged until, stable until
   - **Noun + prep**: period until, time until
   
   **Root Analysis**: until (from Old Norse "und" + till)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "until" → Old Norse "*und" (up to) + "till" → Proto-Germanic
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Until last year, I had never tried sushi—now I'm obsessed with it.
   - We stayed at the party until midnight and then headed home.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Until the intervention, outcomes remained consistently suboptimal.
   - The practice persisted until regulatory changes mandated alternative approaches.

1. **Preposition**: by (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Not later than; at or before a specified time (deadline)
   - *(Spatial)* Near to; beside
   - *(Abstract)* Through the agency or means of
   
   **Synonyms**: before, no later than, by the time of (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: after, beyond
   
   **When to Use**: Use "by" to indicate a deadline or the latest time something should happen; implies completion before or at that moment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse with "until" (continuation). "By Friday" means completed before Friday ends; "until Friday" means continuing through Friday
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by that time, by then, by the end of, by yesterday
   - **Verb + prep**: finished by, completed by, done by, happened by
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready by, available by
   - **Noun + prep**: deadline by, completion by
   
   **Root Analysis**: by (Old English "bī")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bī" (near, in, by, during, about) → Proto-Germanic "*bi" → Proto-Indo-European "*ambhi" (around)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - By the time I arrived, everyone had already left the party.
   - I thought you'd have finished that project by now!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - By the conclusion of the previous quarter, all objectives had been achieved.
   - The documentation must be submitted by the specified date to ensure compliance.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the duration of; for the length of time specified
   - *(Abstract)* Intended to belong to or be used by; in favor of; because of
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, during (though "during" emphasizes a period, "for" emphasizes duration length)
   
   **Antonyms**: against (abstract meaning only)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "for" with time duration amounts (for three years, for decades) with present perfect or past simple; indicates the length of time something lasted or has lasted
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use with specific time periods (use "during" instead). Common error: "for the 1990s" (incorrect) vs. "during the 1990s" (correct) or "for ten years in the 1990s" (correct)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for years, for decades, for a long time, for centuries
   - **Verb + prep**: lasted for, continued for, existed for, persisted for
   - **Adjective + prep**: stable for, consistent for
   - **Noun + prep**: period for (less common)
   
   **Root Analysis**: for (Old English "for")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" (before, in front of, instead of, for, because of) → Proto-Germanic "*fura" → Proto-Indo-European "*pr̥"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We've been friends for over twenty years now—can you believe it?
   - I lived in that apartment for five years before I finally moved out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution has maintained this policy for approximately three decades.
   - Research has been conducted in this field for several generations.

1. **Preposition**: prior to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Before; earlier than; in advance of (more formal than "before")
   
   **Synonyms**: before, earlier than, in advance of, preceding
   
   **Antonyms**: following, after, subsequent to, later than
   
   **When to Use**: Use "prior to" in formal contexts such as academic writing, legal documents, business communications; more formal register than "before"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "before" is more natural. Overuse can sound unnecessarily formal or stilted
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: prior to that, prior to the event, prior to 2020, prior to implementation
   - **Verb + prep**: occurred prior to, existed prior to, happened prior to
   - **Adjective + prep**: different prior to, uncommon prior to
   - **Noun + prep**: period prior to, years prior to, time prior to
   
   **Root Analysis**: prior (Latin "prior" - former, earlier) + to (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "prior" (former, previous, first) → Phrasal construction with "to" in English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used casually; "before" is preferred)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Prior to the implementation of the new system, efficiency metrics were substantially lower.
   - The regulation did not exist prior to 2015, resulting in inconsistent practices.

1. **Preposition**: following (type: simple/phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* After; coming after in time or as a result of (more formal than "after")
   - *(Abstract)* As a consequence of; resulting from
   
   **Synonyms**: after, subsequent to, in the wake of, as a result of
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, preceding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "following" in formal contexts, particularly when emphasizing sequence or causation; more common in written than spoken language
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May sound overly formal in casual contexts where "after" is more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: following that, following the event, following the war, following years of
   - **Verb + prep**: occurred following, changed following, emerged following
   - **Adjective + prep**: different following, improved following
   - **Noun + prep**: changes following, period following, years following
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow (Old English "folgian") + -ing (present participle suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "folgian" (to follow, pursue) → Proto-Germanic "*fulg-" → participle form used as preposition
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Less common; "after" is preferred in casual speech)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Following the economic recession, regulatory frameworks were substantially revised.
   - The organization restructured following a comprehensive external audit.

1. **Preposition**: back in (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* At a specified time in the past; returning mentally or referentially to an earlier period
   
   **Synonyms**: in, during (followed by past time reference)
   
   **Antonyms**: nowadays, currently, these days, in the future
   
   **When to Use**: Use "back in" to nostalgically or retrospectively reference specific past periods; creates conversational, reminiscent tone; common in storytelling
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic or technical writing (use "in" or "during" instead); not appropriate for recent past
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: back in the day, back in 2010, back in my childhood, back in those days
   - **Verb + prep**: happened back in, lived back in, worked back in
   - **Adjective + prep**: different back in
   - **Noun + prep**: memories back in, life back in
   
   **Root Analysis**: back (Old English "bæc") + in (Old English "in")
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal construction: "back" (returning to earlier point) + "in" (within time period)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Back in high school, I was really into skateboarding.
   - Back in the '90s, we didn't have smartphones—life was so different!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Typically avoided in formal writing; use "in" instead: "In 1995, the policy was enacted...")
