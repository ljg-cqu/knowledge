# Adjective: By

1. **Adjective**: surprised
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling or showing surprise because something unexpected has happened; affected by an unexpected event
   
   **Synonyms**: astonished, amazed, startled, shocked, taken aback
   
   **Antonyms**: unsurprised, expecting, anticipating, prepared
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing reactions to unexpected events; commonly paired with "by" to indicate the cause of surprise
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the event was expected or anticipated; don't confuse with "surprising" (which describes the cause, not the reaction)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - surprised by the news
   - surprised by the results
   - pleasantly surprised by
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (over, above) + pris (taken) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: French "surprendre" (to overtake) → Middle English "surprisen"; past participle used as adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was really surprised by how good the movie was.
   - She seemed surprised by his reaction.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers were surprised by the unexpected correlation in the data.
   - The committee was surprised by the volume of public feedback received.

2. **Adjective**: impressed
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling admiration or respect because of something good, skillful, or remarkable; favorably affected
   
   **Synonyms**: struck, affected, influenced, moved, inspired
   
   **Antonyms**: unimpressed, disappointed, underwhelmed, indifferent
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing admiration or positive reaction; typically followed by "by" or "with" to indicate the cause
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing negative reactions; don't use sarcastically without clear context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - impressed by the quality
   - impressed by someone's performance
   - deeply impressed by
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (in, upon) + press (to press) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "imprimere" (to press into, imprint) → French "impresser" → English; figurative sense of making a mark on mind/feelings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really impressed by your cooking skills!
   - They were impressed by the presentation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board was favorably impressed by the comprehensive strategic plan.
   - Evaluators were particularly impressed by the innovative methodology employed.

3. **Adjective**: influenced
   **Meaning**: 
   - Affected or changed by someone or something; having one's opinions, behavior, or development shaped by external factors
   
   **Synonyms**: affected, swayed, shaped, impacted, guided, directed
   
   **Antonyms**: unaffected, independent, uninfluenced, autonomous
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing the effect of external factors on decisions, behavior, or development; typically used with "by"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing direct causation (use "caused by" instead); don't use for physical effects
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - influenced by culture
   - heavily influenced by
   - influenced by external factors
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + flu (flow) + -ence + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "influere" (to flow in) → Medieval Latin "influentia" (astrological power); evolved from astrology to general causation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her style is really influenced by 90s fashion.
   - I was influenced by my teacher's advice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Policy decisions are inevitably influenced by economic considerations.
   - The research findings were not influenced by funding sources.

4. **Adjective**: affected
   **Meaning**: 
   - Influenced or changed by something; impacted emotionally, physically, or in other ways
   - *(Sense 2)* Artificial or pretentious in manner (different usage, not typically with "by")
   
   **Synonyms**: influenced, impacted, touched, changed, altered (for primary sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: unaffected, untouched, unchanged, unaltered
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing impact or influence, especially negative effects; commonly with "by" to indicate cause
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be aware of the secondary meaning (artificial/pretentious) which doesn't take "by"; avoid confusion between the two senses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - affected by the crisis
   - adversely affected by
   - negatively affected by
   
   **Root Analysis**: af- (to, toward) + fect (do, make) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "afficere" (to influence, attack) → French "affecter" → English; developed multiple senses over time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone was affected by the news.
   - The whole town was affected by the storm.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regional economies were significantly affected by trade policy changes.
   - Vulnerable populations are disproportionately affected by environmental degradation.

5. **Adjective**: motivated
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having a reason or incentive to do something; driven by a particular cause, emotion, or goal; showing enthusiasm and determination
   
   **Synonyms**: driven, inspired, stimulated, encouraged, prompted, spurred
   
   **Antonyms**: unmotivated, discouraged, disheartened, apathetic, indifferent
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing what drives behavior or action; commonly followed by "by" to indicate the motivating factor
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing involuntary actions; don't confuse with "motivating" (which describes the cause, not the person)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - motivated by ambition
   - highly motivated by
   - motivated by desire
   
   **Root Analysis**: motiv(e) (reason for action) + -ate + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "motivus" (moving, causing motion) → French "motiver" → English; evolved from physical movement to psychological drive
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's really motivated by the chance to travel.
   - I'm motivated by wanting to help people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were motivated by intrinsic rather than extrinsic rewards.
   - The initiative is motivated by concerns regarding sustainability.

6. **Adjective**: inspired
   **Meaning**: 
   - Filled with creative impulse or emotional stimulation; aroused to creative or intellectual activity; influenced positively
   
   **Synonyms**: stimulated, motivated, moved, encouraged, uplifted, animated
   
   **Antonyms**: uninspired, discouraged, deflated, demotivated
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing positive creative or emotional stimulation; typically with "by" to indicate the source of inspiration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for negative influences (use "influenced" instead); don't use for ordinary or uninspiring causes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inspired by nature
   - inspired by great artists
   - deeply inspired by
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + spir (breathe) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "inspirare" (to breathe into, inspire) → Old French "inspirer"; originally divine or supernatural influence, now general creativity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was really inspired by her story.
   - He's inspired by classic rock music.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The architectural design was inspired by traditional vernacular forms.
   - Contemporary research has been inspired by seminal work in the 1970s.

7. **Adjective**: confused
   **Meaning**: 
   - Unable to think clearly; bewildered; mixed up in one's mind; unable to understand something
   
   **Synonyms**: bewildered, perplexed, puzzled, baffled, disoriented, muddled
   
   **Antonyms**: clear, certain, sure, confident, understanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing mental uncertainty or inability to understand; commonly with "by" to indicate the cause of confusion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing deliberate deception (use "misled"); don't confuse with "confusing" (describing the cause)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - confused by the instructions
   - confused by contradictory information
   - totally confused by
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + fus (pour) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "confundere" (to pour together, mix up) → Anglo-French → English; literal mixing evolved to mental mixing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really confused by what you just said.
   - She looked confused by all the options.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Respondents were confused by ambiguous survey questions.
   - Stakeholders were confused by contradictory statements from authorities.

8. **Adjective**: puzzled
   **Meaning**: 
   - Unable to understand something; confused because something is difficult to understand or solve; perplexed
   
   **Synonyms**: perplexed, bewildered, baffled, mystified, confounded, stumped
   
   **Antonyms**: clear, enlightened, understanding, certain
   
   **When to Use**: Use when something is mysterious or difficult to understand; typically with "by" to indicate the source of puzzlement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for simple confusion (use "confused"); implies more active mental engagement with a problem
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - puzzled by the results
   - puzzled by someone's behavior
   - deeply puzzled by
   
   **Root Analysis**: puzzle (noun/verb) + -ed (past participle); "puzzle" origin uncertain, possibly from "pose" (perplex)
   
   **Etymology**: Uncertain origin, possibly from "oppose" or "pose" (perplex); emerged in late 16th century meaning "to perplex"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm puzzled by why he did that.
   - She was puzzled by the weird email.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers remain puzzled by the anomalous findings.
   - Analysts were puzzled by the unexpected market reaction.

9. **Adjective**: fascinated
   **Meaning**: 
   - Intensely interested in or attracted to something; captivated; having one's attention completely held
   
   **Synonyms**: captivated, enthralled, mesmerized, absorbed, engrossed, intrigued
   
   **Antonyms**: bored, uninterested, indifferent, apathetic
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing strong interest or attraction; commonly with "by" or "with" to indicate the object of fascination
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for mild interest (use "interested"); implies more intense engagement
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fascinated by science
   - fascinated by the story
   - utterly fascinated by
   
   **Root Analysis**: fascin(ate) (from Latin "fascinum" - spell, witchcraft) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "fascinare" (to bewitch, enchant) → English; originally magical spell-binding, now intense interest
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm fascinated by how this works.
   - She's always been fascinated by ancient history.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Scholars have long been fascinated by this historical anomaly.
   - The scientific community was fascinated by the unprecedented observations.

10. **Adjective**: accompanied
    **Meaning**: 
    - Going together with someone or something; occurring together with; attended or escorted
    
    **Synonyms**: attended, escorted, joined, together with, along with
    
    **Antonyms**: alone, unaccompanied, solo, solitary, unattended
    
    **When to Use**: Use when describing someone or something that goes along with another; typically followed by "by" to indicate the companion
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for inanimate objects without personification; avoid redundancy with explicit mention of companions
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - accompanied by music
    - accompanied by symptoms
    - accompanied by a colleague
    
    **Root Analysis**: ac- (to, toward) + compan(y) (from Latin "panis" - bread, companion) + -ed (past participle)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "companio" (one who breaks bread with another) → Old French "acompaigner" → English
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - She came to the party accompanied by her boyfriend.
    - The fever was accompanied by chills.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The delegation was accompanied by security personnel.
    - Economic growth was accompanied by rising inequality.

11. **Adjective**: characterized
    **Meaning**: 
    - Described by particular qualities or features; distinguished or marked by specific traits; having typical qualities
    
    **Synonyms**: distinguished, marked, defined, typified, identified
    
    **Antonyms**: uncharacteristic, atypical, undefined
    
    **When to Use**: Use when describing defining features or typical qualities; commonly followed by "by" to indicate the characteristic features
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when features are not essential or defining; don't use for superficial or temporary qualities
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - characterized by complexity
    - characterized by features
    - uniquely characterized by
    
    **Root Analysis**: character (from Greek "kharakter" - engraved mark) + -ize + -ed (past participle)
    
    **Etymology**: Greek "kharakter" (stamping tool, distinctive mark) → Latin "character" → French "caractériser" → English
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - The region is characterized by hot summers.
    - His work is characterized by attention to detail.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The syndrome is characterized by specific symptoms.
    - The period was characterized by rapid technological advancement.

12. **Adjective**: surrounded
    **Meaning**: 
    - Enclosed or encircled on all sides; having things or people all around
    
    **Synonyms**: encircled, enclosed, encompassed, ringed, hemmed in
    
    **Antonyms**: isolated, alone, separate, exposed
    
    **When to Use**: Use when describing spatial positioning with things on all sides; typically followed by "by" to indicate what is around
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when items are only on one side or partially around; don't use metaphorically without clear context
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - surrounded by people
    - surrounded by nature
    - completely surrounded by
    
    **Root Analysis**: sur- (over, beyond) + round + -ed (past participle)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "super" + "undare" (to rise in waves) → Old French "suronder" → English "surround"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - The house is surrounded by trees.
   - I felt surrounded by friends.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The facility is surrounded by high-security fencing.
    - The proposal was surrounded by controversy.

13. **Adjective**: dominated
    **Meaning**: 
    - Controlled, ruled, or strongly influenced by someone or something; being in a position of lesser power or prominence
    
    **Synonyms**: controlled, ruled, governed, overshadowed, overpowered
    
    **Antonyms**: independent, autonomous, free, equal, uncontrolled
    
    **When to Use**: Use when describing control or overwhelming influence; typically with "by" to indicate the dominating force
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing equal partnerships or balanced influences; be sensitive to power dynamics implications
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - dominated by large corporations
    - dominated by a single issue
    - heavily dominated by
    
    **Root Analysis**: domin(ate) (from Latin "dominus" - lord, master) + -ed (past participle)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "dominari" (to rule, dominate) from "dominus" (lord, master) → English
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - The conversation was dominated by him talking.
    - The team is dominated by experienced players.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The market is dominated by a few major competitors.
    - The discourse was dominated by economic considerations.

14. **Adjective**: overwhelmed
    **Meaning**: 
    - Completely overcome emotionally or mentally; buried or drowned beneath a huge mass; given too much to deal with
    
    **Synonyms**: overcome, inundated, swamped, overburdened, overpowered, engulfed
    
    **Antonyms**: underwhelmed, unaffected, composed, calm, in control
    
    **When to Use**: Use when describing being unable to cope with intensity or quantity; typically with "by" to indicate the overwhelming factor
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for manageable challenges; implies loss of control or coping ability
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - overwhelmed by emotion
    - overwhelmed by work
    - completely overwhelmed by
    
    **Root Analysis**: over- (excessively) + whelm (to engulf) + -ed (past participle)
    
    **Etymology**: Middle English "whelmen" (to overturn, cover) → "overwhelm" (cover completely); originally physical covering, now primarily emotional
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - I'm overwhelmed by all these choices!
    - She was overwhelmed by the support she received.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Healthcare systems were overwhelmed by the surge in cases.
    - Respondents reported feeling overwhelmed by information overload.

15. **Adjective**: unaffected
    **Meaning**: 
    - Not influenced or changed by something; not emotionally moved; genuine and natural in manner
    
    **Synonyms**: unchanged, uninfluenced, untouched, unmoved, impervious (for primary sense); natural, genuine, sincere (for secondary sense)
    
    **Antonyms**: affected, influenced, changed, moved, touched
    
    **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing lack of impact or influence; typically followed by "by" to indicate what failed to cause effect
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Be aware of positive connotation (genuine/natural) vs. neutral/negative connotation (lacking empathy); context determines meaning
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - unaffected by changes
    - remained unaffected by
    - relatively unaffected by
    
    **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + affected (influenced, impacted)
    
    **Etymology**: From "affected" with negative prefix "un-"; both senses (not influenced / genuine) developed in parallel
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - He seemed unaffected by the criticism.
    - The plants were unaffected by the cold.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Control groups remained unaffected by the intervention.
    - Traditional industries were largely unaffected by digital disruption.
