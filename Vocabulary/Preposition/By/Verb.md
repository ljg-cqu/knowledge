# Verb: By

1. **Verb**: pass
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move past something or someone; to go by; to be successful in a test or examination; to transfer or hand something; to approve legislation; to elapse (of time)
   
   **Synonyms**: go by, move past, overtake (for passing); succeed in, clear (for exams); approve, ratify (for legislation); elapse, go by (for time)
   
   **Antonyms**: fail (for exams); stop, halt, remain (for movement); reject, veto (for legislation)
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" for physical movement past something; also for time passing, opportunities missed, or examinations succeeded
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "pass" (go by) with "past" (preposition/adjective); distinguish various meanings by context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pass by the window
   - time passes by
   - pass by an opportunity
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "passus" (step, pace) through Old French "passer"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "passus" (step, pace) → Late Latin "passare" (to step, walk) → Old French "passer" → English "pass"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I saw you pass by earlier but couldn't say hi.
   - Don't let this chance pass you by!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Vehicles must slow when passing by school zones.
   - Opportunities for intervention should not be allowed to pass by unnoticed.

2. **Verb**: go
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move or travel; to proceed; to pass or elapse; to function or operate; to become; to extend or lead
   
   **Synonyms**: move, travel, proceed, advance, journey, pass
   
   **Antonyms**: come, arrive, stop, stay, remain, halt
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" for passing close to something, visiting briefly, or allowing time/events to pass; extremely versatile verb
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when specific movement verbs would be more precise; avoid overuse as generic verb
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go by the store
   - go by the name
   - time goes by
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "gān" (to go, walk, travel)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gān" → Middle English "gon" → Modern English "go"; one of the most irregular verbs in English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's go by the coffee shop on the way home.
   - Years went by before I saw her again.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal must go by established procedures.
   - As time goes by, technological capabilities continue to advance.

3. **Verb**: stand
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be in an upright position on the feet; to be situated; to remain valid or unchanged; to tolerate or endure; to support or defend
   
   **Synonyms**: be upright, be positioned, be located (for position); support, back, defend (for support sense); remain, endure, persist (for validity)
   
   **Antonyms**: sit, lie (for position); oppose, abandon (for support); change, invalidate (for validity)
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" to mean support, remain loyal, or remain near; phrasal verb "stand by" has multiple senses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse different senses of "stand by" (support vs. wait vs. be near); clarify meaning from context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stand by someone
   - stand by a decision
   - stand by and watch
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "standan" (to stand, be in a place)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "standan" → Middle English "standen" → Modern English "stand"; cognate with German "stehen," Latin "stare"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll stand by you no matter what.
   - Don't just stand by—help me!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization stands by its original assessment.
   - Management must stand by employees during organizational transitions.

4. **Verb**: abide
   **Meaning**: 
   - To accept or follow a decision or rule; to remain or continue; to tolerate or endure (archaic in this sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: comply with, follow, observe, adhere to, conform to (for rules); remain, stay, dwell (for continue sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: violate, break, disobey, ignore, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" to mean comply with or accept; formal or literary tone; common in legal and ethical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "follow" or "stick to"); archaic sense (tolerate) mostly obsolete except in negative
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - abide by the rules
   - abide by a decision
   - abide by an agreement
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "ābīdan" (await, remain) from "ā-" (intensive) + "bīdan" (wait, stay)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "ābīdan" (to wait, remain) → Middle English "abiden" → Modern English "abide"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We all have to abide by the same rules.
   - I'll abide by whatever you decide.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants agreed to abide by the terms and conditions of the study.
   - All parties must abide by the arbitrator's decision.

5. **Verb**: swear
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make a solemn promise or oath; to use offensive language; to state or promise emphatically; to take an oath in court
   
   **Synonyms**: vow, pledge, promise, declare (for oath); curse, blaspheme (for offensive language)
   
   **Antonyms**: break a promise, renege, retract
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate what one swears on or by (swear by God, swear by something as reliable); also informal meaning of strongly recommending
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse legal oath with casual recommendation; be aware of context determining meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - swear by God
   - swear by something (recommend highly)
   - swear by a method
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "swerian" (to swear, take an oath)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "swerian" → Middle English "sweren" → Modern English "swear"; cognate with German "schwören"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I swear by this brand of coffee—it's the best!
   - My grandmother swore by home remedies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Witnesses must swear by oath to tell the truth.
   - Many practitioners swear by the efficacy of this traditional approach.

6. **Verb**: judge
   **Meaning**: 
   - To form an opinion or conclusion about; to make a decision in a court of law; to assess or evaluate; to criticize
   
   **Synonyms**: evaluate, assess, appraise, determine, decide, conclude, deem
   
   **Antonyms**: accept without judgment, defer judgment, withhold judgment
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the criteria or standard used for judging (judge by appearance, judge by results)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing formal legal judgments without authority; don't confuse judging (evaluating) with being judgmental (critical)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - judge by appearance
   - judge by results
   - judge by standards
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "iudicare" (to judge, pass judgment) from "iudex" (judge)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ius" (law) + "dicere" (to say) → "iudex" (judge) → "iudicare" → Old French "jugier" → English "judge"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't judge a book by its cover!
   - You can't judge people by first impressions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Performance should be judged by objective metrics rather than subjective impressions.
   - Quality is judged by adherence to established standards and specifications.

7. **Verb**: measure
   **Meaning**: 
   - To ascertain the size, amount, or degree of something using instruments or comparisons; to assess the importance or value of something
   
   **Synonyms**: gauge, assess, quantify, calculate, evaluate, determine
   
   **Antonyms**: estimate, guess, approximate (when used without instruments)
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the unit or method of measurement (measure by weight, measure by inches, measure by results)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse precise measurement with estimation; be clear about units and methods
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - measure by weight
   - measure by results
   - measure by standards
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "mensura" (a measuring, measure) from "metiri" (to measure)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "metiri" (to measure) → "mensura" → Old French "mesure" → English "measure"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We measure ingredients by cups, not grams.
   - Success isn't measured by money alone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Effectiveness was measured by standardized assessment instruments.
   - Variables were measured by validated psychometric scales.

8. **Verb**: know
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be aware of through observation, inquiry, or information; to have developed a relationship with; to recognize; to be absolutely certain
   
   **Synonyms**: be aware of, recognize, understand, comprehend, perceive, realize
   
   **Antonyms**: be ignorant of, be unaware of, not know, misunderstand
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the characteristic or feature by which something is recognized (know by sight, know by name, know by reputation)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "know" (factual knowledge) with "believe" (opinion); distinguish knowing about from knowing personally
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - know by sight
   - know by name
   - know by reputation
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "cnāwan" (to know, recognize)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "cnāwan" → Middle English "knowen" → Modern English "know"; cognate with Latin "cognoscere," Greek "gignōskō"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I know her by sight but we've never talked.
   - You can tell it's him by his laugh.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The species is readily known by its distinctive markings.
   - Participants were known to researchers by identification codes only.

9. **Verb**: recognize
   **Meaning**: 
   - To identify someone or something from previous encounters or knowledge; to acknowledge the existence, validity, or legality of something; to show appreciation for
   
   **Synonyms**: identify, know, distinguish, acknowledge, accept, admit
   
   **Antonyms**: fail to recognize, overlook, ignore, deny, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the characteristic used for recognition (recognize by voice, recognize by features)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse recognizing (identifying) with appreciating (valuing); be clear about different senses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - recognize by voice
   - recognize by appearance
   - recognize by characteristics
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "recognoscere" (to know again) from "re-" (again) + "cognoscere" (to know)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "re-" (again) + "cognoscere" (to know) → "recognoscere" → Old French "recogniss-" → English "recognize"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I recognized him by his voice immediately.
   - You can recognize the plant by its leaves.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Symptoms are recognized by trained clinicians through systematic assessment.
   - The syndrome is recognized by a constellation of distinctive features.

10. **Verb**: increase
    **Meaning**: 
    - To become or make greater in size, amount, intensity, or degree; to multiply; to grow
    
    **Synonyms**: grow, expand, rise, escalate, multiply, augment, enlarge
    
    **Antonyms**: decrease, reduce, diminish, decline, shrink, lessen
    
    **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the amount of increase (increase by 10%, increase by $5); commonly paired with specific numerical values
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "increase by" (adding an amount) with "increase to" (reaching a level); be precise about whether stating change or final value
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - increase by [amount]
    - increase by [percentage]
    - increase by degrees
    
    **Root Analysis**: From Latin "increscere" from "in-" (in) + "crescere" (to grow)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "in-" + "crescere" (to grow) → "increscere" → Old French "encreiss-" → English "increase"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Prices increased by 20% last year.
    - My salary increased by $5,000.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Participation rates increased by 15 percentage points following the intervention.
    - Revenue increased by $2.3 million compared to the previous fiscal year.

11. **Verb**: decrease
    **Meaning**: 
    - To become or make smaller or fewer in size, amount, intensity, or degree; to reduce; to decline
    
    **Synonyms**: reduce, diminish, lessen, decline, drop, fall, shrink
    
    **Antonyms**: increase, grow, expand, rise, escalate, augment
    
    **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the amount of decrease (decrease by 15%, decrease by half); parallel structure to "increase by"
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "decrease by" (amount of reduction) with "decrease to" (final level); be clear about direction of change
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - decrease by [amount]
    - decrease by [percentage]
    - decrease gradually by
    
    **Root Analysis**: From Latin "decrescere" from "de-" (down) + "crescere" (to grow)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "de-" (down, away) + "crescere" (to grow) → "decrescere" → Old French "decreiss-" → English "decrease"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Sales decreased by 30% this quarter.
    - The temperature decreased by ten degrees.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Error rates decreased by 25% following training implementation.
    - Pollution levels decreased by significant margins after regulatory changes.

12. **Verb**: multiply
    **Meaning**: 
    - To increase in number or quantity; to perform mathematical multiplication; to reproduce or breed
    
    **Synonyms**: increase, proliferate, reproduce, expand, grow (for increase); calculate, compute (for mathematics)
    
    **Antonyms**: divide (for mathematics); decrease, diminish, reduce (for quantity)
    
    **When to Use**: Use with "by" in mathematics to indicate the multiplier (multiply by 2, multiply by 10); also for general increase
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse multiplication (×) with addition (+); be clear about mathematical vs. general usage
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - multiply by [number]
    - multiply by a factor of
    - multiply rapidly
    
    **Root Analysis**: From Latin "multiplicare" from "multi-" (many) + "plicare" (to fold)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "multi-" (many) + "plicare" (to fold) → "multiplicare" → Old French "multiplier" → English "multiply"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Just multiply the length by the width.
    - The bacteria multiply quickly.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - To calculate area, multiply the base measurement by the height.
    - Cell populations multiply by exponential growth under optimal conditions.

13. **Verb**: divide
    **Meaning**: 
    - To separate into parts; to perform mathematical division; to cause disagreement; to share or distribute
    
    **Synonyms**: separate, split, partition, segment (for separating); share, distribute, allocate (for sharing); calculate (for mathematics)
    
    **Antonyms**: unite, combine, join, multiply (for mathematics)
    
    **When to Use**: Use with "by" in mathematics to indicate the divisor (divide by 2, divide by 10); also for separating or distributing
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse division (÷) with subtraction (-); distinguish mathematical from general usage
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - divide by [number]
    - divide by zero (error)
    - divide evenly by
    
    **Root Analysis**: From Latin "dividere" from "di-" (apart) + "videre" (to separate)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "dis-" (apart) + "videre" (to separate) → "dividere" → Old French "divider" → English "divide"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Divide the pizza by 8 to get the slices.
    - Let's divide the work evenly.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - To calculate per capita values, divide total amounts by population size.
    - The sample was divided by stratification criteria into distinct subgroups.

14. **Verb**: win
    **Meaning**: 
    - To be successful in a contest, competition, or battle; to gain or achieve something through effort; to persuade someone
    
    **Synonyms**: succeed, triumph, prevail, conquer, achieve, gain
    
    **Antonyms**: lose, fail, be defeated, surrender
    
    **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the margin of victory (win by 2 points, win by a landslide); shows extent of winning
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "win by" (margin) with "win with" (means); be specific about margin vs. method
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - win by a narrow margin
    - win by a landslide
    - win by [number] points
    
    **Root Analysis**: From Old English "winnan" (to struggle, fight, labor)
    
    **Etymology**: Old English "winnan" (to labor, strive, fight) → Middle English "winnen" → Modern English "win"; meaning shifted from "struggle" to "succeed"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - They won by just one point!
    - She won by a huge margin.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The proposition was approved, winning by a two-thirds majority.
    - The incumbent won by a narrow margin of 2.3 percentage points.

15. **Verb**: reduce
    **Meaning**: 
    - To make smaller or less in amount, degree, or size; to bring to a simpler or more basic form; to lower in rank or status
    
    **Synonyms**: decrease, diminish, lessen, lower, cut, minimize, shrink
    
    **Antonyms**: increase, enlarge, expand, augment, raise, maximize
    
    **When to Use**: Use with "by" to indicate the amount of reduction (reduce by 50%, reduce by half); emphasizes extent of reduction
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "reduce by" (amount of decrease) with "reduce to" (final state); be clear about change vs. result
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - reduce by [percentage]
    - reduce by half
    - reduce by [amount]
    
    **Root Analysis**: From Latin "reducere" from "re-" (back) + "ducere" (to lead)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "re-" (back) + "ducere" (to lead) → "reducere" → Old French "reduire" → English "reduce"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - They reduced the price by 25%.
    - I need to reduce my expenses by at least half.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Costs were reduced by 18% through process optimization.
    - Error rates were significantly reduced by implementing quality control measures.
