# Multi-Word-Expressions: By

1. **Expression**: by and large (type: idiomatic phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Generally speaking; on the whole; for the most part; considering everything
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase, components not separable)
   
   **Synonyms**: generally, overall, on the whole, all in all, for the most part, in general, mostly
   
   **Antonyms**: specifically, particularly, in detail, precisely, in every respect
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making general statements or summaries while acknowledging minor exceptions; common in evaluations and concluding remarks; appropriate in both casual and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when precision is required or in technical/legal contexts where exactness matters; somewhat informal for academic writing; don't use when no exceptions exist
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Standard: By and large, [general statement]
   - End position: [Statement], by and large
   - No significant variations due to fixedness
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Nautical origin—sailing "by" the wind (close-hauled) and sailing "large" (with the wind from behind); combined meant "in all conditions/directions," evolved to mean "generally"
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (Old English "bī") + "and" + "large" (Old French "large"); nautical terminology became general idiom
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - By and large, I think the party was a success.
   - The new system works well, by and large.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - By and large, the implementation has met stakeholder expectations.
   - The policy framework has, by and large, proven effective in achieving objectives.

2. **Expression**: by heart (type: collocation/idiomatic phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - From memory; memorized completely; learned so well that it can be recalled perfectly without reference
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed collocation, always "by heart," not "by memory of heart" or variations)
   
   **Synonyms**: from memory, by rote, by memory, off by heart (British), memorized, word for word
   
   **Antonyms**: with notes, with reference to, by reading, from text
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing something learned through complete memorization; common with poems, songs, speeches, or texts that must be recited
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "by rote" (implies mechanical memorization without understanding); avoid when conceptual understanding rather than memorization is emphasized
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Know by heart
   - Learn by heart
   - Recite by heart
   - Off by heart (British variant)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Heart" metaphorically represents the seat of memory and emotion in English; "by heart" = stored in the heart/memory; medieval belief that heart was memory center
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" + "heart" (Old English "heorte"); metaphorical use of heart as memory center
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I know that song by heart—I've heard it a million times.
   - She learned all her lines by heart in just two days.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students were required to memorize the poem by heart for the examination.
   - The speaker delivered the entire address by heart without consulting notes.

3. **Expression**: by hand (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Using the hands rather than a machine; manually; in handwriting rather than typed; through personal delivery
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed collocation; always "by hand," not "by hands" or "with hand")
   
   **Synonyms**: manually, by manual means, handwritten (for writing), hand-delivered (for delivery), hand-crafted
   
   **Antonyms**: by machine, mechanically, automatically, electronically, typed, machine-made
   
   **When to Use**: Use when distinguishing manual processes from automated ones, handwritten from typed, or personal delivery from postal service; emphasizes human agency
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the manual/mechanical distinction is irrelevant or obvious from context; redundant in contexts where only manual methods are possible
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Made by hand
   - Written by hand
   - Delivered by hand
   - Crafted by hand
   - Related: handmade, handwritten, hand-delivered (compound adjectives)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Direct and natural partnership; "by hand" emphasizes the instrument/means of action; semantic compatibility between preposition of means ("by") and instrument ("hand")
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (preposition of means) + "hand" (Old English "hand"); simple but fixed collocation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I made this sweater by hand—it took me weeks!
   - Can you deliver this letter by hand? It's really urgent.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All garments are crafted by hand using traditional artisanal techniques.
   - Confidential documents must be delivered by hand to authorized personnel only.

4. **Expression**: by chance (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Accidentally; unintentionally; randomly; through luck or fortune rather than planning
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed collocation; always "by chance," not "by a chance" or "by the chance")
   
   **Synonyms**: accidentally, by accident, fortuitously, randomly, coincidentally, as luck would have it
   
   **Antonyms**: by design, intentionally, deliberately, on purpose, by plan, purposefully
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing unplanned or random occurrences; emphasizes lack of intention or planning; common in narratives and explanations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing deliberate actions; don't confuse with "by any chance" (polite inquiry); inappropriate when causation or planning should be emphasized
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Happened by chance
   - Met by chance
   - Discovered by chance
   - Found by chance
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "By" indicates means/manner; "chance" indicates randomness; natural partnership expressing random occurrence; high frequency of co-occurrence; conventional usage
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (means) + "chance" (from Latin "cadentia" - falling, via Old French); literally "by the fall/happening"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I met her by chance at the grocery store.
   - We discovered this restaurant by chance and it's amazing!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The correlation was discovered by chance during preliminary data analysis.
   - Participants were selected by chance through random sampling procedures.

5. **Expression**: by accident (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Unintentionally; without meaning to; not on purpose; through mishap or chance
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed collocation; always "by accident," not "by an accident")
   
   **Synonyms**: accidentally, unintentionally, inadvertently, by mistake, not on purpose, unwittingly
   
   **Antonyms**: on purpose, intentionally, deliberately, by design, purposefully, knowingly
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing lack of intention, especially for mishaps or errors; clarifies that action was unintended; common in explanations and apologies
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing deliberate actions; don't use to minimize responsibility for serious harm (can sound dismissive); not appropriate when "accidentally" as adverb is clearer
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Happened by accident
   - Did by accident
   - Broke by accident
   - Found by accident
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "By" indicates means/manner; "accident" indicates unintended event; strong conventional pairing; extremely high frequency of co-occurrence in English
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (means) + "accident" (from Latin "accidens" - happening); literally "by happening/falling out"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I deleted the file by accident—can I get it back?
   - She found out about the surprise party by accident.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data was overwritten by accident due to a system error.
   - The discovery was made by accident during routine laboratory procedures.

6. **Expression**: by mistake (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In error; incorrectly; through misunderstanding or incorrect judgment; not intentionally
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed collocation; always "by mistake," not "by a mistake")
   
   **Synonyms**: in error, incorrectly, erroneously, accidentally, by accident, mistakenly, by misunderstanding
   
   **Antonyms**: on purpose, intentionally, deliberately, correctly, accurately
   
   **When to Use**: Use when explaining errors or unintended actions; emphasizes cognitive error rather than pure chance; common in explanations and corrections
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when action was random rather than based on incorrect understanding; don't confuse with "by accident" (pure chance vs. error in judgment)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Sent by mistake
   - Called by mistake
   - Took by mistake
   - Said by mistake
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "By" indicates means/manner; "mistake" indicates error; natural partnership expressing erroneous action; conventional usage; high frequency pairing
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (means) + "mistake" (from Old Norse "mistaka" - take wrongly)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I sent the email to the wrong person by mistake.
   - Sorry, I called you by mistake—I meant to call Sarah.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The invoice was charged to the incorrect account by mistake.
   - The document was filed under the wrong category by mistake.

7. **Expression**: by far (type: collocation/intensifier)
   **Meaning**: 
   - By a large margin; to a great degree; considerably; without question the most
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed collocation; always "by far," not "by fars" or "with far")
   
   **Synonyms**: easily, clearly, definitely, undoubtedly, without doubt, considerably, significantly, by a long shot
   
   **Antonyms**: barely, marginally, slightly, narrowly, just barely
   
   **When to Use**: Use with comparatives and superlatives to emphasize large degree of difference; strengthens comparison or superlative statements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid with absolute adjectives; don't use redundantly with other intensifiers; inappropriate when downplaying differences is preferred
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - By far the best
   - By far the most
   - Better by far
   - Position varies: [Superlative] by far / By far [superlative]
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "By" indicates margin/degree; "far" indicates distance/extent; combined creates emphatic degree marker; conventional intensifier pairing
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (margin indicator) + "far" (Old English "feor" - distant, remote)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is by far the best restaurant in town.
   - She's the smartest person in our class by far.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed methodology is by far the most rigorous approach available.
   - This intervention proved by far the most effective in reducing error rates.

8. **Expression**: by all means (type: idiomatic phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Certainly; definitely; of course; feel free to; using every possible method
   - Literal: Through every available method
   - Figurative/Idiomatic: Emphatic permission or agreement
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase; must include "all")
   
   **Synonyms**: certainly, definitely, of course, absolutely, please do, feel free to, without question
   
   **Antonyms**: by no means, not at all, under no circumstances, absolutely not, definitely not
   
   **When to Use**: Use to give emphatic permission or express strong agreement; can mean "using all available methods"; appropriate in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when permission is reluctant or conditional; can sound overly formal in very casual contexts; don't use sarcastically without clear signals
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - By all means, [imperative]
   - Should by all means
   - Must by all means
   - Original literal sense: achieve by all means (all methods)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originally literal "through all available methods"; evolved to emphatic permission/agreement (18th century); "all" emphasizes completeness; "means" (methods) metaphorically extends to permission
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (through) + "all" + "means" (methods, from Latin "medianus")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Can I borrow your car?" "By all means, the keys are on the table."
   - If you want to leave early, by all means do so.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Stakeholders should by all means participate in the consultation process.
   - By all means, we must ensure full compliance with regulatory standards.

9. **Expression**: by no means (type: idiomatic phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not at all; certainly not; in no way; definitely not; under no circumstances
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase; must include "no")
   
   **Synonyms**: not at all, in no way, certainly not, definitely not, under no circumstances, on no account
   
   **Antonyms**: by all means, certainly, definitely, absolutely, without doubt
   
   **When to Use**: Use for emphatic negation or rejection; common in formal writing to strongly deny; often corrects assumptions or misunderstandings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "not at all"); can sound overly formal or emphatic in informal contexts; inappropriate for gentle disagreement
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - By no means [adjective]
   - Is by no means
   - By no means should [subject] [verb] (with inversion)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Logical opposite of "by all means"; "no" negates all methods/possibilities; emphatic negative construction; established formulaic negation
   
   **Root Analysis**: "by" (through) + "no" (negation) + "means" (methods); literally "through no methods whatsoever"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is by no means an easy task.
   - I'm by no means an expert, but I can try to help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results are by no means conclusive and require further investigation.
   - By no means should this precedent be interpreted as establishing a general principle.

10. **Expression**: by the way (type: idiomatic discourse marker)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Incidentally; used to introduce information that is related but not central; parenthetically
    - Literal: Beside the path
    - Figurative/Idiomatic: Discourse marker for tangential information
    - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase; always includes "the")
    
    **Synonyms**: incidentally, as an aside, parenthetically, speaking of which, while we're on the subject
    
    **Antonyms**: most importantly, primarily, centrally, as the main point, chiefly
    
    **When to Use**: Use to introduce additional or tangential information; very common in conversation; smooths topic shifts; adds afterthoughts
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in formal academic writing (use "incidentally"); don't use for critical information (signals unimportance); overuse makes discourse disorganized
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - By the way, [new info]
    - [Statement], by the way, [addition]
    - Oh, by the way...
    - Abbreviated in writing: BTW (very informal)
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For idioms*: Originally literal "beside the road/path"; metaphorically "aside from the main topic/route"; common since 17th century; path metaphor for discourse topic is conventional
    
    **Root Analysis**: "by" (beside) + "the" + "way" (Old English "weg" - path, road)
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - By the way, did you remember to call Mom?
    - I finished the report. By the way, the meeting moved to 3 PM.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The analysis is complete. By the way, preliminary results suggest significant findings.
    - All concerns have been addressed. By the way, stakeholder feedback has been positive.

11. **Expression**: by virtue of (type: phrasal preposition)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Because of; as a result of; on the basis of; by reason of possessing a particular quality
    - *Fixedness*: strong (phrasal preposition; fixed components)
    
    **Synonyms**: because of, due to, owing to, on account of, as a result of, by reason of, on the strength of
    
    **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding, without regard to
    
    **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to indicate causation based on inherent qualities, position, or authority; emphasizes legitimacy or automatic nature
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "because of"); can sound pretentious informally; not appropriate for weak relationships
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - By virtue of [status/quality]
    - By virtue of the fact that
    - Minimal variation due to fixedness
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "Virtue" (excellence, power) from Latin "virtus"; "by virtue of" means "by the power/authority of"; formal prepositional phrase; legal/academic register
    
    **Root Analysis**: "by" (through) + "virtue" (Latin "virtus" - excellence, power) + "of"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - She gets free tickets by virtue of being an employee.
    - He's in charge by virtue of owning the company.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Members are entitled to voting rights by virtue of their shareholder status.
    - Authority is exercised by virtue of statutory provisions.

12. **Expression**: by means of (type: phrasal preposition)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Through the use of; using as a method or instrument; via; with the help of
    - *Fixedness*: strong (phrasal preposition; components fixed)
    
    **Synonyms**: through, via, using, with, through the use of, by way of, by use of, by using
    
    **Antonyms**: without, lacking, absent, without the use of
    
    **When to Use**: Use in formal or technical contexts to specify method or mechanism; common in academic, scientific, and professional writing
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "by" or "using"); sounds overly formal or bureaucratic in everyday contexts
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - By means of [method/tool]
    - By means of [technique]
    - Achieved by means of
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: Formal prepositional phrase; "means" (methods) from Latin "medianus"; "by means of" explicitly marks instrumentality; academic/technical register
    
    **Root Analysis**: "by" (through) + "means" (Latin "medianus" - middle, intermediate) + "of"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - They stay in touch by means of video calls.
    - You can open it by means of this special key.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Data security is maintained by means of advanced encryption protocols.
    - The hypothesis was tested by means of controlled experimental procedures.

13. **Expression**: by way of (type: phrasal preposition)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Traveling through or via (for route); as a form of; for the purpose of; as an example of
    - *Fixedness*: strong (phrasal preposition; fixed structure)
    
    **Synonyms**: via, through (for route); as, as a form of, for the purpose of (for form/purpose)
    
    **Antonyms**: directly to (for route); instead of (for alternative)
    
    **When to Use**: Use for describing routes or for introducing examples, explanations, or the nature of something; has both literal (spatial) and figurative (manner) meanings
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "by means of" (which emphasizes method/tool more explicitly); avoid redundancy when simpler prepositions suffice
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - By way of [place] (route)
    - By way of [example/introduction/explanation] (manner)
    - Travel by way of
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "Way" (path, method) from Old English "weg"; dual meanings (literal route + figurative manner) developed in Middle English; versatile phrasal preposition
    
    **Root Analysis**: "by" (through/as) + "way" (Old English "weg" - path, road, method) + "of"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - We drove to Boston by way of Hartford.
    - Just by way of example, here's what I mean.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The delegation traveled to Geneva by way of Frankfurt.
    - By way of introduction, this study examines three core hypotheses.

14. **Expression**: side by side (type: idiomatic collocation)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Next to each other; beside one another; in cooperation; together; in close proximity
    - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed repetitive structure)
    
    **Synonyms**: beside each other, alongside one another, next to each other, together, in tandem, shoulder to shoulder
    
    **Antonyms**: apart, separately, at a distance, opposed, in conflict
    
    **When to Use**: Use for literal physical proximity or figurative cooperation; emphasizes equality and closeness; common in both physical and abstract contexts
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when hierarchy or opposition should be emphasized; not appropriate when distance is the focus
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - Stand side by side
    - Work side by side
    - Compare side by side
    - Position can vary but components remain fixed
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: Repetitive structure "side by side" emphasizes equality and parallelism; "by" indicates proximity/adjacency; highly conventional pairing; can function as adverb or adjective
    
    **Root Analysis**: "side" + "by" (beside) + "side" (Old English "sīde"); repetitive structure for emphasis
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Let's put the photos side by side and compare them.
    - They've worked side by side for twenty years.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The two systems operate side by side to ensure redundancy.
    - Organizations must work side by side to address systemic challenges.

15. **Expression**: little by little (type: idiomatic collocation)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Gradually; slowly and steadily; in small increments; step by step over time
    - *Fixedness*: strong (repetitive structure; fixed form)
    
    **Synonyms**: gradually, bit by bit, step by step, slowly, incrementally, progressively, piece by piece
    
    **Antonyms**: suddenly, all at once, immediately, rapidly, abruptly, in one go
    
    **When to Use**: Use to emphasize gradual process or slow accumulation; highlights incremental nature of change; common in both literal and figurative contexts
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing sudden changes; not appropriate when large-scale or rapid transformation is emphasized
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - Little by little [subject] [verb]
    - Changed little by little
    - Grew little by little
    - Minimal variation due to fixed form
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: Repetitive structure emphasizes incremental nature; "by" indicates units/increments; parallel to "step by step," "bit by bit," "one by one"; conventional gradual process expression
    
    **Root Analysis**: "little" + "by" (indicating increments) + "little"; repetition emphasizes gradual accumulation
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Little by little, I'm getting better at speaking Spanish.
    - The snow melted away little by little.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The organization expanded its operations little by little over the decade.
    - Trust was rebuilt little by little through consistent transparent communication.
