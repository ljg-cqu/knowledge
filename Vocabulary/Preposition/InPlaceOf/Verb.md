1. **Verb**: replace
   **Meaning**: 
   - To take the place of; to provide or find a substitute for something that is lost, broken, or no longer usable (transitive)
   - To put something back in its original position (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute for, take the place of, supplant, supersede, displace
   
   **Antonyms**: retain, keep, maintain, preserve
   
   **When to Use**: When something or someone takes the position of another; in contexts of substitution, renewal, or succession; when describing the act of providing a substitute; in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to addition rather than substitution; inappropriate when the original is retained alongside the new
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - replace X with Y
   - replace someone/something
   - need to replace
   - replace the original
   - difficult to replace
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, back) + place (from Old French placer, Latin platea)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "replacer" (to put back) → English "replace" (to put in place of another, to substitute)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to replace my old phone soon.
   - Can anyone replace me at work tomorrow?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization will replace outdated systems with modern infrastructure.
   - Digital technologies have progressively replaced traditional methods across industries.

1. **Verb**: substitute
   **Meaning**: 
   - To use or add something in place of another thing; to replace one thing with another (transitive/intransitive)
   - To act or serve in place of another person (intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: replace, exchange, swap, switch, stand in for
   
   **Antonyms**: retain, keep original, maintain
   
   **When to Use**: When one thing or person temporarily or permanently takes the place of another; in contexts of exchange or replacement; often with "for" to indicate what is being replaced
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when both items are present together; inappropriate when no replacement occurs
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - substitute X for Y
   - substitute for someone
   - substitute one thing with another
   - can substitute
   - substitute as needed
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under, in place of) + -stitute (from Latin statuere = to place, set up)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin substituere (to put in place of) → substitutus → English "substitute" (to put one thing in place of another)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can substitute butter with oil in that recipe.
   - I'll substitute for her during the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers substituted the original compound with a synthetic alternative.
   - The designated representative will substitute for absent members at official proceedings.

1. **Verb**: supplant
   **Meaning**: 
   - To supersede and replace; to take the place of something or someone, especially by force or through superior performance (transitive)
   - To displace and take the position of another
   
   **Synonyms**: replace, displace, supersede, oust, usurp
   
   **Antonyms**: support, maintain, reinforce, preserve
   
   **When to Use**: When one thing forcefully or competitively replaces another; in contexts of displacement or usurpation; often implies the replaced item is now obsolete; in formal and literary contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when replacement is friendly or neutral; inappropriate in casual contexts (use "replace"); may imply negative connotations of forcing out
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supplant someone/something
   - gradually supplant
   - attempt to supplant
   - supplant the old with the new
   - supplant traditional methods
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (sub-, from below) + plant (from Latin plantare = to plant, fix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplantare (to trip up, overthrow) → Old French supplanter → English "supplant" (to displace and replace)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Digital cameras have supplanted film cameras almost completely.
   - She worries the new hire will supplant her.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Emerging technologies have supplanted conventional manufacturing processes.
   - The new paradigm gradually supplanted earlier theoretical frameworks.

1. **Verb**: supersede
   **Meaning**: 
   - To take the place of something, especially by being more modern, effective, or important; to replace something that has become outdated (transitive)
   - To cause something to be set aside or abandoned
   
   **Synonyms**: replace, supplant, displace, succeed, override
   
   **Antonyms**: continue, maintain, uphold, preserve
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when newer versions replace older ones; in legal, technical, and professional writing; when emphasizing improvement or progress; when one thing makes another obsolete
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "replace"); inappropriate when the replacement is not necessarily better or newer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supersede previous versions
   - supersede outdated methods
   - supersede the need for
   - supersede earlier policies
   - completely supersede
   
   **Root Analysis**: super- (above, beyond) + sede (from Latin sedere = to sit)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supersedere (to sit above, be superior to, refrain from) → Old French superseder → English "supersede" (to take the place of something older)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Streaming services have superseded traditional TV for many people.
   - This version supersedes all earlier ones.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new regulations supersede all previous administrative guidelines.
   - Digital documentation has superseded paper-based record systems in most institutions.

1. **Verb**: displace
   **Meaning**: 
   - To take the place of; to force something or someone out of its usual or original position (transitive)
   - To move something from its place; to replace or supplant
   
   **Synonyms**: replace, supplant, dislodge, oust, remove
   
   **Antonyms**: place, position, establish, settle
   
   **When to Use**: When something forces another out of position; in contexts of replacement that involves removal; when emphasizing the forced or involuntary nature of replacement; in technical, social, and physical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when replacement is voluntary or friendly; inappropriate when no removal or forcing out occurs
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - displace someone/something
   - displace workers
   - displace traditional methods
   - completely displace
   - gradually displace
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (away, apart) + place (from Latin platea via Old French)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "desplacer" (to move from usual place) → English "displace" (to remove from position, to replace)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Automation is displacing many factory workers.
   - New technology has displaced the old methods.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Technological innovations have displaced traditional manufacturing roles.
   - The intervention program aimed to prevent displaced populations from further marginalization.

1. **Verb**: stand in
   **Meaning**: 
   - To temporarily take someone's place or do someone's job while they are absent (intransitive, phrasal)
   - To substitute or deputize for another person
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute, fill in, cover for, replace temporarily, deputize
   
   **Antonyms**: step down, withdraw, vacate
   
   **When to Use**: In contexts of temporary replacement; when someone takes another's role briefly; in work, performance, and organizational contexts; typically followed by "for"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when replacement is permanent; inappropriate when describing long-term succession
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stand in for someone
   - willing to stand in
   - stand in during absence
   - stand in temporarily
   - agree to stand in
   
   **Root Analysis**: stand + in (phrasal verb combining literal and metaphorical meanings)
   
   **Etymology**: English "stand" (to be in position) + "in" → phrasal verb "stand in" (to take someone's position temporarily)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you stand in for me at tomorrow's meeting?
   - She stood in for the lead actor during rehearsals.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vice president will stand in for the CEO during the international conference.
   - A qualified professional was secured to stand in for the absent department head.

1. **Verb**: fill in
   **Meaning**: 
   - To act as a substitute for someone; to temporarily do someone else's job (intransitive, phrasal)
   - To provide information that is missing; to complete by adding required information (transitive, phrasal)
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute, stand in, cover, replace temporarily, deputize
   
   **Antonyms**: leave vacant, abandon post
   
   **When to Use**: In contexts of temporary replacement; when someone covers for another's absence; in work and organizational settings; followed by "for" when indicating who is being replaced
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when replacement is permanent; inappropriate in very formal writing (use "substitute" or "deputize")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fill in for someone
   - willing to fill in
   - fill in temporarily
   - fill in during absence
   - ask someone to fill in
   
   **Root Analysis**: fill + in (phrasal verb)
   
   **Etymology**: English "fill" (to occupy, make full) + "in" → phrasal verb "fill in" (to occupy a vacant position temporarily)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm filling in for Sarah while she's on vacation.
   - Can you fill in at the front desk tomorrow?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A senior associate will fill in for the partner during the sabbatical period.
   - Qualified personnel were recruited to fill in during the transition phase.

1. **Verb**: swap
   **Meaning**: 
   - To exchange one thing for another; to substitute one person or thing for another (transitive/intransitive)
   - To replace something by giving something in return
   
   **Synonyms**: exchange, switch, trade, substitute, replace
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, retain, hold onto
   
   **When to Use**: In informal and semi-formal contexts; when indicating mutual exchange or substitution; when the replacement involves giving something in return; in everyday conversations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal writing; inappropriate when no exchange occurs; may be too casual for academic or legal contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - swap X for Y
   - swap places with someone
   - swap out
   - willing to swap
   - swap shifts
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English "swappen" (to strike, strike hands in bargaining)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "swappen" (to strike a bargain) → Modern English "swap" (to exchange, substitute)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's swap seats so you can see better.
   - I swapped my sandwich for his salad.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were permitted to swap assigned time slots with advance notice.
   - The organization implemented a system allowing employees to swap scheduled shifts.

1. **Verb**: exchange
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give something and receive something else in return; to replace one thing with another, especially of the same kind (transitive)
   - To substitute one person or thing for another
   
   **Synonyms**: swap, trade, switch, substitute, replace
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, retain, hold
   
   **When to Use**: When one thing is given and another received in return; in contexts of mutual replacement; in both formal and informal settings; when emphasizing reciprocity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when no reciprocity exists; inappropriate when describing one-way replacement without return
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exchange X for Y
   - exchange one thing for another
   - exchange places
   - exchange gifts
   - exchange information
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + change (from Latin cambire = to barter)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "eschangier" (to exchange, barter) → English "exchange" (to give and receive reciprocally)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I exchange this shirt for a different size?
   - Let's exchange phone numbers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution established protocols for exchanging sensitive information securely.
   - Participants exchanged traditional research methods for innovative approaches.

1. **Verb**: deputize
   **Meaning**: 
   - To appoint someone as a deputy; to authorize someone to act as a substitute (transitive)
   - To act as a deputy or substitute for someone (intransitive, British English)
   
   **Synonyms**: delegate, authorize, appoint as substitute, commission, empower
   
   **Antonyms**: discharge, dismiss, revoke authority
   
   **When to Use**: In formal organizational and legal contexts; when officially appointing someone to act in place of another; when emphasizing authorized substitution; in British English more commonly than American
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts; inappropriate when no formal appointment or authority is involved
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deputize someone
   - deputize for the director
   - formally deputize
   - deputize in absence
   - authorized to deputize
   
   **Root Analysis**: deputy + -ize (verb-forming suffix meaning "to make, cause to be")
   
   **Etymology**: "Deputy" (from Latin deputare = to assign) + "-ize" → "deputize" (to appoint as deputy, to act as substitute)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The manager deputized her assistant to handle the meeting.
   - He's been deputized to sign documents while the boss is away.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board formally deputized the vice chair to execute all necessary administrative functions.
   - During the director's extended absence, the senior associate was deputized to represent the organization.
