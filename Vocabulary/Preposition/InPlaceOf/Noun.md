1. **Noun**: substitute (C, pl: substitutes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that takes the place of another; a replacement for someone or something
   - (in sports) A player who replaces another player during a game
   
   **Synonyms**: replacement, stand-in, alternative, backup, proxy
   
   **Antonyms**: original, primary, principal
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to a person or thing that temporarily or permanently replaces another; in sports, education, and general contexts; when emphasizing the replacement function
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to the original item or person; inappropriate when no replacement relationship exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - substitute for someone/something
   - find a substitute
   - act as a substitute
   - poor substitute
   - acceptable substitute
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under, in place of) + -stitute (from Latin statuere = to place, set up)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin substituere (to put in place of) → substitutus → English "substitute" (one who stands in for another)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to find a substitute for the meeting tomorrow.
   - Honey is a good substitute for sugar in this recipe.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The designated substitute will assume all responsibilities during the director's absence.
   - Researchers sought an effective substitute for the discontinued compound.

1. **Noun**: replacement (C, pl: replacements)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that takes the place of another, especially someone or something that is lost, broken, or no longer usable
   - The act of replacing someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute, stand-in, successor, alternative, backup
   
   **Antonyms**: original, predecessor
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to someone or something that takes the place of another that is gone, broken, or ineffective; in contexts of repair, succession, or renewal; when emphasizing the act of replacing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to the original item; inappropriate when describing additions rather than replacements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - replacement for something
   - find a replacement
   - hire a replacement
   - replacement cost
   - need a replacement
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, back) + place + -ment (noun suffix indicating result or means)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "replacer" (to put back) → English "replace" → "replacement" (act of replacing or that which replaces)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I ordered a replacement for my broken phone screen.
   - They're looking for a replacement since John quit.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board initiated a comprehensive search for the CEO's replacement.
   - The insurance policy covers the full replacement cost of damaged equipment.

1. **Noun**: alternative (C, pl: alternatives)
   **Meaning**: 
   - One of two or more available possibilities; an option that can be chosen instead of another
   - A choice limited to one of two or more possibilities
   
   **Synonyms**: option, choice, substitute, possibility, other option
   
   **Antonyms**: requirement, necessity, obligation
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to other available options or choices; in decision-making contexts; when presenting multiple possibilities; when emphasizing choice and flexibility
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when only one option exists; inappropriate when referring to requirements rather than choices
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - alternative to something
   - viable alternative
   - better alternative
   - seek alternatives
   - consider alternatives
   
   **Root Analysis**: alter (other) + -native (suffix indicating relationship or tendency)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin alter (other) → Medieval Latin alternativus → French alternatif → English "alternative" (another possibility)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do we have any alternatives if that doesn't work?
   - This is a cheaper alternative to the original brand.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee evaluated several alternatives before reaching a decision.
   - Policymakers must consider sustainable alternatives to current practices.

1. **Noun**: proxy (C, pl: proxies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person authorized to act on behalf of another; a substitute who represents someone else
   - The authority to represent someone else; a document granting such authority
   - (computing) A server that acts as an intermediary
   
   **Synonyms**: representative, agent, substitute, stand-in, delegate
   
   **Antonyms**: principal, original party, direct participant
   
   **When to Use**: In formal, legal, and business contexts; when referring to authorized representation; in voting, decision-making, and computing contexts; when emphasizing delegated authority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where simpler terms suffice; inappropriate when no authorization or delegation exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proxy for someone
   - vote by proxy
   - proxy statement
   - serve as proxy
   - proxy server
   
   **Root Analysis**: from Latin procuratio (management by another) → Middle English procuracie → proxy
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English procuracie (agency, function of an attorney) → contracted to "proxy" (authorized representative)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you be my proxy at the shareholders' meeting?
   - GDP is often used as a proxy for economic well-being.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Shareholders unable to attend may designate a proxy to vote on their behalf.
   - The study employed demographic variables as proxies for socioeconomic status.

1. **Noun**: surrogate (C, pl: surrogates)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that acts or is used in place of another; a substitute
   - A person appointed to act for another in a specific role
   - (medical) A woman who bears a child on behalf of another person
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute, replacement, stand-in, proxy, deputy
   
   **Antonyms**: principal, original, actual person
   
   **When to Use**: In formal, medical, legal, and psychological contexts; when emphasizing authorized or formal substitution; when referring to representatives with specific delegated roles
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts; inappropriate when simple substitution language is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - surrogate for someone/something
   - act as surrogate
   - surrogate mother
   - surrogate parent
   - surrogate marker
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (sub-, in place of) + rogare (Latin = to ask) + -ate
   
   **Etymology**: Latin surrogatus (substitute) → surrogare (to elect as substitute) → English "surrogate" (authorized substitute)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her aunt became a surrogate parent after the accident.
   - This metric serves as a surrogate for customer satisfaction.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court appointed a surrogate decision-maker for the incapacitated patient.
   - Surrogate endpoints were utilized to assess preliminary treatment efficacy.

1. **Noun**: stand-in (C, pl: stand-ins)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who takes someone else's place temporarily; a substitute
   - (in film/theater) A person who substitutes for an actor during technical preparations
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute, replacement, temporary replacement, proxy, deputy
   
   **Antonyms**: principal, permanent employee, original person
   
   **When to Use**: In contexts of temporary replacement; in entertainment industry; when emphasizing the temporary nature of the substitution; in informal and professional settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to permanent replacements; inappropriate when the substitution is formal or authorized (use "proxy" or "deputy")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stand-in for someone
   - serve as stand-in
   - temporary stand-in
   - need a stand-in
   - act as stand-in
   
   **Root Analysis**: stand + in (phrasal verb converted to compound noun)
   
   **Etymology**: English phrasal verb "stand in" (to substitute temporarily) → hyphenated compound noun "stand-in" (temporary substitute)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need a stand-in for tomorrow's presentation.
   - She's just a stand-in until the regular person comes back.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A qualified stand-in was appointed to fulfill the role during the transition period.
   - The production utilized professional stand-ins for complex lighting setups.

1. **Noun**: backup (C/U, pl: backups)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A person or thing that provides support or can be used if needed; a reserve
   - (C) A copy of data stored separately for use if the original is lost or damaged
   - (U) Support or assistance, especially in an emergency
   
   **Synonyms**: reserve, substitute, support, contingency, alternative
   
   **Antonyms**: primary system, main option, original
   
   **When to Use**: In contexts of contingency planning; in technology and computing; when referring to reserves or support systems; in emergency and preparedness contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to the primary system or person; inappropriate in very formal writing (use "reserve" or "contingency")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - backup plan
   - backup for someone
   - have a backup
   - backup system
   - serve as backup
   
   **Root Analysis**: back + up (phrasal verb converted to noun)
   
   **Etymology**: English phrasal verb "back up" (to support, provide reserve) → noun "backup" (reserve, support system)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Always have a backup in case something goes wrong.
   - I keep a backup of all my important files.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization maintained comprehensive backup systems for critical operations.
   - Participants were advised to prepare backup materials for the presentation.

1. **Noun**: option (C, pl: options)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thing that is or may be chosen; one possibility that can be selected instead of others
   - The freedom or ability to choose between different possibilities
   
   **Synonyms**: choice, alternative, possibility, selection, course of action
   
   **Antonyms**: requirement, obligation, necessity, mandate
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to available choices; in decision-making contexts; when emphasizing freedom to choose; in business, planning, and everyday contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when no real choice exists; inappropriate when referring to requirements or obligations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - option to do something
   - have options
   - consider options
   - viable option
   - best option
   
   **Root Analysis**: opt (Latin optare = to choose) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin optare (to choose, wish) → optio → French option → English "option" (that which may be chosen)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What are our options if this doesn't work?
   - Going by train is always an option.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board reviewed all available options before approving the merger.
   - Participants were presented with multiple options for continued engagement.

1. **Noun**: successor (C, pl: successors)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that succeeds another; one who takes over a position, role, or function from another
   - Something that comes after and takes the place of something else
   
   **Synonyms**: replacement, heir, inheritor, descendant, next in line
   
   **Antonyms**: predecessor, precursor, forerunner
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to someone who takes over a position or role; in contexts of succession, inheritance, and organizational transitions; when emphasizing continuity and progression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to the previous holder; inappropriate when no succession or progression exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - successor to someone
   - name a successor
   - appoint a successor
   - worthy successor
   - natural successor
   
   **Root Analysis**: suc- (sub-, under, after) + -cess (from Latin cedere = to go) + -or (agent suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin succedere (to come after, follow) → successor (one who follows after) → English "successor" (one who takes another's place)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They haven't announced her successor yet.
   - The new model is a worthy successor to the original.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board initiated a comprehensive search for the retiring CEO's successor.
   - The successor organization assumed all contractual obligations of its predecessor.

1. **Noun**: deputy (C, pl: deputies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person appointed to act as a substitute for another in performing particular duties; a second-in-command
   - An official who assists or represents a superior
   
   **Synonyms**: representative, delegate, substitute, proxy, assistant
   
   **Antonyms**: principal, superior, chief, head
   
   **When to Use**: In organizational, governmental, and formal contexts; when referring to officially appointed substitutes; when emphasizing delegated authority and second-in-command roles
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts; inappropriate when no formal appointment or authority exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deputy director
   - deputy for someone
   - appoint a deputy
   - act as deputy
   - deputy head
   
   **Root Analysis**: from Latin deputare (to assign, allot)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deputare (to assign, consider as) → Old French depute → English "deputy" (one assigned to act for another)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The deputy will handle things while the boss is away.
   - She's the deputy manager at the store.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The deputy director presided over the meeting in the director's absence.
   - Each department head appointed a deputy to ensure operational continuity.
