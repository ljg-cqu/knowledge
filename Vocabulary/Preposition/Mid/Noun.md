1. **Noun**: sentence (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A set of words, usually containing a verb, that expresses a complete idea in writing or speech.
   
   **Synonyms**: clause, utterance, statement, line, remark
   
   **Antonyms**: fragment, phrase, silence
   
   **When to Use**: Use when talking about units of language in speaking or writing. With "mid", it commonly appears in expressions like "mid-sentence" or "cut off mid-sentence" to highlight that someone was interrupted before finishing what they wanted to say.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "sentence" when you mean a whole text or speech (use "article", "speech", or "paragraph"). In legal contexts, "sentence" also means punishment decided by a court, which is not the meaning used with "mid".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mid-sentence pause
   - cut off mid-sentence
   - stop someone mid-sentence
   
   **Root Analysis**: sent (from Latin "sententia" meaning opinion, judgment) + -ence (noun suffix forming abstract nouns)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "sententia" (opinion, judgment, short saying) → Old French "sentence" → Middle English "sentence" (grammatical unit and wise saying) → Modern English "sentence".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He just walked away mid-sentence while I was still talking.
   - My phone died mid-sentence during the call.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The interview was interrupted mid-sentence due to a technical failure.
   - Participants frequently corrected themselves mid-sentence as they clarified their views.

1. **Noun**: flight (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A journey made by air, especially in an airplane, from one place to another.
   - *(Sense 2)* The act of flying or moving through the air.
   
   **Synonyms**: airplane trip, journey, air travel, route, leg
   
   **Antonyms**: landing, arrival (for the journey sense), stay, halt
   
   **When to Use**: Use for air travel in general. With "mid", it appears in phrases like "mid-flight" to describe events that happen while the aircraft is in the air, often unexpectedly ("an engine warning mid-flight").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "flight" when you only mean the schedule or ticket ("booking" or "reservation" may be clearer). Avoid with ground travel (use "trip", "journey", or "drive").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mid-flight emergency
   - turn back mid-flight
   - conversation mid-flight
   
   **Root Analysis**: flight (from Old English "flyht" related to "to fly")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "flyht" (act of flying) → Middle English "flight" → Modern English "flight" (journey by air).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We hit some nasty turbulence mid-flight.
   - They made a big announcement mid-flight over the loudspeaker.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The crew declared an emergency mid-flight following an instrument malfunction.
   - Data were collected mid-flight to assess passenger responses to unexpected disruptions.

1. **Noun**: conversation (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An informal talk between two or more people, in which they exchange ideas, feelings, or information.
   
   **Synonyms**: talk, discussion, chat, dialogue, exchange
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, monologue, speech (one-way)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for everyday spoken interaction. With "mid", it appears in phrases like "mid-conversation" to show that something happened while people were still talking, often causing interruption or an awkward shift.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the communication is formal and one-directional (use "lecture" or "presentation"). Do not use it when there is no real back-and-forth.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mid-conversation interruption
   - walk away mid-conversation
   - change subject mid-conversation
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + vers (from Latin "vertere" meaning to turn) + -ation (noun-forming suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "conversari" (to associate with) → Old French "conversacion" → Middle English "conversation" (living among, then talking with others).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She checked her phone mid-conversation, which felt a bit rude.
   - The fire alarm went off mid-conversation and everyone had to leave.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants frequently shifted topics mid-conversation when sensitive issues arose.
   - The call dropped mid-conversation, interrupting the negotiation process.

1. **Noun**: crisis (C, pl: crises)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A time of great difficulty, danger, or uncertainty when important decisions must be made.
   
   **Synonyms**: emergency, disaster, turning point, catastrophe, critical situation
   
   **Antonyms**: stability, calm, normality, resolution
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing a serious situation that demands urgent decisions. With "mid", it appears in expressions like "mid-crisis reshuffle" or "mid-crisis pivot" to highlight actions taken while the crisis is still unfolding.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for minor problems (use "issue" or "difficulty"); overuse weakens its impact.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mid-crisis decision
   - lead change mid-crisis
   - mid-crisis leadership switch
   
   **Root Analysis**: crisis (from Greek "krisis" meaning decision, turning point)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "krisis" → Latin "crisis" → English "crisis" (medical turning point, later any decisive difficult situation).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They changed strategy mid-crisis because nothing was working.
   - He took over the team mid-crisis and somehow calmed everyone down.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization appointed a new interim CEO mid-crisis to stabilize governance.
   - Policy shifts introduced mid-crisis significantly altered stakeholder expectations.

1. **Noun**: career (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The series of jobs or roles that a person does in their working life, especially in a particular field.
   
   **Synonyms**: professional life, working life, vocation, occupation, path
   
   **Antonyms**: retirement, unemployment (in some contexts), break from work
   
   **When to Use**: Use when talking about long-term work life. With "mid", it appears in expressions like "mid-career" or "mid-career switch" to describe the middle phase of a working life when people often reconsider direction.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when talking about a single job (use "role" or "position"). For short-term work experiences, "gig" or "project" may be better.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mid-career professional
   - mid-career switch
   - mid-career review
   
   **Root Analysis**: career (from French "carrière" meaning road, racecourse)
   
   **Etymology**: French "carrière" (road, course) → English "career" (course of ones public or professional life).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She made a huge mid-career switch into UX design.
   - He went back to school mid-career to study psychology.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many professionals undertake advanced study mid-career to reposition themselves in the labour market.
   - The program specifically targets mid-career leaders considering a strategic transition.
