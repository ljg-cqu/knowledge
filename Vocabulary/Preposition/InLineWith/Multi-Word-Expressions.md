1. **Expression**: in line with expectations (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - [Collins-style definition]
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning vs. Figurative/Idiomatic meaning
   - *Fixedness*: strong ("in line with" + "expectations" is a very common partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: as expected, in keeping with what we expected, unsurprising, in accordance with forecasts
   
   **Antonyms**: above expectations, below expectations, far from expectations, at odds with expectations
   
   **When to Use**: When performance, results, or behaviour match what was reasonably predicted; common in business, finance, education, and performance reviews
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very emotional contexts (use "just what I hoped" or "disappointing" instead); can sound dry or corporate if overused
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - largely in line with expectations
   - broadly in line with expectations
   - roughly in line with expectations
   - not in line with expectations
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "expectations" is the default noun for predictions or hopes; "in line with" is a standard phrase for alignment, so the combination feels natural and frequent in performance talk.
   
   **Root Analysis**: in line (aligned) + with + expectations (what is expected)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The exam results were pretty much in line with expectations.
   - Sales this month are in line with expectations—nothing shocking either way.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Revenue growth was broadly in line with market expectations.
   - Student outcomes are in line with expectations given the cohort’s entry profile.

1. **Expression**: bring [X] in line with [Y] (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - [Collins-style definition]
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning vs. Figurative/Idiomatic meaning
   - *Fixedness*: strong ("bring" + "in line with" is a highly conventional pattern)
   
   **Synonyms**: align [X] with [Y], adjust [X] to match [Y], bring [X] into compliance with [Y], update [X] to reflect [Y]
   
   **Antonyms**: move [X] away from [Y], decouple [X] from [Y], leave [X] unchanged despite [Y]
   
   **When to Use**: When describing active adjustment so that policies, prices, behaviour, or processes match a reference standard or benchmark
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you are proposing an entirely new direction that breaks from [Y]; then "redefine" or "rethink" may be more accurate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - bring salaries in line with the market
   - bring our practices in line with the new regulations
   - bring the design in line with the brand guidelines
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "bring" naturally introduces change; "in line with" signals alignment; together they form a compact, widely used formula for correcting divergence.
   
   **Root Analysis**: bring (cause to move) + in line with (into alignment with)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should bring our lifestyle more in line with what we can actually afford.
   - Let’s bring the website in line with the new logo.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board decided to bring executive compensation in line with industry benchmarks.
   - Local procedures will be brought in line with updated national standards.

1. **Expression**: keep [X] in line with [Y] (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - [Collins-style definition]
   - *For idioms*: Literal meaning vs. Figurative/Idiomatic meaning
   - *Fixedness*: medium/strong (very common pattern in governance and management)
   
   **Synonyms**: maintain alignment between [X] and [Y], keep [X] aligned with [Y], ensure [X] stays consistent with [Y]
   
   **Antonyms**: let [X] drift away from [Y], lose alignment, fall out of line
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about ongoing monitoring or maintenance so that projects, budgets, or behaviour continue to match strategy, rules, or expectations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For one‑off corrective changes; "bring in line" suits that better
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - keep our roadmap in line with customer needs
   - keep spending in line with the budget
   - keep messaging in line with the brand
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "keep" highlights continuity; "in line with" describes alignment; together they express ongoing control rather than one‑time change.
   
   **Root Analysis**: keep (maintain) + in line with (in alignment with)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Try to keep your expectations in line with what’s realistic.
   - We need to keep our plans in line with what the team can actually deliver.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Governance mechanisms are required to keep investment decisions in line with risk appetite.
   - The committee reviews syllabi annually to keep them in line with accreditation requirements.

1. **Expression**: fall in line (type: idiom/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - [Collins-style definition]
   - *Literal*: To move into a physical line with others.
   - *Figurative*: To start behaving in line with rules, orders, or group expectations, often after resisting.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (verb + preposition used with limited variation)
   
   **Synonyms**: comply, conform, go along, toe the line, follow orders
   
   **Antonyms**: rebel, hold out, break ranks, refuse to comply
   
   **When to Use**: When describing people or groups who eventually accept and match a decision, policy, or group norm; often in commentary or narrative
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in sensitive HR or feedback contexts where it can sound authoritarian or dismissive (suggesting blind obedience)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fall in line with the decision
   - expect everyone to fall in line
   - finally fell in line after negotiations
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Military and marching imagery provides a vivid picture of individuals literally forming a line, then extends metaphorically to social and organizational conformity.
   
   **Root Analysis**: fall (move or shift into position) + in line
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - At first they complained, but they eventually fell in line.
   - Once the boss was firm, everyone fell in line pretty quickly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smaller firms gradually fell in line with the dominant pricing structure.
   - After initial resistance, regional offices fell in line with the centralised reporting model.

1. **Expression**: out of line with [something] (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - [Collins-style definition]
   - *Literal*: Physically not in the same line.
   - *Figurative*: Not matching or respecting rules, norms, expectations, or targets.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very frequent evaluative pattern)
   
   **Synonyms**: at odds with, inconsistent with, in conflict with, misaligned with, far from
   
   **Antonyms**: in line with, in keeping with, consistent with, aligned with
   
   **When to Use**: When highlighting misalignment between behaviour or results and a standard—often in criticism or diagnosis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for gentle, exploratory conversations; choose softer alternatives like "doesn’t fully align with" if you want to reduce defensiveness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - way out of line with expectations
   - clearly out of line with policy
   - out of line with our values
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: The preposition "with" naturally introduces the reference standard; "out of line" strongly conveys deviation, so the phrase is concise and vivid.
   
   **Root Analysis**: out of (beyond) + line (alignment) + with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That comment was really out of line with how we try to treat people here.
   - The bill is completely out of line with what we agreed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Expenditure levels are significantly out of line with approved budget provisions.
   - The practice is out of line with accepted professional standards.

1. **Expression**: march in step with [something] (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - [Collins-style definition]
   - *Literal*: To march so that your steps line up with others.
   - *Figurative*: To develop or act in line with a trend, movement, or group direction.
   - *Fixedness*: medium (variants like "move in step with", "keep in step with" are common)
   
   **Synonyms**: move in step with, stay in step with, keep pace with, remain in sync with
   
   **Antonyms**: fall behind, break step, move against, go against the tide
   
   **When to Use**: When describing close alignment with broader trends, especially in commentary on policy, technology, or culture
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too metaphorical for highly technical writing; avoid when you want to stress independence rather than conformity
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - march in step with global trends
   - keep in step with customer expectations
   - move in step with market developments
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: The rhythmic, military image of marching in a line is a strong metaphor for coordinated movement with a larger group or trend.
   
   **Root Analysis**: march (walk in rhythm) + in step (with the same timing) + with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We don’t always have to march in step with what every other startup is doing.
   - Their brand doesn’t really march in step with their actual culture.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Fiscal policy has not marched in step with demographic developments.
   - The organization strives to march in step with international best practice while respecting local context.

1. **Expression**: in tune with [something] (type: idiom/collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - [Collins-style definition]
   - *Literal*: Musically tuned to the correct pitch.
   - *Figurative*: Aware of and responsive to needs, trends, or emotions; broadly in line with them.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (frequent metaphor in business and interpersonal contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: in sync with, attuned to, responsive to, aligned with, sensitive to
   
   **Antonyms**: out of tune with, oblivious to, disconnected from, insensitive to
   
   **When to Use**: When describing leaders, teams, or organisations that understand and respond well to stakeholders, markets, or moods
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very technical documents where musical metaphors may distract; avoid if literal musical meaning could confuse
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in tune with customer needs
   - in tune with the times
   - in tune with employee concerns
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Music provides a vivid analogy for harmony and alignment; being "in tune" signals both accuracy and sensitivity.
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (within) + tune (correct pitch) + with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She’s really in tune with what her team needs.
   - We need to be more in tune with what’s happening in the market.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Effective leadership requires remaining in tune with evolving stakeholder expectations.
   - The communication strategy is in tune with broader societal concerns about privacy.
