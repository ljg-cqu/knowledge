1. **Verb**: turn off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) To stop the operation of a device or system by using a switch or control; to deactivate (transitive)
   - (2) To stop liking or being interested in something; to cause disgust or loss of interest (transitive, informal)
   - (3) To leave a road by taking a different direction (intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: (1) switch off, shut off, power down, deactivate; (2) repel, disgust, put off; (3) exit, diverge
   
   **Antonyms**: (1) turn on, switch on, activate; (2) attract, interest, appeal to; (3) continue on, stay on
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Universal for devices, lights, systems. (2) Informal contexts for expressing loss of attraction/interest. (3) Driving and navigation contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (2) Avoid in formal contexts; use "discourage interest" or similar. Can have sexual connotations in some contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - turn off the lights
   - turn off the TV
   - turn somebody off
   - really turns me off
   - turn off at the exit
   
   **Root Analysis**: turn (Old English "tyrnan" = rotate) + off (indicating cessation/departure)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb that emerged with gas lighting (1800s) and became standard with electricity. The "disgust" sense developed in 1960s informal American English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't forget to turn off all the lights before you leave.
   - His constant bragging really turns me off.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please ensure all electronic devices are turned off during the examination.
   - Consumer sentiment may be negatively affected by such marketing strategies.

1. **Verb**: switch off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) To stop the operation of a device by using a switch; to deactivate (transitive)
   - (2) To stop paying attention; to mentally disengage (intransitive, informal)
   
   **Synonyms**: (1) turn off, shut off, power down, deactivate; (2) tune out, zone out, disengage
   
   **Antonyms**: (1) switch on, turn on, activate; (2) pay attention, focus, engage
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Universal, especially British English. (2) Informal contexts describing mental disengagement or lack of attention.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (2) Avoid in formal contexts where precise descriptions of attention or cognition are needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - switch off the power
   - switch off at the wall
   - mentally switch off
   - switch off completely
   - brain switches off
   
   **Root Analysis**: switch (Dutch "swijken" = bend, turn) + off (indicating cessation)
   
   **Etymology**: Emerged with electrical switches in the late 1800s. The mental disengagement sense developed in the mid-1900s, particularly in British English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I just switch off whenever he starts talking about work.
   - Make sure you switch off all the appliances before going on holiday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All electronic equipment must be switched off prior to departure.
   - Cognitive fatigue may cause individuals to disengage from complex tasks.

   - (3) To leave suddenly or quickly (intransitive, informal)
   - (4) To become successful or popular suddenly (intransitive)
   - (5) To have time away from work (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: (1) remove, doff; (2) depart, lift off; (3) leave, depart quickly; (4) succeed, become popular; (5) have off, get time off
   
   **Antonyms**: (1) put on, wear; (2) land, touch down; (3) stay, remain; (4) fail, flop
   
   **When to Use**: Universal with multiple meanings. (1) Everyday actions. (2) Aviation. (3) Informal departure. (4) Success contexts. (5) Employment contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent. Multiple meanings require clear context to avoid ambiguity.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take off your shoes
   - plane takes off
   - career took off
   - take off running
   - take a day off
   
   **Root Analysis**: take (Old Norse "taka" = grasp) + off (indicating removal/departure)
   
   **Etymology**: Basic meaning "remove" dates to 1300s. Aviation sense emerged in early 1900s. Success meaning developed mid-1900s. Multiple senses make this highly idiomatic.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Take off your coat and stay a while.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Verb**: put off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) To postpone or delay something to a later time (transitive)
   - (2) To discourage, repel, or cause someone to lose interest (transitive)
   - (3) To cause someone to wait or distract them with excuses (transitive, British English)
   
   **Synonyms**: (1) postpone, delay, defer, reschedule; (2) discourage, deter, repel, dissuade; (3) fob off, stall
   
   **Antonyms**: (1) advance, bring forward, proceed with; (2) encourage, attract, inspire
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Universal for postponing plans, decisions, tasks. (2) Expressing negative reactions or discouragement. (3) British English for evasive behavior.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (1) Don't confuse with "put on" (to wear). (2) Can range from mild discouragement to strong repulsion depending on context.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - put off until later
   - don't put it off
   - put somebody off
   - put off by
   - keep putting off
   
   **Root Analysis**: put (Old English "putian" = push) + off (indicating delay/removal)
   
   **Etymology**: The "postpone" sense dates to the 1300s. The "repel/discourage" sense emerged in the 1800s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stop putting off that dentist appointment—you need to go!
   - I was really put off by how dirty the restaurant was.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee voted to put off the decision pending additional research.
   - Potential applicants may be put off by the complexity of the application process.

1. **Verb**: call off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) To cancel or terminate an event, plan, or activity (transitive)
   - (2) To order someone or something to stop attacking or pursuing (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: (1) cancel, abort, terminate, scrub, abandon; (2) recall, restrain, hold back
   
   **Antonyms**: (1) proceed with, continue, confirm, go ahead with; (2) sic, set on, urge on
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Universal for canceling planned events, especially those already in progress or imminent. (2) Controlling animals or people in pursuit/attack scenarios.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (1) Typically for events that were actively planned or announced. Not usually for things that simply won't happen.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - call off the wedding
   - call off the search
   - call off a strike
   - call off the dogs
   - forced to call off
   
   **Root Analysis**: call (Old Norse "kalla" = cry out) + off (indicating cessation)
   
   **Etymology**: Originally (1600s) meant to summon away, especially dogs from a chase. Extended to mean cancel or terminate in the 1800s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They had to call off the picnic because of the thunderstorm.
   - Call off your dog—it's scaring the kids!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Management agreed to negotiate, and the union called off the strike.
   - Search operations were called off due to deteriorating weather conditions.

   **Root Analysis**: cut (Old English "cyttan") + off (indicating separation/removal)

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   - My phone got cut off because I didn't pay the bill.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Verb**: lay off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) To dismiss employees due to lack of work or economic conditions (transitive)
   - (2) To stop doing, using, or consuming something (transitive, informal)
   - (3) To stop bothering or criticizing someone (transitive, informal)
   
   **Synonyms**: (1) dismiss, make redundant, let go, downsize; (2) quit, stop, give up; (3) leave alone, stop bothering
   
   **Antonyms**: (1) hire, recruit, employ; (2) continue, persist with; (3) pester, bother
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Employment and economic contexts. (2-3) Informal advice or commands to stop something.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (1) Don't confuse with "fire" (termination for cause). Layoffs are specifically economic/organizational, not performance-based.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lay off workers
   - forced to lay off
   - lay off the junk food
   - lay off me
   - plans to lay off
   
   **Root Analysis**: lay (Old English "lecgan" = put down) + off (indicating removal)
   
   **Etymology**: Nautical origin (1800s) meaning to move away from shore. Employment sense emerged in early 1900s. Informal "stop" sense developed mid-1900s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to lay off the caffeine—you're way too jittery!
   - Lay off him, will you? He's had a rough day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The corporation announced plans to lay off 500 employees over the next quarter.
   - Economic pressures forced management to lay off a portion of the workforce.

1. **Verb**: brush off

   - (1) To remove something by brushing (transitive)
   - (2) To dismiss, ignore, or treat something/someone as unimportant (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: (1) dust off, wipe off; (2) dismiss, ignore, disregard, shrug off, wave away
   
   **Antonyms**: (2) take seriously, consider carefully, acknowledge, address
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Physical cleaning. (2) Informal to semi-formal contexts describing dismissive treatment of concerns, people, or ideas.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (2) Has negative connotations of disrespect or negligence. Implies improper dismissal of legitimate concerns.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - brush off concerns
   - brush someone off
   - brush off criticism
   - can't brush off
   - easily brushed off
   
   **Root Analysis**: brush (Middle English "brushe") + off (indicating removal)
   
   **Etymology**: Literal sense of removing with a brush is old. Figurative "dismiss" sense emerged in early 1900s, suggesting casual removal like brushing dust off clothing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He tried to talk to her, but she just brushed him off.
   - Don't brush off the warning signs—they could be serious.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Management cannot brush off employee concerns regarding workplace safety.
   - The committee brushed off objections without adequate consideration.
