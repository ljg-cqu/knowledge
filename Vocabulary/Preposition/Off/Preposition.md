1. **Preposition**: off (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Away from; at a distance from; no longer on or touching a surface
   - *(Spatial)* Leading away from or branching from a main route, street, or area
   - *(Temporal)* Away from work or duty; not attending or available
   - *(Abstract)* No longer using, consuming, or taking something; abstaining from
   - *(Abstract)* Removed or separated from a list, team, or group
   - *(Abstract)* Reduced in price; discounted by a certain amount
   
   **Synonyms**: away from (in some contexts), from (in some contexts), absent from (temporal), without (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: on, onto, toward, at
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage. Essential for describing separation, distance, absence, abstinence, and price reductions. One of the most versatile English prepositions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common learner errors include confusing "off" with "of" (pronunciation similar but meanings different). "Off" implies separation/distance; "of" shows possession/relationship.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: off the table, off duty, off work, off the menu, off the record, off limits
   - **Verb + prep**: get off, jump off, fall off, come off, step off, live off
   - **Adjective + prep**: free off (rare), clear off (rare)
   - **Noun + prep**: way off the road, distance off the coast, time off work
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - from Old English "of" (away from)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "of" meant "away from" and evolved into modern "off" by the 1600s to distinguish from "of" (possession/relationship). The spelling split helped clarify two distinct words with different meanings.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Get your feet off the couch!
   - I'm off work tomorrow, so we can hang out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vessel remained ten kilometers off the coast during the operation.
   - Employees who are off duty are not required to respond to communications.

1. **Preposition**: off (spatial - surface separation) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Away from the surface of; no longer in contact with; removed from position on something
   
   **Synonyms**: from (in context of removal), away from (emphasizing distance)
   
   **Antonyms**: on, onto, upon
   
   **When to Use**: Describing physical removal from surfaces, positions, or contact. Essential for instructions, descriptions of movement, and spatial relationships.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "from" in all contexts. "Off" emphasizes previous contact/attachment; "from" can indicate origin without prior contact.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: off the shelf, off the wall, off the table, off the ground, off the hook
   - **Verb + prep**: take off, knock off, wipe off, brush off, peel off, scrape off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: removal off the surface, distance off the edge
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (away from)
   
   **Etymology**: The spatial "removal from surface" sense has been core to "off" since Old English, distinguishing it from other directional prepositions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Take your shoes off before coming inside.
   - The picture fell off the wall last night.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The specimen must be carefully lifted off the slide for examination.
   - Items removed off the premises require documentation.

1. **Preposition**: off (spatial - distance/divergence) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* At a distance from; away from a main location, route, or point; branching from or near but not on
   
   **Synonyms**: away from (in context), from (indicating origin), near (when indicating proximity)
   
   **Antonyms**: on, along, toward
   
   **When to Use**: Describing locations relative to main routes, coasts, areas. Common in navigation, geography, and directions. Important for indicating proximity without direct contact.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous regarding exact distance. Specify distance when precision is needed ("5 km off the coast").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: off the coast, off the road, off the beaten path, off the main street, off Route 66
   - **Verb + prep**: turn off, branch off, veer off, go off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: house off the highway, island off the mainland, path off the trail
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (away from)
   
   **Etymology**: Extended from basic "away from" meaning to indicate geographic and spatial relationships of proximity without direct connection.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a great little restaurant off Main Street.
   - We found a cabin way off the main road.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The island is located approximately 50 kilometers off the southern coast.
   - The facility is situated off Highway 101, accessible via Exit 47.

1. **Preposition**: off (temporal - absence from duty) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Not at work; absent from duty; having free time from regular obligations
   
   **Synonyms**: away from (in context), absent from, not at
   
   **Antonyms**: at, on (when referring to duty status)
   
   **When to Use**: Workplace and duty contexts. Essential for discussing schedules, leave, and availability. Common in both casual and formal employment discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "out of" in illness contexts. "Off work" can mean vacation or general absence; "out of work" means unemployed.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: off work, off duty, off the job, off the clock, off sick (British)
   - **Verb + prep**: take off, get off, have off, be off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: day off work, time off duty, week off school
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (away from)
   
   **Etymology**: Extension of "away from" to describe temporal absence from regular duties, particularly common with standardization of work schedules in the industrial era.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm off work this Friday, so let's meet up.
   - The boss is off sick today.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees off duty are not authorized to access restricted areas.
   - Personnel off work due to illness must provide medical certification.

1. **Preposition**: off (abstract - abstinence/cessation) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* No longer using, consuming, or taking something; abstaining from; having stopped
   
   **Synonyms**: without, abstaining from, free from, no longer on
   
   **Antonyms**: on, using, taking, consuming
   
   **When to Use**: Health, addiction, diet, and medication contexts. Common in describing cessation of substances, medications, or habits.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "off" (no longer consuming) with "of" (relationship). Common pronunciation error for learners.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: off drugs, off medication, off caffeine, off alcohol, off the grid, off sugar
   - **Verb + prep**: come off, go off, get off, wean off, stay off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: time off medication, period off treatment
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (away from)
   
   **Etymology**: Extended metaphorically from physical separation to describe cessation of consumption or dependence, particularly common in medical and recovery contexts from the mid-1900s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been off coffee for three months now and feel great.
   - He's finally off those painkillers the doctor prescribed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients who have been off medication for six months showed significant improvement.
   - Participants must remain off alcohol for the duration of the study.

1. **Preposition**: off (abstract - removal from list/group) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Removed from; no longer included in a list, team, schedule, or group; excluded from
   
   **Synonyms**: removed from, excluded from, not on, dropped from
   
   **Antonyms**: on, included in, part of, listed on
   
   **When to Use**: Administrative, organizational, and scheduling contexts. Common in sports (off the team), dining (off the menu), and exclusion situations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context must clarify what list or group is referenced. Can be ambiguous without clear specification.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: off the list, off the team, off the menu, off the agenda, off the schedule, off the roster
   - **Verb + prep**: take off, strike off, cross off, knock off, leave off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: removal off the roster, item off the list
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (away from)
   
   **Etymology**: Extended from physical removal to abstract removal from lists, groups, and organizational structures. Common in administrative English from the 1800s onward.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry, the fish is off the menu tonight.
   - He got kicked off the team for breaking the rules.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The item has been struck off the agenda due to time constraints.
   - Candidates may be removed off the roster if they fail to meet requirements.

1. **Preposition**: off (abstract - discount/reduction) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Reduced by a specified amount; discounted by; subtracted from the original price
   
   **Synonyms**: reduced by, discounted by, less, subtracted from
   
   **Antonyms**: added to, increased by, plus, on top of
   
   **When to Use**: Commercial, retail, and pricing contexts. Essential for advertising, sales, and price negotiations. Common in percentage or fixed amount reductions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Specify percentage or amount to avoid ambiguity. "20% off" is clear; "off" alone is vague.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 20% off, $10 off, half off, off the price, off the original cost
   - **Verb + prep**: take off, knock off, get off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: discount off the total, reduction off the price
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (away from)
   
   **Etymology**: Extended metaphorically from physical removal to represent subtraction or reduction in pricing contexts, particularly common in retail from the late 1800s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everything in the store is 30% off this weekend!
   - I got $50 off the original price because I had a coupon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Members receive 15% off the standard rate for all services.
   - The promotion offers $100 off the purchase price of select models.

1. **Preposition**: off (abstract - sourced/derived from) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Obtaining from; deriving sustenance, support, or resources from; living on or supported by
   
   **Synonyms**: from (in context of source), on (when meaning sustained by), by means of
   
   **Antonyms**: without, independent of
   
   **When to Use**: Describing sources of income, sustenance, or support. Common in discussions of livelihoods, parasitism, and resource dependencies. Can have negative connotations when describing exploitation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound negative when describing human relationships (living off parents, living off others). Use carefully to avoid implying exploitation or parasitism.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: live off, survive off, make money off, profit off, feed off
   - **Verb + prep**: live off, survive off, get by off, make off, profit off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: income off investments, living off savings
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (from, out of)
   
   **Etymology**: Retained from Old English "of" the sense of "from" or "out of" as a source. This usage preserves the original meaning before "off" and "of" split into separate words.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After college, he lived off his parents for two years.
   - She makes good money off her YouTube channel.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organism survives off nutrients extracted from its host.
   - Many retirees live off income generated by investment portfolios.

1. **Preposition**: off (idiomatic - informal/casual uses) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Various idiomatic meanings in specific phrases: borrowed from, obtained from, bought from (informal British English)
   
   **Synonyms**: from (standard alternative)
   
   **Antonyms**: to (in context of giving/selling)
   
   **When to Use**: Primarily British English informal speech. Common in casual contexts for buying, borrowing, or obtaining things from people. American English typically uses "from" instead.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in formal writing; use "from" instead. This usage is dialectal and informal, primarily British.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: buy off someone, borrow off someone, get off someone, bought it off a friend
   - **Verb + prep**: buy off, get off, borrow off, obtain off
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: (none standard)
   
   **Root Analysis**: off - Old English "of" (from)
   
   **Etymology**: Retained from the original Old English "of" meaning "from," which persists in British dialectal and informal speech, particularly in northern England.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I bought this bike off a mate at work. (British)
   - Can I borrow a tenner off you? (British)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The item was purchased from a colleague. (use "from" in formal contexts)
   - Funds were obtained from external sources. (use "from" in formal contexts)

   **Antonyms**: onto, on

   - **Verb + prep**: get off of, jump off of, fall off of (all considered redundant)
   - **Adjective + prep**: (none standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: (none standard)
