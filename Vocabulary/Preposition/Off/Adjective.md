1. **Adjective**: better off
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a more favorable financial, social, or general position than previously or than someone/something else; having more money, advantages, or happiness; more fortunate (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: more fortunate, wealthier, in a better position, more advantaged, more prosperous
   
   **Antonyms**: worse off, poorer, less fortunate, disadvantaged
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage across all registers. Common in financial discussions, life advice, comparative situations, and decision-making contexts. Often used in conditional structures to evaluate options.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used attributively before a noun (*a better off person is incorrect; use "a person who is better off")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - much better off
   - better off without
   - financially better off
   - would be better off
   - far better off
   
   **Root Analysis**: better (comparative of "well/good") + off (adverb indicating state/condition)
   
   **Etymology**: "Better" from Old English "betera" (comparative of "good"). "Off" from Old English "of" (away from). The combination "better off" emerged in the 1800s to describe improved circumstances or financial position.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You'd be better off just buying a new one instead of trying to fix it.
   - They're way better off now that they moved to the new house.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Households in the upper income bracket are considerably better off than they were a decade ago.
   - The analysis suggests that investors would be better off diversifying their portfolios.

1. **Adjective**: worse off
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a less favorable financial, social, or general position than previously or than someone/something else; having less money, fewer advantages, or less happiness; more unfortunate (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: less fortunate, poorer, in a worse position, more disadvantaged, less prosperous
   
   **Antonyms**: better off, wealthier, more fortunate, advantaged
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage across all registers. Common in economic analysis, policy discussions, comparative evaluations, and describing declining circumstances.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used attributively before a noun (*a worse off family is incorrect; use "a family that is worse off")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - much worse off
   - worse off than before
   - financially worse off
   - significantly worse off
   - left worse off
   
   **Root Analysis**: worse (comparative of "bad/ill") + off (adverb indicating state/condition)
   
   **Etymology**: "Worse" from Old English "wiersa" (comparative of "bad"). Combined with "off" to create the opposite of "better off," describing deteriorated circumstances.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm actually worse off after taking that job because the commute is killing me.
   - Some people ended up worse off after the changes were implemented.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reform policies have left many rural communities worse off economically.
   - Research indicates that divorced couples are often financially worse off than married counterparts.

1. **Adjective**: well off
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having plenty of money; wealthy or prosperous; in comfortable financial circumstances (gradable, predicative, can be used attributively in British English)
   
   **Synonyms**: wealthy, prosperous, affluent, rich, comfortable, well-to-do
   
   **Antonyms**: poor, badly off, impoverished, struggling, destitute
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage. Common when describing financial status politely without directly saying "rich." More tactful than "wealthy" in some contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts requiring precise financial terminology. Can sound euphemistic or evasive in formal financial reporting.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very well off
   - fairly well off
   - comfortably well off
   - reasonably well off
   - not particularly well off
   
   **Root Analysis**: well (adverb indicating satisfactory state) + off (indicating condition)
   
   **Etymology**: "Well off" emerged in the mid-1700s as a genteel way to describe financial prosperity without the directness of "rich" or "wealthy."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her family is pretty well off—they own three houses!
   - We're not super well off, but we're comfortable enough.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The survey targeted households that are reasonably well off, with annual incomes exceeding $100,000.
   - Well off families have greater access to educational resources and opportunities.

1. **Adjective**: badly off
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having very little money; poor or in difficult financial circumstances; lacking resources or advantages (gradable, predicative, primarily British English)
   
   **Synonyms**: poor, impoverished, struggling financially, hard up, in straitened circumstances
   
   **Antonyms**: well off, wealthy, prosperous, comfortable, affluent
   
   **When to Use**: Primarily British English. Used to politely describe financial hardship without using harsh terms like "poor" or "destitute." Common in social and economic discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in American English (where "badly off" might sound unusual). Avoid in formal financial analysis where precise terms are required.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - quite badly off
   - very badly off
   - not too badly off
   - rather badly off
   - financially badly off
   
   **Root Analysis**: badly (adverb indicating unsatisfactory state) + off (indicating condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Parallel formation to "well off," using "badly" to indicate the opposite condition. More common in British English from the 1800s onward.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're quite badly off these days since he lost his job.
   - We were badly off when I was growing up, but we managed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Families who are badly off often struggle to access basic healthcare services.
   - The report identified communities that are particularly badly off in terms of infrastructure.

1. **Adjective**: switched off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) Not operating; deactivated or turned to the off position (non-gradable, predicative or attributive)
   - (2) Not paying attention; mentally disengaged or uninterested (gradable, predicative, informal)
   
   **Synonyms**: turned off, deactivated, shut down, powered down; (sense 2) disengaged, inattentive, zoned out
   
   **Antonyms**: switched on, activated, turned on, operating; (sense 2) engaged, attentive, alert
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Universal for describing electrical/electronic devices in off state. (2) Informal contexts to describe lack of mental engagement or awareness.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (2) Avoid in formal contexts where precise psychological terms are needed. Don't confuse with "turned off" (disgusted by something).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - completely switched off
   - switched off at the mains
   - mentally switched off
   - switched off mode
   - lights switched off
   
   **Root Analysis**: switch (verb from Dutch "swijken" = bend, turn) + -ed (past participle) + off (indicating cessation)
   
   **Etymology**: "Switch" entered English in the 1500s, but "switch off" emerged with electrical technology in the late 1800s. The metaphorical sense of mental disengagement developed in the mid-1900s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure everything is switched off before you leave the house.
   - I was completely switched off during that boring lecture.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All electronic devices must be switched off during takeoff and landing.
   - The system automatically enters a switched off state after a period of inactivity.

1. **Adjective**: turned off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) Not operating; deactivated or rotated to the off position (non-gradable, predicative)
   - (2) Disgusted, repelled, or losing interest in something (gradable, predicative, informal)
   
   **Synonyms**: switched off, deactivated, shut down; (sense 2) repelled, disgusted, put off, disinterested
   
   **Antonyms**: turned on, activated, operating; (sense 2) attracted, interested, engaged
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Universal for devices with rotary controls or general deactivation. (2) Informal contexts to express loss of interest or attraction, often related to romantic/sexual disgust.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (2) Avoid in professional contexts due to informal and sometimes sexual connotations. Be careful of the negative emotional implications.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - turned off by
   - completely turned off
   - turned off the lights
   - turned off at the source
   - totally turned off
   
   **Root Analysis**: turn (Old English "tyrnan" = rotate) + -ed (past participle) + off (indicating cessation)
   
   **Etymology**: "Turn off" as a phrasal verb developed in the 1800s with gas lamps and later electricity. The figurative sense of losing attraction emerged in the 1960s in informal American English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was turned off by his arrogant attitude on the first date.
   - All the lights should be turned off when you lock up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The water supply must be turned off at the main valve before repairs begin.
   - Consumer interest has been turned off by recent quality control issues.

1. **Adjective**: cut off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) Isolated or separated from others; lacking access to communication, supplies, or contact (gradable, predicative)
   - (2) Disconnected from a service due to non-payment (non-gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: isolated, separated, disconnected, stranded, severed; (sense 2) disconnected, terminated
   
   **Antonyms**: connected, accessible, in contact, supplied
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage. Common for describing physical isolation, communication breakdown, utility disconnection, or social exclusion. Important in emergency and geographic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be careful with social contexts as "cut off" can imply rejection or abandonment, which may sound harsh.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - completely cut off
   - cut off from civilization
   - cut off from family
   - feel cut off
   - geographically cut off
   
   **Root Analysis**: cut (Old English "cyttan") + off (indicating separation/removal)
   
   **Etymology**: "Cut off" has been used since Middle English to indicate separation or amputation. Extended to mean social isolation and utility disconnection in modern usage.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We were completely cut off during the snowstorm—no phone, no internet, nothing.
   - My phone got cut off because I forgot to pay the bill.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Remote villages remain cut off from essential services during the monsoon season.
   - The electricity supply will be cut off if payment is not received within 14 days.

1. **Adjective**: laid off
   **Meaning**: 
   - Dismissed from employment due to lack of work, economic conditions, or organizational restructuring rather than personal performance (non-gradable, predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: dismissed, made redundant (British), let go, terminated, downsized
   
   **Antonyms**: employed, hired, recruited, retained
   
   **When to Use**: Universal, especially in employment and economic contexts. More neutral than "fired" as it doesn't imply fault or poor performance. Essential in labor market discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "fired" (dismissal for cause). "Laid off" specifically indicates economic/organizational reasons, not employee misconduct.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - recently laid off
   - temporarily laid off
   - permanently laid off
   - laid off workers
   - laid off due to
   
   **Root Analysis**: lay (Old English "lecgan" = put down) + off (indicating removal)
   
   **Etymology**: "Lay off" originated as a nautical term meaning to move away from shore. The employment sense emerged in the early 1900s during industrial restructuring periods.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My brother got laid off last month when the company downsized.
   - Thousands of workers were laid off during the recession.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The corporation announced that 500 employees would be laid off as part of a cost-reduction initiative.
   - Laid off workers are entitled to severance packages under the collective bargaining agreement.

1. **Adjective**: put off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) Postponed or delayed to a later time (non-gradable, predicative)
   - (2) Discouraged, repelled, or disinclined toward something (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: postponed, delayed, deferred, rescheduled; (sense 2) discouraged, deterred, dissuaded, repelled
   
   **Antonyms**: proceeded, advanced, encouraged; (sense 2) attracted, encouraged, inspired
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Universal for describing postponed events or decisions. (2) Common when expressing that something has created negative feelings or reduced enthusiasm.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (2) Be careful as "put off" can range from mild discouragement to strong repulsion depending on context and intensity.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - put off until later
   - put off by
   - easily put off
   - put off indefinitely
   - completely put off
   
   **Root Analysis**: put (Old English "putian" = push) + off (indicating delay/removal)
   
   **Etymology**: "Put off" has been used since the 1300s to mean postpone. The sense of being repelled or discouraged developed in the 1800s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was really put off by how rude the staff were at that restaurant.
   - The meeting has been put off till next week because the boss is traveling.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The decision has been put off pending further consultation with stakeholders.
   - Many investors are put off by the high level of volatility in emerging markets.

1. **Adjective**: called off
   **Meaning**: 
   - Cancelled or terminated; no longer taking place as planned (non-gradable, predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: cancelled, terminated, abandoned, scrapped, discontinued
   
   **Antonyms**: confirmed, proceeding, scheduled, going ahead
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage. Common for describing cancelled events, strikes, searches, engagements, or plans. Often used when cancellation happens close to the scheduled time.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Typically used for events or activities that were actively planned or in progress, not for things that simply didn't happen.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - called off due to
   - completely called off
   - called off the strike
   - called off the search
   - wedding called off
   
   **Root Analysis**: call (Old Norse "kalla" = cry out) + off (indicating cessation)
   
   **Etymology**: "Call off" emerged in the 1600s, originally meaning to summon away (especially dogs from a chase). Extended to mean cancel or terminate in the 1800s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The game was called off because of rain.
   - They called off their engagement after dating for three years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The strike was called off after management agreed to negotiate wage increases.
   - Search and rescue operations were called off when darkness fell.

1. **Adjective**: closed off
   **Meaning**: 
   - (1) Blocked, sealed, or made inaccessible (non-gradable, predicative or attributive)
   - (2) Emotionally withdrawn or unwilling to communicate feelings (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: sealed, blocked, barricaded, shut off; (sense 2) withdrawn, guarded, emotionally unavailable, reserved
   
   **Antonyms**: open, accessible, available; (sense 2) open, expressive, communicative
   
   **When to Use**: (1) Physical barriers or access restrictions. (2) Informal contexts describing emotional unavailability or communication difficulties, often in relationship discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (2) In formal psychological contexts, use more precise terms like "emotionally guarded" or "interpersonally avoidant."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - closed off to traffic
   - completely closed off
   - emotionally closed off
   - closed off from the world
   - area closed off
   
   **Root Analysis**: close (Latin "clausus" = shut) + -ed (past participle) + off (indicating separation)
   
   **Etymology**: "Close off" combines "close" (from Latin "claudere" = shut) with "off" to emphasize complete separation. The emotional sense emerged in popular psychology discourse in the late 1900s.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That whole section of the highway is closed off for repairs.
   - He's been really closed off since the breakup—won't talk about his feelings at all.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The area was closed off to the public following the discovery of structural damage.
   - Patients exhibiting trauma may appear emotionally closed off as a protective mechanism.

1. **Adjective**: brushed off
   **Meaning**: 
   - Dismissed, ignored, or treated as unimportant; rejected casually or without proper consideration (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: dismissed, ignored, disregarded, rejected, shrugged off, waved away
   
   **Antonyms**: considered seriously, addressed, acknowledged, taken seriously
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to semi-formal contexts. Common when describing someone's concerns, suggestions, or presence being dismissed dismissively or rudely. Often implies lack of respect.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in formal reports where neutral language is required. Has negative connotations of dismissiveness or disrespect.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - completely brushed off
   - easily brushed off
   - brushed off as insignificant
   - concerns brushed off
   - casually brushed off
   
   **Root Analysis**: brush (Middle English "brushe") + -ed (past participle) + off (indicating removal)
   
   **Etymology**: Originally referred to physically removing something with a brush. The figurative sense of dismissing someone or their ideas developed in the early 1900s, suggesting casual removal like brushing dust off clothing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tried to tell him about the problem, but I was totally brushed off.
   - Don't let them brush off your concerns like they don't matter.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employee complaints were brushed off by management, leading to increased tensions.
   - The initial warnings were brushed off as alarmist, delaying the response.
