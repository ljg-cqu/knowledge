1. **Functional Expression**: Let's work together on [X] (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A suggestion to collaborate or cooperate on a task or project
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (softened by "let's")
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's collaborate on [X], How about we work on [X] together, Why don't we tackle [X] together, Shall we work together on [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Team settings, peer relationships, when proposing collaboration on projects or tasks; appropriate for most professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When addressing superiors (use more formal suggestion); when unilateral decision has already been made; in competitive contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's team up on [X] (more casual)
   - I suggest we work together on [X] (more formal)
   - What if we work together on [X]? (more tentative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses positive politeness strategy by including speaker in the action ("let's"); minimizes imposition by framing as joint decision
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Sounds good," "Great idea," "I'm in," "Let's do it"
   - Declining: "I appreciate the offer, but...", "I'm not sure I can commit to that," "Can I think about it?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's work together on the presentation—I think we'd make a good team.
   - Hey, let's work together on this project. Two heads are better than one!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let's work together on developing the strategic framework for the initiative.
   - I propose we work together on this analysis to ensure comprehensive coverage.

1. **Functional Expression**: I think we should [collaborate/work together] (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing opinion that collaborative action would be beneficial
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (hedged by "I think")
   
   **Synonyms**: I believe we should work together, In my view we should collaborate, It seems to me we should team up, I feel we should join forces
   
   **When to Use**: When offering reasoned suggestion, professional settings, when hedging is appropriate to minimize imposition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When decisive direction is needed; when authority should be clear; in urgent situations requiring direct instruction
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I think we could work together on this (more tentative)
   - I strongly think we should collaborate (more emphatic)
   - I'm thinking we should work together (more casual)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedges with "I think" to present as opinion rather than directive; shows respect for others' autonomy; negative politeness strategy
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "I agree," "That makes sense," "Good thinking," "You're right"
   - Declining: "I see your point, but...", "I'm not convinced that...", "Have you considered..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think we should work together on this—it's too much for one person.
   - I really think we should team up. It'll be more fun that way!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I think we should collaborate on this initiative to leverage our complementary expertise.
   - Given the complexity, I think we should work together to ensure comprehensive analysis.

1. **Functional Expression**: Would you like to [work together/collaborate] on [X]? (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely requesting someone's participation in collaborative work
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (question form with modal "would")
   
   **Synonyms**: Would you be interested in working together on [X], Are you interested in collaborating on [X], Would you want to team up on [X], Care to work together on [X]
   
   **When to Use**: When inviting collaboration from someone, appropriate for various professional contexts, shows respect for other person's choice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When collaboration is mandatory; when asking superior (may be too presumptuous); in casual settings with close friends (too formal)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would you be willing to work together on [X]? (more formal, acknowledges effort)
   - Want to work together on [X]? (much more casual)
   - Would it be possible to work together on [X]? (even more deferential)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses negative politeness by acknowledging other person's autonomy; modal "would" adds formality and politeness; question form invites rather than imposes
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "I'd love to," "Yes, that sounds great," "Absolutely," "I'm interested"
   - Declining: "I'd like to, but I don't have capacity," "That's not really my area," "Can I get back to you?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would you like to work together on this? I could really use your expertise.
   - Hey, would you like to team up on the event planning? It's a lot more fun with two people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would you like to collaborate on this research project? Your expertise would be invaluable.
   - I'm wondering if you would like to work together on developing the proposal.

1. **Functional Expression**: I was wondering if we could [work together/collaborate] on [X] (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very polite, indirect request for collaboration
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: very indirect (past progressive hedge + conditional)
   
   **Synonyms**: I was thinking we might work together on [X], I wanted to ask if we could collaborate on [X], I was hoping we could team up on [X]
   
   **When to Use**: When making polite request to someone you don't know well, when there's power distance, when you want to be particularly respectful of their time
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With close colleagues (too formal), in urgent situations (too indirect), when you need direct response (ambiguity problematic)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I was wondering if you'd be interested in working together on [X] (even more indirect)
   - I'm wondering if we could collaborate on [X] (present progressive, slightly less formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Multiple hedges ("was wondering," "could") create maximum politeness; shows deference and respects autonomy; strong negative politeness strategy
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Of course," "I'd be happy to," "That would be great," "Certainly"
   - Declining: "I appreciate you thinking of me, but...", "I'm afraid I can't...", "That's not possible right now, but..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was wondering if we could work together on this—I think your skills would really complement mine.
   - Hey, I was wondering if you'd want to team up on the charity event?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I was wondering if we could collaborate on this research initiative. Your expertise in the field would be most valuable.
   - I was wondering if we could work together on developing the strategic framework for the department.

1. **Functional Expression**: We need to [coordinate/work together] on this (function: expressing necessity)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Stating that collaborative action is necessary or required
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (modal "need" indicates necessity)
   
   **Synonyms**: We have to work together on this, We must coordinate on this, It's necessary to collaborate on this, We'll need to team up on this
   
   **When to Use**: When collaborative work is genuinely required, project planning, when authority or expertise justifies directive tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When collaboration is optional (too strong), when making initial invitation (too pushy), with superiors (too presumptuous)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We really need to work together on this (more emphatic)
   - We probably need to collaborate on this (softened with "probably")
   - We should work together on this (less direct, recommendation vs. necessity)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Direct expression of necessity; appropriate when speaker has authority or when situation genuinely requires it; can sound bossy if overused
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Agreed," "You're right," "Absolutely," "Let's set it up"
   - Questioning: "Why is that necessary?", "What makes you say that?", "Can you explain more?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to work together on this or we're never going to finish on time.
   - Guys, we really need to coordinate on this. It's getting messy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We need to coordinate our efforts on this initiative to ensure consistent messaging.
   - Given the project complexity, we need to work together systematically to achieve our objectives.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd be happy to [work with you/collaborate with you] on [X] (function: offering)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offering one's willingness and eagerness to collaborate
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (clear offer) with politeness marker
   
   **Synonyms**: I'd be glad to work with you on [X], I'd love to collaborate with you on [X], I'm happy to team up on [X], I'd be willing to work together on [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Responding to requests for collaboration, volunteering assistance, building positive relationships, showing enthusiasm
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When actually reluctant (insincere), when you genuinely cannot commit (misleading), in highly competitive contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd be more than happy to work with you on [X] (more emphatic)
   - I'm happy to work together on [X] (present tense, slightly more casual)
   - I'd be delighted to collaborate with you on [X] (more formal, enthusiastic)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Happy to" signals positive attitude and willingness; builds rapport through enthusiasm; positive politeness strategy showing eagerness to cooperate
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That's great!", "Thank you so much," "Perfect, let's set it up," "Wonderful"
   - Follow-up: "When can you start?", "What's your availability?", "How should we proceed?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd be happy to work with you on the project—sounds like fun!
   - Sure, I'm happy to team up on this with you anytime.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'd be happy to collaborate with you on this research initiative.
   - I'd be delighted to work with you on developing the proposal for the committee.

1. **Functional Expression**: Why don't we [work together/team up] on [X]? (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Making a suggestion framed as a question about lack of obstacles
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (negative question form)
   
   **Synonyms**: How about we work together on [X], What if we collaborate on [X], Shall we team up on [X], Maybe we could work together on [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Casual to neutral professional settings, when making friendly suggestions, brainstorming, peer relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal presentations or documents, when addressing superiors (too casual), in situations requiring clear directives
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Why don't we just team up on this? (more casual, "just" minimizes effort)
   - Why don't we try working together on [X]? (more tentative with "try")
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative question form implies there's no reason not to do it; friendly and collegial tone; positive politeness strategy assuming shared goals
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Good idea," "Yeah, let's do it," "Sounds good to me," "That works"
   - Declining: "Actually, I think...", "I'm not sure that would work because...", "I'd rather..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why don't we team up on this? It'll go way faster with two of us.
   - Hey, why don't we work together on the party planning? More fun that way.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Why don't we work together on this component of the project to ensure integration?
   - Why don't we collaborate on the analysis to leverage our different perspectives?

1. **Functional Expression**: It would be great if we could [work together/collaborate] on [X] (function: expressing wish/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing that collaboration would be positive or desirable
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (conditional, expresses wish rather than direct request)
   
   **Synonyms**: It would be good if we could work together on [X], It would be beneficial to collaborate on [X], It would help if we worked together on [X]
   
   **When to Use**: When suggesting collaboration while acknowledging it may not be possible, maintaining diplomatic tone, professional settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When collaboration is necessary (too weak), in urgent situations (too indirect), when clear commitment is needed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It would be really great if we could work together on [X] (more emphatic)
   - It might be good if we could collaborate on [X] (more tentative)
   - It'd be awesome if we could team up on [X] (more casual and enthusiastic)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conditional "would" keeps suggestion hypothetical; acknowledges autonomy and possibility of refusal; negative politeness through indirectness
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "I think so too," "Let's make it happen," "I'd like that," "Agreed"
   - Declining/Uncertain: "It would, but...", "I'm not sure my schedule allows...", "In principle yes, but..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It would be great if we could work together on this—I think we'd complement each other well.
   - It'd be awesome if we could team up on the fundraiser. Way more fun!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - It would be great if we could collaborate on this research to produce a comprehensive analysis.
   - It would be beneficial if we could work together on developing the strategic framework.

   **Meaning**:

   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Sure," "Yeah, let's do that," "Good idea," "I'm in"

1. **Functional Expression**: We should [coordinate/align] our efforts (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggesting that coordinated action would be beneficial
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (recommendation with "should" rather than necessity)
   
   **Synonyms**: We ought to coordinate our efforts, We need to align our work, It would be good to coordinate, We should synchronize our activities
   
   **When to Use**: Professional settings, project planning, when recommending coordination, team meetings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual settings with friends (too formal), when urgent directive needed (too soft), very formal documents (consider stronger language)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We should probably coordinate our efforts (softened with "probably")
   - We really should align our efforts (emphasized with "really")
   - We should work together to coordinate efforts (more explicit about collaboration)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Should" indicates recommendation/advice rather than requirement; implies reasoned judgment; maintains professional tone
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "I agree," "Good point," "Yes, we should," "Let's do that"
   - Questioning: "How do you propose we do that?", "What did you have in mind?", "In what way?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should really coordinate our efforts here—too many things are falling through the cracks.
   - Guys, we should align our efforts on this or it's going to be a mess.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We should coordinate our efforts to ensure consistent implementation across departments.
   - Given the project scope, we should align our efforts to maximize efficiency and avoid duplication.
