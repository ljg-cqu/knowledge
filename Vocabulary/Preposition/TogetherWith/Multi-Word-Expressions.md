1. **Expression**: work together (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To cooperate or collaborate with others toward a common goal
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very natural partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: collaborate, cooperate, team up, join forces, work in partnership
   
   **Antonyms**: work independently, work alone, work separately, compete
   
   **When to Use**: Any context requiring cooperation—professional projects, team activities, social endeavors; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing individual work or competitive situations
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - work closely together
   - work well together
   - work together on [something]
   - working together as a team
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Work" and "together" naturally combine to express cooperative action; high frequency of co-occurrence in cooperative contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: work (Old English weorc) + together (Old English tōgædere)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's work together on this—it'll be more fun!
   - We work together really well; our skills complement each other.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The departments must work together to achieve organizational objectives.
   - Research teams work together to advance scientific knowledge.

1. **Expression**: team up (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To join with another person or group to work together, especially for a specific purpose
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: partner up, join forces, collaborate, work together, pair up
   
   **Antonyms**: work alone, go solo, split up, separate
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to neutral contexts, suggesting partnership for specific purpose; common in workplace and social settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal documents (use "collaborate"), when long-term permanent partnership is meant
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - team up with [someone]
   - team up on [project]
   - team up to [do something]
   - teaming up together
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Team" as verb + "up" particle creates action-oriented expression for forming partnerships; sports and informal business influence
   
   **Root Analysis**: team (Old English team meaning group) + up (particle indicating completion/formation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Want to team up for the presentation? We'd be unstoppable.
   - Let's team up and knock this project out of the park.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations can team up to address complex social challenges.
   - Researchers team up across institutions to conduct large-scale studies.

1. **Expression**: join forces (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To unite efforts with others to achieve a common goal; to combine strength or resources
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiomatic expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: unite, team up, combine efforts, work together, collaborate
   
   **Antonyms**: work separately, split up, go separate ways, compete
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing strength through unity, dramatic or motivational contexts; neutral to informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts (may sound dramatic), highly technical or academic writing (use "collaborate")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - join forces with [someone]
   - join forces to [achieve something]
   - joining forces together
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Military origin referring to combining military units; metaphorically extended to any cooperative effort requiring combined strength
   
   **Root Analysis**: join (Latin jungere, to yoke) + forces (Latin fortis, strong)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's join forces and show them what we can do!
   - When we join forces, nothing can stop us.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations joined forces to advocate for policy reform.
   - Public and private sectors can join forces to address infrastructure challenges.
