1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In the company of; accompanied by; alongside
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating association, cooperation, or relationship between entities
   - *(Abstract)* Having; possessing (characteristics, qualities)
   
   **Synonyms**: alongside (in context of cooperation), together with, along with, in company with
   
   **Antonyms**: without, against, versus, apart from
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating accompaniment, cooperation, possession, association; extremely versatile across all contexts and registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When clear separation needs emphasis (use "without"); when opposition is meant (use "against")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with [person/group], with [quality], with [tool/means]
   - **Verb + prep**: work with, cooperate with, collaborate with, partner with
   - **Adjective + prep**: compatible with, consistent with, associated with
   - **Noun + prep**: relationship with, partnership with, connection with
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "wið" (against, toward, with)
   
   **Etymology**: Proto-Germanic *wiþra (against) → Old English wið → Middle English with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm working with Sarah on the project.
   - Want to come with us to the movies?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization collaborates with international partners on research initiatives.
   - This approach is consistent with established best practices.

1. **Preposition**: together with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In combination with; along with; accompanied by
   - *(Abstract)* As well as; in addition to
   
   **Synonyms**: along with, in conjunction with, coupled with, as well as, in addition to
   
   **Antonyms**: apart from, separate from, without, excluding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts, adding supplementary information while maintaining focus on primary subject; written communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (formal), when both elements should be equally emphasized as subjects (use "and")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: [Subject] together with [additional element]
   - **Verb + prep**: works together with, combines together with
   - **Adjective + prep**: associated together with
   - **Noun + prep**: [main noun] together with [supplementary noun]
   
   **Root Analysis**: to- (toward) + gather (assemble) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Old English tōgædere (together) + with → phrasal preposition together with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The manager, together with the team, decided to postpone the launch.
   - My skills, together with yours, make for a strong combination.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee, together with external advisors, developed the strategic framework.
   - This methodology, together with supporting analysis, provides comprehensive understanding.

1. **Preposition**: alongside (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Next to; by the side of
   - *(Abstract)* In cooperation with; together with; in addition to
   
   **Synonyms**: beside, next to (spatial); together with, in conjunction with, in partnership with (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: opposite, across from (spatial); against, in competition with (abstract)
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating cooperation or parallel action; neutral to formal registers; both spatial and abstract meanings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When clear hierarchy rather than equality should be emphasized; very formal contexts may prefer "in conjunction with"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: alongside [person/group], work alongside, operate alongside
   - **Verb + prep**: function alongside, serve alongside, develop alongside
   - **Adjective + prep**: positioned alongside, placed alongside
   - **Noun + prep**: partnership alongside, collaboration alongside
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + long (beside) + side
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English along + side → alongside (compound preposition, 1700s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll be working alongside you on this project.
   - She stood alongside her colleagues in support of the decision.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The department functions alongside partner organizations to deliver services.
   - This initiative operates alongside existing programs to enhance impact.

1. **Preposition**: among (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Surrounded by; in the middle of; in the company of (three or more)
   - *(Abstract)* Within a group; shared by members of a group; in the number or class of
   
   **Synonyms**: amongst, amidst, amid, within, between (when more than two)
   
   **Antonyms**: outside, beyond, apart from, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating membership or position within a group (three or more); showing shared relationships; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to only two entities (use "between"); when exclusion from group is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: among [group], among [plural noun], among themselves
   - **Verb + prep**: distribute among, share among, collaborate among
   - **Adjective + prep**: common among, popular among, divided among
   - **Noun + prep**: cooperation among, consensus among, relationship among
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "on gemang" (in the crowd)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English on gemang → Middle English among → Modern English among
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need better cooperation among team members.
   - This issue is common among groups working remotely.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Collaboration among stakeholders is essential for program success.
   - The study examines relationships among multiple organizational variables.

1. **Preposition**: between (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the space separating two things, people, or places
   - *(Abstract)* Involving two parties; shared by two entities; connecting two elements
   - *(Temporal)* In the interval separating two points in time
   
   **Synonyms**: betwixt (archaic), among (when more than two, though traditionally "between" for two)
   
   **Antonyms**: outside, beyond, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating relationship or position involving two entities; showing connection or sharing; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to three or more (traditionally use "among," though "between" is increasingly accepted)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: between [entity] and [entity], between [two parties]
   - **Verb + prep**: collaborate between, coordinate between, share between
   - **Adjective + prep**: divided between, split between, shared between
   - **Noun + prep**: relationship between, partnership between, cooperation between, link between
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "be-" (by) + "twēonum" (two each)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English betwēonum → Middle English betwene → Modern English between
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need better communication between our teams.
   - The partnership between the two companies is working well.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Coordination between departments enhances organizational effectiveness.
   - The study examines relationships between collaborative processes and outcomes.

1. **Preposition**: through (type: simple)

   - **Verb + prep**: work through, collaborate through, coordinate through, achieve through
   - **Adjective + prep**: achievable through, possible through
   - **Noun + prep**: success through, collaboration through, coordination through

   **Etymology**: Proto-Germanic *thurkh → Old English þurh → Modern English through
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Preposition**: within (type: simple)

   - *(Temporal)* Before the end of; not exceeding

   **Antonyms**: outside, beyond, external to, exceeding

   - There's great cooperation within our group.

   **Examples [Formal]**:
