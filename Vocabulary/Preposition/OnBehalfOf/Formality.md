1. **Original Expression**: I'm calling on behalf of Dr. Smith to schedule an appointment.  
   **Context**: Business/medical context - receptionist or assistant making phone call for a professional
   
   **Core Intent**: To identify oneself as a representative making contact for another person
   
   **Casual**: I'm calling for Dr. Smith—she needs to set up an appointment.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple "for" instead of "on behalf of," conversational tone, contracted verb  
   *Use when*: Informal business settings, calling for family/friends, relaxed professional relationships
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'm calling on behalf of Dr. Smith to arrange an appointment.  
   *Characteristics*: Full "on behalf of" phrase, professional vocabulary, clear purpose statement  
   *Use when*: Standard business calls, medical/professional offices, polite distance maintained
   
   **Formal**: I am calling on behalf of Dr. Smith to request an appointment at your earliest convenience.  
   *Characteristics*: No contractions, formal request language ("at your earliest convenience"), complete prepositional phrase  
   *Use when*: Executive assistance, legal/medical professionals, first contact with high-status individuals
   
   **Humorous**: I'm the official phone-call-maker for Dr. Smith—can we get her an appointment?  
   *Characteristics*: Self-deprecating job description, playful tone while maintaining professional purpose  
   *Use when*: Rapport already established, friendly office culture, casual professional relationships  
   *Avoid when*: First contact, serious medical emergencies, formal corporate settings
   
   **Lively**: Hi! Dr. Smith asked me to reach out about getting her scheduled for an appointment—when works for you?  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic greeting, active verbs ("reach out"), collaborative tone ("when works for you")  
   *Use when*: Friendly business relationships, customer service contexts, peer-level interactions
   
   **Empathetic**: I'm calling on behalf of Dr. Smith, who's been hoping to schedule an appointment with you.  
   *Characteristics*: Personal connection ("hoping to"), acknowledges principal's wishes, respectful tone  
   *Use when*: Medical appointments, senior professionals, contexts where personal touch matters
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status of principal requires formal; equal status allows casual
   - *Social Distance*: First contact with stranger requires formal; established relationship allows casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Medical/legal contexts lean formal; routine scheduling allows semi-formal
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more formality in professional representation
   - *Medium*: Phone calls allow slightly more informality than written correspondence
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual "for" in very formal legal/diplomatic contexts (sounds insufficiently respectful)
   - Overusing "on behalf of" in casual settings (sounds pompous or bureaucratic)
   - Forgetting to identify the principal clearly, leaving recipient confused about who you represent
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American English tends toward informality in business contexts compared to British English or Asian business cultures, where "on behalf of" is preferred even in routine matters
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Calling law firm to schedule consultation for a celebrity client → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("I am calling on behalf of...") to show respect and professionalism
   - **Situation 2**: Texting friend's workplace to say she'll be late → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("Hey, calling for Sarah—she's running late")
   - **Situation 3**: Executive assistant calling another executive's office → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal (standard professional courtesy expected)

1. **Original Expression**: On behalf of the entire team, I want to thank you for your hard work.  
   **Context**: Workplace recognition - manager or team member expressing gratitude representing the group
   
   **Core Intent**: To express collective gratitude while identifying oneself as the spokesperson
   
   **Casual**: Everyone wanted me to say thanks for all your hard work!  
   *Characteristics*: Simple language ("everyone"), exclamation mark for warmth, "wanted me to say" as casual agency marker  
   *Use when*: Team member to team member, informal workplace culture, friendly relationships
   
   **Semi-Formal**: On behalf of the team, thank you for your hard work on this project.  
   *Characteristics*: Standard "on behalf of," direct gratitude expression, specific reference to work  
   *Use when*: Manager to employee, professional settings, balanced formality with warmth
   
   **Formal**: On behalf of the entire team, I wish to express our sincere gratitude for your exceptional dedication and effort.  
   *Characteristics*: "Wish to express," "sincere gratitude," elevated vocabulary ("exceptional dedication")  
   *Use when*: Senior management, written communications, award ceremonies, public recognition
   
   **Humorous**: The team made me promise to thank you—apparently they think you're some kind of superhero or something!  
   *Characteristics*: Self-deprecating messenger role, playful exaggeration ("superhero"), light tone  
   *Use when*: Close team relationships, celebratory contexts, after successful project completion  
   *Avoid when*: Performance reviews, formal awards, situations requiring professional gravitas
   
   **Lively**: On behalf of everyone, we're so grateful for your amazing work—you absolutely crushed it!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic intensifiers ("so grateful," "amazing"), contemporary slang ("crushed it")  
   *Use when*: Young professionals, creative industries, celebrating major achievements, team victories
   
   **Empathetic**: On behalf of the team, I want you to know how much your hard work has meant to all of us during this challenging time.  
   *Characteristics*: "Want you to know," emotional acknowledgment ("meant to all of us"), context awareness ("challenging time")  
   *Use when*: Difficult projects, personal sacrifices made, stressful periods, showing genuine appreciation
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Manager to subordinate allows more range; peer to peer can be more casual
   - *Social Distance*: Long-term colleagues allow casual; newer relationships require more formality
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Major achievements or sacrifices warrant more formal recognition
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect hierarchical formality even in thanks
   - *Medium*: Written thanks (email, card) typically more formal than verbal; public recognition most formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using humor in recognition when recipient made significant personal sacrifices (seems dismissive)
   - Being too casual in formal recognition events like awards ceremonies (undermines significance)
   - Forgetting to actually specify what you're thanking the person for (generic and impersonal)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American workplace culture increasingly values enthusiasm and casual warmth in recognition, while European and Asian business cultures often maintain more formal gratitude expressions
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Annual company awards ceremony with CEO present → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (dignifies achievement and respects occasion)
   - **Situation 2**: Team celebration after successful product launch → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively (matches celebratory mood and team camaraderie)
   - **Situation 3**: Written thank-you email from manager after employee worked overtime during crisis → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic (acknowledges sacrifice and shows genuine care)

1. **Original Expression**: I am authorized to act on behalf of my client in this matter.  
   **Context**: Legal representation - attorney asserting authority to represent client
   
   **Core Intent**: To establish legal authority to represent and make decisions for another party
   
   **Casual**: I'm here for my client—I can handle everything for them.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple "for," conversational confidence ("can handle"), informal pronoun ("them")  
   *Use when*: Explaining representation to friends/family, very informal legal consultations  
   *NOTE*: Rarely appropriate in actual legal contexts
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'm authorized to represent my client in this matter.  
   *Characteristics*: "Authorized," "represent," clear scope ("this matter"), professional without excessive formality  
   *Use when*: Standard legal consultations, business negotiations, professional correspondence
   
   **Formal**: I am duly authorized to act on behalf of my client with respect to all matters pertaining to this proceeding.  
   *Characteristics*: "Duly authorized," "with respect to," "pertaining to," comprehensive scope statement, no contractions  
   *Use when*: Court proceedings, formal legal documents, written retainer agreements, depositions
   
   **Humorous**: [Not appropriate for this expression type]  
   *Avoid when*: Legal representation requires professional credibility; humor undermines authority and client interests
   
   **Lively**: I'm fully authorized and ready to represent my client throughout this process.  
   *Characteristics*: Confident tone ("fully," "ready"), active engagement implied  
   *Use when*: Client meetings to inspire confidence, mediation sessions, settlement conferences (where relationships matter)
   
   **Empathetic**: I'm here to represent my client and ensure their interests are protected throughout this process.  
   *Characteristics*: Protective language ("ensure," "protected"), client-centered focus ("their interests")  
   *Use when*: Vulnerable clients (family law, personal injury), first meetings to establish trust, emotionally charged situations
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Court requires formal; client meetings allow semi-formal; opposing counsel typically formal
   - *Social Distance*: All legal representation maintains professional distance; formality protects client
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Criminal proceedings most formal; small claims or mediation can be semi-formal
   - *Cultural Context*: Legal system demands formality to protect rights and establish authority
   - *Medium*: Written documents most formal; verbal statements in court formal; client meetings less formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being too casual in court or formal proceedings (undermines professionalism and harms client)
   - Failing to clearly establish scope of authority (can lead to unauthorized representation issues)
   - Using humor in serious legal matters (inappropriate and damages credibility)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Legal representation is universally formal across cultures due to rights protection, though degree of formality varies (UK/Commonwealth more formal than US; civil law systems very formal)
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: First appearance in court for client → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (establishes credibility and protects client interests)
   - **Situation 2**: Initial client consultation to explain representation → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic (builds trust while maintaining professionalism)
   - **Situation 3**: Email to opposing counsel asserting representation → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (protects client and establishes professional boundaries)

1. **Original Expression**: The ambassador spoke on behalf of her country.  
   **Context**: Diplomatic/governmental - official representative making statements for a nation
   
   **Core Intent**: To attribute statements or actions to official governmental representative speaking for a nation
   
   **Casual**: The ambassador spoke for her country.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple "for," casual verb, basic structure  
   *Use when*: Informal news summaries, casual conversation about diplomacy, explanatory contexts
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The ambassador represented her country's position on the matter.  
   *Characteristics*: "Represented" instead of "spoke for," "position" adds precision, "on the matter" provides context  
   *Use when*: News articles, educational contexts, professional diplomatic discussions
   
   **Formal**: The ambassador delivered remarks on behalf of her government regarding the bilateral agreement.  
   *Characteristics*: "Delivered remarks," "government" (more formal than "country"), specific context ("bilateral agreement")  
   *Use when*: Official press releases, diplomatic communiqués, formal news reporting, governmental documents
   
   **Humorous**: [Not appropriate for this expression type]  
   *Avoid when*: Diplomatic representation is serious governmental function; humor inappropriate in this context
   
   **Lively**: The ambassador passionately articulated her country's stance on the issue.  
   *Characteristics*: Evaluative adverb ("passionately"), stronger verb ("articulated"), "stance" suggests conviction  
   *Use when*: Feature journalism, opinion pieces, contexts emphasizing diplomatic advocacy or conviction
   
   **Empathetic**: The ambassador spoke on behalf of her nation, expressing deep concerns about the humanitarian crisis.  
   *Characteristics*: "Expressing deep concerns," "humanitarian crisis," emotional dimension acknowledged  
   *Use when*: Humanitarian issues, crisis response, contexts where emotional stakes are high and acknowledged
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Official governmental representation always maintains formality
   - *Social Distance*: Diplomatic contexts require professional distance and formal language
   - *Gravity of Situation*: All diplomatic representation is grave; international relations stakes are high
   - *Cultural Context*: Diplomatic protocol demands formality across all cultures
   - *Medium*: Official statements most formal; news reporting can be semi-formal; casual discussion least formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual language for official diplomatic statements (undermines gravity of representation)
   - Failing to distinguish between personal views and official governmental positions
   - Overusing "lively" register for serious diplomatic issues (can seem disrespectful)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Diplomatic representation is one of the most universally formal contexts across all cultures; international protocol demands respect for sovereignty and official status
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Official diplomatic communiqué issued through proper channels → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (diplomatic protocol requires highest formality)
   - **Situation 2**: Newspaper article explaining ambassador's speech to general audience → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal (accessible but respectful)
   - **Situation 3**: Friend explaining what happened at UN meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual (conversational explanation acceptable in personal contexts)

1. **Original Expression**: I'm writing on behalf of our organization to request funding.  
   **Context**: Grant writing/fundraising - representative formally requesting resources for an organization
   
   **Core Intent**: To establish representative authority while making a formal request for financial support
   
   **Casual**: I'm writing for our group to ask for funding.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple "for," "group" instead of "organization," "ask" instead of "request"  
   *Use when*: Informal community groups, peer organizations, very small-scale funding requests  
   *NOTE*: Rarely effective for substantial funding requests
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'm writing on behalf of [Organization Name] to request funding for [Project Name].  
   *Characteristics*: Full "on behalf of," specific names, clear purpose, professional structure  
   *Use when*: Standard grant applications, foundation requests, professional nonprofit correspondence
   
   **Formal**: On behalf of [Organization Name], I am writing to respectfully request your consideration of our funding proposal for [Project Name].  
   *Characteristics*: Fronted "on behalf of," "respectfully request," "consideration," "proposal" (elevated vocabulary)  
   *Use when*: Major grant applications, government funding requests, corporate philanthropy, first contact with funders
   
   **Humorous**: [Not appropriate for this expression type]  
   *Avoid when*: Funding requests require professional credibility; humor risks seeming unprofessional or desperate
   
   **Lively**: I'm excited to write on behalf of our organization about an opportunity we'd love your support for!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiasm ("excited," "love"), opportunity framing, engagement-seeking  
   *Use when*: Creative/arts organizations, youth programs, contexts where passion demonstrates commitment  
   *Caution*: Can seem unprofessional to conservative funders
   
   **Empathetic**: I'm writing on behalf of our organization to share the urgent needs of the community we serve and respectfully request your partnership.  
   *Characteristics*: "Urgent needs," "community we serve," "partnership" (collaborative), emotional appeal balanced with respect  
   *Use when*: Human services, humanitarian work, crisis response, funders known to value impact stories
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Funder has power; applicant should use formal or semi-formal register
   - *Social Distance*: New funder relationship requires formal; established relationships allow semi-formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Larger amounts and competitive processes require more formality
   - *Cultural Context*: Corporate funders expect business formality; family foundations vary by culture
   - *Medium*: Written proposals always more formal than verbal pitches; letters of inquiry can be semi-formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being too casual for the amount requested (seems unprofessional or naive)
   - Using overly emotional appeals without professional framing (seems manipulative)
   - Forgetting to clearly establish authority to represent organization (raises credibility questions)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American nonprofit culture increasingly values storytelling and impact narratives, while traditional philanthropy maintains formal proposal structures; international funders vary widely
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Federal grant application for $500,000 → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (competitive process and large amount demand highest professionalism)
   - **Situation 2**: Local foundation with established relationship, $10,000 request → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal (balance professionalism with warmth)
   - **Situation 3**: Arts organization seeking support from known arts patron → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively (shows passion that arts funders value)
