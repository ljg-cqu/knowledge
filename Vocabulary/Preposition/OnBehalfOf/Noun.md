1. **Noun**: representative (C, pl: representatives)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person chosen or appointed to act or speak for another person or group; an agent or delegate
   - *(Sense 2)* A typical example of a particular category or type
   
   **Synonyms**: agent, delegate, spokesperson, proxy, envoy
   
   **Antonyms**: principal (the person represented), opponent, adversary
   
   **When to Use**: Use "representative" when referring to someone authorized to act or speak for others; common in political, legal, business, and organizational contexts; emphasizes official capacity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when referring to the person being represented (that's the "principal" or "constituent"); avoid when informal advocacy rather than official representation is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - authorized representative
   - legal representative
   - sales representative
   - elected representative
   - serve as a representative
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, back) + present (bring before) + -ative (adjective/noun suffix indicating tendency or function)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin repraesentare (to present again, show) → Old French representatif → Middle English "representative" (14th century) → meaning of "person who represents others" emerged in 17th century political contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's the representative for our department at the company meeting.
   - I need to speak with a customer service representative about my order.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legal representative of the estate must sign all documents pertaining to the property transfer.
   - Each member state sent a representative to the international conference on climate policy.

1. **Noun**: agent (C, pl: agents)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who acts on behalf of another person or organization; someone authorized to transact business or negotiate for another
   - *(Sense 2)* A person employed to obtain or distribute information or conduct espionage
   
   **Synonyms**: representative, delegate, proxy, intermediary, broker
   
   **Antonyms**: principal (person represented), client, direct party
   
   **When to Use**: Use "agent" when referring to someone with authority to conduct business, negotiate, or act for another; emphasizes active role and often implies contractual or professional relationship; common in legal, real estate, entertainment, and business contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for ceremonial or spokesperson roles without transactional authority (use "representative" instead); avoid confusion with "secret agent" meaning in general contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - real estate agent
   - insurance agent
   - literary agent
   - authorized agent
   - act as an agent
   
   **Root Analysis**: ag- (do, drive) + -ent (noun suffix indicating one who does)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin agere (to do, drive, act) → Latin agens (doer, actor) → Old French "agent" → Middle English (15th century) → meaning "one who acts for another" developed in commercial/legal contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My agent is negotiating a better contract for me.
   - You'll need to talk to the booking agent about changing your flight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agent, acting on behalf of the seller, presented the purchase offer to all interested parties.
   - Under the power of attorney, she serves as the agent for her elderly mother's financial affairs.

1. **Noun**: spokesperson (C, pl: spokespersons/spokespeople)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who speaks or makes statements on behalf of a group, organization, or another person; an official representative for public communication
   
   **Synonyms**: representative, speaker, mouthpiece (informal), voice, advocate
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (functional role without clear opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "spokesperson" when referring to someone designated to speak to media, public, or make official statements for an organization; emphasizes communication role rather than decision-making authority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for legal representation (use "attorney" or "legal representative"); not appropriate for transactional agency (use "agent"); avoid when decision-making authority is implied
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - company spokesperson
   - official spokesperson
   - serve as spokesperson
   - spokesperson for the organization
   - appoint a spokesperson
   
   **Root Analysis**: spoke(n) (past participle of speak) + -s- (genitive marker) + person (individual)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound word formed in mid-20th century as gender-neutral alternative to "spokesman/spokeswoman" → combines "spoke" (from "speak") + "person" → emphasizes communication function
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The team's spokesperson announced the player's retirement yesterday.
   - Who's the spokesperson for your group presentation?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The White House spokesperson declined to comment on the ongoing investigation.
   - As the designated spokesperson for the coalition, Dr. Lee will address the press conference.

1. **Noun**: delegate (C, pl: delegates)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person chosen or elected to represent others, typically at a conference, convention, or meeting; someone sent with authority to act for others
   - *(Sense 2)* A member of a deliberative assembly representing a constituency
   
   **Synonyms**: representative, envoy, deputy, emissary, proxy
   
   **Antonyms**: constituent (person represented), principal
   
   **When to Use**: Use "delegate" when referring to someone chosen to represent others at meetings, conferences, or conventions; emphasizes selection process and temporary representative status; common in political and organizational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for permanent representative roles (prefer "representative"); not appropriate for legal or business agency (use "agent"); avoid when spokesperson function is primary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - conference delegate
   - elected delegate
   - union delegate
   - delegation of delegates
   - send delegates
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, from) + leg (send, appoint) + -ate (noun/verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin delegare (to send away, assign) → Old French "delegat" → English "delegate" (16th century) → political sense of "representative at assembly" emerged in 18th-19th century American usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm a delegate to the state convention next month.
   - The union is sending three delegates to the negotiation meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Each member organization may appoint two delegates to vote at the annual general assembly.
   - The delegates from seventy nations convened to discuss international trade regulations.

1. **Noun**: proxy (C, pl: proxies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person authorized to act on behalf of another, especially in voting; the authority or power to act for another
   - *(Sense 2)* A document giving such authorization (proxy form)
   
   **Synonyms**: representative, agent, substitute, surrogate, stand-in
   
   **Antonyms**: principal, the person themselves
   
   **When to Use**: Use "proxy" when referring to someone authorized to vote or act in another's place; emphasizes substitution and is especially common in corporate governance, shareholder meetings, and legal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate for spokesperson roles (use "spokesperson"); less common for general representation (use "representative"); can sound technical or legalistic in casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vote by proxy
   - proxy holder
   - proxy statement
   - act as proxy
   - appoint a proxy
   
   **Root Analysis**: prox- (near, next) + -y (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin procuratio (management, administration) → Middle English "procuracie" → contracted to "proxie/proxy" (15th century) → legal meaning "person authorized to act for another" → extended to voting contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't attend the meeting, so my sister will be my proxy.
   - He voted by proxy because he was traveling abroad.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Shareholders unable to attend the annual meeting may designate a proxy to vote on their behalf.
   - The proxy statement must be filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission thirty days prior to the meeting.

1. **Noun**: advocate (C, pl: advocates)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who publicly supports, recommends, or speaks in favor of a cause, policy, or person; one who pleads on behalf of another
   - *(Sense 2)* (Legal) A professional legal representative, especially in Scotland or civil law jurisdictions
   
   **Synonyms**: supporter, champion, proponent, spokesperson, defender
   
   **Antonyms**: opponent, adversary, critic, detractor
   
   **When to Use**: Use "advocate" when referring to someone who actively promotes or defends a cause, person, or policy; emphasizes active support and persuasion; common in activism, legal, and policy contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when neutral representation is meant (use "representative"); not appropriate for business agency (use "agent"); implies partisan support, so avoid when objectivity is required
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strong advocate
   - advocate for change
   - patient advocate
   - advocate on behalf of
   - consumer advocate
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + voc (call, voice) + -ate (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin advocatus (one called to aid, legal adviser) → Old French "avocat" → Middle English "advocate" (14th century) → meaning expanded from legal representation to general support and promotion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's a big advocate for animal rights.
   - My teacher has been a strong advocate for me getting into that program.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Dr. Martinez has served as a tireless advocate for healthcare reform throughout her career.
   - The patient advocate ensures that individuals receive appropriate medical care and that their rights are protected.

1. **Noun**: envoy (C, pl: envoys)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A messenger or representative, especially one sent on a diplomatic mission; a diplomat ranking below an ambassador
   
   **Synonyms**: emissary, representative, ambassador, delegate, messenger
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (functional role)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "envoy" when referring to official representatives sent on diplomatic, peace, or special missions; implies high-level representation and often international context; more formal than general "representative"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for most business or organizational contexts (use "representative" or "delegate"); not used for legal or commercial agency; primarily diplomatic/governmental usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - special envoy
   - diplomatic envoy
   - envoy to the United Nations
   - presidential envoy
   - send an envoy
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (in) + voy (way, journey) from Latin via (road)
   
   **Etymology**: French envoyé (sent, messenger) from envoyer (to send) → Latin in via (on the way) → English "envoy" (17th century) → primarily diplomatic usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The president sent a special envoy to negotiate the peace deal.
   - She served as an envoy between the two groups.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The United Nations Secretary-General appointed a special envoy to mediate the territorial dispute.
   - Ambassador Thompson serves as the envoy extraordinary and plenipotentiary to the Republic of Indonesia.

1. **Noun**: attorney (C, pl: attorneys)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person appointed to act for another in business or legal matters; a legal representative authorized to practice law
   - *(US)* A lawyer; one qualified to represent clients in legal proceedings
   
   **Synonyms**: lawyer, legal representative, counsel, solicitor (UK), legal agent
   
   **Antonyms**: client, defendant, plaintiff (depending on context)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "attorney" when referring to legal professionals representing clients; emphasizes legal agency and authorization; in US, synonymous with "lawyer"; in broader sense, anyone authorized to act for another
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In UK, "solicitor" or "barrister" are more common; don't use for non-legal representation (use "representative" or "agent"); avoid confusion between "attorney" (representative) and "attorney at law" (lawyer)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - attorney at law
   - power of attorney
   - attorney for the defense
   - attorney-client privilege
   - retain an attorney
   
   **Root Analysis**: at- (to) + torn (turn) + -ey (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "atorne" (one appointed) from "atorner" (to appoint) → Middle English "attourney" → legal sense "one appointed to act for another" → in US, became synonymous with "lawyer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My attorney is handling all the paperwork for the house sale.
   - You should talk to an attorney before signing that contract.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The attorney representing the plaintiff filed a motion for summary judgment.
   - Under the durable power of attorney, she is authorized to make healthcare decisions on his behalf.

1. **Noun**: behalf (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Interest, benefit, or support of someone; representation or agency for someone (used almost exclusively in phrases "on behalf of" or "in behalf of")
   
   **Synonyms**: interest, benefit, sake, support, favor
   
   **Antonyms**: detriment, disadvantage, opposition
   
   **When to Use**: Use "behalf" almost exclusively in the phrases "on behalf of" (representing/for) or "in behalf of" (for the benefit of); rarely used as a standalone noun in modern English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "on the behalf of" (archaic); avoid standalone usage outside established phrases; "in behalf of" is rare in modern English (prefer "on behalf of")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - on behalf of
   - on someone's behalf
   - act on behalf of
   - speak on behalf of
   - in behalf of (archaic/rare)
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + half (side) → "by the side of"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "be healfe" (by the side of) → Middle English "behalve" → Modern English "behalf" (side, interest, benefit) → primarily preserved in prepositional phrases
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks on behalf of everyone for organizing this party.
   - I'm calling on her behalf because she's not feeling well.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The foundation works on behalf of underprivileged children throughout the region.
   - On behalf of the university community, I extend our warmest congratulations to this year's graduates.

1. **Noun**: intermediary (C, pl: intermediaries)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who acts as a link between people to bring about an agreement or reconciliation; a mediator or go-between
   - *(Sense 2)* An intermediate agency or means
   
   **Synonyms**: mediator, go-between, middleman, agent, broker
   
   **Antonyms**: direct party, principal
   
   **When to Use**: Use "intermediary" when referring to someone who facilitates communication or transactions between other parties; emphasizes mediating role and indirect relationship; common in diplomatic, business, and conflict resolution contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when direct representation is meant (use "representative"); not appropriate when advocacy rather than mediation is primary; implies neutral facilitation, not partisan support
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - serve as intermediary
   - financial intermediary
   - act through an intermediary
   - trusted intermediary
   - intermediary role
   
   **Root Analysis**: inter- (between) + medi (middle) + -ary (adjective/noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin intermedius (in the middle) → Late Latin intermediarius → French "intermédiaire" → English "intermediary" (18th century) → emphasizes position between parties
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My friend served as an intermediary between me and my landlord.
   - We need someone to act as an intermediary to resolve this dispute.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The diplomat served as an intermediary in the peace negotiations between the two nations.
   - Financial intermediaries such as banks facilitate the flow of capital between savers and borrowers.
