1. **Verb**: represent
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To act or speak officially for someone else; to be an authorized agent or spokesperson for a person, organization, or group
   - *(Sense 2)* To be a specimen, example, or embodiment of something
   
   **Synonyms**: act for, speak for, stand for, advocate for, serve as representative
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, work against, undermine
   
   **When to Use**: Use "represent" when describing official agency, spokesperson roles, or legal representation; emphasizes authorized status and active role; common in legal, political, business contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when informal support rather than official representation is meant; avoid when simple description rather than agency is intended (sense 2 confusion)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - represent a client
   - represent the company
   - legally represent
   - represent interests
   - represent the people
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, back) + present (bring before, show)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin repraesentare (to present again, make present) → Old French representer → Middle English "representen" (14th century) → legal/political sense "act as agent for" emerged in 16th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll be representing our school at the science fair next week.
   - Who's going to represent us at the neighborhood meeting?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Attorney Wilson will represent the defendant throughout the trial proceedings.
   - The senator represents the interests of over two million constituents in her district.

1. **Verb**: act for
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive phrasal) To perform actions on behalf of someone; to serve as an agent or representative with authority to take action
   
   **Synonyms**: represent, serve as agent for, work on behalf of, operate for, stand in for
   
   **Antonyms**: act against, oppose, work contrary to
   
   **When to Use**: Use "act for" when emphasizing the performance of actions as someone's representative; implies authorized agency and active execution; common in legal and business contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less appropriate for speaking or ceremonial roles (use "speak on behalf of"); can be ambiguous without context about authority relationship
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - act for the client
   - act for the principal
   - authorized to act for
   - act for the estate
   - act for and on behalf of
   
   **Root Analysis**: act (do, perform) + for (in place of, on behalf of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin actus (doing) → Old French "acte" → English "act" + preposition "for" → phrasal verb emphasizing agency relationship (developed in legal/commercial English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you act for me at the meeting? I'll be out of town.
   - He's acting for his father while dad recovers from surgery.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The trustee is authorized to act for the beneficiaries in all matters concerning the trust.
   - Under the power of attorney, she may act for her mother in financial transactions.

1. **Verb**: speak for
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive phrasal) To express the opinions, wishes, or views of someone else; to be a spokesperson for; to represent verbally
   - *(Sense 2)* To provide evidence for; to testify to the quality of something
   
   **Synonyms**: represent, voice for, express for, advocate for, be spokesperson for
   
   **Antonyms**: speak against, contradict, oppose
   
   **When to Use**: Use "speak for" when emphasizing verbal representation or expressing others' views; implies authorization to express opinions; suitable across registers when representation is verbal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when claiming to represent without authority ("speak for yourself"); avoid when written representation is primary; can sound presumptuous if authority unclear
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - speak for the group
   - speak for oneself
   - speak for the majority
   - let the facts speak for themselves
   - actions speak for themselves
   
   **Root Analysis**: speak (express verbally) + for (on behalf of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "specan/sprecan" (to speak) + "for" → phrasal verb emphasizing verbal representation → idiomatic meanings developed over time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think I speak for everyone when I say we're grateful for your help.
   - Don't speak for me—I can express my own opinion!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegate spoke for the entire coalition during the United Nations assembly.
   - I cannot speak for the board, but personally I support the proposed initiative.

1. **Verb**: advocate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To publicly recommend, support, or plead in favor of something; to speak, plead, or act on behalf of a cause or person
   
   **Synonyms**: support, champion, promote, endorse, plead for
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, condemn, denounce, discourage
   
   **When to Use**: Use "advocate" when describing active promotion, support, or pleading for a cause, policy, or person; implies passionate or sustained support; common in activism, policy, and social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when neutral representation is meant (use "represent"); implies partisan support, so avoid when objectivity required; too formal for casual support
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advocate for change
   - advocate on behalf of
   - strongly advocate
   - advocate for reform
   - advocate for the rights of
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + voc (call, voice) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin advocatus (one called to aid) → advocare (to call to one's aid) → Middle English "advocaten" → meaning "plead for" or "support publicly" emphasized in modern usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's always advocating for better working conditions at the restaurant.
   - I've been advocating for a new playground in our neighborhood.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization advocates on behalf of refugees seeking asylum and legal protection.
   - Dr. Chen has consistently advocated for increased funding for mental health services.

1. **Verb**: negotiate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To discuss with others in order to reach an agreement; to act as an intermediary or agent in business transactions on behalf of another
   
   **Synonyms**: bargain, mediate, arrange, broker, work out
   
   **Antonyms**: refuse to discuss, impose, dictate
   
   **When to Use**: Use "negotiate" when describing the process of reaching agreements through discussion, especially when acting on behalf of others; emphasizes active engagement and compromise; common in business, labor, diplomatic contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when unilateral decisions rather than agreements are made; not appropriate for simple speaking or ceremonial representation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - negotiate on behalf of
   - negotiate a contract
   - negotiate terms
   - negotiate with
   - successfully negotiate
   
   **Root Analysis**: neg- (not) + oti (leisure) + -ate (verb suffix) → "not at leisure, doing business"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin negotiari (to carry on business) from negotium (business, not leisure) → Old French "negocier" → English "negotiate" (16th century) → developed sense of "discuss to reach agreement"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My agent is negotiating a better deal for me right now.
   - Can you negotiate with the landlord on our behalf about the rent?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The union representatives will negotiate on behalf of all employees during the collective bargaining sessions.
   - Counsel has been authorized to negotiate a settlement on behalf of the plaintiff.

1. **Verb**: deputize
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To appoint or authorize someone to act as a deputy or representative; to delegate authority to someone to act on one's behalf
   
   **Synonyms**: appoint, delegate, authorize, empower, commission
   
   **Antonyms**: remove authority, dismiss, strip of power
   
   **When to Use**: Use "deputize" when describing the formal appointment of someone to act as a representative with delegated authority; emphasizes official authorization; common in legal, governmental, and organizational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual contexts (use "ask to represent" or "appoint"); not appropriate when authority delegation is not involved; primarily used in official capacities
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deputize someone to act
   - officially deputized
   - deputize a representative
   - deputize authority
   - deputize on behalf of
   
   **Root Analysis**: deput(y) (appointed representative) + -ize (verb suffix meaning "make, cause to be")
   
   **Etymology**: French député (delegate) from Latin deputare (to assign) → English "deputy" → verb form "deputize" (19th century) emphasizing act of delegation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The manager deputized Sarah to run the meeting while he's away.
   - I've been deputized to organize the office party this year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board deputized the executive committee to act on its behalf during the interim period.
   - The sheriff may deputize citizens to assist in law enforcement during emergencies.

1. **Verb**: delegate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To entrust a task or responsibility to another person; to authorize someone to act as a representative
   - *(Sense 2)* To send someone as a representative to a meeting or conference
   
   **Synonyms**: assign, entrust, authorize, commission, depute
   
   **Antonyms**: retain responsibility, keep control, handle personally
   
   **When to Use**: Use "delegate" when describing the assignment of tasks or authority to representatives; emphasizes transfer of responsibility; common in management, organizational, and political contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when no actual transfer of authority occurs; avoid when simple information sharing rather than empowerment is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - delegate authority
   - delegate responsibility
   - delegate tasks
   - delegate to representatives
   - effectively delegate
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, from) + leg (send, appoint) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin delegare (to send away as a representative) → English "delegate" (16th century) → dual meaning: send representatives and transfer authority
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to delegate some of this work to my team members.
   - She delegated me to pick up the supplies for the event.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The CEO delegates operational decisions to senior vice presidents while retaining strategic oversight.
   - The member states agreed to delegate negotiating authority to the committee chairman.

1. **Verb**: authorize
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To give official permission or power to someone to do something; to empower someone to act on behalf of an entity or person
   
   **Synonyms**: empower, permit, sanction, license, commission
   
   **Antonyms**: forbid, prohibit, unauthorized, revoke permission
   
   **When to Use**: Use "authorize" when describing the granting of official permission or power to act; emphasizes legitimacy and official status; common in legal, business, governmental contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when informal permission rather than official authorization is meant; avoid when no actual power transfer occurs; implies formal process
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - authorize to act on behalf of
   - duly authorized
   - authorize someone to represent
   - legally authorized
   - authorize representatives
   
   **Root Analysis**: author (originator, creator) + -ize (verb suffix meaning "make, give")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin auctor (originator, promoter) → Old French "autoriser" → Middle English "auctorisen" → Modern English "authorize" (14th century) → meaning "give official power to act"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My parents authorized me to sign for the package on their behalf.
   - Who authorized you to make changes to the schedule?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board of directors authorized the CFO to execute contracts on behalf of the corporation.
   - Only personnel specifically authorized by the department head may access confidential records.

1. **Verb**: champion
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To fight for, defend, or support a cause, person, or principle; to act as an advocate or defender on behalf of something
   
   **Synonyms**: advocate, defend, support, promote, uphold
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, attack, undermine, criticize
   
   **When to Use**: Use "champion" when describing vigorous support or defense of a cause, person, or principle; implies passionate advocacy and active promotion; suitable across registers for describing strong support
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when neutral representation is meant (too strong); avoid when official agency without advocacy is primary; implies partisanship
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - champion a cause
   - champion the rights of
   - champion on behalf of
   - champion reform
   - champion innovation
   
   **Root Analysis**: champ (field, battlefield) + -ion (noun suffix) → verb meaning "fight for"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin campus (field, battlefield) → Old French "champion" (warrior, defender) → English noun "champion" → verb usage "to fight for, defend" emerged in 19th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's been championing our proposal at every meeting.
   - Thanks for championing my idea to the boss!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Senator Martinez has championed environmental legislation on behalf of coastal communities for over a decade.
   - The foundation champions the rights of indigenous peoples throughout the Amazon region.

1. **Verb**: proxy (for)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To act as a proxy; to vote or act on behalf of someone as their authorized representative (primarily used as noun, less common as verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: represent, stand in for, substitute for, act on behalf of
   
   **Antonyms**: attend personally, act directly
   
   **When to Use**: Use "proxy" as a verb (rare) when describing the act of serving as someone's authorized substitute, especially in voting contexts; more commonly used as a noun
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Uncommon as verb (prefer "vote by proxy" or "act as proxy"); limited to specific contexts like shareholder voting; sounds technical
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proxy for someone (rare verb usage)
   - vote by proxy (noun usage more common)
   - serve as proxy
   - proxy voting
   - proxy representation
   
   **Root Analysis**: prox- (near, next) + -y (noun/verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin procuratio (management) → Middle English "procuracie" contracted to "proxy" → primarily noun usage, verb usage rare and specialized
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you proxy for me at the vote? I'll be traveling.
   - I'm proxying for three other shareholders at the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In accordance with corporate bylaws, shareholders may proxy for absent members during annual meetings.
   - The designated representative will proxy on behalf of all affiliated organizations.
