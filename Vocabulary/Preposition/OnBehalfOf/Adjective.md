1. **Adjective**: representative (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Typical of a particular group, category, or type; serving as an example or specimen
   - *(Sense 2)* Consisting of people chosen to act or speak on behalf of others; serving as a delegate
   
   **Synonyms**: typical, characteristic, exemplary, archetypal, illustrative
   
   **Antonyms**: atypical, unrepresentative, unusual, exceptional
   
   **When to Use**: Use "representative" when describing something that typifies a group or when referring to democratic representation; suitable across all registers; emphasizes how well something stands for others
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when uniqueness rather than typicality should be emphasized; avoid when representation is unofficial or informal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - representative sample
   - representative democracy
   - representative government
   - representative example
   - truly representative
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again) + present (bring before) + -ative (adjective suffix indicating tendency or relation)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin repraesentare (to present again) → Late Latin repraesentativus → English "representative" (17th century) → dual meaning: typical and delegated representation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This dish is pretty representative of traditional Italian cooking.
   - Is this a representative example of your work?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The survey data was collected from a representative sample of the national population.
   - Representative democracy requires elected officials to act on behalf of their constituents.

1. **Adjective**: authorized (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having official permission or power to do something; officially approved to act on behalf of an entity or person
   
   **Synonyms**: official, approved, sanctioned, licensed, permitted
   
   **Antonyms**: unauthorized, unapproved, illegitimate, forbidden
   
   **When to Use**: Use "authorized" when describing someone or something with official permission or power; emphasizes legitimacy and official status; common in legal, business, and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when informal permission rather than official authorization is meant; avoid when no formal approval process exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - authorized representative
   - authorized to act on behalf of
   - duly authorized
   - authorized agent
   - authorized personnel
   
   **Root Analysis**: author (originator) + -ize (make) + -ed (past participle/adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin auctor (originator) → Old French "autoriser" → English "authorize" → adjective form "authorized" emphasizing official approval
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure you're dealing with an authorized dealer.
   - Only authorized people can access that room.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Only authorized representatives may sign contracts on behalf of the corporation.
   - The document must be notarized by an authorized official to be legally binding.

1. **Adjective**: official (gradable in some contexts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to an authority or public body and its duties, actions, or responsibilities; authorized or issued by an authority
   - *(Sense 2)* Holding public office or having official position to act on behalf of an organization
   
   **Synonyms**: formal, authorized, legitimate, sanctioned, approved
   
   **Antonyms**: unofficial, informal, unauthorized, private
   
   **When to Use**: Use "official" when describing something authorized, formal, or connected to an authority; emphasizes legitimacy and institutional backing; suitable across registers when emphasizing formal status
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when informal or personal rather than institutional; avoid overuse in casual contexts where "formal" would suffice
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - official representative
   - official capacity
   - official spokesperson
   - official statement
   - in an official capacity
   
   **Root Analysis**: offic(e) (duty, service) + -ial (adjective suffix relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin officialis (relating to duty) from officium (duty, service) → Middle English → Modern English "official" emphasizing connection to authority
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is that the official version or just a rumor?
   - She's the official photographer for the wedding.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The ambassador issued an official statement on behalf of the government.
   - In his official capacity as director, he represents the institution at international conferences.

1. **Adjective**: designated (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Appointed or chosen for a specific purpose or role; officially selected to act on behalf of a group or organization
   
   **Synonyms**: appointed, assigned, selected, nominated, specified
   
   **Antonyms**: undesignated, unassigned, random
   
   **When to Use**: Use "designated" when describing someone or something officially appointed for a specific role or purpose; emphasizes deliberate selection and assigned responsibility
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when selection is informal or unofficial; avoid when no specific assignment has been made
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - designated representative
   - designated spokesperson
   - designated agent
   - designated authority
   - officially designated
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (completely) + sign (mark, indicate) + -ate (verb suffix) + -ed (adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin designare (to mark out, specify) → English "designate" → adjective form emphasizing official selection for specific purpose
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's the designated driver tonight, so she's not drinking.
   - Who's the designated contact person for this project?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The designated representative will speak on behalf of all member organizations at the hearing.
   - Each department must identify a designated official to coordinate emergency response procedures.

1. **Adjective**: proxy (non-gradable, attributive only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Acting or authorized to act as a substitute; serving on behalf of another person (used before nouns)
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute, surrogate, representative, stand-in, delegated
   
   **Antonyms**: direct, personal, principal
   
   **When to Use**: Use "proxy" as attributive adjective before nouns to describe substitute or representative relationships; emphasizes acting in place of another; common in technical, legal, business contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used predicatively (*"the vote was proxy"); limited to specific collocations; sounds technical in casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proxy vote
   - proxy statement
   - proxy server
   - proxy representative
   - proxy authority
   
   **Root Analysis**: prox- (near, next) + -y (noun/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin procuratio → Middle English "proxie" → used attributively to describe substitution relationship
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I submitted a proxy vote because I couldn't attend in person.
   - It's like a proxy relationship—he speaks for the whole group.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Shareholders may exercise their voting rights through proxy representation at the annual meeting.
   - The proxy statement must disclose all material information regarding the matters to be voted upon.

1. **Adjective**: delegated (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Entrusted or assigned to someone as a representative; transferred to someone to act on behalf of another
   
   **Synonyms**: assigned, entrusted, transferred, devolved, authorized
   
   **Antonyms**: retained, kept, undelegated, personal
   
   **When to Use**: Use "delegated" when describing authority, responsibility, or tasks that have been transferred to representatives; emphasizes transfer of power; common in management, legal, governmental contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when authority remains with the original holder; avoid when informal rather than official transfer is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - delegated authority
   - delegated responsibility
   - delegated power
   - delegated tasks
   - delegated representation
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, from) + leg (send) + -ate (verb) + -ed (adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin delegare (to send away) → English "delegate" → adjective form emphasizing transferred authority
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These are delegated tasks—you have full authority to complete them your way.
   - It's a delegated responsibility now, so she's in charge.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee exercises delegated authority on behalf of the full board between meetings.
   - Under the Constitution, certain powers remain delegated to state governments.

1. **Adjective**: appointed (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Selected or designated for a position, role, or office; officially chosen to act on behalf of an organization or authority
   
   **Synonyms**: designated, nominated, selected, chosen, assigned
   
   **Antonyms**: elected, self-appointed, dismissed, removed
   
   **When to Use**: Use "appointed" when describing someone officially selected for a position, especially by authority rather than election; emphasizes selection by others; common in governmental, organizational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when election rather than appointment is the selection method; avoid when informal selection is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - appointed representative
   - appointed official
   - appointed agent
   - duly appointed
   - appointed to act on behalf of
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to, toward) + point (fix, designate) + -ed (adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "apointer" (to arrange) → Middle English "appointen" → Modern English adjective form emphasizing official selection
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's the newly appointed team captain.
   - Who's the appointed person to handle complaints?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The appointed representatives will negotiate on behalf of the union membership.
   - As the duly appointed executor, he is authorized to act on behalf of the estate.

1. **Adjective**: accredited (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Officially recognized, authorized, or certified; given credentials to represent an organization or institution
   - *(Sense 2)* (Of institutions) officially recognized as meeting established standards
   
   **Synonyms**: certified, authorized, licensed, recognized, credentialed
   
   **Antonyms**: unaccredited, unauthorized, uncertified, unofficial
   
   **When to Use**: Use "accredited" when describing someone with official credentials to represent or someone/something officially recognized; emphasizes formal certification; common in diplomatic, educational, professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when no formal credentialing process exists; avoid when simple authorization without certification is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accredited representative
   - accredited diplomat
   - accredited institution
   - accredited agent
   - fully accredited
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to, toward) + credit (trust, believe) + -ed (adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin accreditare (to give credit to) → French "accréditer" → English "accredit" → adjective form emphasizing official recognition and credentials
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure you go to an accredited institution if you want your degree to count.
   - Is he an accredited representative, or just a volunteer?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Only accredited journalists are permitted to attend the presidential press conference.
   - The accredited diplomatic mission represents its nation's interests in the host country.

1. **Adjective**: empowered (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Given the authority or power to do something; authorized and enabled to act on behalf of oneself or others
   
   **Synonyms**: authorized, enabled, permitted, licensed, entitled
   
   **Antonyms**: disempowered, forbidden, powerless, unauthorized
   
   **When to Use**: Use "empowered" when describing someone given authority, confidence, or means to take action; can emphasize both official authorization and personal agency; suitable across registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when no actual transfer of power or authority occurred; can sound like corporate jargon if overused; be specific about what powers were granted
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - empowered to act on behalf of
   - legally empowered
   - empowered representative
   - fully empowered
   - empowered to make decisions
   
   **Root Analysis**: em- (make, cause to be) + power (ability, authority) + -ed (adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: English "power" + prefix "em-" + "-ed" → Modern English emphasizing both granted authority and personal agency (20th century usage expanded)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The team feels really empowered to make their own decisions now.
   - I'm empowered to offer you a discount up to twenty percent.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The attorney is fully empowered to negotiate a settlement on behalf of the plaintiff.
   - Board members are empowered by the bylaws to act on behalf of the organization between meetings.

1. **Adjective**: legitimate (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Conforming to the law or established rules; officially recognized and authorized; having valid authority to act
   - *(Sense 2)* Reasonable, valid, or justifiable
   
   **Synonyms**: legal, lawful, valid, authorized, genuine
   
   **Antonyms**: illegitimate, unauthorized, invalid, unlawful
   
   **When to Use**: Use "legitimate" when emphasizing legal validity, proper authorization, or conformity to rules; suitable across registers when emphasizing authenticity or proper authority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when moral legitimacy rather than legal validity is meant (can be ambiguous); avoid when simple "legal" or "authorized" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - legitimate representative
   - legitimate authority
   - legitimate claim
   - legitimate interest
   - legitimate right to act on behalf of
   
   **Root Analysis**: legitim(ate) from Latin legitimus (lawful, legal)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin legitimus (lawful, according to law) from lex (law) → Middle English "legitimat" → Modern English emphasizing lawfulness and proper authority
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is this a legitimate offer, or is it a scam?
   - She has legitimate reasons for speaking on their behalf.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legitimate representatives of the government are authorized to negotiate on behalf of the nation.
   - To establish standing, the plaintiff must demonstrate a legitimate interest in the matter.
