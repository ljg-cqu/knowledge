1. **Noun**: absence (U/C, pl: absences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (U) The state of being away from a place or person
   - *(Sense 2)* (C) An occasion or period of being away
   - *(Sense 3)* (U) The lack or non-existence of something
   
   **Synonyms**: non-attendance, non-appearance, lack, deficiency, want
   
   **Antonyms**: presence, attendance, existence, availability
   
   **When to Use**: Use "absence" for formal contexts describing someone not present, or metaphorically for lack of something. Countable when referring to specific occasions (three absences), uncountable for the state itself.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "absence" when "lack" would be clearer for missing qualities. Avoid in casual contexts where "not being there" is more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the absence of
   - conspicuous by one's absence
   - leave of absence
   - absence makes the heart grow fonder
   - unauthorized absence
   
   **Root Analysis**: ab- (away from) + -sence (state of being) = state of being away
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "absentia" (being away) → from "absentem" → Old French → English "absence"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your absence was really noticed at the party last night.
   - In the absence of any better ideas, let's just go with this one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The employee's repeated absences have become a performance concern.
   - The absence of credible evidence undermines the prosecution's case.

2. **Noun**: absentee (C, pl: absentees)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who is expected to be present at a place or event but is not
   - *(Sense 2)* (Attributive) Relating to voting or participation from a distance
   
   **Synonyms**: no-show, missing person, non-attendee, truant
   
   **Antonyms**: attendee, participant, present member
   
   **When to Use**: Use "absentee" for formal identification of someone not present, especially in voting (absentee ballot), workplace, or educational contexts. Often used attributively.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "absentee" for people legitimately away with permission unless specifically about distance participation (absentee voting). Avoid when "missing person" implies danger.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - absentee ballot
   - absentee landlord
   - absentee rate
   - absentee parent
   - absentee voter
   
   **Root Analysis**: absent + -ee (person affected by action) = person who is absent
   
   **Etymology**: "Absent" + "-ee" suffix (indicating person in a state) → one who is absent, particularly in formal/legal contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had several absentees at the meeting today.
   - He's pretty much an absentee parent—never around.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Absentee voting has increased significantly in recent elections.
   - The property is owned by an absentee landlord residing overseas.

3. **Noun**: truancy (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action of staying away from school without good reason; the practice of being a truant
   
   **Synonyms**: absenteeism (school context), skipping school, unauthorized absence
   
   **Antonyms**: attendance, presence, compliance
   
   **When to Use**: Use "truancy" specifically for unauthorized school absences, particularly in educational administration, legal, or policy contexts. Implies pattern of behavior rather than single incident.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "truancy" for workplace absences (use "absenteeism"). Avoid for legitimate excused absences from school.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - truancy rate
   - habitual truancy
   - truancy law
   - truancy officer
   - chronic truancy
   
   **Root Analysis**: truant (absent without permission) + -cy (state/quality) = state of being truant
   
   **Etymology**: From "truant" (Old French "truant" = vagabond) + "-cy" suffix → the state or practice of unauthorized school absence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The school is cracking down on truancy this year.
   - His truancy problems started after his parents divorced.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The district has implemented a comprehensive truancy intervention program.
   - Truancy rates correlate strongly with subsequent dropout rates.

4. **Noun**: non-attendance (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The failure to attend or be present at an event, meeting, or institution
   
   **Synonyms**: absence, absenteeism, non-appearance, failure to attend
   
   **Antonyms**: attendance, presence, participation
   
   **When to Use**: Use "non-attendance" in formal, administrative, or legal contexts when describing failure to appear. More formal and official than "absence."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "non-attendance" in casual conversation (use "not going" or "skipping"). Avoid when "absence" would be clearer and less bureaucratic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - non-attendance at school
   - penalty for non-attendance
   - non-attendance rate
   - persistent non-attendance
   - non-attendance policy
   
   **Root Analysis**: non- (not) + attendance (act of attending) = not attending
   
   **Etymology**: "Non-" (Latin negation prefix) + "attendance" (from "attend") → formal term for not attending
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Non-attendance at family events is becoming a pattern with him.
   - There's a fine for non-attendance if you don't show up to jury duty.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Persistent non-attendance may result in removal from the program.
   - The policy regarding non-attendance at mandatory sessions is clearly stated.

5. **Noun**: vacancy (C, pl: vacancies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An available room in a hotel or other accommodation
   - *(Sense 2)* An unoccupied position or job
   - *(Sense 3)* (U) The state of being empty or available
   
   **Synonyms**: opening, available position, empty space, unoccupied room
   
   **Antonyms**: occupancy, filled position, tenant
   
   **When to Use**: Use "vacancy" for available positions (employment), rooms (accommodation), or empty spaces (buildings). Countable when referring to specific openings, uncountable for the state of being vacant.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "vacancy" for temporary absences of people. Avoid when "opening" or "job" would be clearer in employment contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - job vacancy
   - no vacancy
   - vacancy rate
   - fill a vacancy
   - board vacancy
   
   **Root Analysis**: vac- (empty) + -ancy (state/quality) = state of being empty
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "vacans" (empty, vacant) → "vacantia" (emptiness) → English "vacancy"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The motel sign says "No Vacancy" so they must be full.
   - Is there a vacancy on the board? I'd like to apply.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has announced a vacancy for the position of director.
   - Urban vacancy rates have declined significantly over the past decade.

6. **Noun**: void (C/U, pl: voids)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A completely empty space; a gap or emptiness
   - *(Sense 2)* (C, figurative) A feeling of emptiness or loss
   - *(Sense 3)* (U, legal) The state of being legally invalid
   
   **Synonyms**: emptiness, gap, vacuum, absence, nullity
   
   **Antonyms**: fullness, presence, substance, validity
   
   **When to Use**: Use "void" for complete emptiness, significant gaps, or emotional emptiness. Also legal term for invalidity (null and void). More dramatic than "absence."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "void" for minor gaps or absences (too dramatic). Avoid in casual contexts where "empty space" or "gap" is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fill the void
   - void in one's life
   - null and void
   - void left by someone
   - empty void
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "vocuus/viduus" (empty) → void = emptiness
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "voide" (empty) → from Latin "vocuus" → English "void" as noun for emptiness or gap
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After she moved away, there was this huge void in my life.
   - The investigation revealed a void in the documentation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract was declared null and void due to misrepresentation.
   - Structural analysis revealed significant voids within the foundation.

7. **Noun**: gap (C, pl: gaps)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An empty space or opening; a break in continuity
   - *(Sense 2)* A difference or disparity between things
   - *(Sense 3)* An absence or lack of something needed
   
   **Synonyms**: opening, space, interval, disparity, deficiency
   
   **Antonyms**: continuity, connection, presence, bridge
   
   **When to Use**: Use "gap" for physical spaces, time intervals, missing information, or disparities between things. Very versatile—works in casual and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "gap" when "difference" or "disparity" would be more precise for comparisons. Avoid when physical "hole" is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - gap in knowledge
   - generation gap
   - gap year
   - bridge the gap
   - mind the gap
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old Norse "gap" (chasm) = opening or empty space
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "gap" (chasm, empty space) → Middle English → modern English, maintaining meaning of opening or space
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a huge gap in my memory about what happened that night.
   - She took a gap year before starting university.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study identifies significant gaps in current research literature.
   - Addressing the skills gap remains a priority for workforce development.

8. **Noun**: deficiency (C/U, pl: deficiencies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A lack or shortage of something essential
   - *(Sense 2)* (U) The state of not having enough of something
   - *(Sense 3)* (C) A failing or shortcoming
   
   **Synonyms**: shortage, lack, insufficiency, inadequacy, deficit
   
   **Antonyms**: sufficiency, abundance, excess, adequacy
   
   **When to Use**: Use "deficiency" for formal contexts describing lack of essential elements, especially nutrients, skills, or resources. Often technical or medical.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "deficiency" when "lack" would be simpler and clearer. Avoid when describing temporary shortages (use "shortage").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vitamin deficiency
   - deficiency in skills
   - nutrient deficiency
   - address deficiencies
   - immune deficiency
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, lacking) + fic- (make, do) + -iency (state) = state of lacking
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "deficere" (to fail, lack) → "deficientia" → English "deficiency" meaning shortage or inadequacy
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The doctor said I have an iron deficiency and need supplements.
   - The report pointed out several deficiencies in the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Nutritional deficiencies were prevalent among the study population.
   - The audit revealed significant deficiencies in financial controls.
