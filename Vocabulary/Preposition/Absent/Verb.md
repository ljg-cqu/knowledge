1. **Verb**: absent (oneself)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Reflexive, transitive, formal) To stay away from a place or activity deliberately; to remove oneself
   
   **Synonyms**: remove oneself, withdraw, stay away, excuse oneself, keep away
   
   **Antonyms**: attend, present oneself, appear, participate, show up
   
   **When to Use**: Use "absent oneself" in formal writing when describing deliberate removal from a situation. Always used reflexively (absent oneself/himself/herself). Relatively formal and uncommon.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "absent" as a simple transitive verb without reflexive pronoun—grammatically incorrect. Avoid in casual conversation (use "skip" or "not go").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - absent oneself from
   - absent oneself from duty
   - repeatedly absent oneself
   - choose to absent oneself
   - absent oneself from proceedings
   
   **Root Analysis**: ab- (away from) + -sent (being) → make oneself away
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin "absentare" (to be away) → English verb form requiring reflexive construction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He basically absented himself from family life after the divorce.
   - She tends to absent herself whenever there's conflict.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The senator absented himself from the vote due to conflict of interest.
   - Board members may not absent themselves from mandatory sessions.

2. **Verb**: miss
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To fail to be present at something; to not attend
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To feel regret or sadness at the absence of someone or something
   - *(Sense 3)* (Transitive) To fail to notice, hear, or understand something
   
   **Synonyms**: skip, be absent from, fail to attend, (emotional sense: long for, pine for)
   
   **Antonyms**: attend, catch, notice, be present at
   
   **When to Use**: Use "miss" for both physical absence and emotional experience of absence. Very versatile—works in all registers. Can indicate accident or intention depending on context.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse "miss" (fail to hit/attend) with "Miss" (title). Avoid ambiguity between senses—context must clarify meaning.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - miss school/work
   - miss the point
   - miss out on
   - miss someone dearly
   - miss the mark
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "missan" = fail to hit or reach
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "missan" (fail to hit target) → Middle English → modern multiple senses including absence and longing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I missed class yesterday because I was sick.
   - I really miss hanging out with you guys.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees who miss mandatory training sessions must reschedule promptly.
   - The analysis misses several critical factors affecting outcomes.

3. **Verb**: skip
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive, informal) To deliberately not attend or do something; to omit
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To move with quick light jumps
   
   **Synonyms**: miss, omit, bypass, pass over, avoid
   
   **Antonyms**: attend, include, participate in, do
   
   **When to Use**: Use "skip" informally when describing deliberate absence or omission. Implies intentional choice. Very common in casual speech. Less serious than "absent" or "truant."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "skip" in formal professional contexts (use "miss" or "be absent from"). Avoid when describing unintentional absence.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - skip class
   - skip a meal
   - skip town
   - skip ahead
   - skip over details
   
   **Root Analysis**: Uncertain origin, possibly Scandinavian, related to jumping/hopping movement
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "skippen" (leap, spring) → extended meaning to omit or pass over
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's skip the meeting and grab coffee instead.
   - I usually skip breakfast on weekdays.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report skips over several important methodological considerations.
   - Participants were instructed not to skip any survey questions.

4. **Verb**: withdraw
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To remove oneself from participation or presence; to leave or pull back
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To take back or remove something
   
   **Synonyms**: remove oneself, pull out, retreat, leave, depart
   
   **Antonyms**: enter, join, participate, engage, contribute
   
   **When to Use**: Use "withdraw" for formal removal from situations, competitions, courses, or positions. Can be physical or metaphorical. More formal than "pull out."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "withdraw" for casual absences (too formal). Avoid when "leave" would be simpler and clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - withdraw from competition
   - withdraw from a course
   - withdraw one's support
   - withdraw into oneself
   - withdraw troops
   
   **Root Analysis**: with- (back, away) + draw (pull) = pull back/away
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "with-" (back) + "drawen" (pull) → to pull back or remove
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He withdrew from the race after the injury.
   - She's kind of withdrawn lately, not talking to anyone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate withdrew from consideration for personal reasons.
   - Students may withdraw from courses before the deadline without penalty.

5. **Verb**: vacate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To leave a place, making it empty or available; to give up occupancy
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive, legal) To cancel or annul (a judgment or contract)
   
   **Synonyms**: leave, evacuate, abandon, relinquish, quit
   
   **Antonyms**: occupy, fill, inhabit, move into
   
   **When to Use**: Use "vacate" formally for leaving premises, positions, or seats, making them available. Also legal term for invalidating judgments. More formal than "leave."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "vacate" in casual contexts (use "leave" or "move out"). Avoid when describing temporary absences—implies permanent departure.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vacate the premises
   - vacate a position
   - vacate a seat
   - vacate a judgment
   - vacate one's office
   
   **Root Analysis**: vac- (empty) + -ate (make) = make empty
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "vacare" (be empty) → "vacatus" → English "vacate" meaning to make empty or leave
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The hotel wants us to vacate the room by 11 AM.
   - When are you vacating your apartment?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Tenants must vacate the premises within thirty days of notice.
   - The court decided to vacate the previous judgment.

6. **Verb**: disappear
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To cease to be visible; to vanish
   - *(Sense 2)* To go missing; to become absent suddenly or mysteriously
   
   **Synonyms**: vanish, fade away, evaporate, go missing, become invisible
   
   **Antonyms**: appear, materialize, emerge, show up, surface
   
   **When to Use**: Use "disappear" for sudden absence, vanishing, or when someone becomes missing. Can be literal (visibility) or metaphorical (availability). Implies mystery or suddenness.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "disappear" for planned absences (use "leave" or "go away"). Avoid when describing gradual fading (use "fade" or "diminish").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disappear without a trace
   - disappear into thin air
   - make something disappear
   - mysteriously disappear
   - disappear from view
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (away, apart) + appear (come into view) = go away from view
   
   **Etymology**: "Dis-" (reversal prefix) + "appear" (from Latin "apparere") → cease to be visible
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My phone just disappeared—I can't find it anywhere!
   - He tends to disappear when there's work to be done.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Critical evidence disappeared from the evidence locker.
   - The symptoms disappeared following treatment intervention.

7. **Verb**: abandon
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To leave permanently, especially when one should stay; to desert
   - *(Sense 2)* To give up completely; to discontinue
   
   **Synonyms**: desert, forsake, leave, give up, relinquish
   
   **Antonyms**: maintain, continue, stay with, support, keep
   
   **When to Use**: Use "abandon" for permanent leaving, especially with negative connotation of neglecting responsibility. Also for giving up plans or efforts. Stronger than "leave."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "abandon" for temporary absences. Avoid when "leave" or "stop" would be less emotionally charged and more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - abandon ship
   - abandon hope
   - abandon a project
   - abandon one's post
   - abandon all pretense
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old French "abandoner" = surrender, give up freely
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "abandoner" (surrender) → from "à bandon" (at one's disposal) → English meaning to give up or desert
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had to abandon the camping trip because of the weather.
   - Don't abandon me here—I don't know anyone!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team was forced to abandon the project due to funding constraints.
   - Military personnel may not abandon their posts under any circumstances.

8. **Verb**: neglect
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To fail to care for properly; to disregard or pay insufficient attention to
   - *(Sense 2)* To fail to do something required
   
   **Synonyms**: ignore, disregard, overlook, fail to attend to, shirk
   
   **Antonyms**: attend to, care for, maintain, fulfill, prioritize
   
   **When to Use**: Use "neglect" when describing failure to give proper attention or care, implying responsibility not fulfilled. Often has serious or negative connotations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "neglect" for simple forgetting without responsibility context. Avoid when "overlook" or "miss" would be less accusatory.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - neglect one's duties
   - neglect to mention
   - neglect one's health
   - willful neglect
   - neglect of duty
   
   **Root Analysis**: neg- (not) + lect (gather, choose) = not choose/gather = disregard
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "neglegere" (disregard) → from "neg-" (not) + "legere" (choose, gather) → English "neglect"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been neglecting the gym lately—too busy with work.
   - Don't neglect to lock the door when you leave.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation found evidence of systematic neglect of safety protocols.
   - Employees who neglect their responsibilities face disciplinary action.
