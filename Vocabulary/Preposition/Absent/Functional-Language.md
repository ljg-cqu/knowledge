1. **Functional Expression**: I'm sorry I missed [event/meeting] (apologizing for absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Apologizing for not attending an event or meeting
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I apologize for missing, I'm sorry I couldn't make it, I regret being absent from, Sorry I wasn't there
   
   **Antonyms**: Glad I could attend, Thank you for having me, It was great to be there
   
   **When to Use**: Use when apologizing for absence after the fact. Works in both professional and social contexts. Shows acknowledgment of missed commitment.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use if you deliberately skipped without good reason (insincere). Avoid when advance notice was given and accepted—acknowledgment rather than apology needed.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm really sorry I missed it
   - Sorry I couldn't be there
   - Apologies for missing the meeting
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines apology with factual statement. Often followed by brief explanation. Assumes your absence was noticed and mattered.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "No problem, we missed you!"
   - "Don't worry about it, hope everything's okay"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm sorry I missed your birthday party—I had a family emergency.
   - Sorry I missed the game last night. How did it go?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'm sorry I missed yesterday's meeting. Could someone brief me on the outcomes?
   - I apologize for missing the presentation. I was unavoidably detained.

2. **Functional Expression**: [Name] sends his/her apologies (explaining someone's absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Conveying that someone cannot attend and apologizes for their absence
   - *Formality*: formal to very formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: [Name] apologizes for not being able to attend, [Name] regrets they cannot join us, [Name] is unable to attend
   
   **Antonyms**: [Name] will be joining us, [Name] has confirmed attendance
   
   **When to Use**: Use formally when conveying someone else's regrets about absence at meetings or events. Common in British English. Very polite and diplomatic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use in casual American English (sounds overly formal). Avoid when person should explain their own absence directly.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - sends their apologies
   - has sent apologies
   - asked me to convey her apologies
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Indirect politeness strategy. Speaker acts as intermediary. Maintains face for absent person while explaining absence. Very British usage.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Thank you, please send our regards"
   - "That's understood, we hope everything is well"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sarah sends her apologies—she's got a family thing.
   - Tom sends his apologies for tonight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The director sends her apologies and will be represented by the deputy director.
   - Dr. Smith sends his apologies; he is attending an emergency consultation.

3. **Functional Expression**: I won't be able to make it (declining/announcing absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Informing someone that you cannot attend or be present
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but softened
   
   **Synonyms**: I can't come, I can't attend, I won't be there, I'm unable to attend
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll be there, I can make it, Count me in, I'll attend
   
   **When to Use**: Use informally to neutral contexts when declining invitation or announcing you'll be absent. Softened with "won't be able to" instead of flat "can't" or "won't."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use for very formal contexts (use "I regret I am unable to attend"). Avoid if you need to provide detailed explanation immediately.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm not going to be able to make it
   - I won't be able to attend
   - Can't make it
   - Won't make it
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness—acknowledges obligation while declining. "Make it" is informal. Often followed by brief reason or apology.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "That's okay, we'll miss you"
   - "No worries, maybe next time"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry, I won't be able to make it tonight—got to work late.
   - I won't be able to make it to your party, but have a great time!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I won't be able to make the meeting on Friday due to a prior commitment.
   - Unfortunately, I won't be able to attend the conference this year.

4. **Functional Expression**: Unfortunately, I'll be away (informing about absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Notifying others that you will be absent during a specific time
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct with hedging
   
   **Synonyms**: I'll be out, I'll be absent, I won't be available, I'll be traveling
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll be here, I'll be available, I'll be present, I'll be around
   
   **When to Use**: Use to inform others of planned absence. "Unfortunately" softens the message and shows regret. Works in professional and social contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "unfortunately" if your absence is entirely positive for you (insincere). Avoid when absence needs more detailed explanation.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll be away that week
   - I'm going to be away
   - I'll be out of town
   - I won't be around
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Unfortunately" shows you recognize your absence may inconvenience others. Softens potential negative impact. Face-saving device.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Thanks for letting us know"
   - "Okay, we'll work around that"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Unfortunately, I'll be away next weekend for a family thing.
   - I'll be away during that time, but good luck with the project!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Unfortunately, I'll be away on business during the proposed dates.
   - I will unfortunately be absent from the office next week on annual leave.

5. **Functional Expression**: [Name] is not available at the moment (explaining current absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Informing someone that a person cannot be reached or is temporarily absent
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: [Name] is not here right now, [Name] is unavailable, [Name] is away from their desk, [Name] is out
   
   **Antonyms**: [Name] is available, [Name] is here, You can speak with [Name] now
   
   **When to Use**: Use professionally when taking messages or explaining someone's temporary absence. Common in receptionist/administrative contexts. Neutral and diplomatic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use when person is permanently gone or no longer with organization (use "no longer with us" or "has left"). Avoid when more specific information would be helpful.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - is not available right now
   - is unavailable at present
   - is away from the office
   - is currently out
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Protects absent person's privacy while acknowledging their absence. Professional standard phrase. Does not specify reason for absence.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Can I leave a message?"
   - "When will they be available?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sarah's not available at the moment—can I help you with something?
   - He's not available right now, but I'll have him call you back.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Dr. Johnson is not available at the moment. May I take a message?
   - The manager is currently unavailable. Would you like to schedule an appointment?

6. **Functional Expression**: I'll be out of the office (notification of absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Professional notification that you will be away from workplace
   - *Formality*: neutral (workplace standard)
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I'll be away, I won't be in, I'll be absent, I'll be unavailable
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll be in the office, I'll be available, I'll be at my desk
   
   **When to Use**: Use in professional workplace contexts to inform colleagues of absence. Standard in email subject lines and auto-reply messages. Clear and neutral.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use in social contexts (too formal). Avoid when working remotely is your norm—doesn't apply if you're not usually "in office."
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll be out of office
   - I'm out of the office
   - will be out of the office
   - OOO (out of office - abbreviation)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Standard professional formula. Often includes dates and alternative contact. Shows professionalism and maintains communication expectations.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Thanks for letting me know"
   - "Who should I contact in your absence?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just so you know, I'll be out of the office tomorrow.
   - I'll be out of the office next week—Sam can help you while I'm gone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I will be out of the office from March 15-22 with limited email access.
   - Please note that I will be out of the office and unavailable for meetings next week.

7. **Functional Expression**: Where were you? (questioning absence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asking someone to explain their absence from expected location
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Where have you been?, Why weren't you here?, You weren't there, I didn't see you
   
   **Antonyms**: Good to see you, Glad you made it, Thanks for coming
   
   **When to Use**: Use informally when someone's absence was noticed and you want explanation. Tone varies from concerned to accusatory depending on intonation. Very direct question.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use in formal professional contexts (too direct). Avoid if accusatory tone could damage relationship—rephrase more gently. Can sound confrontational.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Where were you yesterday?
   - Where have you been?
   - You weren't there
   - I didn't see you at [event]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Direct question that can be face-threatening. Implies you expected person to be somewhere. Tone crucial—can range from friendly concern to criticism.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Sorry, I got held up with..."
   - "I couldn't make it because..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Where were you last night? We missed you at the party!
   - Where were you this morning? I needed your help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Too direct for formal contexts; rephrase as "I noticed you were unable to attend...")

8. **Functional Expression**: If [Name] is not available, who can help me? (requesting alternative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asking for an alternative contact when primary person is absent
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct but polite
   
   **Synonyms**: Who can I speak to instead?, Who else can help?, Is there someone else I can contact?, Who's covering for [Name]?
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll wait for [Name], Only [Name] can help me, I need to speak with [Name] specifically
   
   **When to Use**: Use professionally when you need assistance but primary contact is absent. Shows flexibility and problem-solving. Respectful of both parties' time.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use if only specific person can actually help—be clear about that. Avoid if you're trying to go around someone deliberately.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Who else can assist with this?
   - Who's handling [Name]'s responsibilities?
   - Is there an alternative contact?
   - Who should I speak to instead?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Pragmatic problem-solving. Shows you respect people's time and understand absences happen. Helps receptionist/assistant provide better service.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "[Name] is covering for them"
   - "Let me connect you with [alternative]"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If Sarah's not available, who can help me with this issue?
   - Who else knows about this project in case Tom's not around?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In Dr. Chen's absence, who would be the appropriate contact for this matter?
   - If the primary investigator is unavailable, whom should I contact regarding protocol questions?
