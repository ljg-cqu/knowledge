1. **Original Expression**: He's not here right now
   **Context**: Common statement explaining someone's absence

   **Core Intent**: Inform someone that a person is temporarily not present

   **Casual**: He's not here right now  
   *Characteristics*: Contracted verb, simple present, informal tone  
   *Use when*: Talking to friends, family, known colleagues

   **Semi-Formal**: He isn't available at the moment  
   *Characteristics*: Uncontracted negative, euphemistic "available," time marker "at the moment"  
   *Use when*: Professional phone calls, workplace communication, customer service

   **Formal**: Mr. Smith is currently unavailable  
   *Characteristics*: Full title, formal adverb "currently," single formal verb "unavailable"  
   *Use when*: Formal business settings, speaking to clients, official correspondence

   **Humorous**: He's done a disappearing act  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor suggesting magic trick  
   *Use when*: Close relationships, lighthearted situations, when absence is amusing  
   *Avoid when*: Professional contexts, serious absences, speaking to strangers

   **Lively**: He just stepped out!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic verb "stepped out," exclamation mark, dynamic feeling  
   *Use when*: Fast-paced environments, showing responsiveness, indicating brief absence

   **Empathetic**: He's had to step away, but I'm here to help  
   *Characteristics*: Soft explanation "had to," immediately offers alternative support  
   *Use when*: Customer might be disappointed, person might be concerned, support needed

   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Use formal with authority figures, casual with peers
   - *Social Distance*: Formal for strangers, casual for acquaintances, very casual for friends
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious inquiries require formal, routine matters allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more formality in workplace regardless of familiarity
   - *Medium*: Phone/in-person allows casual, written email requires semi-formal minimum

   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual "not here" in professional phone situations—sounds unprofessional and unhelpful
   - Over-formalizing with friends—"Mr. Smith is unavailable" to his buddy sounds ridiculous
   - Not offering alternative help—leaving caller hanging after explaining absence

   **Cultural Notes**: American business culture accepts more casual communication than British or Asian business cultures, where formality is expected even among colleagues

   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend calling your roommate → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "He's not here right now, want me to have him call you?"
   - **Situation 2**: Client calling your manager → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "Ms. Johnson is currently unavailable. May I assist you or take a message?"
   - **Situation 3**: Family member asking about another family member → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Humorous - "She's MIA—probably at the store again"
   - **Situation 4**: Customer asking for specific employee → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal/Empathetic - "I'm sorry, he's stepped away, but I'd be happy to help you with that"

2. **Original Expression**: I can't come to the meeting
   **Context**: Declining to attend a scheduled meeting

   **Core Intent**: Inform others that you will not attend the meeting

   **Casual**: I can't make the meeting  
   *Characteristics*: Simple modal "can't," informal verb "make"  
   *Use when*: Team meetings with close colleagues, informal work environments

   **Semi-Formal**: I won't be able to attend the meeting  
   *Characteristics*: Future modal "won't be able to," formal verb "attend"  
   *Use when*: Standard professional contexts, general workplace communication

   **Formal**: I regret that I am unable to attend the meeting  
   *Characteristics*: Formal regret expression, full modal phrase "am unable to," complete formulation  
   *Use when*: Important stakeholder meetings, communications with senior leadership, written formal notice

   **Humorous**: I'm going to have to ghost that meeting  
   *Characteristics*: Slang "ghost" meaning absent oneself, playful tone  
   *Use when*: Very casual workplace, joking with close colleagues  
   *Avoid when*: Actually communicating absence—too flippant

   **Lively**: Afraid I have to miss this one!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic opening "afraid," exclamation mark, conversational "this one"  
   *Use when*: Regular team meetings, casual work culture, showing you'll miss the interaction

   **Empathetic**: Unfortunately, I have a conflict and won't be able to join you  
   *Characteristics*: Shows regret with "unfortunately," explains reason gently, includes "you" to maintain connection  
   *Use when*: You genuinely regret absence, relationship maintenance important, want to soften impact

   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: More formal when declining meetings with superiors
   - *Social Distance*: Colleagues you know well allow casual, formal meetings require formal decline
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Critical strategic meetings require formal decline with explanation
   - *Cultural Context*: Some organizations expect formal meeting declines regardless of level
   - *Medium*: Email allows semi-formal, calendar decline can be brief, verbal needs more politeness markers

   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Simply declining without any explanation—seems dismissive of others' time
   - Over-explaining with extensive personal details—TMI in professional contexts
   - Using casual language for important meetings—"Can't make it" to board meeting is inappropriate

   **Cultural Notes**: Japanese and Korean business cultures expect more elaborate apologies and explanations for meeting absences than Western cultures

   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Weekly team standup with your immediate team → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "Can't make standup today—I'll send updates via Slack"
   - **Situation 2**: Quarterly review meeting with stakeholders → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "I regret that I am unable to attend the quarterly review due to a prior commitment"
   - **Situation 3**: Coffee chat with colleague → **Appropriate Variant**: Humorous/Casual - "Gotta bail on coffee—totally double-booked myself"
   - **Situation 4**: Client presentation → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal/Empathetic - "I sincerely regret that I am unable to attend the presentation. [Colleague] will represent our team and has been fully briefed"

3. **Original Expression**: Where were you?
   **Context**: Questioning someone's absence when they were expected to be present

   **Core Intent**: Ask someone to explain why they were not present

   **Casual**: Where were you?  
   *Characteristics*: Direct question, potentially confrontational depending on tone  
   *Use when*: Close friends, family, informal contexts where direct questions acceptable

   **Semi-Formal**: I didn't see you at the meeting  
   *Characteristics*: Statement of observation rather than direct question, less confrontational  
   *Use when*: Workplace contexts, when you want explanation without seeming accusatory

   **Formal**: I noticed you were unable to attend yesterday's session  
   *Characteristics*: Euphemistic "unable to attend," acknowledges without demanding explanation  
   *Use when*: Professional documentation, formal workplace, maintaining politeness

   **Humorous**: You pulled a Houdini on us!  
   *Characteristics*: Reference to famous magician known for disappearing, playful accusation  
   *Use when*: Joking with friends or close colleagues about missed social event  
   *Avoid when*: Person might have legitimate serious reason for absence

   **Lively**: Dude, you totally disappeared!  
   *Characteristics*: Informal address "dude," emphatic "totally," casual verb "disappeared"  
   *Use when*: Friends, very casual work environments, expressing surprise at absence

   **Empathetic**: I missed you at the meeting—is everything okay?  
   *Characteristics*: Expresses care with "missed you," shows concern with "is everything okay?"  
   *Use when*: You're genuinely concerned, relationship is important, absence was unusual

   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Never question upward directly—phrase as observation. Can question peers directly if relationship allows
   - *Social Distance*: Direct questions only appropriate with close relationships
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious absences require empathetic approach, casual absences allow lighter tone
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures see direct questioning as rude regardless of relationship
   - *Medium*: Face-to-face allows more direct questions, written should be more diplomatic

   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Questioning superior's absence directly—inappropriate and presumptuous
   - Accusatory tone when person may have legitimate reason—damages trust
   - Public calling out of absence—embarrassing and unprofessional

   **Cultural Notes**: In hierarchical cultures (Asian, Middle Eastern), questioning anyone's absence directly is inappropriate. Use indirect observations instead.

   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend missed casual hangout → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "Where were you last night? We missed you!"
   - **Situation 2**: Colleague missed important meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal - "I didn't see you at the stakeholder meeting. Should I fill you in on what was discussed?"
   - **Situation 3**: Employee absent without notification → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "I noticed you were absent from work yesterday without prior notification"
   - **Situation 4**: Family member missed holiday gathering → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "We really missed you at Thanksgiving. Is everything alright?"

4. **Original Expression**: He didn't show up
   **Context**: Reporting or commenting on someone's absence

   **Core Intent**: State that someone failed to appear as expected

   **Casual**: He didn't show up  
   *Characteristics*: Phrasal verb "show up," simple past, neutral to slightly negative  
   *Use when*: Informal conversation, stating facts to friends or colleagues

   **Semi-Formal**: He didn't attend  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "attend," neutral tone, professional  
   *Use when*: Workplace communication, professional emails, factual reporting

   **Formal**: He was absent from the proceedings  
   *Characteristics*: Formal noun "proceedings," passive construction, very formal  
   *Use when*: Legal contexts, official documentation, very formal business reports

   **Humorous**: He totally bailed on us  
   *Characteristics*: Slang "bailed," intensifier "totally," casual accusation  
   *Use when*: Friends, casual complaint about social absence  
   *Avoid when*: Professional contexts, serious situations

   **Lively**: He was a no-show!  
   *Characteristics*: Informal compound noun "no-show," exclamation mark, emphatic  
   *Use when*: Expressing surprise or frustration, casual work environments

   **Empathetic**: He wasn't able to make it  
   *Characteristics*: Gentle phrasing "wasn't able to," gives benefit of doubt  
   *Use when*: Explaining absence charitably, maintaining person's reputation, uncertain of reason

   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Use empathetic or formal when discussing superior's absence
   - *Social Distance*: Casual with friends, formal when discussing stranger or client's absence
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious professional absence requires neutral formal reporting
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures require very careful language about anyone's absence
   - *Medium*: Written documentation requires formal neutral language

   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "didn't show up" in formal reports—too casual
   - Judgmental tone without knowing reason—unfair and unprofessional
   - Harsh language when discussing someone's absence with third parties—gossip

   **Cultural Notes**: In collective cultures, reporting someone's absence negatively can be seen as betrayal rather than factual reporting

   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend missed birthday party → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "John didn't show up, but Sarah said he's sick"
   - **Situation 2**: Reporting meeting attendance to manager → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal - "All team members attended except Marcus, who didn't attend due to illness"
   - **Situation 3**: Court documentation → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "The defendant was absent from the scheduled hearing"
   - **Situation 4**: Explaining colleague's absence to their friend → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "She wasn't able to make it today—she had something come up"

5. **Original Expression**: I need to take a day off
   **Context**: Requesting absence from work or commitment

   **Core Intent**: Inform/request permission to be absent

   **Casual**: I need to take a day off  
   *Characteristics*: Simple "need to," direct request  
   *Use when*: Casual workplace, informal notification to peers

   **Semi-Formal**: I'd like to request a day off  
   *Characteristics*: Polite conditional "I'd like to," formal verb "request"  
   *Use when*: Standard professional request to supervisor, normal workplace

   **Formal**: I wish to request a leave of absence  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "wish to," official term "leave of absence"  
   *Use when*: Very formal workplace, extended absence, written formal requests

   **Humorous**: I'm calling in "mental health day"  
   *Characteristics*: Euphemistic quotes around "mental health day," playful justification  
   *Use when*: Close colleagues in casual environment, joking about needing break  
   *Avoid when*: Actual serious mental health situations, formal requests

   **Lively**: I'm taking a personal day!  
   *Characteristics*: Definitive statement, exclamation mark, positive spin "personal day"  
   *Use when*: Casual workplace that trusts employees, announcing rather than requesting

   **Empathetic**: I need to take care of something personal—could I have tomorrow off?  
   *Characteristics*: Brief explanation "something personal," polite question form  
   *Use when*: Good relationship with supervisor, prefer to give some context, want approval

   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher formality when requesting from authority, can be casual between peers
   - *Social Distance*: More formal request language for distant supervisor relationships
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious reasons allow more direct requests, casual reasons need more politeness
   - *Cultural Context*: Some workplaces expect formal written requests; others accept informal notification
   - *Medium*: Email allows semi-formal, in-person can be more casual, official forms require formal

   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Telling rather than asking when supervisor approval required—seems presumptuous
   - Over-sharing personal details—TMI makes everyone uncomfortable
   - Last-minute requests without acknowledgment of short notice—seems inconsiderate

   **Cultural Notes**: American workplaces increasingly casual about time-off requests; European and Asian workplaces maintain formal request procedures

   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Close-knit startup team → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "Hey, I need to take tomorrow off—family thing"
   - **Situation 2**: Corporate environment with formal supervisor → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "I would like to formally request leave on June 15th for personal reasons"
   - **Situation 3**: Healthcare or service job with scheduling → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal/Empathetic - "I need to request a day off next Tuesday—I have a medical appointment I can't reschedule"
   - **Situation 4**: Remote flexible workplace → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively - "Taking a PTO day Friday—I'll have my email covered!"
