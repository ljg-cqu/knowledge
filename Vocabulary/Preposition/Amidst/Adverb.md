1. **Adverb**: nevertheless (type: sentence/discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In spite of that; notwithstanding; however (front or mid position)
   - Used to introduce a statement that contrasts with something just mentioned
   
   **Synonyms**: nonetheless, however, yet, still, even so
   
   **Antonyms**: therefore, thus, consequently, accordingly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to introduce contrast or concession; often used with "amidst" constructions to show persistence despite challenges; formal register; appropriate in academic, professional, and formal writing; emphasizes determination
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech (use "but" or "still"); not for cause-effect relationships; cannot start independent clause without punctuation; avoid overuse in same text
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nevertheless continue/persist (amidst)
   - nevertheless succeed/thrive
   - nevertheless manage/maintain
   
   **Root Analysis**: never (Old English "næfre") + the (article) + less (Old English "læs" meaning less)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "never the less" → "nevertheless" (14th century, originally three words, meaning "not any the less for that")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Things were chaotic, but she nevertheless kept going amidst all the problems.
   - He was scared; nevertheless, he moved forward amidst the uncertainty.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization nevertheless maintained operational capacity amidst severe resource constraints.
   - Research nevertheless continued amidst considerable methodological challenges.

1. **Adverb**: nonetheless (type: sentence/discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In spite of that; nevertheless; however (front or mid position)
   - Used to introduce a contrasting statement despite what has just been said
   
   **Synonyms**: nevertheless, however, yet, still, even so
   
   **Antonyms**: therefore, thus, consequently, hence
   
   **When to Use**: Use to show contrast while maintaining formal tone; common with "amidst" contexts to emphasize persistence; formal register; appropriate in academic and professional writing; slightly less formal than "nevertheless"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; not for cause-effect relationships; requires proper punctuation; avoid redundancy with "but" or "however"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nonetheless proceed/continue (amidst)
   - nonetheless achieve/succeed
   - nonetheless remain/persist
   
   **Root Analysis**: none (no, not) + the (article) + less (meaning less, Old English "læs")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "none the less" → "nonetheless" (14th century, originally three words)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It was tough, but she nonetheless pushed through amidst all the chaos.
   - He was worried; nonetheless, he kept working amidst the uncertainty.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative nonetheless advanced amidst substantial institutional resistance.
   - Implementation nonetheless proceeded amidst widespread stakeholder concerns.

1. **Adverb**: particularly (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a higher degree than usual; especially; very (mid or end position)
   - Used to single out one person or thing over all others
   
   **Synonyms**: especially, notably, specifically, very, exceptionally
   
   **Antonyms**: generally, ordinarily, typically, commonly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize degree or specificity; frequently modifies adjectives in "amidst" constructions; appropriate in all registers; emphasizes exceptional nature; can intensify or specify
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when emphasis is unneeded; can sound formal if overused; not for equal emphasis on multiple items; redundant with other intensifiers
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - particularly challenging/difficult (amidst)
   - particularly intense/severe
   - particularly significant/notable
   
   **Root Analysis**: particular (from Latin "particularis" meaning relating to a part) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "particula" (small part) → "particularis" → French "particulier" → English "particularly" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She stayed calm amidst particularly chaotic circumstances.
   - Things got particularly tough amidst all the changes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization adapted successfully amidst particularly challenging market conditions.
   - Research progressed amidst particularly severe methodological constraints.

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:
