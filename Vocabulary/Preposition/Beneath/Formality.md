1. **Original Expression**: That's beneath you  
   **Context**: Expressing that someone is acting in a way that's unworthy of their character or status
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that someone's behavior or action is not worthy of their standards or dignity
   
   **Casual**: That's not like you / You're better than that  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary, direct address, conversational tone  
   *Use when*: With friends, family, peers in informal settings; when offering friendly advice or gentle criticism
   
   **Semi-Formal**: That behavior doesn't reflect well on you / This isn't up to your usual standards  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral vocabulary, professional but approachable, indirect criticism  
   *Use when*: In workplace settings with colleagues, professional but not overly formal relationships
   
   **Formal**: Such conduct is beneath your professional dignity / This action falls below the standards expected of your position  
   *Characteristics*: Abstract nouns (dignity, standards), passive constructions, institutional tone  
   *Use when*: Official contexts, addressing authority figures, formal written communication
   
   **Humorous**: Come on, you're way too classy for that kind of thing / That's so beneath your royal highness!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful exaggeration, mock formality, lighthearted teasing  
   *Use when*: Close relationships, rapport-building, when humor is welcome  
   *Avoid when*: Serious situations, recent mistakes, when person is sensitive
   
   **Lively**: Hey! You're so much better than this! / No way—that's totally not your style!  
   *Characteristics*: Exclamations, emphatic adverbs, energetic delivery  
   *Use when*: Encouraging someone, showing enthusiasm for their higher standards
   
   **Empathetic**: I know you're capable of better than this / I understand you're struggling, but this doesn't represent who you are  
   *Characteristics*: Recognition of feelings, "I" statements, validating language  
   *Use when*: Someone is acting out due to stress, emotional situations, when support is needed
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status (formal: "beneath your position"), equal status (casual: "not like you")
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/acquaintances (semi-formal/formal), close friends (casual/humorous)
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious misconduct (formal), minor lapse (casual), ongoing pattern (empathetic)
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more indirect criticism regardless of relationship
   - *Medium*: Written (more formal), face-to-face (can be more casual)
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "beneath you" too formally in casual contexts—sounds preachy or condescending
   - Being too casual about serious professional misconduct—undermines the severity
   - Using humorous variants when someone is genuinely upset or the situation is serious
   
   **Cultural Notes**: The concept of something being "beneath" one's dignity varies across cultures; some emphasize personal honor more strongly, while others focus on collective reputation
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Close friend gossiping maliciously → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("You're better than that") or Empathetic
   - **Situation 2**: Employee engaging in petty office politics → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("This doesn't reflect well on you")
   - **Situation 3**: Senior official involved in scandal → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Beneath your professional dignity")

2. **Original Expression**: Hidden beneath the surface  
   **Context**: Describing something that's not immediately visible or apparent
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that there's more to a situation, person, or issue than what's visible at first glance
   
   **Casual**: There's more to it than meets the eye / It's not what it seems on the surface  
   *Characteristics*: Idiomatic expressions, simple words, conversational phrases  
   *Use when*: Everyday conversations, explaining situations to friends, casual analysis
   
   **Semi-Formal**: There are underlying factors at play / The situation is more complex than it appears  
   *Characteristics*: Professional vocabulary, clear structure, neutral tone  
   *Use when*: Business meetings, professional discussions, workplace analysis
   
   **Formal**: Significant complexities lie beneath the apparent simplicity / Deeper examination reveals concealed dimensions  
   *Characteristics*: Abstract nouns, sophisticated vocabulary, academic tone  
   *Use when*: Academic writing, formal presentations, research contexts
   
   **Humorous**: It's like an iceberg—most of the crazy is underwater / The drama beneath the surface could fill a novel  
   *Characteristics*: Metaphors, exaggeration, playful comparisons  
   *Use when*: Gossiping with friends, lighthearted discussions, storytelling  
   *Avoid when*: Serious problems, professional analysis, sensitive topics
   
   **Lively**: Oh, you wouldn't believe what's really going on underneath all that! / There's a whole world hiding beneath what you see!  
   *Characteristics*: Exclamations, dramatic language, engaging delivery  
   *Use when*: Storytelling, engaging an audience, creating intrigue
   
   **Empathetic**: I sense there are deeper issues here that we're not seeing / It seems like there might be more beneath the surface that's affecting you  
   *Characteristics*: Tentative language ("seems," "might"), person-focused, validating  
   *Use when*: Counseling contexts, supporting someone, discussing personal issues
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Analyzing superior's situation (formal), peer's situation (casual/semi-formal)
   - *Social Distance*: Professional contexts (semi-formal/formal), personal relationships (casual)
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious problems (formal/empathetic), interesting complexity (lively), gossip (humorous)
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct cultures prefer explicit statement; indirect cultures appreciate metaphorical language
   - *Medium*: Written analysis (formal), conversation (casual/semi-formal)
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Overusing metaphorical language in formal reports—be more explicit
   - Being too clinical about emotional situations—lacks empathy
   - Using humorous variants for serious psychological or safety issues
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some cultures value reading between the lines more than others; Western cultures often prefer explicit communication while East Asian cultures may expect hidden meanings
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend acting strangely → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("deeper issues here")
   - **Situation 2**: Business proposal analysis → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("underlying factors")
   - **Situation 3**: Academic paper about psychological phenomena → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("complexities lie beneath")

3. **Original Expression**: Buried beneath layers of complexity  
   **Context**: Describing information or truth that's obscured by complicated details or explanations
   
   **Core Intent**: To express that something simple or important is hidden under excessive complication
   
   **Casual**: Lost in all the complicated stuff / Hidden under a bunch of confusing details  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary, vague quantifiers ("bunch," "stuff"), informal  
   *Use when*: Everyday frustration, casual explanations, complaining to friends
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Obscured by unnecessary complexity / Hidden within overly technical explanations  
   *Characteristics*: Professional vocabulary, clear attribution of fault, moderate formality  
   *Use when*: Business critique, professional feedback, workplace discussions
   
   **Formal**: Concealed beneath multiple strata of technical abstraction / Obfuscated by excessive procedural complexity  
   *Characteristics*: Latin-derived vocabulary, metaphorical layers, academic register  
   *Use when*: Academic writing, formal critiques, technical documentation analysis
   
   **Humorous**: Buried under a mountain of jargon / Lost in a maze of overcomplicated nonsense  
   *Characteristics*: Exaggerated metaphors, dismissive language, comedic frustration  
   *Use when*: Venting with colleagues, informal critiques, when humor deflects frustration  
   *Avoid when*: Formal reviews, when criticizing superiors directly, sensitive projects
   
   **Lively**: It's totally drowning in complications! / The simple truth is buried under this huge pile of technical stuff!  
   *Characteristics*: Emphatic expressions, energetic metaphors, exclamations  
   *Use when*: Presentations, trying to engage audience, advocating for simplification
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand this feels overwhelming with all the complexity / The core issue seems buried—let's work together to uncover it  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of difficulty, collaborative language, supportive tone  
   *Use when*: Teaching contexts, helping someone understand, managing confusion
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Critiquing superiors (careful semi-formal), peers (casual/humorous), own work (any)
   - *Social Distance*: Professional unknown (formal), colleagues (semi-formal), friends (casual)
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious consequences of complexity (formal), general frustration (casual), teaching moment (empathetic)
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct cultures accept explicit criticism; indirect cultures need softer language
   - *Medium*: Written critique (formal/semi-formal), verbal venting (casual/humorous)
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using humorous variants in formal documentation—appears unprofessional
   - Being too harsh when complexity is legitimately necessary—consider empathetic approach
   - Oversimplifying genuinely complex issues while claiming they're "just complicated"
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Attitudes toward complexity vary; some cultures value elaborate explanations as thorough, while others see them as obscuring clarity
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Student struggling with overly technical textbook → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic
   - **Situation 2**: Critiquing colleague's unnecessarily complex proposal → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal
   - **Situation 3**: Academic paper about communication clarity → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal

4. **Original Expression**: Beneath my notice / Not worth my attention  
   **Context**: Indicating something is too trivial or unworthy to pay attention to
   
   **Core Intent**: To express that something doesn't deserve consideration or attention due to low importance or value
   
   **Casual**: Not worth my time / Can't be bothered with that / That's too petty for me  
   *Characteristics*: Contracted forms, dismissive tone, simple vocabulary  
   *Use when*: With peers, dismissing minor irritations, casual conversations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: That's not a priority / I don't consider that significant / That falls outside my concerns  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral vocabulary, professional boundary-setting, diplomatic  
   *Use when*: Workplace contexts, professional boundaries, declining unimportant requests
   
   **Formal**: Such matters fall beneath the purview of this office / That lies outside the scope of appropriate consideration  
   *Characteristics*: Institutional language, abstract nouns, formal structures  
   *Use when*: Official communications, high-status individuals, formal dismissals
   
   **Humorous**: That's too small-time for me! / I don't sweat the small stuff like that  
   *Characteristics*: Playful exaggeration, idiomatic expressions, light self-mockery  
   *Use when*: Close relationships, self-deprecating humor, lighthearted contexts  
   *Avoid when*: Could appear genuinely arrogant, professional settings, when others care about the issue
   
   **Lively**: Oh, I'm way too busy for that kind of nonsense! / Life's too short to worry about stuff like that!  
   *Characteristics*: Emphatic dismissal, energetic delivery, focus on priorities  
   *Use when*: Motivational contexts, encouraging others to focus on important things
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand why that matters to you, but I need to focus on other priorities / I respect your concern, but that's not something I can attend to right now  
   *Characteristics*: Validation before dismissal, respectful boundaries, acknowledgment  
   *Use when*: Saying no to requests, maintaining boundaries with empathy, differing priorities
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status (can use formal dismissal), equal/lower status (empathetic/semi-formal)
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers (semi-formal/formal), close relationships (casual, use humor carefully)
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Truly trivial matters (casual), someone's genuine concern (empathetic), official capacity (formal)
   - *Cultural Context*: Individualistic cultures more direct; collectivist cultures need softer dismissal
   - *Medium*: Written (semi-formal/formal), spoken (can be more casual)
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "beneath my notice" literally—sounds extremely arrogant in modern English
   - Being too dismissive of others' concerns—appears disrespectful even if matter is minor
   - Using casual dismissals in professional contexts where diplomacy is required
   
   **Cultural Notes**: The concept of something being "beneath notice" can appear arrogant; many cultures prefer softer boundary-setting that acknowledges the other person's perspective
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend obsessing over trivial social media drama → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Lively
   - **Situation 2**: Colleague requesting help with unrelated minor task → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic/Semi-Formal
   - **Situation 3**: Official declining to address petition on trivial matter → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal
