1. **Preposition**: beneath (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Directly under something or covered by something; at a lower level than something, especially when touching or very close
   - *(Abstract)* Considered to be of lower status, value, or dignity than someone or something; not worthy of someone
   - *(Figurative)* Hidden by or under the surface appearance; underneath the visible exterior
   
   **Synonyms**: under (spatial contact), below (lower position), underneath (covered by), lower than (comparison), unworthy of (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: above, over, atop, on top of, beyond
   
   **When to Use**: When describing something directly under and in contact with or very close to something else, expressing that something is hidden or obscured by a surface layer, or indicating that something is considered unworthy or below someone's standards
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to measurements or numbers (use "below" instead: "below 50 degrees" not "beneath 50 degrees"), when describing non-physical superiority without the connotation of unworthiness (use "below" for neutral hierarchy), or in casual measurement contexts where "below" is standard
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: beneath the surface, beneath contempt, beneath notice, beneath dignity, beneath one's station, beneath the waves, beneath ground level, beneath the skin, beneath suspicion
   - **Verb + prep**: lie beneath, hide beneath, lurk beneath, bury beneath, conceal beneath, sink beneath, fall beneath
   - **Adjective + prep**: hidden beneath, buried beneath, visible beneath, trapped beneath
   - **Noun + prep**: truth beneath, layer beneath, ground beneath, feelings beneath, meaning beneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by, about) + neath (below, from Old English neoþan "below, down")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English bineoþan, from bi- (by) + neoþan (below, beneath) → Middle English benethe → modern "beneath" meaning "in a lower place, directly under"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a spare key beneath the flowerpot by the door if you need to get in.
   - She acts tough, but beneath all that attitude, she's actually really caring.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Beneath the apparent simplicity of the theory lies a sophisticated mathematical framework.
   - The archaeological team discovered a previously unknown civilization buried beneath layers of sediment.

2. **Preposition**: from beneath (type: compound/phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Starting from a position that is under something; originating from below or underneath
   - *(Figurative)* Emerging from a hidden or covered state
   
   **Synonyms**: from under, from below, from underneath, emerging from
   
   **Antonyms**: from above, from over, from atop
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing the origin point or starting location of movement or emergence from under something, particularly when describing something coming out or being revealed
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple location without movement is described (use "beneath" alone), or when the source is not literally under something
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from beneath the rubble, from beneath the covers, from beneath the ground, from beneath the waves
   - **Verb + prep**: emerge from beneath, appear from beneath, crawl from beneath, rise from beneath, extract from beneath
   - **Adjective + prep**: visible from beneath, audible from beneath
   - **Noun + prep**: sound from beneath, voice from beneath, light from beneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: from (Old English fram "forward, away") + beneath (be- + neath)
   
   **Etymology**: Combination of "from" (indicating source/origin) + "beneath" (below) → used to indicate movement or origin from an under-position
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A weird sound was coming from beneath the floorboards, so we called someone to check it out.
   - She peeked at him from beneath her lashes, trying not to be too obvious.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The rescue team successfully extracted survivors from beneath the collapsed structure.
   - Data retrieved from beneath the ocean floor provides crucial evidence for tectonic plate movement theories.

3. **Preposition**: out from beneath (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving away from a position that was underneath something; emerging or escaping from under
   - *(Abstract)* Freeing oneself from a difficult, oppressive, or burdensome situation
   
   **Synonyms**: from under, away from beneath, escaping from under, emerging from underneath
   
   **Antonyms**: into beneath, back under, returning beneath
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing the complete movement or escape from being underneath something, particularly when there's a sense of freedom, relief, or revelation involved
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the movement is minor or doesn't involve complete emergence, or when describing static positions
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: out from beneath the debris, out from beneath the weight, out from beneath control
   - **Verb + prep**: pull out from beneath, drag out from beneath, come out from beneath, slide out from beneath, crawl out from beneath
   - **Adjective + prep**: freed out from beneath, released out from beneath
   - **Noun + prep**: emergence out from beneath, escape out from beneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: out (Old English ūt "outside, outward") + from (away from origin) + beneath (below)
   
   **Etymology**: Combination of directional prepositions: "out" (outward motion) + "from" (source) + "beneath" (below position) → emphasizes complete emergence from under-position
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The cat finally came out from beneath the bed after hiding there all morning.
   - I'm so glad to be out from beneath all that debt—it feels like I can breathe again.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The nation emerged out from beneath decades of authoritarian rule to establish a democratic system.
   - Researchers carefully extracted the artifact out from beneath multiple geological strata.
