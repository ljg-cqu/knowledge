1. **Noun**: surface (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The outside or top layer of something; the uppermost boundary of an object or body
   - *(When used with "beneath")* The visible exterior under which hidden realities exist
   
   **Synonyms**: exterior, outside, top layer, facade, face
   
   **Antonyms**: interior, inside, core, depth, center
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing the outer layer that covers or conceals what lies beneath; particularly common in phrases like "beneath the surface"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing the actual hidden content itself (use "interior" or "underneath"), or when referring to subject matter rather than physical layers
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beneath the surface
   - lies beneath the surface
   - hidden beneath the surface
   - look beneath the surface
   - scratch beneath the surface
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (above, over) + face (from Latin facies "face, appearance")
   
   **Etymology**: French surface (outer face) → English "surface" (1600s) → commonly used with "beneath" to contrast visible exterior with hidden interior
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everything seems fine on the surface, but there's a lot of tension beneath it.
   - You have to look beneath the surface to see what's really going on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines the psychological mechanisms that operate beneath the surface of conscious awareness.
   - Geothermal activity beneath the surface generates significant heat for energy production.

2. **Noun**: layer (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thickness of material covering a surface or forming one level in a series of levels; a level of material that exists under or over another
   
   **Synonyms**: stratum, level, tier, coating, sheet
   
   **Antonyms**: none specific (can contrast with "whole" or "mass")
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing multiple levels of material, meaning, or complexity stacked on top of each other, with "beneath" indicating what's under upper layers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing a single undifferentiated mass or when there's no stratification involved
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beneath layers of meaning
   - beneath layers of paint
   - buried beneath layers
   - hidden beneath layers
   - beneath multiple layers
   
   **Root Analysis**: lay (from Old English lecgan "to place") + -er (agent suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English leyer (one who lays) → "layer" (a thing laid down) → used with "beneath" to indicate what's under covering layers
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's probably a nice wood floor beneath all those layers of old linoleum.
   - Beneath all those layers of sarcasm, he's actually pretty sensitive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Archaeological excavation revealed artifacts beneath multiple sedimentary layers.
   - The algorithm processes information beneath several layers of abstraction.

3. **Noun**: ground (U, sometimes C as "grounds")
   **Meaning**: 
   - The solid surface of the earth; soil or earth
   - *(Abstract)* The foundation or basis for something
   
   **Synonyms**: earth, soil, land, terrain, floor
   
   **Antonyms**: sky, air, above, surface (in some contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to the earth's surface, particularly in contexts where something is buried, hidden, or located beneath it
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to reasons or justifications (that's a different sense of "grounds"), or when discussing elevated locations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beneath the ground
   - buried beneath the ground
   - beneath ground level
   - lies beneath the ground
   - tunnel beneath the ground
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English grund (bottom, foundation, ground)
   
   **Etymology**: Proto-Germanic *grunduz (ground, bottom) → Old English grund → Middle English ground → modern usage with "beneath" for sub-surface location
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The utility lines run beneath the ground so you don't see them.
   - There's supposedly treasure buried beneath the ground somewhere around here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is located entirely beneath ground level for security purposes.
   - Extensive root systems extend beneath the ground to anchor large trees.

4. **Noun**: debris (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Scattered pieces of waste or remains; broken or destroyed material
   
   **Synonyms**: rubble, wreckage, ruins, remains, fragments
   
   **Antonyms**: intact structure, whole, completeness
   
   **When to Use**: When describing broken or scattered material that covers or buries something, particularly after destruction or collapse
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to organized waste or intentional storage (use "materials" or "storage"), or clean undamaged items
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - buried beneath debris
   - trapped beneath debris
   - hidden beneath debris
   - found beneath debris
   - clear debris from beneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: French débris (broken pieces, remains)
   
   **Etymology**: French débris (from débriser "to break apart") → English "debris" (1700s) → commonly used with "beneath" in disaster/rescue contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They finally found the car keys beneath all the debris in the garage.
   - Survivors were pulled from beneath the debris hours after the earthquake.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Rescue operations focused on locating survivors trapped beneath structural debris.
   - Archaeological artifacts were discovered beneath debris from the medieval period.

5. **Noun**: veneer (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thin decorative covering of fine wood applied to furniture; an attractive appearance that covers something less pleasant
   - *(Figurative)* An attractive but superficial appearance that conceals true character
   
   **Synonyms**: facade, surface, covering, coating, mask
   
   **Antonyms**: reality, substance, core, truth, interior
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing a thin superficial layer that covers something, often used metaphorically with "beneath" to contrast appearance with reality
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing thick or substantial coverings, or when there's no sense of deception or contrast
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beneath the veneer
   - beneath a veneer of civility
   - beneath a thin veneer
   - truth beneath the veneer
   - hidden beneath the veneer
   
   **Root Analysis**: French fournir (to furnish) → via German Furnier
   
   **Etymology**: German Furnier (thin layer of wood) → French vernis → English "veneer" (1700s) → metaphorically extended to superficial appearances
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Beneath that veneer of confidence, she's actually pretty nervous about the presentation.
   - The table looks expensive, but it's just cheap wood beneath the veneer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Beneath the veneer of professional courtesy lay deep organizational conflicts.
   - The analysis penetrates beneath the veneer of official statistics to reveal underlying trends.

6. **Noun**: facade (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The front of a building, especially an impressive or decorative one
   - *(Figurative)* An outward appearance that conceals a less pleasant reality
   
   **Synonyms**: front, exterior, face, appearance, pretense
   
   **Antonyms**: reality, truth, interior, substance, core
   
   **When to Use**: When describing a superficial or deceptive outward appearance that hides true nature or condition, often used with "beneath" to emphasize the contrast
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing genuine appearances or when there's no element of concealment or deception
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beneath the facade
   - beneath a cheerful facade
   - hidden beneath the facade
   - truth beneath the facade
   - beneath a facade of normalcy
   
   **Root Analysis**: French façade (frontage, face)
   
   **Etymology**: French façade (from Italian facciata) → English "facade" (1600s) → metaphorically used with "beneath" to contrast appearance with reality
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Beneath his tough-guy facade, he's really sweet and caring.
   - The company's success was just a facade—beneath it, they were nearly bankrupt.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Beneath the facade of political unity lay significant ideological divisions.
   - The organization maintained a facade of legitimacy that concealed fraudulent operations beneath.

7. **Noun**: contempt (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The feeling that someone or something is worthless or deserving of no respect; scorn
   - *(In the phrase "beneath contempt")* So bad or despicable as to not even deserve contempt
   
   **Synonyms**: scorn, disdain, derision, disrespect, disgust
   
   **Antonyms**: respect, admiration, esteem, regard, honor
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing extreme disapproval or the sense that something is utterly worthless, particularly in the phrase "beneath contempt"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral or positive contexts, or when mild disapproval (not scorn) is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beneath contempt
   - beneath one's contempt
   - hold in contempt
   - show contempt for
   - treat with contempt
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (intensive) + temn (from Latin temnere "to despise")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin contemptus (despised) → Old French contempt → English "contempt" (1300s) → "beneath contempt" means too despicable to even deserve disdain
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The way they treated those workers was beneath contempt.
   - His behavior at the party was just beneath contempt—everyone was disgusted.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Such flagrant violations of ethical standards are beneath contempt and warrant immediate investigation.
   - The tribunal found the defendant's actions to be beneath contempt and deserving of maximum sanctions.

8. **Noun**: dignity (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The quality of being worthy of honor or respect; self-respect
   - *(In the phrase "beneath one's dignity")* Not worthy of someone; too demeaning for someone to do
   
   **Synonyms**: honor, self-respect, pride, decorum, propriety
   
   **Antonyms**: indignity, humiliation, disgrace, shame
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing what is appropriate or worthy of respect, particularly in the phrase "beneath one's dignity" to indicate something is too demeaning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing appropriate or worthy actions (those are "within" or "befitting" dignity, not "beneath" it)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beneath one's dignity
   - beneath the dignity of
   - consider beneath one's dignity
   - thought it beneath her dignity
   - beneath their dignity
   
   **Root Analysis**: dignus (Latin: worthy) + -ity (state of being)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin dignitas (worthiness) → Old French dignité → English "dignity" (1200s) → "beneath one's dignity" meaning too lowly or demeaning for someone
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He thought washing dishes was beneath his dignity, which is ridiculous.
   - She acts like talking to us is beneath her dignity or something.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The ambassador considered such petty negotiations beneath the dignity of his office.
   - Engaging in such conduct would be beneath the dignity expected of a member of this institution.
