1. **Adjective**: responsible (for)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the duty or obligation to deal with something or take care of someone; being the cause of something; answerable or accountable for something. (predicative and attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: accountable for, answerable for, in charge of, liable for
   
   **Antonyms**: irresponsible, unaccountable, not liable for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when assigning or describing accountability; in professional contexts defining roles; when explaining causation; combines with "for" to specify domain of responsibility; appropriate in formal and semi-formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "responsible to" (accountable to a person) is meant; avoid when no actual accountability exists; be careful distinguishing "responsible for causing" vs. "responsible for managing."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - responsible for managing/overseeing
   - responsible for one's actions
   - held responsible for
   - directly responsible for
   - ultimately responsible for
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (intensive) + spondere (pledge, promise) + -ible (able to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "respondere" (to respond, answer for) → French "responsable" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Who's responsible for cleaning the kitchen this week?
   - I'm not responsible for what he said—that's on him.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manager is responsible for ensuring compliance with all safety regulations.
   - The committee is responsible for reviewing grant applications.

1. **Adjective**: ready (for)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Prepared or equipped to do or deal with something; in a suitable state for an action or situation; willing and eager. (predicative and attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: prepared for, set for, equipped for, primed for, willing to
   
   **Antonyms**: unprepared for, unready for, unwilling to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing preparedness or willingness; before actions or events; when expressing eagerness; combines with "for" to specify what one is prepared for; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "ready for" (prepared for something) with "ready to" (prepared to do something); avoid when actual preparation is lacking; don't use when "willing" (mental state) vs. "ready" (practical preparation) is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ready for action
   - ready for a challenge
   - get ready for
   - more than ready for
   - not ready for
   
   **Root Analysis**: ready (Old English "ræde" - prepared, prompt)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "geræde" (arranged, prepared) → Middle English "redi" → English "ready"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you ready for this? It's gonna be intense!
   - I don't think I'm ready for that conversation yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization is now ready for the next phase of expansion.
   - Participants should arrive ready for a full day of training.

1. **Adjective**: famous (for)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Known about by many people; renowned or celebrated for a particular quality, achievement, or product. (attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: renowned for, celebrated for, well-known for, noted for, famed for
   
   **Antonyms**: unknown for, infamous for (negatively famous), obscure
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing widespread recognition; when identifying what someone/something is known for; in contexts of celebrity or reputation; combines with "for" to specify reason for fame; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "infamous" (famous for bad things); avoid when local recognition doesn't constitute widespread fame; be careful with ironic usage ("famous for being late").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - famous for [achievement/quality]
   - world-famous for
   - become famous for
   - most famous for
   - made famous for
   
   **Root Analysis**: fam(e) (Latin "fama" - rumor, reputation) + -ous (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "fama" (reputation) → "famosus" → Old French "fameus" → English "famous"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This place is famous for its amazing tacos.
   - She's famous for always being late to everything.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution is famous for its groundbreaking research in genetics.
   - Leonardo da Vinci is famous for masterpieces such as the Mona Lisa.

   **Synonyms**: appropriate for, fitting for, proper for, right for, apt for
   
   **Antonyms**: unsuitable for, inappropriate for, unfit for, wrong for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing appropriateness or fitness for purpose; in contexts of matching requirements; when evaluating compatibility; combines with "for" to specify purpose or context; appropriate in formal and semi-formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when simpler "good for" or "right for" suffices in casual contexts; avoid when "appropriate" focuses more on social propriety vs. "suitable" on practical fitness.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - suitable for all ages
   - suitable for beginners
   - most suitable for
   - perfectly suitable for
   - not suitable for
   
   **Root Analysis**: suit (Old French "suite" - following) + -able (capable of)
   
   **Etymology**: French "suite" → "suit" (match, be fitting) + "-able" → "suitable"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This movie isn't really suitable for kids—it's pretty violent.
   - Are those shoes suitable for hiking?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate possesses qualifications eminently suitable for the position.
   - This methodology is particularly suitable for large-scale data analysis.

   - truly/deeply sorry for
   - nothing to be sorry for
   
   **Root Analysis**: sorry (Old English "sarig" - distressed, grieved)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "sarig" (pained, distressed) → Middle English "sory" → "sorry"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm sorry for snapping at you earlier.
   - I feel sorry for anyone who has to work on weekends.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company is deeply sorry for the errors in the report.
   - We are sorry for any confusion this announcement may have caused.

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Am I eligible for the discount?

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applicants must meet specific criteria to be eligible for consideration.

1. **Adjective**: anxious (for)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Eager or earnestly desirous for something to happen; worried about something; experiencing anxiety about or desiring something. (predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: eager for, keen for, desirous of, worried about, concerned about
   
   **Antonyms**: indifferent to, unconcerned about, reluctant to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing strong desire or eagerness (positive); when describing worry or concern (negative); combines with "for" to specify object of eagerness or concern; appropriate in formal and semi-formal contexts; note: "anxious for" (eager) is less common in modern usage than "anxious about" (worried).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "anxious for" (eager) with "anxious about" (worried about); in modern usage, "eager for" is clearer than "anxious for" for positive meanings; avoid when simple "worried about" is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anxious for news
   - anxious for someone's safety (worried)
   - anxious for change (eager)
   - anxious for the future (worried)
   - deeply anxious for
   
   **Root Analysis**: anxi(ety) (Latin "anxius" - troubled) + -ous (characterized by)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "angere" (to choke, distress) → "anxius" → English "anxious"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really anxious for the test results to come back.
   - She's anxious for him to get home safely.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The community is anxious for resolution of the ongoing dispute.
   - Stakeholders remain anxious for clarity regarding the new regulations.

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:
