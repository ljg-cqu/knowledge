1. **Verb**: wait for
   **Meaning**: 
   - To stay in a place or delay action until someone arrives or something happens. (transitive)
   - To remain patient or inactive in anticipation of a person, event, or desired outcome.
   
   **Synonyms**: await, anticipate, expect, stand by, hold on for
   
   **Antonyms**: leave, proceed without, give up on
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing remaining in place or delaying action until someone/something arrives; in contexts of anticipation; when expressing patience or expectation; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "await" would be more formal and elegant; avoid when you mean "expect" without the delay component; don't confuse with "wait on" (serve someone).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wait for someone/something to arrive
   - wait for ages/hours/years
   - wait for the right moment
   - can't wait for (expressing eagerness)
   - worth waiting for
   
   **Root Analysis**: wait (Old English "wætan" - to watch) + for (preposition indicating target)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wætan" → Middle English "waiten" → combined with "for" to indicate object of waiting
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been waiting for you for like an hour!
   - Can't wait for summer vacation to start.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has been waiting for your response to proceed with the evaluation.
   - Investors are waiting for clear signals from the central bank.

1. **Verb**: ask for
   **Meaning**: 
   - To request or demand something from someone. (transitive)
   - To seek or try to obtain something through verbal request or formal application.
   
   **Synonyms**: request, seek, demand, solicit, apply for, call for
   
   **Antonyms**: give, offer, provide, decline
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making requests; in contexts of seeking assistance, information, or objects; when describing applications or petitions; appropriate from casual to formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when you mean "inquire about" (use "ask about" instead); avoid when "demand" or "request" would be more precise; don't confuse with "ask after" (inquire about someone's well-being).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ask for help/assistance
   - ask for permission
   - ask for trouble (idiom: invite problems)
   - ask for a favor
   - ask for too much
   
   **Root Analysis**: ask (Old English "ascian" - to request) + for (indicating desired object)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "ascian" → combines naturally with "for" to indicate object of request
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't be afraid to ask for help when you need it.
   - He asked for extra cheese on his burger.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization is asking for increased funding to support its programs.
   - Applicants should ask for clarification if any requirements are unclear.

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Verb**: care for
   **Meaning**: 
   - To look after or provide care for someone or something; to feel affection or concern for. (transitive)
   - To like or want something (usually in negative or interrogative).
   
   **Synonyms**: look after, take care of, tend to, nurture, love, have affection for
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, abandon, ignore, dislike
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing caregiving activities; when expressing affection or concern; in questions about preferences (Would you care for...?); appropriate in all registers with varying formality.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "care about" (be concerned about); in modern English, "care for" meaning "like/want" is formal or old-fashioned; avoid when simpler "take care of" suffices.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - care for someone (look after)
   - care deeply for someone (love)
   - would you care for (polite offer)
   - who will care for (future caregiving)
   - not care for (dislike)
   
   **Root Analysis**: care (Old English "caru" - sorrow, anxiety, concern) + for (indicating object of concern)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "caru" → naturally pairs with "for" to indicate recipient of care
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's caring for her elderly mother at home.
   - I don't really care for spicy food.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility provides specialized services for caring for patients with dementia.
   - Would you care for some refreshments? (polite offer)

   **Etymology**: Old English "standan" → metaphorical extension to represent or advocate
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I won't stand for that kind of behavior in my house!

   **Examples [Formal]**:

   - sincerely apologize for
   - refuse to apologize for
   - apologize profusely for

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to apologize for being such a jerk yesterday.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - compete for resources
   - compete for attention
   - compete for first place
   - compete for the championship
   - companies competing for market share
   
   **Root Analysis**: compete (Latin "competere" - strive together) + for (indicating prize or goal)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "com" (together) + "petere" (seek) → "compete for" specifies goal
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Five teams are competing for the trophy.
   - We're all competing for the same job—may the best person win!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Multiple firms are competing for the government contract.
   - Athletes from 30 countries will compete for medals in the championship.
