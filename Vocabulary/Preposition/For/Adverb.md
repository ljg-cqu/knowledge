1. **Adverb**: just (type: degree/emphasis)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Exactly; precisely; only or simply; used to emphasize the limited nature of something or to soften requests. Often used with "for" to emphasize purpose or reason.
   - *(Sense 2)* Very recently; a moment ago (in perfect tenses)
   
   **Synonyms**: only, merely, simply, exactly, precisely
   
   **Antonyms**: completely, entirely, fully
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize exactness ("just for this"), to minimize or soften ("just for a minute"), to express limitation ("just for you"), or with perfect tenses to indicate recent completion; appropriate in all registers with varying levels of formality.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse as a filler word; avoid in formal writing where precision is key; be careful with "just" + "for" combinations that might sound dismissive.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - just for now
   - just for fun
   - just for you
   - just for the record
   - just for clarification
   
   **Root Analysis**: just (Latin "justus" - lawful, right, proper)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "justus" → Old French "juste" (exact, precise) → English "just" (exactly, only)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm doing this just for you, you know.
   - Can we talk just for a minute?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This policy applies just for the current fiscal year.
   - The exemption is granted just for specific circumstances.

1. **Adverb**: specifically (type: manner/focus)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is exact and clear; with particularity or precision; for a specific purpose or reason. Often used with "for" to emphasize targeted purpose.
   
   **Synonyms**: particularly, especially, precisely, exactly, explicitly
   
   **Antonyms**: generally, broadly, vaguely, roughly
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing precision or particularity; when highlighting specific purposes or targets; in formal or technical contexts requiring clarity; with "for" to specify exact purpose; appropriate in formal and semi-formal registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when general statements are more appropriate; don't overuse (can sound pedantic); avoid in casual conversation where simpler language suffices.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - specifically for [purpose]
   - designed specifically for
   - created specifically for
   - specifically tailored for
   - developed specifically for
   
   **Root Analysis**: specific (Latin "species" - particular kind) + -ally (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "species" → "specificus" (particular) → "specifically"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I bought this specifically for our camping trip.
   - This tool is made specifically for left-handed people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The program was developed specifically for underserved communities.
   - These protocols were established specifically for emergency situations.

   **Synonyms**: particularly, specially, notably, above all, specifically
   
   **Antonyms**: generally, ordinarily, typically
   
   **When to Use**: Use when singling out something as particularly noteworthy; when emphasizing exceptional degree; to highlight special relevance; with "for" to specify particular beneficiary; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "specially" (for a special purpose) vs. "especially" (to a great degree); avoid overuse which diminishes emphasis.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - especially for you
   - especially for this occasion
   - especially important for
   - especially true for
   - especially designed for
   
   **Root Analysis**: special (Latin "specialis" - particular) + -ly (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "species" → "specialis" (particular) → "especially"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I made this especially for you!
   - This restaurant is great, especially for seafood.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This consideration is especially important for vulnerable populations.
   - The findings are especially relevant for policy makers.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Adverb**: desperately (type: manner/intensity)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a desperate manner; with urgent need or intense desire; extremely or very much. Used with verbs that take "for" to emphasize urgent need or intense desire.
   
   **Synonyms**: urgently, frantically, intensely, extremely, critically
   
   **Antonyms**: calmly, casually, indifferently, apathetically
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing urgent need or intense desire; when describing frantic searching or hoping; with verbs like "search for," "hope for," "look for"; appropriate in all registers but conveys strong emotion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when calm or moderate intensity is more accurate; don't overuse (can sound melodramatic); be careful in professional contexts where it might seem unprofessional.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - desperately need for
   - desperately searching for
   - desperately hoping for
   - desperately looking for
   - desperately waiting for
   
   **Root Analysis**: desperate (Latin "desperatus" - without hope) + -ly (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "de" (without) + "sperare" (hope) → "desperatus" → "desperately"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm desperately looking for my keys!
   - We're desperately hoping for good weather.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization is desperately searching for sustainable funding sources.
   - Communities are desperately waiting for disaster relief assistance.
