1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Intended to reach or go to a destination. Used to indicate direction or destination of movement.
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the duration of a specified period. Indicates how long something lasts or continues.
   - *(Abstract)* In support of, on behalf of, or in favor of someone or something. Also used to indicate purpose, reason, recipient, exchange, or suitability.
   
   **Synonyms**: in favor of (in context of support), during (in context of duration), intended for (in context of purpose), on behalf of (in context of representation)
   
   **Antonyms**: against (opposition), from (source/origin)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "for" to express purpose (training for a marathon), duration (lived there for five years), benefit/recipient (a gift for you), reason (famous for his paintings), destination (leaving for Paris), exchange (bought it for $50), support (voting for the candidate), or suitability (right tool for the job).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "for" with "during" (use one or the other, not both), don't use "for" with specific points in time (use "at" or "on"), avoid "for" when expressing possession (use "of"), don't confuse with "since" (use "for" for duration, "since" for starting point).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for a while, for now, for sale, for rent, for free, for sure, for instance, for example
   - **Verb + for**: wait for, look for, search for, ask for, pay for, work for, fight for, prepare for, apply for, thank someone for
   - **Adjective + for**: good for, bad for, ready for, responsible for, famous for, known for, suitable for, grateful for, sorry for
   - **Noun + for**: need for, reason for, demand for, respect for, love for, time for, room for, chance for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for-" (before, in front of) + Germanic roots
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → from Proto-Germanic *fura (before, in front of) → related to Greek "pro" and Latin "per"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been waiting for you for like twenty minutes!
   - Can you grab some milk for me when you're at the store?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has been deliberating for several hours to reach a consensus.
   - This research was conducted for the purpose of understanding climate change impacts.

1. **Preposition**: for (purpose/benefit usage) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Intended to benefit or be used by a particular person or purpose. Indicates the intended recipient, beneficiary, or purpose of an action or object.
   
   **Synonyms**: intended for, meant for, designed for, in order to serve
   
   **Antonyms**: against (in opposition to)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when indicating who will receive or benefit from something (this book is for students), what purpose something serves (a tool for cutting), or the intended use of an object or action.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when the relationship is possession rather than purpose (use "of" instead), avoid in contexts where "to" indicates direction of action more clearly.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for your benefit, for this purpose, for the sake of, for your information
   - **Verb + for**: do something for someone, make for, buy for, cook for, write for
   - **Adjective + for**: perfect for, ideal for, appropriate for, designed for
   - **Noun + for**: gift for, message for, solution for, recipe for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for-" (in favor of, on account of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → evolved from indicating spatial "before" to abstract "on behalf of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I made this cake for your birthday.
   - These shoes are way too big for me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The scholarship was established for students demonstrating exceptional academic merit.
   - These guidelines are for the benefit of all stakeholders involved.

1. **Preposition**: for (duration) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the continuance or duration of a specified period of time. Indicates how long an action or state continues.
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, during (with some distinction), over the course of, spanning
   
   **Antonyms**: since (starting point rather than duration)
   
   **When to Use**: Use with periods of time to show duration (for three hours, for several years, for a moment), in perfect tenses to indicate how long something has lasted, with future time expressions to show planned duration.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use with specific time points (use "at," "on," "in" instead), don't confuse with "since" which indicates starting point, avoid with "during" in the same phrase, don't use when referring to a point in the future without duration.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for a long time, for ages, for hours, for years, for a while, for the time being, for the moment
   - **Verb + for**: last for, continue for, persist for, remain for, stay for
   - **With time expressions**: for two weeks, for six months, for several days, for decades
   - **Perfect tenses**: have lived for, has worked for, had studied for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (duration sense developed from spatial "through")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → semantic extension from spatial "through" to temporal "throughout"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've lived in this city for ten years now.
   - Let's just chill here for a bit before heading out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research project has been ongoing for approximately eighteen months.
   - The policy will remain in effect for an indefinite period pending further review.

1. **Preposition**: for (exchange/price) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In exchange for; at the price or cost of. Indicates what is given in return for something else, including monetary value.
   
   **Synonyms**: in exchange for, at the price of, costing, at the cost of
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (no direct opposite in exchange context)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when indicating the price of something (bought for $100), what was traded or exchanged (sold his car for a motorcycle), or what was given up to obtain something (sacrificed his career for family).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when indicating possession (use "of"), avoid when describing method of payment without exchange (use "by" or "with"), don't confuse with "at" for simple price statements without transaction.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for sale, for nothing, for free, for a price, for a song
   - **Verb + for**: buy for, sell for, pay for, exchange for, trade for, swap for
   - **With monetary amounts**: for $50, for ten dollars, for a hundred euros
   - **Non-monetary exchange**: for a favor, for love, for friendship
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (exchange sense from "in return for")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → meaning extended from "before" to "in place of" to "in exchange for"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I got this awesome jacket for just twenty bucks!
   - Would you trade your sandwich for my chips?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The property was acquired for three million dollars.
   - The company exchanged its legacy systems for modern cloud-based infrastructure.

1. **Preposition**: for (reason/cause) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Because of; on account of; as a result of. Indicates the reason, cause, or grounds for something.
   
   **Synonyms**: because of, due to, on account of, owing to, by reason of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of (contrast rather than cause)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when explaining why something happened (punished for lying), what quality or characteristic something is known by (famous for his kindness), or the grounds for an action or state (thankful for your help).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "because" (which introduces clauses, not noun phrases), avoid overuse when "due to" or "because of" might be clearer, don't use in formal contexts where "owing to" might be more appropriate.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for this reason, for that matter, for the record, for all I know
   - **Verb + for**: thank for, apologize for, blame for, punish for, reward for, praise for, criticize for
   - **Adjective + for**: famous for, known for, responsible for, guilty for, sorry for, thankful for, grateful for
   - **Noun + for**: reason for, excuse for, explanation for, justification for, basis for, grounds for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (causal sense from "in return for")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → extended from exchange meaning to causal meaning (what gives rise to)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She got grounded for coming home late.
   - Thanks so much for helping me move!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The professor is renowned for her groundbreaking research in neuroscience.
   - The defendant was convicted for committing fraud.

1. **Preposition**: for (support/favor) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In support of, in favor of, or in agreement with someone or something. Indicates alignment, advocacy, or endorsement.
   
   **Synonyms**: in favor of, in support of, pro (prefix), on the side of
   
   **Antonyms**: against, opposed to, anti (prefix)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing support (vote for a candidate), advocacy (fight for justice), or alignment with a position (argue for change), or when choosing between options (for or against).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when expressing opposition (use "against"), avoid when neutrality is intended, don't confuse with "to" when indicating direction of support rather than advocacy.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for or against, all for it, for the cause, for the team
   - **Verb + for**: vote for, fight for, stand for, argue for, campaign for, root for, cheer for, advocate for
   - **Adjective + for**: supportive for (though "of" is more common)
   - **Be + for**: I'm for it, who's for pizza?, are you for or against?
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (support sense from "in place of, on behalf of")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → meaning extended from "before" to "in place of" to "in favor of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally for this idea—let's do it!
   - Who are you rooting for in the game?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The senator has consistently voted for environmental protection legislation.
   - The organization advocates for human rights worldwide.

1. **Preposition**: for (destination) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the direction of; toward; intending to reach or arrive at a place. Indicates the destination or target of movement.
   
   **Synonyms**: toward, bound for, headed for, destined for
   
   **Antonyms**: from (origin/source)
   
   **When to Use**: Use with verbs of motion to indicate destination (leave for Paris, head for home), with transportation to show intended destination (train for London), or to express intended arrival point.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "to" (more common for simple destination with most verbs), avoid when "toward" expresses direction without definite arrival, don't use when indicating current location (use "in," "at," or "on").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: bound for, headed for, destined for, for home, for the hills
   - **Verb + for**: leave for, depart for, set out for, head for, make for
   - **Transportation**: train for, flight for, bus for, ship for
   - **Idiomatic**: run for the hills, make for the exit, head for trouble
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (direction sense from "before, in front of")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → from spatial "before" retained as directional "toward"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're leaving for the beach early tomorrow morning.
   - The bus for downtown leaves in five minutes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegation will depart for Geneva on Monday.
   - Flight 237 bound for Tokyo is now boarding at gate 15.

1. **Preposition**: for (suitability/appropriateness) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Appropriate to; suitable to; right or fitting for a particular purpose, person, or situation.
   
   **Synonyms**: suitable for, appropriate for, right for, fitting for, proper for
   
   **Antonyms**: unsuitable for, inappropriate for, wrong for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing compatibility (perfect for the job), appropriateness (not for children), fitness for purpose (tool for the task), or matching qualities (right person for the role).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "to" in infinitive constructions, avoid when describing inherent properties rather than suitability, don't use when "of" indicates possession or composition.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for all ages, for beginners, for experts, for this occasion
   - **Verb + for**: suited for, designed for, made for, intended for, meant for, built for
   - **Adjective + for**: perfect for, ideal for, good for, bad for, right for, wrong for, appropriate for, suitable for, fit for
   - **Noun + for**: candidate for, match for, choice for, option for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (suitability from "corresponding to")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → extended from "in exchange for" to "corresponding to" to "suitable for"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This movie isn't really for kids—it's pretty scary.
   - Those sneakers are perfect for running.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate possesses all the qualifications requisite for the position.
   - This software solution is specifically designed for enterprise-level applications.

1. **Preposition**: for (representation/behalf) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* On behalf of; as a representative of; in place of; acting in the interest of someone or something.
   
   **Synonyms**: on behalf of, representing, in place of, as a representative of, for the sake of
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (no direct opposite for representation)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when someone acts as a representative (speaking for the group), substitutes for another (signing for my boss), or acts in someone's interest (doing this for you, not me).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "of" when indicating possession or belonging, avoid when simple agency is meant (use "by"), don't use when direct action rather than representation is meant.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for the company, for the people, for someone else
   - **Verb + for**: speak for, act for, work for, represent for (rare), sign for, stand in for
   - **Noun + for**: representative for, spokesperson for, agent for, attorney for, proxy for
   - **Professional contexts**: counsel for the defense, attorney for the plaintiff
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (representative sense from "in place of, before")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → meaning "before" extended to "in place of" to "on behalf of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm calling for my friend—she can't make it today.
   - Can you sign for this package? I need to run.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The attorney is speaking for the defendant in this matter.
   - As the designated representative for the organization, I hereby submit this proposal.

1. **Preposition**: for (concerning/regarding) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* With regard to; concerning; as far as something is concerned; in respect to.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, as for, with regard to, in respect to, when it comes to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (no direct opposite for topical reference)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to introduce a topic or subject of discussion (as for the budget), to indicate what something pertains to (rules for conduct), or to show scope of relevance (good news for investors).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse when simpler constructions exist, avoid in highly formal writing where "regarding" or "concerning" might be preferred, don't confuse with "about" when general topic is meant.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as for, for one thing, for example, for instance, for all I care, for my part
   - **Introductory**: As for me, For my part, For our purposes
   - **Noun + for**: plans for, rules for, implications for, consequences for
   - **Idiomatic**: for all intents and purposes, for what it's worth, for all I know
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (concerning sense from extension of other meanings)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → semantic broadening to include topical reference
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As for dinner, I'm thinking pizza or Chinese.
   - For me, the best part was the ending.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As for the financial projections, we anticipate moderate growth.
   - For the purposes of this discussion, we will focus on domestic markets.
