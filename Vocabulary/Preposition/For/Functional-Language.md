1. **Functional Expression**: Thank you for [noun/gerund] (function: expressing gratitude)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Express appreciation for something someone has done or provided. Acknowledges benefit received from someone's action.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal (can range depending on context)
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Thanks for, I appreciate, I'm grateful for, Many thanks for
   
   **Antonyms**: No thanks for (rejecting/refusing acknowledgment)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing appreciation for actions, gifts, help, or qualities; in professional emails and correspondence; after receiving assistance or favors; appropriate in most contexts from casual to formal.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use sarcastically unless relationship permits humor; avoid when apology rather than thanks is appropriate; don't use for actions you disapproved of (unless ironic).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Thanks so much for [doing this]
   - Thank you very much for [your help]
   - I'd like to thank you for [your support]
   - Many thanks for [your time]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Gratitude expression using positive politeness (acknowledging debt). "For" introduces the reason for thanks. Gerund form is standard after "for" (thank you for coming, not *thank you for come). Degree of formality adjusted through intensifiers (so much, very much) or choice of "thanks" vs "thank you."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're welcome / My pleasure / No problem / Anytime
   - Don't mention it / Happy to help / Glad I could help
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for picking me up—I really appreciate it!
   - Thank you so much for the birthday present!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We would like to thank you for your participation in this study.
   - Thank you for bringing this matter to our attention.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm sorry for [noun/gerund] (function: apologizing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Express regret or apology for one's actions, mistakes, or situation. Acknowledges fault or expresses sympathy.
   - *Formality*: neutral (can be formal or informal depending on context)
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I apologize for, My apologies for, Please forgive me for, I regret
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm not sorry for, No apologies for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when taking responsibility for mistakes or harm caused; when expressing sympathy for someone's situation; in professional and personal contexts; when acknowledging inconvenience caused to others.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't over-apologize (can diminish credibility); avoid when you're not actually at fault; don't use sarcastically unless relationship permits; avoid when stronger apology is warranted.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm so sorry for [the inconvenience]
   - I apologize for [any confusion]
   - Sorry about that / Sorry for that
   - I'm terribly sorry for [the delay]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Apology using negative politeness (acknowledging imposition or fault). "For" introduces the offense or situation. Choice between "sorry" and "apologize" affects formality (apologize is more formal). Can express both regret for one's own actions and sympathy for others' situations.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's okay / No worries / Don't worry about it / It's fine
   - Apology accepted / I understand / These things happen
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm sorry for being late—traffic was crazy.
   - Sorry for the mess; I haven't had time to clean.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We sincerely apologize for any inconvenience this may have caused.
   - I am sorry for the oversight and will ensure it does not recur.

1. **Functional Expression**: What's [this/that] for? (function: asking about purpose)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request information about the purpose, function, or intended use of something. Seeks explanation of why something exists or was done.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: What is the purpose of this?, What's the use of that?, Why do we have this?, What do you use that for?
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (interrogative function)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when genuinely confused about purpose or function; in instructional contexts when learning about tools or processes; when questioning the reason behind decisions or actions; appropriate in casual to semi-formal settings.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the purpose is obvious (can sound obtuse); don't use in highly formal settings (rephrase more formally); be careful of tone—can sound challenging if not genuinely curious.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What's this thing for?
   - What do you need that for?
   - What are these for?
   - What's it for?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Information-seeking question using "what" + "for" to inquire about purpose. Can be genuine inquiry or rhetorical challenge depending on intonation and context. "For" introduces purpose/reason. Informal structure with contracted "what's."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - It's for [purpose]
   - You use it for [function]
   - That's to [infinitive purpose]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's this weird tool for? I found it in the garage.
   - What do you need all these boxes for?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you clarify the purpose of this requirement? (formal alternative)
   - For what purpose was this mechanism designed? (formal alternative)

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm looking for [something/someone] (function: requesting assistance/stating need)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Indicate that you are searching for or need something or someone. Can be request for help in finding or statement of search activity.
   - *Formality*: neutral (works in most contexts)
   - *Directness*: indirect (softer than direct request)
   
   **Synonyms**: I need, I'm trying to find, I'm searching for, Do you have, Can you help me find
   
   **Antonyms**: I found, I'm not interested in, I'm avoiding
   
   **When to Use**: Use in retail/service contexts when seeking products or assistance; when entering someone's space searching for a person; when networking or job searching; less direct and more polite than "I need" in requests.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use if you're not actually searching (use "I need" instead); avoid if more direct request would be clearer; don't use when "do you have" would be simpler.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm looking for someone who can [help with X]
   - Are you looking for something? (offer of assistance)
   - What are you looking for?
   - I was looking for [person's name]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Search/need statement that can function as indirect request for assistance. More polite than direct "I want" or "Give me." Common in service encounters. Progressive form ("am looking") emphasizes ongoing search. Often prompts offer of help from staff or others.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Over there / Aisle 5 / Right this way
   - Can I help you find something?
   - What exactly are you looking for?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hey, I'm looking for the bathroom—can you point me in the right direction?
   - I'm looking for a birthday gift for my mom.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I am looking for opportunities to expand my professional network.
   - We are currently looking for candidates with expertise in data analytics.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm here for [purpose/person] (function: stating reason for presence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Explain why you have come to a location or what appointment/meeting you have. States purpose of visit.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I have an appointment for, I'm here to see, I came for, My purpose here is
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm leaving, I'm not here for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when checking in at reception or entrance; when explaining your presence to someone; at appointments or meetings; in professional and service contexts; when collecting items or people.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when you need to provide more detailed explanation; avoid when more formal announcement is expected; don't use sarcastically unless appropriate.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm here for my appointment
   - I'm here to pick up [person/item]
   - I'm just here for [specific purpose]
   - I'm here for a meeting with [person]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Statement of purpose using "for" to introduce reason. Common at receptions, check-ins, and arrival contexts. Helps others understand your presence and direct you appropriately. Can be professional or casual depending on setting.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Please have a seat / One moment please
   - May I see your ID? / Sign in here, please
   - [Name] will be right with you
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hi, I'm here for Sarah—is she around?

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Good morning, I'm here for my 10:00 appointment with Dr. Anderson.

1. **Functional Expression**: Can/Could I ask you for [something]? (function: requesting politely)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Make a polite request for something from someone. Uses modal verb to soften directness of request.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite request)
   
   **Synonyms**: Could you give me, Would you mind giving me, May I have, I'd like to request
   
   **Antonyms**: Give me (direct/impolite demand)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making requests where imposition is acknowledged; in professional contexts; when requesting favors or assistance; with strangers or in formal situations; "could" is more polite than "can."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use in emergencies (be direct); avoid overuse (can sound tentative); don't use when you have authority to direct; avoid when simpler request would suffice in casual contexts.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could I ask you for a favor?
   - Can I ask for your help with something?
   - May I ask you for some advice?
   - I wanted to ask you for [something]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Polite request using modal interrogative + "ask for" to minimize imposition. Two-part politeness: (1) asking permission to ask, (2) using modal "can/could." "Could" is more formal/polite than "can." "For" introduces requested item. Common negative politeness strategy.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Of course / Sure / Certainly / Go ahead
   - I'm not sure I can, but... / What do you need?
   - Sorry, but... (refusal)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hey, can I ask you for a huge favor?
   - Could I ask you for a ride to the airport?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could I ask you for clarification on this policy?
   - May I ask you for a few minutes of your time?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd like to thank you for [noun/gerund] (function: formal gratitude)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Express appreciation in formal contexts. More ceremonial or official than simple "thank you."
   - *Formality*: formal to very formal
   - *Directness*: direct but ceremonial
   
   **Synonyms**: I wish to express my gratitude for, I want to thank you for, Allow me to thank you for
   
   **Antonyms**: I wish to complain about, I must criticize you for
   
   **When to Use**: Use in speeches, formal letters, ceremonies, or public acknowledgments; in business contexts for significant contributions; when formality and gravity are appropriate; at conclusions of projects or events.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (overly formal); don't use in routine email responses; avoid when simple "thanks" is more natural; don't use sarcastically.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I would like to take this opportunity to thank you for
   - I want to personally thank you for
   - We would like to thank you for
   - Allow me to thank you for
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Formal gratitude expression using conditional "would like" to add ceremony and formality. Common in public speaking, formal letters, and ceremonial contexts. Often precedes detailed explanation of contribution. "For" introduces reason for gratitude.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - It's my pleasure / The pleasure is mine
   - I'm honored / Thank you
   - I was happy to contribute
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *Typically too formal for casual contexts*
   - I'd like to thank you guys for an awesome evening. (somewhat formal even in casual setting)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would like to thank you for your years of dedicated service to this organization.
   - Allow me to take this opportunity to thank you for your invaluable contributions to this project.

1. **Functional Expression**: What do you need [it/that] for? (function: questioning necessity/purpose)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Question why something is needed or what purpose it will serve. Can be genuine inquiry or challenge to necessity.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (can sound challenging)
   
   **Synonyms**: Why do you need that?, What's your purpose?, For what reason do you require this?
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (interrogative function)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when genuinely curious about someone's needs; in contexts where justification is appropriate (budgets, permissions); when questioning decisions in appropriate relationships; in problem-solving to understand requirements.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when tone might sound judgmental or intrusive; don't use with superiors without proper context; be careful as it can sound confrontational; avoid when you should simply provide what's requested.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Why do you need it?
   - What are you going to use that for?
   - Do you really need that for [purpose]?
   - What would you need that for?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Information-seeking or challenging question. Tone is crucial—can be neutral inquiry or challenge to necessity. Rising intonation = curiosity; falling intonation = skepticism. "For" introduces questioned purpose. Can be face-threatening if perceived as challenging someone's judgment.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I need it for [purpose]
   - To [infinitive purpose]
   - Why do you ask? / Is there a problem?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What do you need all those batteries for?
   - Why do you need my password for?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you clarify the business rationale for this request?
   - What is the intended application for this equipment?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm responsible for [task/area] (function: stating responsibility/role)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Indicate your area of responsibility, duty, or accountability. Establishes what you manage or are accountable for.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm in charge of, I handle, I manage, I oversee, My job is to
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm not responsible for, That's not my responsibility
   
   **When to Use**: Use in professional introductions; when clarifying roles and responsibilities; in job interviews; when establishing accountability; in team contexts to define boundaries.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when blame is being assigned (unless accepting responsibility); avoid when deflecting might be more appropriate; don't use boastfully about responsibilities that aren't impressive.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm responsible for managing [area]
   - I've been responsible for [task] for [time period]
   - Who's responsible for [area]?
   - The person responsible for [task]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Role/responsibility statement using "responsible for" to establish accountability or scope of duty. Common in professional contexts for role clarification. "For" introduces area of responsibility. Can be positive (claiming achievement) or negative (accepting blame) depending on context.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I see / Good to know
   - So you're the one to talk to about [topic]
   - How long have you been doing that?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm responsible for feeding the cat while they're away.
   - Who's responsible for bringing the drinks?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I am responsible for overseeing the company's digital marketing strategy.
   - Our department is responsible for ensuring regulatory compliance.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm here for you (function: offering support)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offer emotional support, assistance, or solidarity to someone. Express willingness to help or listen.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but emotional
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm here to support you, You can count on me, I've got your back, I'm always here if you need me
   
   **Antonyms**: You're on your own, I can't help you, Don't count on me
   
   **When to Use**: Use when offering emotional support during difficult times; with friends, family, or close colleagues; when someone expresses need or distress; in empathetic contexts; to reinforce relationship.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in professional contexts where it might seem inappropriate or boundary-crossing; don't use if you can't actually provide support; avoid with people you don't know well; don't use as empty platitude.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm always here for you
   - We're here for you
   - I'll be here for you
   - Remember I'm here for you
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Support offer using "here" (availability) + "for you" (beneficiary). Expresses solidarity and willingness to help. Strengthens positive face through explicit support. Common in close relationships. Should be genuine offer, not empty phrase. Can precede or follow more specific offers of help.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you, that means a lot
   - I appreciate that / I know
   - Thanks, I may take you up on that
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I know things are tough right now, but I'm here for you.
   - You know I'm always here for you, right?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Our support services are available to assist you with this transition. (formal alternative)
   - The team is here to support you through this process. (professional context)

1. **Functional Expression**: Just to be clear / For the sake of clarity (function: clarifying/preventing misunderstanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Signal that you are about to state something explicitly to prevent or correct misunderstanding. Prefaces clarification.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To clarify, Let me be clear, To be clear, Just so we're clear, To avoid confusion
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (clarification marker has no opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: Use before important clarifications; when misunderstanding is likely or has occurred; in negotiations or discussions where precision matters; to emphasize key points; in written and spoken communication.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse (can sound condescending); don't use when no clarification is needed; avoid when it might imply the other person is confused (unless that's the situation); don't use defensively.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just so we're clear
   - Let me be clear about this
   - To clarify this point
   - For clarity's sake
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Clarification marker signaling explicit statement to prevent misunderstanding. Can be polite prevention or correction of misunderstanding, or can be face-threatening if it implies misunderstanding has occurred. Tone matters significantly. "For" introduces purpose (clarity).
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay / I understand / Yes, go ahead
   - Thank you for clarifying
   - I didn't mean to suggest... (if misunderstanding occurred)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just to be clear, you're picking me up at 7, right?
   - For the sake of clarity—I said Thursday, not Tuesday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - For the sake of clarity, let me restate the proposal's key terms.
   - Just to be clear, this policy applies to all employees without exception.
