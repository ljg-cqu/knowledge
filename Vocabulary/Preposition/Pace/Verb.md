1. **Verb**: pace
   **Meaning**: 
   - To walk with regular steps, often back and forth, especially when nervous or thinking.
   - To set or control the speed at which something happens (for example, to pace a run or a meeting).
   
   **Synonyms**: stride, walk back and forth, regulate, time, moderate
   
   **Antonyms**: rush, dawdle, sprint (for uncontrolled extremes)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about physically walking up and down, or about controlling the pace of work, learning, or exercise (for example, pace yourself, pace the race, pace the discussion).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you only mean walk casually (use walk) or run hard (use sprint); also avoid confusing the verb with the preposition pace ("with all due respect to").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pace up and down
   - pace the floor
   - pace yourself
   - pace the meeting
   - carefully paced lesson
   
   **Root Analysis**: from noun pace (step, rate) used as a verb meaning "walk in measured steps" then "control the rate".
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin passus (step) → Old French pas → English pace; verb sense "walk" and "set the speed" developed in Middle and Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He kept pacing the room while he waited for the call.
   - If you do not pace yourself, you will burn out halfway through the project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The workshop was carefully paced to balance depth with participants' attention span.
   - Runners were instructed to pace the first half of the race conservatively.

1. **Verb**: accelerate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move or happen more quickly; to increase the pace or speed of something.
   
   **Synonyms**: speed up, quicken, hasten, intensify
   
   **Antonyms**: decelerate, slow down, ease off
   
   **When to Use**: Describing faster movement, growth, progress, or change (for example, accelerate the pace of reform, accelerate learning).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very informal speech where speed up is more natural; also avoid when you mean begin rather than increase speed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accelerate the pace
   - accelerate growth
   - accelerate progress
   - accelerate learning
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + celer (swift) + -ate (verb ending)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin accelerare (to hasten, quicken) → English accelerate.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The conversation accelerated once everyone relaxed.
   - We had to accelerate our pace to catch the last train.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization decided to accelerate the pace of digital transformation.
   - Investment in training can accelerate employees' learning curves.

1. **Verb**: decelerate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To reduce speed; to slow the pace of movement or change.
   
   **Synonyms**: slow down, ease off, reduce speed, slacken
   
   **Antonyms**: accelerate, speed up, quicken
   
   **When to Use**: Technical or formal contexts where a controlled reduction of pace is important (for example, decelerate safely before a turn, decelerate the project schedule).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where slow down is more common; also avoid when movement stops completely (then use stop or halt).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - decelerate gradually
   - decelerate the pace
   - decelerate before impact
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down, away) + celer (swift) + -ate
   
   **Etymology**: Modelled on accelerate, from Latin celer (swift), used especially in technical and scientific English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The driver decelerated as we approached the traffic jam.
   - We need to decelerate the pace a bit or people will burn out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Economic growth began to decelerate after several years of rapid expansion.
   - The training plan requires athletes to decelerate gradually to avoid injury.

1. **Verb**: maintain
   **Meaning**: 
   - To keep something at the same level, rate, or pace.
   
   **Synonyms**: keep, preserve, sustain, uphold
   
   **Antonyms**: reduce, neglect, abandon, disrupt
   
   **When to Use**: Describing efforts to keep a certain speed or workload (for example, maintain a steady pace, maintain current output).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you are starting something (use begin) or increasing pace (use increase or accelerate).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - maintain a steady pace
   - maintain the current pace
   - maintain momentum
   - maintain performance levels
   
   **Root Analysis**: main (hand) + -tain (hold) from Latin manus + tenere (hold in the hand)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French maintenir (to hold in the hand, keep) → English maintain, now widely used for keeping pace or level.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Try to maintain the same pace for the whole run.
   - It is hard to maintain this work pace for weeks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The team successfully maintained a sustainable pace throughout the release cycle.
   - Policies are needed to maintain the current pace of innovation without overloading staff.

1. **Verb**: adjust
   **Meaning**: 
   - To change something slightly to make it more suitable, effective, or comfortable; often used for modifying pace.
   
   **Synonyms**: modify, alter, fine-tune, calibrate, adapt
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, preserve, maintain (when no change is made)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about small, deliberate changes to speed or workload (for example, adjust the pace, adjust your running speed, adjust the schedule).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for sudden, dramatic changes; in that case use shift or overhaul.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - adjust the pace
   - adjust your speed
   - adjust the schedule
   - adjust gradually
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + just (fair, proper) → adjust (make proper)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French ajuster (to make right) → English adjust.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We can always adjust the pace if it feels too fast.
   - I had to adjust my running pace after the first kilometre.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Managers should adjust the pace of change in response to employee feedback.
   - The curriculum can be adjusted so that students progress at a realistic pace.

1. **Verb**: sprint
   **Meaning**: 
   - To run or move at full speed for a short distance or period.
   
   **Synonyms**: dash, race, bolt, run flat out
   
   **Antonyms**: jog, stroll, wander, walk
   
   **When to Use**: Physical movement at maximum pace, or metaphorically for short bursts of intense effort (for example, sprint to the finish, end-of-quarter sprint).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for long-term effort or sustainable pace; sprinting is by definition short and intense.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sprint to the finish
   - final sprint
   - sprint phase
   - sprint through a task
   
   **Root Analysis**: Probably related to Old Norse spretta (to jump, burst forth).
   
   **Etymology**: Entered English in the 19th century in sports contexts for short, fast races, then broadened to any short intense effort.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We sprinted the last hundred metres at an all-out pace.
   - I can sprint for a bit, but I cannot keep that pace for long.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Agile teams often work in short sprints rather than maintaining a constant high pace.
   - Participants were instructed not to sprint during the warm-up phase to avoid injuries.
