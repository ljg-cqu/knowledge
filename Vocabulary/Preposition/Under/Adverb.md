1. **Adverb**: underneath (type: place/position)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In or to a position directly below or beneath something; below the surface; at a lower level
   - *(Sense 2)* Hidden below an outer appearance or surface; fundamentally or essentially
   
   **Synonyms**: beneath, below, under, at the bottom
   
   **Antonyms**: above, on top, over, overhead
   
   **When to Use**: Use when indicating position below something, or when revealing hidden qualities beneath a surface
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for abstract hierarchical relationships (use "under" for "report underneath someone"—say "report to someone" or "work under someone")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look underneath
   - hidden underneath
   - underneath it all
   - what lies underneath
   - scratch the surface and underneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (below) + neath (beneath, from Old English *neothan*)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English, compound of *under* + *neath/nethe* (below). Used as both preposition and adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The keys were underneath, right where you said they'd be.
   - She seems tough, but underneath she's actually really caring.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The artifact was discovered underneath the foundation layer during excavation.
   - Underneath the diplomatic language, substantial disagreements remained evident.

1. **Adverb**: under (type: place/position)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In or to a position that is below or beneath something
   - *(Sense 2)* Into a state of unconsciousness, especially through anesthesia
   - *(Sense 3)* Into a condition of subordination, control, or submersion
   
   **Synonyms**: below, beneath, underneath, down
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, up
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate position below, submersion, loss of consciousness, or falling into subordinate states
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context matters greatly. "Under" as an adverb is less common than as a preposition; ensure you're not using it where a prepositional phrase is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go under (anesthesia, bankruptcy)
   - put someone under
   - keep someone under
   - stay under (water)
   - pull under
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *under* (unchanged)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *under* used as both preposition and adverb. Adverbial uses often indicate motion or state change
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The ship went under in less than five minutes.
   - The anesthesia put me under almost instantly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company went under following prolonged financial difficulties.
   - Patients are carefully monitored when under anesthesia to ensure safety.

1. **Adverb**: understandably (type: sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is reasonable, natural, or to be expected given the circumstances; as one would expect; justifiably
   
   **Synonyms**: naturally, reasonably, predictably, unsurprisingly, justifiably
   
   **Antonyms**: surprisingly, unexpectedly, unreasonably, inexplicably
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate that a reaction, feeling, or outcome is reasonable and expected given the situation. Often used to express empathy or acknowledgment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when you mean "clearly" or "comprehensibly" (that's a different sense of "understand"). Use sentence-initially or mid-sentence with commas
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - understandably upset
   - understandably concerned
   - understandably reluctant
   - understandably enough
   - quite understandably
   
   **Root Analysis**: understandable + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From *understandable* + *-ly*. Modern formation reflecting the "justifiable" sense of "understandable"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She was understandably upset when she found out they'd left without her.
   - He's understandably nervous about his first day at the new job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Stakeholders were, understandably, concerned about the project's financial viability.
   - The committee was understandably reluctant to approve such a significant expenditure without further analysis.

1. **Adverb**: underground (type: place/position)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Beneath the surface of the ground; in or into a subterranean position
   - *(Sense 2)* In or into secrecy or hiding; in a concealed manner outside the mainstream
   
   **Synonyms**: below ground, subterraneously, beneath the surface, secretly, covertly
   
   **Antonyms**: above ground, on the surface, openly, publicly
   
   **When to Use**: Use for literal position beneath ground level, or figuratively for operating in secret or outside mainstream visibility
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse adverb "underground" (go underground) with adjective "underground" (underground movement) or noun "the underground" (resistance group)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go underground
   - work underground
   - buried underground
   - operate underground
   - drive underground
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (beneath) + ground (earth)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *undergrund*. Adverbial use means "to/in a position under the ground." Figurative "into hiding" sense from 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The miners work underground for eight-hour shifts.
   - The band went underground for a few years before their comeback.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The cable network was installed underground to prevent weather-related damage.
   - Following the coup, opposition leaders were forced to operate underground.

1. **Adverb**: underfoot (type: place/position)
   **Meaning**: 
   - On the ground beneath one's feet; in the way so as to hinder or obstruct
   - *(Sense 2)* In a position where one can be trampled or is in danger of being stepped on
   
   **Synonyms**: beneath one's feet, in the way, on the ground
   
   **Antonyms**: overhead, out of the way, above
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing things on the ground that may be stepped on, or things/people that are in the way and causing obstruction
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Underfoot" specifically refers to the ground/floor area. Don't use for obstacles at other heights
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get underfoot
   - things underfoot
   - trampled underfoot
   - slippery underfoot
   - always underfoot
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (beneath) + foot
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English compound. Original meaning "under one's feet" extended to "in the way" by metaphorical extension
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The kids are always underfoot when I'm trying to cook dinner.
   - Watch out—it's really slippery underfoot after the rain.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The terrain was treacherous underfoot, requiring careful navigation.
   - Small children must be supervised to ensure they do not get underfoot during the evacuation.

1. **Adverb**: unduly (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To an excessive or unwarranted degree; more than is reasonable, appropriate, or necessary; excessively
   
   **Synonyms**: excessively, overly, unreasonably, disproportionately, unnecessarily
   
   **Antonyms**: appropriately, reasonably, moderately, adequately
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to indicate that something is excessive, disproportionate, or beyond what is justified or appropriate
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Unduly" is formal; in casual contexts, use "too" or "overly" instead. Don't use for extreme positive situations (use "exceptionally" or "remarkably")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unduly concerned
   - unduly influenced
   - unduly harsh
   - unduly optimistic
   - unduly burdensome
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + duly (appropriately, properly)
   
   **Etymology**: From *un-* (not) + *duly* (in due manner). "Duly" from Middle English *dewely* (properly), itself from *due* + *-ly*
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't think you should be unduly worried about the test—you studied hard.
   - He's not unduly concerned about the deadline; he's confident he'll finish.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The restrictions were deemed unduly burdensome by the regulatory review committee.
   - The court found that the defendant had been unduly influenced in signing the agreement.
