1. **Preposition**: under (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In or to a position below or beneath something; covered by something above
   - *(Temporal)* Less than a particular age, amount, or level
   - *(Abstract)* Subject to the authority, control, or influence of; affected or governed by; experiencing a particular condition or process
   
   **Synonyms**: below (spatial), beneath (formal spatial), underneath (spatial emphasis), less than (quantitative), subject to (control)
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, on top of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "under" for spatial relationships (position below), quantitative comparisons (less than), hierarchical relationships (reporting structure), conditions/circumstances (experiencing), and processes (undergoing)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "under" when referring to surface contact (use "on" instead), or when something is directly touching from above without covering (use "below"). Avoid "under" for abstract superiority concepts where "over" is more natural (e.g., "advantage over" not "advantage under")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: under pressure, under control, under construction, under investigation, under the circumstances, under the impression, under the influence
   - **Verb + prep**: come under, fall under, go under, operate under, work under, live under
   - **Adjective + prep**: None (prepositions don't typically follow adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: position under, supervision under, rule under, conditions under
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "under" (beneath, below) - unchanged single morpheme
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *under* → Middle English *under* → Modern English *under*. From Proto-Germanic *under, related to German *unter*, Dutch *onder*. Indo-European root *n̥dhér meaning "under, between"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you check under the couch? I think my keys fell down there.
   - She's still under 18, so she can't vote yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project is currently under review by the oversight committee.
   - Under Article 5 of the treaty, member states must provide mutual defense.

1. **Preposition**: underneath (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Directly beneath something; in a lower position covered or concealed by something
   - *(Abstract)* Hidden below the surface or outward appearance
   
   **Synonyms**: under (general), beneath (formal), below (position)
   
   **Antonyms**: on top of, above, over
   
   **When to Use**: Use "underneath" when emphasizing the direct spatial relationship of being covered or concealed below something, or when referring to hidden qualities beneath a surface appearance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in temporal, quantitative, or authority contexts where "under" is standard. "Underneath" is less common in abstract uses and may sound awkward in expressions like "underneath pressure" (use "under pressure" instead)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: underneath the surface, underneath it all, underneath the covers
   - **Verb + prep**: hide underneath, look underneath, buried underneath, found underneath
   - **Adjective + prep**: None
   - **Noun + prep**: layer underneath, truth underneath, reality underneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: under + neath (below) - "neath" from Old English *neothan* (below)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English, from *under* + *neathe* (beneath), itself from Old English *neoþan*. The compound emphasizes the "below" meaning more emphatically than "under" alone
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I finally found my phone—it was underneath a pile of papers on my desk.
   - Underneath all that tough attitude, she's actually pretty sensitive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Archaeological excavations revealed a Roman villa underneath the medieval foundations.
   - Underneath the veneer of economic prosperity, significant structural weaknesses remained.

1. **Preposition**: beneath (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In or to a lower position than something; under or underneath
   - *(Abstract)* Not worthy of; below the dignity or level of someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: under (general), underneath (direct below), below (position), unworthy of (dignity sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: above, over, worthy of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "beneath" in formal or literary contexts for spatial relationships, or when expressing that something is beneath someone's dignity, standards, or abilities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Beneath" sounds overly formal in casual conversation for spatial relationships (prefer "under" or "underneath"). Don't use for temporal or quantitative comparisons (use "under" instead)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: beneath the surface, beneath contempt, beneath notice, beneath one's dignity
   - **Verb + prep**: lie beneath, fall beneath, sink beneath, consider beneath
   - **Adjective + prep**: None
   - **Noun + prep**: layers beneath, truth beneath, level beneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "beneothan" (be- + neothan) - "be-" (by) + "neothan" (below)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *beneothan* (by below) → Middle English *binethe* → Modern English *beneath*. Related to "neath" in "underneath"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He thought the job was beneath him, which is why he didn't apply.
   - There's a whole network of tunnels beneath the city.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee considered such petty concerns to be beneath their consideration.
   - Beneath the ocean floor lie vast reserves of untapped mineral resources.

1. **Preposition**: below (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* At a lower level or layer than; further down than
   - *(Quantitative)* Lower in grade, rank, or amount than; less than
   - *(Abstract)* Lower in rank or importance; not good enough for
   
   **Synonyms**: under (general), beneath (formal), lower than (comparative), less than (quantity)
   
   **Antonyms**: above, over, higher than
   
   **When to Use**: Use "below" for vertical position without direct contact, temperature/measurement comparisons, hierarchical rankings, or reference points in texts (as noted below)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "below" when something is directly covered or in contact (use "under"). Avoid "below" for abstract control or conditions (use "under" for "under control" not "below control")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: below average, below par, below zero, below the surface, below deck, as mentioned below
   - **Verb + prep**: fall below, drop below, sink below, rank below
   - **Adjective + prep**: None
   - **Noun + prep**: temperature below, performance below, rank below, line below
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "be-" (by) + "low" - compound emphasizing downward position
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English *bilooghe*, *bilow*, from *be-* (by) + *low*. Became standardized as "below" in Early Modern English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The temperature dropped below freezing last night, so watch out for ice.
   - Check the diagram below for more details on how it works.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Performance metrics fell below acceptable standards during the third quarter.
   - As outlined in the analysis below, several factors contributed to this outcome.
