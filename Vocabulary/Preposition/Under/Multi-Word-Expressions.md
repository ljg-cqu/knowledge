1. **Expression**: under pressure (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Experiencing stress, urgency, or demands that create a sense of strain or force; being subjected to coercion or stress to act in a particular way
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: under stress, under strain, pressured, stressed, coerced
   
   **Antonyms**: at ease, relaxed, unpressured, calm
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing situations involving stress, deadlines, coercion, or demanding circumstances affecting performance or decisions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for literal physical pressure (use "compressed" or "pressurized"). Not appropriate for positive excitement or anticipation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - under great/enormous pressure
   - under time pressure
   - under increasing pressure
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Pressure" metaphorically represents psychological/social force, and "under" positions the subject as experiencing force from above, creating semantic coherence. This collocation is deeply conventionalized in English
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (experiencing) + *pressure* (force, stress)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally under pressure to finish this before Friday—my boss keeps asking about it.
   - She works really well under pressure; she actually seems to thrive on it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Decision-makers operated under considerable pressure to respond to the crisis.
   - The witness testified that she had signed the document under pressure from her employer.

1. **Expression**: under control (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Being managed, regulated, or restrained effectively; not chaotic or out of hand; stable and directed
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: managed, contained, handled, restrained, in hand
   
   **Antonyms**: out of control, uncontrolled, chaotic, unmanageable
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing situations, emotions, events, or systems that are being effectively managed or have been stabilized
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for situations where control hasn't been established yet (use "trying to control" or "attempting to manage")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get under control
   - bring under control
   - keep under control
   - completely under control
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Control" represents dominance/management, and "under" positions the subject in a state of being controlled or managed. This is a strong conventional collocation
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (in state of) + *control* (regulation, command)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't worry, everything's under control—I've got it handled.
   - The fire was finally under control after about three hours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Emergency response teams brought the situation under control within twenty minutes.
   - The outbreak has been brought under control through aggressive containment measures.

1. **Expression**: under the circumstances (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Given the particular conditions or situation; considering what has happened or the current state of affairs; all things considered
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: given the situation, considering the conditions, all things considered, given what happened
   
   **Antonyms**: ideally, in ideal circumstances, normally
   
   **When to Use**: Use when acknowledging that expectations, judgments, or actions must be adjusted based on specific situational constraints or difficulties
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use in positive situations where circumstances are favorable (awkward phrasing). Better for acknowledging challenges or suboptimal conditions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - under these circumstances
   - under such circumstances
   - under any circumstances (different meaning: in no case)
   - under normal circumstances
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - This is a fixed phrase meaning "given the situation." "Circumstances" represent conditions, and "under" indicates being subject to or within those conditions. Highly conventionalized
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (subject to) + *the circumstances* (conditions, situation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think he handled it pretty well, under the circumstances.
   - Under the circumstances, I'm just glad everyone's safe.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Under the circumstances, the committee determined that postponement was appropriate.
   - The defendant's actions, while irregular, were deemed reasonable under the circumstances.

1. **Expression**: under construction (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Being built; in the process of being constructed or created; not yet completed
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: being built, in progress, being erected, in development
   
   **Antonyms**: completed, finished, operational, open
   
   **When to Use**: Use for buildings, infrastructure, websites, or projects that are actively being built or developed
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for things being repaired (use "under repair" or "under renovation"). Not for planning phases before construction begins
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - still under construction
   - currently under construction
   - under active construction
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Construction" is a process, and "under" indicates being in the midst of that process. This is a fixed collocation in English; *"in construction" is not standard
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (in process of) + *construction* (building)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The website's still under construction, so some links don't work yet.
   - You can't use that entrance—the whole wing is under construction.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is currently under construction with completion anticipated in Q3 2026.
   - Road access will be limited while the bridge is under construction.

1. **Expression**: under investigation (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Being examined, inquired into, or researched, especially by authorities; subject to official scrutiny or inquiry
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: being investigated, being examined, subject to inquiry, being looked into
   
   **Antonyms**: cleared, concluded, closed (investigation)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when authorities, organizations, or researchers are actively examining something, particularly in legal, regulatory, or formal research contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for casual inquiry (use "looking into" or "checking"). "Under investigation" implies formal, systematic examination
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - under active investigation
   - currently under investigation
   - under criminal investigation
   - under federal investigation
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Investigation" is a process, and "under" indicates being subject to that process. Standard legal/official collocation
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (subject to) + *investigation* (formal inquiry)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The accident is under investigation, so they can't tell us much yet.
   - His disappearance is under investigation by the police.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The allegations are currently under investigation by the ethics committee.
   - Three officers have been placed on leave while the matter remains under investigation.

1. **Expression**: under the impression (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Believing something to be true (often mistakenly); having a particular belief or understanding about something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase, always with "the")
   
   **Synonyms**: believing that, thinking that, assuming that, with the understanding that
   
   **Antonyms**: knowing for certain, confirmed understanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use when stating a belief that may be incorrect, or when clarifying what someone believed to be true
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for firm knowledge (use "knowing" or "aware that"). This phrase often implies uncertainty or potential error
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - under the impression that
   - under the mistaken impression
   - under the false impression
   - give someone the impression
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Impression" is a mental image/belief, and "under" indicates being subject to or influenced by that impression. Fixed phrase in English
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (influenced by) + *the impression* (belief, understanding)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was under the impression that the meeting was tomorrow, not today!
   - She's under the impression that you don't like her, which isn't true.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The respondents were under the impression that participation was mandatory.
   - I was under the impression that these terms had been agreed upon previously.

1. **Expression**: go under (type: phrasal verb idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To fail financially; to go bankrupt or out of business
   - *(Sense 2)* To sink beneath the surface of water
   - *(Sense 3)* To lose consciousness, especially under anesthesia
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: fail, collapse, go bankrupt (sense 1), sink (sense 2), lose consciousness (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: succeed, survive, stay afloat, emerge
   
   **When to Use**: Use for business failure, sinking, or losing consciousness. Context usually makes the sense clear
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for minor setbacks or temporary difficulties. "Go under" implies complete failure or submersion
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - go under financially
   - about to go under
   - watch something go under
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - Literal meaning of going beneath the surface extended metaphorically to business failure and loss of consciousness. "Under" represents submersion or descent
   
   **Root Analysis**: *go* (move, proceed) + *under* (beneath)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A lot of small businesses went under during the pandemic.
   - The ship hit the iceberg and went under in minutes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Despite restructuring efforts, the company went under in 2019.
   - The patient went under anesthesia without complications.

1. **Expression**: under the weather (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Slightly ill or unwell; not feeling one's best; mildly sick
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiom)
   
   Literal meaning: No longer used literally
   Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: Feeling somewhat ill or unwell
   
   **Synonyms**: unwell, ill, sick, poorly, not feeling well, off-color
   
   **Antonyms**: well, healthy, feeling great, in good health
   
   **When to Use**: Use informally to indicate mild illness; suggests temporary, not serious illness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for serious illness (too casual/euphemistic). Don't use in medical contexts requiring precision
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - feeling under the weather
   - a bit under the weather
   - looking under the weather
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - Nautical origin: sailors who felt seasick would go below deck (under the weather side of the ship) to recover. Metaphor extended to general mild illness
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (below) + *the weather* (atmospheric conditions/weather side of ship)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm feeling a bit under the weather today—I think I'm coming down with something.
   - She's been under the weather all week but won't stay home.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts*

1. **Expression**: under no circumstances (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In no situation; not for any reason; never; regardless of what happens
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase, emphatic negation)
   
   **Synonyms**: never, not under any conditions, in no case, not for any reason, absolutely not
   
   **Antonyms**: always, under any circumstances, without exception
   
   **When to Use**: Use for emphatic prohibition or refusal; indicates absolute restriction with no exceptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very emphatic; don't use for negotiable situations or preferences. This phrase indicates absolute, non-negotiable prohibition
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - under no circumstances should/must/will
   - not under any circumstances
   - under no circumstances whatsoever
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - Fixed phrase using "under" + "circumstances" to indicate that no situational conditions would justify the action. Highly emphatic construction
   
   **Root Analysis**: *under* (within) + *no* (negation) + *circumstances* (conditions)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Under no circumstances are you to open that door—do you understand?
   - I wouldn't lend him money under any circumstances after what he did.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Under no circumstances shall confidential information be disclosed to third parties.
   - The regulations stipulate that under no circumstances may safety protocols be circumvented.
