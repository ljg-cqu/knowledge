1. **Original Expression**: Everyone came except John  
   **Context**: Describing attendance with one person missing
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that all people attended or participated with one specified exclusion
   
   **Casual**: Everyone showed up except John  
   *Characteristics*: Simple past tense, phrasal verb "showed up", direct statement  
   *Use when*: Casual conversation, friends, informal reporting
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Everyone attended except John  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "attended", straightforward structure, neutral tone  
   *Use when*: Professional contexts, workplace communication, standard business writing
   
   **Formal**: All participants were present with the exception of John  
   *Characteristics*: Passive voice, formal noun "participants", elaborate exception phrase  
   *Use when*: Official reports, academic writing, formal documentation
   
   **Humorous**: The whole gang made it except John—fashionably absent as usual!  
   *Characteristics*: Informal "gang," playful phrase "fashionably absent," lighthearted tone  
   *Use when*: Among friends who appreciate humor, casual social contexts  
   *Avoid when*: John might be offended, formal settings, when absence has serious cause
   
   **Lively**: Everybody was there except John—can you believe it?!  
   *Characteristics*: Exclamation, engaging question, emphatic "everybody," energetic tone  
   *Use when*: Storytelling, expressing surprise, animated conversation
   
   **Empathetic**: Everyone came except John, who unfortunately couldn't make it  
   *Characteristics*: Adds sympathetic "unfortunately," acknowledges John's situation  
   *Use when*: When absence was disappointing or unavoidable, showing consideration
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher formality with authority figures; casual with peers
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers require formal; friends allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Social event allows casual; official meeting requires formal
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures prefer explicit empathy; others value brevity
   - *Medium*: Spoken allows casual; written reports require formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "accept" instead of "except" (spelling error with different meaning)
   - Overly formal language in casual contexts ("All individuals were present save John")
   - Insensitive humor about absence when person had valid or sad reason
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some cultures consider publicly noting someone's absence as highlighting failure; others see it as factual reporting
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Casual friend gathering → **Casual**: "Everyone showed up except John"
   - **Situation 2**: Professional team meeting minutes → **Formal**: "All team members attended with the exception of John Smith"
   - **Situation 3**: Telling story to friends → **Lively**: "Guess what? Everybody came except John!"

1. **Original Expression**: Can you make an exception?  
   **Context**: Requesting special treatment or exemption from a rule
   
   **Core Intent**: To politely ask for deviation from standard rules or procedures
   
   **Casual**: Can you let this slide just this once?  
   *Characteristics*: Idiomatic "let slide," informal phrasing, minimizing request  
   *Use when*: Casual relationships, small infractions, friendly environments
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Would it be possible to make an exception?  
   *Characteristics*: Conditional "would," question about possibility, polite indirectness  
   *Use when*: Customer service interactions, professional requests, standard business
   
   **Formal**: I respectfully request an exemption from standard policy  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "request," noun "exemption," explicit politeness marker  
   *Use when*: Official requests, written applications, dealing with authorities
   
   **Humorous**: Think you could bend the rules for your favorite customer?  
   *Characteristics*: Playful "bend the rules," self-deprecating humor, winking tone  
   *Use when*: Established rapport, service providers who appreciate humor, light situations  
   *Avoid when*: Serious violations, no relationship established, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: Come on, can't you make a tiny exception? Please?  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic "come on," minimizer "tiny," added "please," persuasive energy  
   *Use when*: Friendly persuasion, rapport-building contexts, negotiation situations
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand this is unusual, but would you be able to make an exception given my circumstances?  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges other's position, explains reason, shows understanding  
   *Use when*: Difficult circumstances, seeking understanding, when explanation helps case
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher formality when requester has lower status; casual with equals
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers require formal approach; friends allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Minor rules allow casual; important policies require formal
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures value direct requests; others prefer elaborate indirectness
   - *Medium*: Face-to-face allows casual persuasion; written requests require formality
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Too direct/demanding ("Make an exception for me") without acknowledging imposition
   - Overly elaborate justification making request seem suspicious or desperate
   - Using humor with people who don't appreciate it or in serious situations
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Anglo cultures often use elaborate indirectness for requests; other cultures may find this insincere or unclear
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Asking store clerk to return item past deadline → **Semi-Formal**: "Would it be possible to make an exception?"
   - **Situation 2**: Friend enforcing game rules → **Casual**: "Can you let this slide?"
   - **Situation 3**: Formal academic extension request → **Formal**: "I respectfully request an exemption from the standard deadline"

   *Characteristics*: Contraction, simple vocabulary, straightforward structure  
   *Use when*: Casual sales, informal quoting, everyday transactions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: All items are included, excluding taxes  
   *Characteristics*: Present participle "excluding," neutral business tone, clear structure  
   *Use when*: Business quotes, standard commercial transactions, professional pricing
   
   **Formal**: The price encompasses all specified items with the exception of applicable taxes  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "encompasses," legal precision, elaborate exception phrase  
   *Use when*: Contracts, legal documents, formal business proposals

   *Avoid when*: Formal contracts, customers who want serious treatment, legal contexts
   
   **Lively**: Great news! Everything's in there except—you guessed it—taxes!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic opening, interactive "you guessed it," energetic delivery  
   *Use when*: Sales presentations, engaging customers, building excitement
   
   **Empathetic**: The good news is everything's included; the only additional cost is taxes, which I wish I could waive  
   *Characteristics*: Frames positively "good news," expresses wish to help, sympathetic tone  
   *Use when*: Customer might be price-sensitive, building goodwill, explaining costs
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Customer has power in transaction; accommodate their preferred register
   - *Social Distance*: New customers need professional tone; regulars allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Small purchases allow casual; major contracts require formal precision
