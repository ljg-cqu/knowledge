1. **Functional Expression**: Calm down (function: managing emotions/de-escalation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Directive to become less agitated, angry, or excited; request to regain emotional control
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (can be perceived as dismissive)
   
   **Synonyms**: Relax, Take it easy, Settle down, Cool off, Take a breath
   
   **Antonyms**: Get worked up, Get upset
   
   **When to Use**: De-escalating tense situations; helping someone manage emotions; appropriate among equals or when you have authority/relationship to give such direction
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound dismissive or patronizing, especially if recipient's concerns are valid; power dynamics matter—subordinate saying "calm down" to superior is inappropriate; avoid in highly formal or sensitive contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just calm down (more emphatic)
   - You need to calm down (more directive)
   - Let's all calm down (inclusive, less confrontational)
   - Try to calm down (softer, more supportive)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: High face-threat potential—can invalidate recipient's emotions; soften with "I know this is upsetting, but let's try to calm down" or similar empathetic framing; tone and relationship matter significantly
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "You're right, I'm sorry." / "Okay, give me a minute."
   - Defensive: "I AM calm!" / "Don't tell me to calm down!"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Calm down! It's not that big of a deal.
   - Okay, okay, let's both calm down and talk about this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Perhaps we could all take a moment to regain composure.
   - Let's pause briefly and reconvene when emotions are less elevated.

1. **Functional Expression**: Settle down (function: requesting quiet/order)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request to become quiet, orderly, and attentive; directive to stop disruptive behavior
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Quiet down, Pipe down (more informal), Be quiet, Settle (shortened)
   
   **Antonyms**: Carry on, Continue
   
   **When to Use**: Classroom management, group settings, parent-child interactions; speaker has authority; appropriate when group is rowdy or distracted
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Requires authority relationship; peers saying this to peers can sound bossy; inappropriate in formal meetings (use "May I have your attention, please?")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Settle down, everyone
   - Time to settle down
   - Settle down, please
   - Let's settle down
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Authority-based directive; teacher-to-student, parent-to-child typical contexts; tone matters—can be firm but should not be hostile; often repeated if compliance is slow
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Compliance: [group quiets down]
   - Partial compliance: [some comply, others need reminding]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Okay everyone, settle down! Let's get started.
   - Settle down, kids. It's bedtime.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If everyone could please take their seats, we'll begin.
   - May I request your attention as we commence the proceedings.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's get down to business (function: transitioning to main topic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposal to stop preliminary talk and begin addressing main issues or work; signals transition to serious work
   - *Formality*: neutral to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (phrased as suggestion but functions as transition marker)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's get started, Let's begin, Time to start work, Shall we begin?
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's keep chatting, Let's take a break
   
   **When to Use**: Meetings, work sessions, negotiations; signals end of small talk and beginning of substantive work; appropriate when speaker has facilitation role or meeting leadership
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound abrupt if used too early; allow some rapport-building first; highly formal contexts might prefer "Shall we commence?"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's get down to work
   - Let's get down to it
   - Time to get down to business
   - Shall we get down to business?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conventional meeting transition marker; signals shift from relational talk to task focus; generally well-received as it shows respect for participants' time; can establish speaker as organized and focused
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Yes, let's do that." / "Sounds good."
   - Acknowledgment: [participants prepare materials, shift attention]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Okay, enough chatting—let's get down to business.
   - We should probably get down to business. We have a lot to cover.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let's get down to business. The first item on our agenda is...
   - Shall we proceed to the substantive matters before us?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm down (function: expressing agreement/willingness)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Declaration of willingness, agreement, or enthusiasm to participate in suggested activity
   - *Formality*: very informal (slang)
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm in, I'm up for it, Count me in, Sure, Sounds good
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm out, Count me out, No thanks, I'll pass
   
   **When to Use**: Casual conversations among friends, peers, or close colleagues; expressing enthusiasm for plans; informal social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for professional, academic, or formal contexts; use "I'm interested" or "I'd be happy to participate" instead; generational—may not be understood by all age groups
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm so down
   - I'm totally down
   - I'm down for that
   - I'm down if you are
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Contemporary slang (emerged 1990s-2000s); shows positive enthusiasm; common in making social plans; "down" metaphorically = "solid, committed"
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Coordination: "Great! Let's do it." / "Awesome, I'll text you details."
   - Confirmation: "Really? Cool!"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Want to grab dinner tonight?" "Yeah, I'm down!"
   - I'm totally down for a road trip this weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Avoid; use formal alternatives)
   - I would be pleased to participate in that initiative.

1. **Functional Expression**: Don't let me down (function: requesting reliability/expressing trust)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request that someone fulfill expectations and not disappoint; expresses trust while emphasizing importance
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (framed as negative request but conveys positive expectation)
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm counting on you, Please come through, Don't disappoint me, I'm trusting you
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't expect much, No pressure
   
   **When to Use**: Situations requiring reliability; when trusting someone with important responsibility; appropriate when you have relationship that permits expressing vulnerability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can create pressure or guilt; use carefully; can damage relationships if recipient feels excessive pressure; avoid if person has already committed and extra pressure is unnecessary
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Please don't let me down
   - Don't let us down
   - I hope you won't let me down
   - Try not to let me down
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-threatening act—places obligation on recipient; works because it appeals to relationship and trust; implies consequences (disappointment, damaged trust) if expectations aren't met; soften with explanations of why something matters
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Commitment: "I won't, I promise." / "You can count on me."
   - Acknowledgment: "I'll do my best." / "I understand how important this is."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is really important to me. Please don't let me down.
   - I'm trusting you with this. Don't let me down, okay?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We have confidence in your abilities and trust you will meet these expectations.
   - The success of this project depends on your contributions.

1. **Functional Expression**: Can you break it down for me? (function: requesting clarification/simplification)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request to explain something in simpler terms or component parts; asks for detailed, understandable explanation
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (but polite request form)
   
   **Synonyms**: Can you explain that more simply?, Can you clarify?, Could you elaborate?, Walk me through it
   
   **Antonyms**: I understand, That's clear, Got it
   
   **When to Use**: When explanation was too complex, technical, or fast; educational contexts; appropriate when you need better understanding and feel comfortable admitting confusion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May signal you weren't listening or didn't understand; some contexts punish admission of confusion; gauge whether asking will be received positively
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you break that down for me?
   - Can you break down how this works?
   - Break it down for me in simple terms
   - Let me break it down for you (offering to explain)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Admits knowledge gap but frames it as request for good pedagogy; "break down" emphasizes analytical division into understandable parts; more face-saving than "I don't understand"; shows engagement and desire to learn
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acceptance: "Sure, let me explain it differently." / "Okay, basically..."
   - Clarification: "Which part is confusing?" / "What would help?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That went over my head. Can you break it down for me?
   - I'm not following. Break it down for me?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you perhaps elaborate on that point with additional detail?
   - I would appreciate if you could explain the process step by step.

1. **Functional Expression**: I don't want to let you down (function: expressing commitment/managing expectations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expression of concern about meeting expectations; shows awareness of responsibility and desire to succeed
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (frames concern rather than direct commitment)
   
   **Synonyms**: I want to meet your expectations, I hope to come through for you, I don't want to disappoint you
   
   **Antonyms**: I might disappoint you, I can't promise anything
   
   **When to Use**: When accepting responsibility; expressing concern about meeting standards; appropriate when you want to show you value the relationship and take commitments seriously
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound like hedging or lack of confidence; if you're certain you can deliver, stronger commitment language may be better; can inadvertently plant doubt
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I really don't want to let you down
   - I hope I don't let you down
   - I'd hate to let you down
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows awareness of other's expectations and values relationship; can be relationship-building by showing care; risk: may communicate uncertainty when confidence would be better received; use when genuinely uncertain but committed to trying
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Reassurance: "I know you'll do your best." / "I have faith in you."
   - Understanding: "I appreciate you saying that." / "Just do what you can."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is important to you, and I don't want to let you down.
   - I'm going to try my hardest—I don't want to let you down.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I am committed to meeting the project requirements to the best of my ability.
   - I take these responsibilities seriously and will work diligently to fulfill them.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's not get bogged down in... (function: refocusing discussion/avoiding tangents)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggestion to avoid getting overly detailed or distracted by particular issue; proposes staying focused on main points
   - *Formality*: neutral to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (phrased as collaborative suggestion)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's not dwell on..., Let's move past..., Let's focus on the main issue, We're getting off track
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's explore this in depth, Let's examine this closely
   
   **When to Use**: Meetings or discussions going off track; when minor details threaten to derail main discussion; appropriate for facilitators or when you have standing to redirect
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can dismiss legitimate concerns; ensure you're not shutting down important discussion; be sensitive to whether others see the issue as peripheral or central
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's not get bogged down in the details
   - We shouldn't get bogged down in...
   - Try not to get bogged down in...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Bogged down" metaphor = stuck in swamp/mud; implies current discussion is unproductive obstacle; use diplomatically—can offend if others view topic as important; frame as time management rather than dismissal
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "You're right, let's move on." / "Good point."
   - Resistance: "But this is important." / "Can we come back to this?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's not get bogged down in the details right now. We can figure that out later.
   - We're getting bogged down. Let's refocus on the main question.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Perhaps we could table the minutiae and focus on strategic considerations.
   - In the interest of time, I suggest we address this matter separately.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm feeling down (function: expressing emotional state/seeking support)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Statement of sadness, depression, or low mood; indirect request for support or understanding
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct about feeling, indirect about requesting support
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm feeling sad, I'm feeling blue, I'm not feeling great, I'm a bit low
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm feeling great, I'm on top of the world, I'm feeling up
   
   **When to Use**: With friends, family, trusted colleagues, or counselors; when you need support or want to explain your mood; appropriate when relationship permits emotional disclosure
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Highly professional contexts may not be appropriate for emotional disclosure; gauge relationship and context; chronic complaints can burden relationships
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm feeling really down
   - I'm feeling a bit down
   - I've been feeling down lately
   - I'm feeling down about...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Vulnerability disclosure; typically elicits supportive responses; "down" is euphemistic—less intense than "depressed"; opens door for listener to offer support or ask what's wrong; requires trust in relationship
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Supportive: "What's going on?" / "I'm sorry to hear that. Want to talk?"
   - Empathetic: "That's tough. I'm here if you need me."
   - Advice-giving: "Have you tried...?" / "Maybe you should..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been feeling down since I didn't get the job.
   - I'm feeling really down today. Can we talk?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Generally avoid; use more clinical language if necessary)
   - I've been experiencing some challenges with my mood recently.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let me write that down (function: showing attention/ensuring accuracy)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Statement that speaker will record information; shows information is important and speaker wants to remember it
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (statement of action)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let me make a note of that, I'll jot that down, I should write that down
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll remember that, I've got it
   
   **When to Use**: Taking information (phone numbers, appointments, instructions); shows conscientiousness and respect for information given; appropriate in all contexts where note-taking is accepted
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Some contexts (intimate conversations, counseling) may be disrupted by note-taking; gauge appropriateness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let me jot that down
   - I should write that down
   - Can you hold on while I write that down?
   - Let me make a note of that
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive pragmatic function—shows you value information and speaker; demonstrates organization and responsibility; implicit compliment to speaker (what you said is worth recording); pause in conversation while writing
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accommodation: [slows down or pauses] / "Sure, take your time."
   - Repetition: "That's..." [repeats information]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's a great idea! Let me write that down.
   - What's your number again? Let me write it down.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Allow me to record those specifications for accuracy.
   - I'll make a note of those requirements.

1. **Functional Expression**: Turn down (function: refusing/rejecting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To refuse or reject an offer, invitation, or request
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct (though often softened in use)
   
   **Synonyms**: Decline, Refuse, Say no to, Reject, Pass on
   
   **Antonyms**: Accept, Agree to, Take up
   
   **When to Use**: Describing refusal of offers, opportunities, or requests; appropriate when explaining past decisions or considering options
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Direct refusal may be harsh; typically soften with explanation, appreciation, or alternative; face-threatening act requires politeness strategies
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I had to turn down...
   - I'm going to turn down...
   - She turned down the offer
   - I can't turn down...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-threatening act requiring mitigation; typically accompanied by: (1) appreciation for offer, (2) explanation/excuse, (3) regret expression, (4) or alternative suggestion; direct "I turn you down" is harsh—use indirect forms
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Understanding: "That's okay, I understand." / "No problem."
   - Disappointment: "That's too bad." / "Are you sure?"
   - Acceptance: "Thanks for considering it anyway."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I had to turn down the job—the salary was too low.
   - I'm sorry, but I'm going to have to turn down your invitation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After careful consideration, I must respectfully decline the offer.
   - The committee regretfully turned down the proposal due to budget constraints.
