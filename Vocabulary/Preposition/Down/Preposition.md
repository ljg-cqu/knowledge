1. **Preposition**: down (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* From a higher to a lower point or position; toward the ground or a lower level
   - *(Spatial)* Along the length of something; following a path or route
   - *(Temporal)* Through a period of time; throughout
   - *(Abstract)* At or to a place or level regarded as lower or less important
   
   **Synonyms**: toward (spatial), along (directional), throughout (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: up, upward, toward a higher point
   
   **When to Use**: Extremely versatile preposition for indicating downward direction, movement along routes, progression through time, or hierarchical relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid confusion with "below" (position) vs "down" (direction); don't use when "along" is clearer for horizontal movement
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down the street, down the hall, down the road, down the line
   - **Verb + prep**: go down, walk down, run down, fall down, slide down, come down
   - **Adjective + prep**: located down, positioned down
   - **Noun + prep**: way down, path down, route down
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English doun → Old English dūne (from the hill)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English dūne (adverbial "from the hill") → prepositional use developed in Middle English → highly productive in modern English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She walked down the street to the store.
   - Let's go down to the beach this afternoon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegation proceeded down the corridor to the conference room.
   - Trends indicate movement down the socioeconomic spectrum.

1. **Preposition**: down to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Including even; extending to include the smallest or least significant item
   - *(Sense 2)* As far as; reaching a particular point or level
   - *(Sense 3)* Being the responsibility of; dependent upon
   
   **Synonyms**: including, even to, up to and including (sense 1), dependent on (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, up to (but not including)
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasis on comprehensiveness or extent; shows thoroughness by including smallest details
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sense 3 ("it's down to you") is more British; American English might prefer "up to you"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down to the last detail, down to the wire, down to earth
   - **Verb + prep**: come down to, get down to, narrow down to
   - **Adjective + prep**: reduced down to
   - **Noun + prep**: everything down to
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (to the lowest point) + to (extent marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition emphasizing complete extent or final responsibility
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They planned everything down to the last detail.
   - It's down to you to make the final decision.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation examined every aspect down to the minutest detail.
   - Success is down to effective implementation of strategic initiatives.

1. **Preposition**: down from (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving or located from a higher to lower position relative to something
   - *(Numerical)* Decreased or reduced from a previous level or amount
   
   **Synonyms**: descending from, reduced from, decreased from
   
   **Antonyms**: up from, increased from
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates starting point of downward movement or reduction; common in statistical and comparative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ensure direction is clear; "from" indicates origin point
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down from the mountain, down from last year, down from previous levels
   - **Verb + prep**: come down from, bring down from, climb down from
   - **Adjective + prep**: different down from
   - **Noun + prep**: decline down from, reduction down from
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (downward direction) + from (origin point)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound prepositional phrase indicating origin point and direction of movement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She came down from her office to meet us.
   - Sales are down from last quarter.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Revenue decreased 15% down from the comparable period.
   - The climbers descended down from the summit after weather deteriorated.

1. **Preposition**: down through (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving downward while passing through something
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout a period of time; across generations or eras
   
   **Synonyms**: through (spatial), throughout, across (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: up through
   
   **When to Use**: Historical contexts emphasizing continuity across time; physical contexts showing downward passage through space
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous; clarify whether spatial or temporal meaning is intended
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down through the ages, down through history, down through generations
   - **Verb + prep**: pass down through, come down through, filter down through
   - **Adjective + prep**: transmitted down through
   - **Noun + prep**: traditions down through, knowledge down through
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (downward/through time) + through (passage)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal combination emphasizing passage through time or space with downward component
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These stories have been passed down through generations.
   - The light filtered down through the trees.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Cultural traditions transmit down through successive generations.
   - The principle has endured down through centuries of jurisprudence.

1. **Preposition**: down on (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Having a negative opinion of; critical toward; disapproving of
   - *(Spatial)* In a lower position relative to something
   
   **Synonyms**: critical of, disapproving of, opposed to (sense 1)
   
   **Antonyms**: supportive of, favorable toward
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts expressing criticism or disapproval; sense 1 is idiomatic
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for highly formal writing; can sound judgmental
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down on someone, down on the idea
   - **Verb + prep**: be down on, come down on, get down on
   - **Adjective + prep**: really down on, pretty down on
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (negative attitude) + on (directed toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Idiomatic expression → "down" metaphorically represents negative attitude directed "on" target
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why are you so down on this plan?
   - My parents were really down on the idea of me taking a gap year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regulatory agencies remain critical of such practices.
   - (Note: "down on" is too informal; use "critical of" or "opposed to")

1. **Preposition**: down by (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Located at or near something in a lower position
   - *(Numerical)* Decreased by a specific amount or margin
   
   **Synonyms**: near, beside (spatial), reduced by, decreased by (numerical)
   
   **Antonyms**: up by, increased by (numerical)
   
   **When to Use**: Location descriptions; statistical comparisons showing specific reduction amounts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ensure "by" clearly indicates either location or amount; context must clarify
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down by the river, down by the dock, down by 10%
   - **Verb + prep**: meet down by, wait down by, reduced down by
   - **Adjective + prep**: located down by
   - **Noun + prep**: house down by, sales down by
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (lower position/reduction) + by (proximity/amount)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal combination with dual meanings—spatial proximity and numerical difference
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's meet down by the river.
   - We're down by two goals at halftime.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is located down by the waterfront district.
   - Revenue declined down by 12% relative to projections.

1. **Preposition**: down with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In opposition to; against; expressing desire to get rid of something (often in protests)
   - *(Sense 2)* Suffering from; afflicted with (illness)
   
   **Synonyms**: opposed to, against (sense 1), suffering from, ill with (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: supportive of, in favor of (sense 1), recovered from (sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Protest slogans and political contexts (sense 1); medical contexts for illness (sense 2)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sense 1 is aggressive and confrontational; use carefully; sense 2 is somewhat dated
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down with tyranny, down with corruption, down with the flu
   - **Verb + prep**: come down with (illness), be down with
   - **Exclamation**: Down with...! (protest slogan)
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (overthrow/affliction) + with (association)
   
   **Etymology**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* Political slogan usage → "bring down" combined with target
   - *(Sense 2)* Medical idiom → "brought down by" illness
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been down with a cold all week.
   - Down with unfair practices!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Protesters chanted "Down with corruption" during demonstrations.
   - The employee is currently down with influenza and unable to attend.

1. **Preposition**: down in (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Located at a lower level within a place or area
   - *(Abstract)* Feeling low spirits about something; depressed regarding
   
   **Synonyms**: in a lower part of, at the bottom of (spatial), depressed about (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: up in, elevated in
   
   **When to Use**: Geographic descriptions; emotional states (informal)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Emotional sense is informal; prefer clinical language in formal contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down in the basement, down in the dumps, down in spirits
   - **Verb + prep**: be down in, feel down in, located down in
   - **Adjective + prep**: situated down in
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (lower position/low mood) + in (within)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal combination with literal spatial and metaphorical emotional meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The tools are down in the basement.
   - I've been feeling down in the dumps lately.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Archaeological excavations extended down in the stratigraphic layers.
   - Participants reported diminished mood states. (rather than "down in spirits")

1. **Preposition**: down at (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Located in or at a place regarded as lower or at the end of something
   - *(Abstract)* Expressing location at a specific place (somewhat informal)
   
   **Synonyms**: at, located at, over at
   
   **Antonyms**: up at
   
   **When to Use**: Casual directions; informal location descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Down" may be unnecessary and colloquial; often "at" alone suffices in formal writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down at the corner, down at the office, down at the store
   - **Verb + prep**: meet down at, work down at, wait down at
   - **Noun + prep**: people down at, situation down at
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (toward a place) + at (location)
   
   **Etymology**: Colloquial combination adding directional flavor to location marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll meet you down at the coffee shop.
   - She works down at the community center.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Prefer: "at the coffee shop" or "at the community center")
   - Staff members convened at the regional office. (rather than "down at")

1. **Preposition**: down over (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving downward across or covering a surface
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout or during a descending period
   
   **Synonyms**: across, over, throughout
   
   **Antonyms**: up over
   
   **When to Use**: Describing movement or coverage that combines downward motion with lateral extent
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Relatively uncommon combination; often "over" or "down" alone is clearer
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: down over the cliff, down over the years
   - **Verb + prep**: cascade down over, flow down over, spread down over
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (downward) + over (across surface)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound preposition combining vertical and horizontal spatial components
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Water cascaded down over the rocks.
   - Prices have come down over the past few months.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The landslide debris flowed down over the highway.
   - Performance metrics declined down over the observation period.
