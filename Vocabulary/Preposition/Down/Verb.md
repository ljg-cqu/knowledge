1. **Verb**: down
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To knock or bring someone or something to the ground
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To consume food or drink quickly
   - *(Sense 3)* (Transitive) To defeat or stop someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: knock down, fell (sense 1), gulp, consume (sense 2), defeat, overcome (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: raise, lift, erect, lose to
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to semi-formal contexts; sense 1 common in sports/conflict; sense 2 casual for eating/drinking; sense 3 in competition contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Down" as verb is less formal; in academic writing, use more specific alternatives
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - down a drink
   - down an opponent
   - down tools (British: stop working)
   - down a plane
   
   **Root Analysis**: From adverb/preposition "down" → verbified usage
   
   **Etymology**: Verb use emerged 16th-17th century → derived from directional adverb → means "cause to go down"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He downed his beer in one gulp.
   - The boxer downed his opponent in round three.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The pilot successfully downed the enemy aircraft.
   - Labor unions downed tools in protest of working conditions.

1. **Verb**: download
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To transfer data from a remote computer or server to a local device
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To be transferred in this way
   
   **Synonyms**: transfer, retrieve, fetch, pull down
   
   **Antonyms**: upload, send, transmit
   
   **When to Use**: Computing and digital contexts; appropriate in technical documentation, instructions, and general technology discussion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Older or non-technical audiences may need clarification; increasingly universal in digital age
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - download a file
   - download an app
   - download illegally
   - available to download
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (to local device) + load (transfer)
   
   **Etymology**: Computer science neologism (1980s) → metaphor of bringing data "down" from remote source to user's device
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you download that app and try it out?
   - I'm downloading the new album right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Users may download the software from the official repository.
   - The system downloads updates automatically during off-peak hours.

1. **Verb**: downsize
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To make a company or organization smaller by reducing the number of employees
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive/Intransitive) To move to a smaller home, vehicle, or lifestyle
   
   **Synonyms**: reduce, cut back, scale down, restructure
   
   **Antonyms**: expand, upsize, grow, enlarge
   
   **When to Use**: Corporate/business contexts (often euphemism for layoffs); personal contexts for lifestyle simplification
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound euphemistic or insensitive when discussing job losses; more direct language may be appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - downsize the workforce
   - downsize operations
   - downsize to a condo
   - forced to downsize
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (reduce) + size (magnitude)
   
   **Etymology**: American business jargon (1970s-80s) → euphemism for workforce reduction → extended to personal lifestyle choices
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're downsizing to a smaller apartment.
   - The company downsized after the merger.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Management announced plans to downsize operations by 15%.
   - Organizations frequently downsize during economic contractions.

1. **Verb**: downplay
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To make something seem less important, serious, or significant than it actually is; to minimize
   
   **Synonyms**: minimize, understate, de-emphasize, play down, make light of
   
   **Antonyms**: emphasize, exaggerate, highlight, stress, amplify
   
   **When to Use**: Contexts involving communication strategy, risk management, public relations, or impression management
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply dishonesty or manipulation; be careful when accuracy is paramount
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - downplay the risk
   - downplay concerns
   - downplay the significance
   - attempt to downplay
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (reduce) + play (present, perform)
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century → theatrical metaphor → "play down" meaning present something with reduced emphasis
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She tried to downplay her achievements.
   - Don't downplay how serious this is!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Officials attempted to downplay the severity of the security breach.
   - The report downplays significant environmental risks.

1. **Verb**: downgrade
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To reduce to a lower grade, rank, or level of importance
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To reduce the estimated value or quality rating of something
   
   **Synonyms**: demote, lower, reduce, devalue
   
   **Antonyms**: upgrade, promote, elevate, enhance
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts (job changes), technical contexts (software/systems), financial contexts (credit ratings)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be harsh regarding personnel decisions; consider diplomatic alternatives
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - downgrade the system
   - downgrade from first class
   - downgrade a rating
   - downgrade the threat level
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (lower) + grade (rank, level)
   
   **Etymology**: American English (19th century) → railway term → extended to status/quality reductions (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They downgraded my flight to economy.
   - The storm was downgraded to a tropical depression.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The credit agency downgraded the corporate bond rating.
   - Security officials downgraded the threat assessment level.

1. **Verb**: downshift
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To change to a lower gear in a vehicle
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To reduce the speed or intensity of activity; to adopt a simpler lifestyle
   
   **Synonyms**: shift down, gear down (sense 1), slow down, simplify (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: upshift, accelerate, intensify
   
   **When to Use**: Automotive contexts (literal); lifestyle/career contexts (metaphorical) for choosing slower pace
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Metaphorical sense may not be clear to all audiences; clarify meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - downshift to second gear
   - downshift your lifestyle
   - downshift your career
   - need to downshift
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (lower) + shift (change)
   
   **Etymology**: Automotive term (mid-20th century) → metaphorical extension to lifestyle choices (1990s-2000s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to downshift when going uphill.
   - I'm planning to downshift and work part-time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Operators should downshift appropriately when descending steep grades.
   - Many professionals choose to downshift careers mid-life to achieve work-life balance.

1. **Verb**: downpour
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive, rare as verb) To rain heavily and suddenly
   
   **Synonyms**: pour, rain heavily, deluge
   
   **Antonyms**: drizzle, sprinkle
   
   **When to Use**: Primarily used as noun; verb form is uncommon—"pour down" is more natural
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Uncommon as verb; use "pour" or "rain heavily" instead for more natural expression
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - (More common as noun: "a downpour")
   - rain/pour down
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (from above) + pour (flow heavily)
   
   **Etymology**: Primarily nominal usage; verb form rarely used → "pour down" is preferred verbal form
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Uncommon: "It downpoured all day")
   - Better: "It poured down all day" or "There was a downpour all day"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Uncommon as verb; use nominal form or "rain fell heavily")

1. **Verb**: download
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive, informal) To tell someone information, typically in detail
   - *(Sense 2)* To unburden oneself by talking
   
   **Synonyms**: inform, tell, share, offload (emotional sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: withhold, keep secret
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts; metaphorical extension of digital "download" meaning to transfer information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for professional or academic writing; may not be understood by all audiences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - download someone about
   - download your thoughts
   - download the details
   
   **Root Analysis**: Metaphorical extension from computer term
   
   **Etymology**: Late 20th/early 21st century slang → metaphor from data download → informal meaning of information transfer or emotional unburdening
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me download you on what happened at the meeting.
   - I need to download all my frustrations to someone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Avoid in formal contexts; use "inform," "brief," or "share information")

1. **Verb**: downvote
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To register disapproval or negative feedback on content, typically on social media or forums, by clicking a down arrow or similar indicator
   
   **Synonyms**: vote down, give thumbs down, register disapproval
   
   **Antonyms**: upvote, like, approve
   
   **When to Use**: Social media, forum, and online community contexts; specific to platforms with voting systems (Reddit, Stack Exchange, etc.)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Platform-specific; may not be understood outside online contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - downvote a comment
   - downvote to oblivion
   - get downvoted
   - downvote brigade
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (negative) + vote (express preference)
   
   **Etymology**: Internet/social media neologism (2000s) → emerged with Reddit and similar platforms → opposite of "upvote"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why would someone downvote this helpful comment?
   - That post got downvoted immediately.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Community members may downvote content that violates guidelines.
   - The comment received 847 downvotes within the first hour.

1. **Verb**: down tools
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive, British) To stop working as a form of protest; to go on strike
   
   **Synonyms**: strike, walk out, stop work, cease work
   
   **Antonyms**: continue working, return to work
   
   **When to Use**: British English, labor relations contexts; describes workers stopping work as protest
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Primarily British usage; American English prefers "go on strike" or "walk out"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - down tools in protest
   - workers downed tools
   - threat to down tools
   
   **Root Analysis**: down (set down) + tools (work implements)
   
   **Etymology**: British English idiom (19th-20th century) → literal action of setting down tools → metaphor for work stoppage/strike
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The workers downed tools over safety concerns.
   - They threatened to down tools if demands weren't met.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Union members voted to down tools following failed negotiations.
   - Construction crews downed tools in solidarity with striking colleagues.
