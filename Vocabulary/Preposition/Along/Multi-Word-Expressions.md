1. **Expression**: go along with (type: phrasal verb collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To agree with or support a plan, idea, or decision; to accept and cooperate with something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: agree with, support, accept, comply with, consent to
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, resist, disagree with, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Common in decision-making contexts, negotiations, group discussions; implies agreement or compliance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply passive acceptance; use "support" or "endorse" for more active agreement
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - go right along with
   - willing to go along with
   - refuse to go along with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Along with" emphasizes accompaniment and cooperation, making "go" + "along with" natural for expressing agreement through metaphorical movement together
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (proceed) + along (in company) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll go along with whatever you decide.
   - They went along with the plan even though they had doubts.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board agreed to go along with management's restructuring proposal.
   - Stakeholders were willing to go along with the recommended changes.

1. **Expression**: get along with (type: phrasal verb collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have a harmonious, friendly relationship with someone; to be compatible
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: be compatible with, have good relations with, be on good terms with, hit it off with
   
   **Antonyms**: clash with, conflict with, not get on with, be at odds with
   
   **When to Use**: Describing interpersonal relationships, compatibility, social harmony; very common in everyday conversation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for very formal writing; prefer "maintain cordial relations" in official contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get along well with
   - get along fine with
   - can't get along with
   - don't get along with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Get" indicates achieving a state, "along" suggests harmony and parallel movement, making this natural for describing relationship compatibility
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (achieve) + along (harmoniously) + with (in relation to)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I get along great with my new roommate.
   - Do you get along with your in-laws?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Team members must get along with each other to ensure effective collaboration.
   - The ability to get along with diverse personalities is essential in this role.

1. **Expression**: all along (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Throughout the entire time from the beginning; from the start (often implying the speaker knew or suspected something continuously)
   - Literal meaning: throughout the entire length
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: continuously from the beginning, often with hindsight
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed)
   
   **Synonyms**: from the start, from the beginning, the whole time, throughout, all the while
   
   **Antonyms**: only recently, just now, at the end, finally
   
   **When to Use**: Revealing information that was true the entire time; expressing realization or vindication; emphasizing duration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound accusatory or "I told you so" in some contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - knew all along
   - all along the way
   - there all along
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: From spatial "all" (entire) + "along" (length) extended metaphorically to time, suggesting continuous duration from beginning to end
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (entire, complete) + along (through extent/duration)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I knew all along you'd be late!
   - The answer was right there all along.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research has supported this hypothesis all along.
   - Management was aware of these risks all along.

1. **Expression**: along the way (type: fixed phrase)

   - During the course or process of something; at some point during a journey or development
   - Literal meaning: at points on a physical path
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: during the process or progression
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very common idiom)
   
   **Synonyms**: in the process, during the journey, en route, in the course of, meanwhile
   
   **Antonyms**: at the destination, at the end, at the beginning
   
   **When to Use**: Describing things that happen during a process, journey, or development; emphasizes progression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be vague about timing; use specific time references when precision needed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - somewhere along the way
   - all along the way
   - picked up along the way
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Spatial "along the way" (points on a path) → temporal/process metaphor (during the journey of life/project)
   
   **Root Analysis**: along (through the extent) + the way (path/journey/process)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We stopped for coffee along the way.
   - I picked up some skills along the way.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Additional insights emerged along the way during the research process.
   - The methodology was refined along the way based on preliminary findings.

1. **Expression**: along the lines of (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Similar to; in the same general direction or manner as; resembling
   - *Fixedness*: strong (idiomatic expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: similar to, in the style of, resembling, like, in the manner of, comparable to
   
   **Antonyms**: unlike, different from, contrary to
   
   **When to Use**: Making comparisons, suggesting alternatives, describing similarity while allowing flexibility
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be vague; use specific comparisons when precision needed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - something along the lines of
   - along those lines
   - along similar lines
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: From "lines" (direction/pattern) + "along" (following) → following similar pattern or direction
   
   **Root Analysis**: along (following) + the lines (direction/pattern) + of (similarity)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was thinking something along the lines of a pizza party.
   - He said something along those lines.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal recommends policies along the lines of successful European models.
   - We're considering approaches along the lines of evidence-based interventions.
