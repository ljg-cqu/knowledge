1. **Adjective**: along (attributive use in compounds)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Accompanying or going together with; present or available (in combinations like "come-along" tools)
   - Limited adjectival use; primarily functions as preposition/adverb
   
   **Synonyms**: accompanying, concurrent, parallel
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, independent, solitary
   
   **When to Use**: Rare as standalone adjective; primarily used in compound terms or technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not standard as pure adjective; "along" functions primarily as preposition or adverb
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - come-along (tool/device name)
   - along position (rare technical use)
   - go-along attitude
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + long (length)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English along → Old English andlang → rarely developed true adjectival function
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He has a real go-along personality.
   - We need a come-along to pull that.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A come-along device was employed for the extraction procedure.
   - The team demonstrated a cooperative, go-along approach to problem-solving.

1. **Adjective**: get-along (informal adjective from phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Easy to work with; cooperative; compatible; having ability to maintain harmonious relationships
   - (gradable: easy to get along with, easier to get along with)
   
   **Synonyms**: cooperative, agreeable, compatible, easy-going, personable
   
   **Antonyms**: difficult, incompatible, uncooperative, contentious
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts describing personality traits; emphasizes social harmony and compatibility
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for formal evaluations; use "cooperative" or "collegial" in professional contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get-along person
   - easy get-along nature
   - good get-along skills
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (achieve) + along (harmony) → adjectival conversion
   
   **Etymology**: From phrasal verb "get along" → nominal/adjectival use (20th century colloquial)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's a real get-along kind of guy.
   - She has great get-along skills with everyone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The position requires strong interpersonal and get-along abilities.
   - Candidates must demonstrate get-along compatibility with diverse team members.

1. **Adjective**: go-along (informal adjective from phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Willing to agree or comply; accommodating; cooperative without strong opinions
   - Can carry slight negative connotation of being too passive
   - (non-gradable in most uses)
   
   **Synonyms**: compliant, accommodating, cooperative, acquiescent, agreeable
   
   **Antonyms**: resistant, oppositional, stubborn, independent-minded
   
   **When to Use**: Describing someone who readily agrees; can be positive (cooperative) or slightly negative (too passive)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply lack of independent thinking; use carefully to avoid negative implications
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go-along attitude
   - go-along type
   - too go-along
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (proceed) + along (in agreement) → adjectival conversion
   
   **Etymology**: From phrasal verb "go along with" → descriptive adjective (mid-20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's too go-along—he never stands up for himself.
   - We need someone with a go-along attitude for this team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization sought individuals with a go-along disposition for collaborative projects.
   - Excessive go-along behavior may indicate lack of critical engagement.

1. **Adjective**: longside / alongside (positional adjective - note: "alongside" is separate word)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Positioned at the side of; parallel to; beside
   - Note: "alongside" is the standard form, not "along" + "side" as separate words
   - (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: beside, next to, parallel to, adjacent to, side by side
   
   **Antonyms**: opposite, across from, perpendicular to
   
   **When to Use**: Describing position or arrangement parallel to something; spatial relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "beside" which doesn't emphasize length/parallel nature
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - alongside position
   - moored alongside
   - parked alongside
   
   **Root Analysis**: along (following length) + side (lateral position)
   
   **Etymology**: "Along" + "side" → fused into single word "alongside" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The boat was tied up alongside the dock.
   - Park your car alongside mine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is positioned alongside the primary transportation corridor.
   - New infrastructure will be constructed alongside existing systems.

1. **Adjective**: ongoing (derived from "go along" concept)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Continuing to exist or develop; still in progress; not yet concluded
   - Note: "ongoing" is related conceptually to "going along" but is independent word
   - (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: continuing, current, in progress, active, developing
   
   **Antonyms**: completed, finished, concluded, terminated
   
   **When to Use**: Describing processes, situations, or activities that are currently in progress
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for things that have clear end points that have passed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ongoing process
   - ongoing investigation
   - ongoing concern
   - ongoing basis
   
   **Root Analysis**: on (in state of) + going (continuing/proceeding)
   
   **Etymology**: On + going (present participle) → fused adjective meaning "continuing" (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's an ongoing problem we're dealing with.
   - The renovation is still ongoing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The matter remains under ongoing investigation.
   - Ongoing monitoring is essential to ensure compliance.

1. **Adjective**: forthcoming (conceptually related to "come along")
   **Meaning**: 
   - About to happen or appear; approaching; upcoming
   - Willing to give information; cooperative in providing details
   - (non-gradable for temporal sense; gradable for personality sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: upcoming (temporal), approaching, cooperative (personality), open, communicative
   
   **Antonyms**: past (temporal), uncommunicative (personality), secretive, reticent
   
   **When to Use**: For future events, publications, or describing someone willing to share information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse temporal and personality senses; context determines meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - forthcoming event
   - forthcoming publication
   - not very forthcoming
   - forthcoming with information
   
   **Root Analysis**: forth (forward) + coming (approaching/arriving)
   
   **Etymology**: Forth + coming → compound adjective (15th century) → dual senses developed
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check out my forthcoming book!
   - He wasn't very forthcoming about what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Details will be available in the forthcoming report.
   - Witnesses were not forthcoming with information regarding the incident.

1. **Adjective**: accompanying (participial adjective related to "along" concept)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Going along with; provided together with something else; attendant
   - (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: attendant, associated, concurrent, supplementary, related
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, independent, unrelated, isolated
   
   **When to Use**: Describing things that come together with main items; supplementary materials or features
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for mere proximity without relationship; requires association
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accompanying documentation
   - accompanying materials
   - accompanying symptoms
   - accompanying person
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to/toward) + company (companionship) + -ing (participial)
   
   **Etymology**: From "accompany" (French accompagner) → participial adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Fill out the form and send the accompanying documents.
   - Who's your accompanying guest?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please review the accompanying documentation before the meeting.
   - Accompanying symptoms include fatigue and nausea.

1. **Adjective**: parallel (conceptually related to "along")
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of lines, planes, or surfaces) Side by side and having the same distance continuously between them
   - Similar and taking place at the same time; corresponding
   - (non-gradable for geometric sense; somewhat gradable for similarity sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: alongside (spatial), concurrent, simultaneous, corresponding, analogous
   
   **Antonyms**: perpendicular, intersecting, divergent, contrasting
   
   **When to Use**: Geometric relationships; simultaneous processes; comparable situations or developments
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when items intersect or converge; requires maintained distance/similarity
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - parallel lines
   - parallel development
   - parallel process
   - run parallel to
   
   **Root Analysis**: para- (beside) + allel (one another)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin parallelus → Greek parallēlos (para "beside" + allēlōn "one another")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These two streets run parallel to each other.
   - We had parallel experiences growing up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research identified parallel trends in both populations.
   - Parallel processing improves computational efficiency.

1. **Adjective**: concurrent (related to "going along together")
   **Meaning**: 
   - Existing, happening, or done at the same time; simultaneous
   - In agreement; harmonious (less common use)
   - (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: simultaneous, coexisting, contemporaneous, coinciding, synchronous
   
   **Antonyms**: sequential, separate, consecutive, successive
   
   **When to Use**: Describing events, processes, or states that occur simultaneously; technical and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for simple proximity without temporal overlap; requires simultaneity
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - concurrent sessions
   - concurrent processing
   - concurrent jurisdiction
   - run concurrent with
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + current (running/flowing)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin concurrentem (con- "together" + currere "to run") → simultaneous occurrence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm taking three concurrent classes this semester.
   - The two events are concurrent.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Concurrent sessions will address various specialized topics.
   - The sentences will run concurrent rather than consecutive.

1. **Adjective**: cooperative (related to "going along" concept)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Willing to work with others; helpful and accommodating; involving cooperation
   - Done by people working together; involving mutual assistance
   - (gradable: more cooperative, most cooperative)
   
   **Synonyms**: helpful, accommodating, collaborative, willing, supportive
   
   **Antonyms**: uncooperative, obstructive, resistant, antagonistic
   
   **When to Use**: Describing helpful attitudes, collaborative behaviors, or joint efforts; positive connotation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound patronizing if used to describe expected professional behavior
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cooperative attitude
   - cooperative effort
   - cooperative behavior
   - very cooperative
   
   **Root Analysis**: co- (together) + operate (work) + -ive (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin cooperari (co- "together" + operari "to work") → mutually helpful
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for being so cooperative!
   - The kids were really cooperative today.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The witness was cooperative throughout the investigation.
   - A cooperative approach will facilitate successful implementation.
