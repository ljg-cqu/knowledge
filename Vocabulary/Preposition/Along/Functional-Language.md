1. **Functional Expression**: Why don't you come along? / Would you like to come along? (function: inviting/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Invitation to accompany the speaker; suggesting someone join an activity
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite invitation)
   
   **Synonyms**: Would you like to join us?, Want to come with us?, Care to join?, How about coming with us?
   
   **Antonyms**: Please don't come, You can't come, Stay here
   
   **When to Use**: Inviting friends, colleagues, or acquaintances to join activities; casual to semi-formal settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal events may require more formal invitation language; avoid if invitation isn't genuinely meant
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Why not come along? (slightly more persuasive)
   - Feel free to come along (permission-granting)
   - You're welcome to come along (inclusive invitation)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Come along" softens invitation by emphasizing accompaniment rather than focusing on person; reduces face threat
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: I'd love to! / Sure, sounds great! / Thanks, I will!
   - *Declining*: I appreciate the offer, but... / Thanks, but I can't / Maybe another time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're going to the beach tomorrow—why don't you come along?
   - A: Would you like to come along to dinner? B: Sure, I'd love to!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - You're welcome to come along to the conference if your schedule permits.
   - Colleagues are invited to come along to the site visit next week.

1. **Functional Expression**: Just go along with it / Play along (function: requesting cooperation/compliance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request to cooperate temporarily or pretend to agree; asking someone to accept situation without resistance
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: somewhat direct but softened by "just"
   
   **Synonyms**: Just accept it, Go with it, Don't argue, Cooperate for now, Bear with it
   
   **Antonyms**: Resist, Challenge it, Speak up, Object
   
   **When to Use**: Requesting temporary compliance, asking someone to cooperate in situation that may not be ideal; among equals or with lower power distance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can seem manipulative; don't use when genuine concerns should be addressed; inappropriate with authority figures
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you just go along with this? (more polite)
   - Please play along for now (time-limited request)
   - Trust me—just go along with it (appeal to relationship)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often used when speaker has plan they can't fully explain; implies temporary compliance with future explanation; can minimize concerns
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: Alright, if you say so / Okay, I trust you / Fine, I'll play along
   - *Declining*: I need to understand first / I can't do that / Tell me what's going on
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just play along—I'll explain later, I promise!
   - A: This seems weird... B: I know, but just go along with it for now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The team agreed to go along with the interim procedures pending review.
   - We request that participants play along with the simulation scenario.

1. **Functional Expression**: How's [it] coming along? (function: asking about progress)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Inquiry about the progress or development of something; asking for status update
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect but clear
   
   **Synonyms**: How's it going?, What's the progress?, How are things progressing?, How's it developing?
   
   **Antonyms**: (No direct antonyms, but contrast with not asking about progress)
   
   **When to Use**: Checking progress on projects, tasks, recovery, or any ongoing development; friendly and non-threatening
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound too casual for very formal progress reports; may seem minimizing if serious problems exist
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How's the project coming along? (specific)
   - Is it coming along well? (yes/no question)
   - How are things coming along? (broader scope)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Softer than direct "What's the status?"; implies expectation of positive progress; shows friendly interest
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Positive*: It's coming along great! / Really well, thanks! / Pretty smoothly
   - *Negative*: It's been challenging / Not as well as hoped / We've hit some snags
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: How's your thesis coming along? B: Slowly but surely!
   - Hey, how's that new project coming along?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you provide an update on how the implementation is coming along?
   - The board inquired about how negotiations are coming along.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'll bring [someone/something] along (function: announcing accompaniment/addition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Statement of intention to bring person or thing as accompaniment; informing about addition
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct statement
   
   **Synonyms**: I'll bring [X] with me, I'll take [X] along, [X] will come too, I'll include [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll leave [X] behind, I'll come alone, [X] won't be coming
   
   **When to Use**: Informing about accompanied persons/items; seeking implicit permission or giving notice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Some contexts require explicit permission rather than announcement; gauge appropriateness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Can I bring [X] along? (asking permission)
   - I'm planning to bring [X] along (softer announcement)
   - I'll bring [X] along if that's okay (permission-seeking)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Statement form can be announcement or implicit permission request depending on context and intonation; power dynamics affect interpretation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: Sure, no problem / That's fine / Great!
   - *Declining*: Actually, this is just for... / Could we keep it to just us? / Maybe another time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll bring my guitar along—we can jam!
   - A: I'll bring my sister along. B: Sounds great!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'll bring along the relevant documentation for your review.
   - Team members may bring along one colleague to observe.

1. **Functional Expression**: We'll figure it out as we go along (function: expressing flexible/adaptive approach)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Statement that planning will be incremental rather than complete upfront; expressing adaptive, learning-by-doing approach
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: We'll work it out as we proceed, We'll adapt along the way, We'll handle it progressively, We'll learn as we go
   
   **Antonyms**: We need to plan everything first, We must have it all figured out, We can't proceed without a complete plan
   
   **When to Use**: Situations requiring flexibility; when complete planning isn't possible; encouraging adaptive mindset
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound unprepared or irresponsible in contexts requiring thorough planning; avoid with stakeholders expecting detailed plans
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We can figure it out as we go along (softer, capability statement)
   - Let's figure it out as we go along (suggestion)
   - We've been figuring it out as we go along (report of past strategy)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Can be reassuring (flexibility) or concerning (lack of preparation) depending on context and audience; frame as intentional agile strategy when appropriate
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: Sounds good / That works / I'm comfortable with that
   - *Declining*: I'd prefer to have a clearer plan / Can we plan more upfront? / That makes me nervous
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't worry about every detail—we'll figure it out as we go along.
   - A: What's the plan? B: We'll figure it out as we go along!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology allows for adjustments; we'll refine our approach as we go along.
   - Given the evolving situation, we'll adapt our strategy as we go along.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's move along to [topic/location] (function: transitioning/directing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Directive to transition to next topic or location; managing progression of meeting, discussion, or movement
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct but polite
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's proceed to, Let's move on to, Shall we continue to, Let's transition to
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's stay on this topic, Let's remain here, We're not ready to move on
   
   **When to Use**: Meeting facilitation, tour guiding, classroom management; when speaker has authority to direct progression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Requires facilitator authority; participants without authority should suggest rather than direct
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Should we move along to [X]? (less directive, seeking consensus)
   - Let's keep moving along (general progression without specific destination)
   - Time to move along to [X] (emphasizing timing)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Move along" softer than "move on"; implies natural progression rather than dismissal; appropriate for managing time
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: (Usually no verbal response, just compliance)
   - *Declining*: Could we spend a bit more time on this? / I have one more question / Can we circle back later?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Okay, let's move along to the next item on our list.
   - Let's move along—we've got a lot to cover!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let's move along to item three on today's agenda.
   - We should move along to the next phase of the implementation.

1. **Functional Expression**: Do you get along with [person]? (function: asking about relationship quality)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Inquiry about the quality of relationship or compatibility between people
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: fairly direct but socially acceptable
   
   **Synonyms**: Do you have a good relationship with?, Are you compatible with?, How are things with?, Do you work well with?
   
   **Antonyms**: Do you have problems with?, Are you in conflict with?
   
   **When to Use**: Checking compatibility for team assignments, living situations, or social concern; appropriate among friends or in professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can seem intrusive in very formal settings or with strangers; sensitive in conflict situations
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How do you get along with [X]? (softer, open-ended)
   - Do you two get along? (about relationship between two others)
   - Are you getting along okay with [X]? (shows concern)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Question implies speaker has reason to ask (assignment decision, concern about conflict); acceptable in professional contexts for team formation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Positive*: Yes, we get along great / Really well / No problems at all
   - *Negative*: We have our differences / Not really / It's complicated / We're working on it
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: Do you get along with your new roommate? B: Yeah, she's great!
   - So, how do you get along with your boss?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - For team assignments, we need to know: do you get along well with colleague X?
   - It's important that team members get along effectively.

1. **Functional Expression**: Can I tag along? (function: requesting permission to accompany)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request to accompany others, often implying speaker wasn't originally included; seeking permission to join
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite request)
   
   **Synonyms**: Can I come too?, Can I join you?, Mind if I come along?, Could I come with you?
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll stay here, Count me out, I won't join you
   
   **When to Use**: Casual settings, with friends or friendly colleagues; when comfortable with possible rejection; often self-deprecating
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for professional or formal contexts; can seem presumptuous; not appropriate for serious or private occasions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Mind if I tag along? (more polite, permission-focused)
   - Could I tag along? (slightly more formal)
   - Is it okay if I tag along? (explicit permission request)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Tag along" acknowledges speaker wasn't originally invited; self-deprecating tone minimizes imposition; requires close enough relationship to make request
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: Sure! / Of course! / The more the merrier!
   - *Declining*: Actually, this is kind of a private thing / Maybe next time / It's just going to be the two of us
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You guys are going to the mall? Can I tag along?
   - A: Can I tag along to the movies? B: Sure, why not!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Too informal for formal contexts; use "May I join you?" or "Would it be possible to attend?"*

1. **Functional Expression**: I knew all along that [statement] (function: expressing vindication/prior knowledge)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Claiming to have known or suspected something throughout; expressing vindication or "I told you so"
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct, potentially confrontational
   
   **Synonyms**: I always knew that, I suspected all along that, I knew from the start that, I was right that
   
   **Antonyms**: I never suspected, I just found out, This is news to me, I was wrong
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing vindication when prediction proves true; sharing that information was known throughout; can be self-satisfied
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound like "I told you so"; may damage relationships; use carefully to avoid seeming smug or unhelpful
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I suspected all along that [X] (softer, acknowledges uncertainty)
   - We all knew all along (group knowledge, less personal)
   - Didn't I tell you? I knew all along! (more confrontational)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often carries implication of vindication or criticism; can be face-threatening to others who didn't know; tone and relationship context critical
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: You were right / You did call it / I should have listened
   - *Defensive*: Well, it wasn't obvious / Easy to say now / Why didn't you say something?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I knew all along that he'd change his mind!
   - A: You were right! B: I knew all along it would work out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team suspected all along that this variable was significant.
   - Management was aware all along of these operational inefficiencies.

1. **Functional Expression**: Bring [X] along with you (function: instructing/requesting to accompany with item)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Instruction or request to bring specific item or person when coming; ensuring necessary accompaniment
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct instruction or polite request depending on modal
   
   **Synonyms**: Bring [X] with you, Don't forget [X], Make sure to bring [X], Remember to bring [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: Leave [X] behind, Don't bring [X], Come alone
   
   **When to Use**: Giving instructions, reminders, or requests about what to bring; appropriate across contexts from casual to professional
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Commanding tone can sound bossy; use "please" and "could you" for politeness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Please bring [X] along (more polite)
   - Could you bring [X] along? (request rather than instruction)
   - Don't forget to bring [X] along (reminder)
   - You'll need to bring [X] along (informative requirement)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Directness depends on speaker authority and relationship; modal verbs soften directives; stating reason increases compliance
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - *Accepting*: Will do / Got it / I'll make sure to bring it
   - *Questioning*: Do I need to bring anything else? / What specifically should I bring?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Bring your swim suit along with you—we might go to the pool.
   - Remember to bring your ID along!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please bring along any supporting documentation to the meeting.
   - Participants should bring along valid identification.
