1. **Verb**: come along
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive) To accompany someone; to go somewhere with another person
   - (intransitive) To make progress; to develop or improve
   - (intransitive) To arrive or appear, especially by chance or opportunity
   
   **Synonyms**: accompany (sense 1), progress (sense 2), develop (sense 2), arrive (sense 3), appear (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: stay behind, fall behind, regress, depart
   
   **When to Use**: Invitations, progress reports, describing unexpected arrivals; versatile across contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple senses require context clarity; specify meaning if ambiguous
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - come along with us
   - coming along nicely
   - when opportunity comes along
   - come along to the party
   
   **Root Analysis**: come (move toward) + along (accompanying/progressing)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English cuman + along → phrasal verb with multiple senses (movement, progress, arrival)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Want to come along to the movies?
   - How's your project coming along?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants are invited to come along to the symposium.
   - Research has been coming along according to projected timelines.

1. **Verb**: get along
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive) To have a harmonious relationship with someone; to be compatible
   - (intransitive) To manage or cope with a situation; to make do
   - (intransitive) To leave; to depart (less common)
   
   **Synonyms**: be compatible (sense 1), manage (sense 2), cope (sense 2), leave (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: clash, conflict, struggle, fail to cope
   
   **When to Use**: Describing relationships, ability to cope, or (rarely) departure; very common in everyday speech
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Get along with" specifically for relationships; "get along without" for managing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get along well
   - get along with someone
   - get along without
   - can't get along
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (achieve state) + along (harmony/progression)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English geten + along → relationship harmony and coping ability (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you get along with your boss?
   - We get along fine without cable TV.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Team members must get along effectively to ensure productivity.
   - The organization has learned to get along with reduced funding.

1. **Verb**: go along
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive) To agree with or accept something; to comply
   - (intransitive) To accompany someone; to attend
   - (intransitive) To proceed or continue (with an activity)
   
   **Synonyms**: agree (sense 1), comply (sense 1), accompany (sense 2), proceed (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: disagree, refuse, resist, oppose
   
   **When to Use**: "Go along with" for agreement; "go along to" for attendance; versatile expression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply passive acceptance in sense 1; choose "support" for active agreement
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go along with the plan
   - go along with you
   - go along fine
   - as we go along
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (proceed) + along (in company/agreement)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English gan + along → agreement and accompaniment (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll just go along with whatever you decide.
   - Can I go along with you?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee agreed to go along with the proposed amendments.
   - Stakeholders were willing to go along with the restructuring plan.

1. **Verb**: move along
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive) To continue moving forward; to proceed without stopping
   - (transitive) To cause to proceed or advance
   - (intransitive/imperative) To disperse; to leave an area (often as directive)

   **Antonyms**: stop, halt, stay, remain, linger
   
   **When to Use**: Literal movement, figurative progress, or authority directive to disperse

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move along now
   - keep moving along
   - move things along
   - Move along! (imperative)
   
   **Root Analysis**: move (change position) + along (forward)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French movoir + along → continued forward motion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's move along—we're late.
   - Nothing to see here, move along!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project continues to move along according to schedule.
   - Officers instructed crowds to move along in an orderly fashion.

1. **Verb**: bring along

   **Synonyms**: bring with, take with, bring with you, develop (sense 2), mentor (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: leave behind, abandon, neglect

   - bring along your documents
   - bringing along junior staff
   - bring along nicely
   
   **Root Analysis**: bring (transport/carry) + along (in company)

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Feel free to bring along your kids.
   - I'll bring along some drinks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants may bring along one guest to the conference.
   - Senior staff help bring along junior employees through mentorship programs.

1. **Verb**: play along

   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound manipulative in sense 1; clarify intentions to avoid misunderstanding

   - play along with the music
   - willing to play along
   
   **Root Analysis**: play (perform/pretend) + along (cooperating)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English plegan + along → musical accompaniment → pretend cooperation (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just play along—I'll explain later.
   - She played along with his silly game.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The undercover agent played along to maintain credibility.
   - Investors chose to play along during the management transition.

1. **Verb**: tag along
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive) To accompany someone, especially casually or when not originally invited
   
   **Synonyms**: follow, accompany casually, come along, trail along
   
   **Antonyms**: lead, stay behind, go separately
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts for casual, often uninvited accompaniment; can be self-deprecating
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for professional contexts; can imply being unwelcome
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tag along with them
   - tag along behind
   - let someone tag along
   - mind if I tag along
   
   **Root Analysis**: tag (follow from game) + along (accompanying)
   
   **Etymology**: From tag (children's game) + along → casual following (early 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I tag along with you guys?
   - My little brother always wants to tag along.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; rarely appropriate in formal contexts*

1. **Verb**: string along
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To deceive someone by maintaining false hope or expectations; to mislead deliberately over time
   
   **Synonyms**: deceive, mislead, lead on, give false hope, manipulate
   
   **Antonyms**: be honest with, be straightforward, tell the truth
   
   **When to Use**: Describing deliberate deception in relationships or business; strongly negative connotation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very negative; don't use lightly; implies intentional wrongdoing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - string someone along
   - being strung along
   - stop stringing me along
   - stringing people along
   
   **Root Analysis**: string (control like puppet) + along (through time)
   
   **Etymology**: From "string" (puppet manipulation) + along → maintaining deception (early 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's been stringing her along with false promises.
   - Don't string me along—just tell me the truth!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company was accused of stringing along investors with misleading projections.
   - Management allegedly strung along employees regarding promotion opportunities.

1. **Verb**: run along
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive/imperative) To go away; to leave (often dismissive, especially to children)
   - (intransitive) To proceed or continue smoothly
   
   **Synonyms**: go away (sense 1), leave (sense 1), proceed (sense 2), continue (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: stay, come here, remain, stop
   
   **When to Use**: Gentle dismissal of children; can be condescending to adults; neutral for smooth proceedings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sounds patronizing to adults; use carefully in professional settings
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - run along now
   - run along home
   - things run along smoothly
   - best run along
   
   **Root Analysis**: run (move quickly) + along (away/forward)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse renna + along → quick departure or smooth continuation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Run along now, kids, I need to work.
   - You'd better run along before it gets dark.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal or condescending toward adults*
   - Operations continued to run along smoothly.

   **Antonyms**: leave behind, drop, abandon, resist

   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple senses require context; clarify whether literal or figurative
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - carry along supplies
   - swept along by enthusiasm
   - carried along by the current
   - sweep people along
   
   **Root Analysis**: carry/sweep (transport/move) + along (forward/with)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin carricare / Old English swapan + along → transport and emotional engagement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The river carried along all sorts of debris.
   - I got swept along by their excitement.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The current carried along sediment deposits.
   - Participants were swept along by the compelling narrative.
