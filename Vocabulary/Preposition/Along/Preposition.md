1. **Preposition**: along (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving in a constant direction on or beside a path, road, or line; following the length or course of something
   - *(Spatial)* At a point on a line, path, or edge; somewhere on the length of something
   - *(Temporal)* During the course of; at some point in the progress of something
   
   **Synonyms**: beside (spatial), following (direction), down (path), through (course)
   
   **Antonyms**: across (perpendicular), against (opposite direction)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "along" to indicate movement or position parallel to a line, path, or edge; common for describing routes, boundaries, and progressive movement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when movement is perpendicular (use "across"); avoid when direct contact with surface is emphasized (prefer "on")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: along the way, along the road, along the coast, along the line
   - **Verb + prep**: walk along, drive along, run along, move along
   - **Adjective + prep**: located along, situated along
   - **Noun + prep**: path along, houses along, trees along
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + long (extending in length)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English along → Old English andlang (and- "against" + lang "long") → movement following something's length
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We walked along the beach at sunset.
   - There's a café somewhere along this street.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The infrastructure extends along the entire coastal region.
   - Archaeological sites are distributed along the ancient trade route.

1. **Preposition**: all along (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the entire time; from the beginning; continuously during a period
   - *(Abstract)* From the very start (often with implications of prior knowledge or expectation)
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, all the time, from the start, from the beginning, the whole time
   
   **Antonyms**: only recently, just now, at the end
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizes duration from beginning to end; often used to reveal that something was true the entire time; can imply hindsight or realization
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for spatial descriptions; exclusively temporal or abstract
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: all along the way, knew all along, there all along
   - **Sentence position**: typically mid-sentence or end position
   - **Verb + prep**: knew all along, suspected all along, understood all along
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (entire, complete) + along (through the extent)
   
   **Etymology**: Temporal extension from spatial meaning → continuous duration (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I knew all along you'd change your mind!
   - The keys were in my pocket all along.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hypothesis has been supported by evidence all along.
   - Management was aware of the challenges all along.

1. **Preposition**: along with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Together with; in addition to; accompanied by; as well as
   
   **Synonyms**: together with, in addition to, as well as, alongside, in company with
   
   **Antonyms**: without, excluding, apart from
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates accompaniment or additional items/people; more formal than "with"; emphasizes items being grouped together
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound more formal than simple "with" in casual contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: along with others, along with these factors, along with the team
   - **List addition**: introduces additional elements in enumeration
   - **Verb + prep**: comes along with, brought along with, included along with
   
   **Root Analysis**: along (in company) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Etymology**: Extension from spatial sense → abstract accompaniment (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going along with Sarah and Tom.
   - Can I bring my friend along with me?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal, along with supporting documentation, was submitted yesterday.
   - Economic factors, along with social pressures, influenced the decision.

1. **Preposition**: come along (type: phrasal verb + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Movement)* To accompany someone; to go somewhere with someone
   - *(Progress)* To make progress; to develop or improve
   - *(Arrival)* To arrive or appear, especially by chance
   
   **Synonyms**: accompany (movement), progress (development), arrive (appearance), show up
   
   **Antonyms**: stay behind, regress (progress), depart
   
   **When to Use**: Versatile expression for accompaniment, progress, or arrival; can be invitation or description of development
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent meaning requires clear context to avoid ambiguity
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: come along with us, come along nicely, when opportunity comes along
   - **Verb patterns**: come along to, come along for, come along well
   - **Imperative**: Come along! (invitation or urging)
   
   **Root Analysis**: come (movement toward) + along (accompanying or progressing)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb combining come + along → multiple senses from spatial to abstract
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why don't you come along to the party?
   - How's your project coming along?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants are invited to come along to the symposium.
   - The rehabilitation program is coming along as expected.

1. **Preposition**: get along (type: phrasal verb + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Relationship)* To have a harmonious relationship with someone; to be compatible
   - *(Progress)* To manage or cope in a situation; to make progress
   - *(Departure)* To leave; to go away
   
   **Synonyms**: be compatible (relationship), manage (cope), cope (survival), leave (departure)
   
   **Antonyms**: conflict with, clash with, struggle, stay
   
   **When to Use**: Common for describing interpersonal harmony or ability to cope; versatile in various contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple meanings require clear context; "get along with" typically for relationships
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: get along well, get along with someone, can't get along
   - **Verb patterns**: get along without, get along fine, get along together
   - **Question form**: How do you get along?
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (achieve state) + along (in harmony/progression)
   
   **Etymology**: From literal movement → abstract harmony and coping (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you get along with your roommate?
   - I get along fine without a car.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Team members must get along effectively to ensure productivity.
   - The organization has learned to get along with limited resources.

1. **Preposition**: move along (type: phrasal verb + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Movement)* To continue moving forward; to proceed without stopping
   - *(Progress)* To make progress; to advance in development
   - *(Directive)* Command to disperse or leave an area; to not linger
   
   **Synonyms**: proceed, advance, progress, continue, keep going
   
   **Antonyms**: stop, halt, stay, remain, linger
   
   **When to Use**: For literal movement or figurative progress; often used as directive by authority figures to disperse crowds
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound brusque or authoritative when used as command
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: move along now, move along quickly, move along the path
   - **Verb patterns**: keep moving along, moving along smoothly, moving along steadily
   - **Imperative**: Move along! (directive)
   
   **Root Analysis**: move (change position) + along (forward progression)
   
   **Etymology**: Physical movement → figurative progress + authoritative dispersal command
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's move along, we're running late.
   - The traffic finally started moving along.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project continues to move along according to schedule.
   - Officers instructed the crowd to move along peacefully.

1. **Preposition**: go along (type: phrasal verb + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Agreement)* To agree with or accept something; to comply or cooperate
   - *(Accompaniment)* To accompany someone; to attend
   - *(Progress)* To proceed or continue with an activity
   
   **Synonyms**: agree (compliance), accompany (attendance), accept, cooperate, proceed
   
   **Antonyms**: disagree, refuse, resist, stay behind
   
   **When to Use**: Common for expressing agreement, compliance, or accompaniment; "go along with" typically for agreement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply passive acceptance when you mean active agreement
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: go along with the plan, go along with you, go along fine
   - **Verb patterns**: go along with, just go along, willing to go along
   - **As + clause**: as we go along (during the process)
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (proceed) + along (in company/agreement)
   
   **Etymology**: Physical accompaniment → abstract agreement and compliance (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll just go along with whatever you decide.
   - Can I go along with you guys?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee agreed to go along with the proposed amendments.
   - Stakeholders were willing to go along with the restructuring plan.

1. **Preposition**: play along (type: phrasal verb + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Compliance)* To pretend to agree or cooperate, often temporarily or strategically
   - *(Musical)* To accompany musically; to perform music together with someone
   
   **Synonyms**: cooperate, humor (pretend), go along with, comply temporarily, accompany (musical)
   
   **Antonyms**: resist, refuse, oppose, challenge
   
   **When to Use**: Often implies temporary or strategic cooperation; can suggest insincerity or patience; musical context is literal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound manipulative or insincere if context isn't clear
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: play along with the joke, play along for now, play along with the music
   - **Verb patterns**: just play along, willing to play along, decided to play along
   
   **Root Analysis**: play (perform/pretend) + along (in cooperation)
   
   **Etymology**: Musical performance together → pretending to cooperate (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just play along, I'll explain later.
   - She played along with his silly game.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The undercover agent played along to maintain credibility.
   - Investors chose to play along with management during the transition period.

1. **Preposition**: bring along (type: phrasal verb + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Accompaniment)* To take someone or something with you to a place
   - *(Development)* To help someone develop or improve their skills
   
   **Synonyms**: bring with, take with, bring with you, develop (skills)
   
   **Antonyms**: leave behind, abandon, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: For physical accompaniment of people or things; also for mentoring and development contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "bring around" (persuade) or "bring about" (cause)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: bring along a friend, bring along your notes, bring along the team
   - **Verb patterns**: can bring along, should bring along, bringing along nicely
   
   **Root Analysis**: bring (transport) + along (in company)
   
   **Etymology**: Physical transportation → abstract development and mentoring
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Feel free to bring along your kids.
   - I'll bring along some snacks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants may bring along one guest to the conference.
   - Senior staff members help bring along junior employees through mentorship.

1. **Preposition**: tag along (type: phrasal verb + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Accompaniment)* To accompany someone, especially when not originally invited; to follow along casually
   
   **Synonyms**: follow, accompany, come along, trail along
   
   **Antonyms**: lead, stay behind, go separately
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts; often implies casual or uninvited accompaniment; can be self-deprecating
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for professional or formal contexts; can imply being unwelcome
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: tag along with them, tag along behind, let someone tag along
   - **Verb patterns**: can I tag along, mind if I tag along, always tags along
   
   **Root Analysis**: tag (attach/follow) + along (accompanying)
   
   **Etymology**: From children's game "tag" → casual following (early 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I tag along with you guys?
   - My little brother always wants to tag along.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; rarely used in formal contexts*
   - Observers were permitted to tag along during the field research.
