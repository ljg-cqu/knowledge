1. **Functional Expression**: I'm against [proposal] (function: expressing opposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express disagreement with or opposition to an idea, plan, or action
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I oppose [proposal], I don't support [proposal], I'm opposed to [proposal], I disagree with [proposal]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm for [proposal], I support [proposal], I agree with [proposal], I'm in favor of [proposal]
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing clear opposition in discussions, meetings, or debates; when you want to state your position directly
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In highly formal contexts (use "I object to" or "I oppose"); when trying to maintain diplomatic relations; in hierarchical cultures where direct opposition is considered rude
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm totally against this (emphatic, casual)
   - I'm very much against that (emphatic, slightly formal)
   - I have to say I'm against it (hedged, apologetic)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Direct opposition can threaten face in some cultures; consider softening with explanations ("I'm against this because...") or hedging ("I tend to be against...")
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Why are you against it?
   - Fair enough, what's your alternative?
   - Can you explain your concerns?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: Should we move the meeting to Monday? B: I'm against that—Monday's too busy.
   - I'm totally against working on weekends.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'm against implementing this policy without further consultation.
   - While I understand the rationale, I'm against proceeding at this time.

1. **Functional Expression**: What do you have against [something/someone]? (function: asking about opposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to ask why someone opposes or dislikes something or someone
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct (can sound confrontational)
   
   **Synonyms**: Why don't you like [X]?, What's your problem with [X]?, Why do you oppose [X]?
   
   **Antonyms**: What do you like about [X]?, What attracts you to [X]?
   
   **When to Use**: Informal conversations when you want to understand someone's opposition; can express surprise or challenge
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal or professional contexts; when relationship is tense (sounds accusatory); with superiors
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What have you got against it? (more casual)
   - What's your issue with [X]? (more confrontational)
   - Why are you opposed to [X]? (more neutral, formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often implies surprise or disagreement with the person's opposition; tone of voice is crucial—can sound genuinely curious or confrontational
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Nothing personal, I just think...
   - Well, for one thing...
   - It's not that I have something against it, it's just...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: I don't want to go to that restaurant. B: What do you have against it?
   - What have you got against trying something new?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Rarely used formally; prefer: "Could you explain your concerns about this proposal?")

1. **Functional Expression**: I have nothing against [something/someone] (function: denying opposition/clarifying)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to clarify that you don't oppose or dislike something/someone before presenting a different concern
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (often precedes disagreement)
   
   **Synonyms**: I don't oppose [X], I have no problem with [X], I'm not against [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I have something against [X], I do oppose [X], I'm against [X]
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to soften disagreement by first acknowledging you don't oppose the main thing; face-saving before introducing concerns
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you actually do oppose something (sounds dishonest); in very direct cultures where this sounds evasive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I've got nothing against [X] (more casual)
   - It's not that I have anything against [X], but... (hedged version)
   - I have no issue with [X] per se (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Classic hedging device; "nothing against X, but..." often signals upcoming criticism or concern; helps maintain positive face
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - But...? (anticipating the contrast)
   - So what's the issue then?
   - I hear a 'but' coming...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have nothing against John, but I think we need someone with more experience.
   - I've got nothing against the idea—I just think the timing is bad.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I have nothing against the proposed methodology per se, but I question its applicability here.
   - While I have no objection to the concept, implementation concerns remain.

1. **Functional Expression**: To argue/speak against [something] (function: expressing opposition publicly)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To present arguments or speak in opposition to an idea, proposal, or position
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct but structured
   
   **Synonyms**: To oppose, to contest, to challenge, to object to, to take a stand against
   
   **Antonyms**: To argue for, to speak in favor of, to support, to advocate for
   
   **When to Use**: Formal debates, meetings, or discussions where structured argumentation is expected; professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations (sounds too formal); when gentle disagreement is more appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - To speak out against (more emphatic, implies moral dimension)
   - To make a case against (focus on structured argumentation)
   - To voice opposition to (emphasizes verbal expression)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies organized, rational opposition rather than emotional reaction; suggests preparation and structured thinking
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I'd like to hear your arguments
   - On what grounds?
   - Let's hear the case for and against
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Less common casually; more natural: "I'm going to tell them why I disagree")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several board members argued against the merger proposal.
   - I must speak against this motion on procedural grounds.

1. **Functional Expression**: To be up against [obstacle/deadline] (function: expressing difficulty/challenge)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To face or confront a difficult situation, obstacle, or time pressure
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To face, to confront, to be dealing with, to be facing
   
   **Antonyms**: To have plenty of time, to have it easy, to face no obstacles
   
   **When to Use**: Describing challenging situations, tight deadlines, or difficult competition; conveys urgency or difficulty
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing easy situations; in contexts where showing stress is inappropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Really up against it (emphatic, very challenging)
   - Up against a tight deadline (specific time pressure)
   - Up against tough competition (competitive context)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often used to explain limitations or seek understanding for constraints; can be used to request help or extensions
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What can I do to help?
   - How much time do you need?
   - Can we adjust the deadline?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're really up against it this week with the deadline.
   - I'm up against some tough competition for that job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The team is up against significant technical challenges.
   - We find ourselves up against unprecedented market conditions.

1. **Functional Expression**: To go against [advice/rules/norm] (function: refusing/defying)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To act contrary to advice, rules, expectations, or norms; to defy or disobey
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To defy, to disobey, to contradict, to violate, to ignore
   
   **Antonyms**: To follow, to comply with, to obey, to adhere to, to respect
   
   **When to Use**: Describing actions that contradict expectations or rules; can be neutral or negative depending on context
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to emphasize deliberate rebellion (use "defy"); in legal contexts requiring precision
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Go against someone's wishes (personal desires)
   - Go against the grain (idiomatic: contrary to norm)
   - Go against better judgment (acknowledge poor decision)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Can imply criticism when describing others; self-deprecating when admitting one's own poor choice
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Why would you do that?
   - That was risky
   - You should have listened
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He went against his parents' advice and quit his job.
   - I went against my better judgment and tried it anyway.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The decision goes against established guidelines.
   - This action went against explicit board directives.

1. **Functional Expression**: To hold/have [something] against [someone] (function: bearing grudge/maintaining resentment)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To remain resentful or maintain negative feelings toward someone because of past actions
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To resent someone for, to bear a grudge about, to blame someone for
   
   **Antonyms**: To forgive, to let go, to move past, to not blame
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing forgiveness, resentment, or past conflicts; often used in negative ("I don't hold it against you")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal professional contexts; when discussing serious grievances requiring formal language
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I don't hold it against you (forgiveness)
   - Still holding that against me? (questioning continued resentment)
   - Don't hold it against him (requesting forgiveness for someone)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often used negatively to signal forgiveness or positively in questions about ongoing resentment
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I hope you don't hold it against me
   - Of course not, it's in the past
   - Should I? (challenging)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I made a mistake, but I hope you don't hold it against me.
   - She still holds that argument against him years later.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Less common formally; prefer: "I trust this error won't impact future relations")

1. **Functional Expression**: To guard/protect against [risk/threat] (function: expressing prevention/protection)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To take measures to prevent or defend against potential harm, risk, or negative outcomes
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To prevent, to defend against, to shield from, to safeguard against
   
   **Antonyms**: To expose to, to leave vulnerable to, to risk
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing safety measures, risk management, preventive strategies; professional and technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing active combat (use "defend"); in casual contexts (sounds formal)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Protect yourself against (personal safety)
   - Guard against the possibility (preventive thinking)
   - Build in protection against (systematic safeguards)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Emphasizes proactive prevention rather than reactive response; implies foresight and planning
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What measures are in place?
   - How do we protect against that?
   - Good thinking—better safe than sorry
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You should protect against identity theft online.
   - We need to guard against getting too comfortable.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Systems must guard against unauthorized access.
   - Policies protect against conflicts of interest.

1. **Functional Expression**: To work against [goal/interest] (function: describing counterproductive action)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have an effect that prevents or hinders achievement of goals or interests; to be counterproductive
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (often used to point out problems diplomatically)
   
   **Synonyms**: To undermine, to hinder, to impede, to be counterproductive to, to sabotage
   
   **Antonyms**: To work toward, to support, to advance, to promote, to facilitate
   
   **When to Use**: Pointing out counterproductive actions or effects; giving feedback about conflicting objectives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When intentional sabotage is meant (too soft); when you want to emphasize deliberate obstruction
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Working against your own interests (self-sabotage)
   - That works against what we're trying to do (counterproductive)
   - Working at cross-purposes (mutual interference)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Softer than "sabotage" or "undermine"; allows for unintentional harm; face-saving when giving criticism
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I didn't realize that
   - How should we adjust?
   - What would work better?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Staying up late works against your goal of being more energetic.
   - You're working against yourself with that attitude.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These policies work against stated organizational objectives.
   - The current approach works against our strategic priorities.

1. **Functional Expression**: To set [one thing] against [another] (function: comparing/contrasting/creating conflict)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To compare or contrast two things; to create opposition or conflict between parties
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To compare with, to contrast with, to pit against, to oppose to
   
   **Antonyms**: To unite, to combine, to reconcile, to harmonize
   
   **When to Use**: Analytical discussions comparing options; describing creation of conflict; weighing alternatives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple comparison is meant without oppositional framing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Set one option against another (comparison)
   - Set them against each other (creating conflict)
   - When set against the alternatives (comparison context)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Can imply deliberate manipulation when describing social dynamics; neutral in analytical contexts
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - How do they compare?
   - Why create division?
   - Let's evaluate both fairly
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't try to set us against each other.
   - Set against the other options, this looks pretty good.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When costs are set against benefits, the proposal appears viable.
   - Leadership must avoid setting departments against one another.
