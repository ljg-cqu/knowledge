1. **Noun**: opposition (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Resistance or dissent expressed in action or argument; the act of opposing or resisting
   - *(Sense 2)* A group of people who oppose and compete with those in power; political adversaries
   
   **Synonyms**: resistance, disagreement, objection, dissent, antagonism
   
   **Antonyms**: support, agreement, approval, cooperation, alliance
   
   **When to Use**: Political contexts, decision-making discussions, describing resistance or contrary positions; both individual disagreement and organized resistance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When neutral disagreement without active resistance is meant; context determines political vs. general meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strong opposition to
   - face opposition
   - in opposition to
   - the opposition party
   - voice opposition
   
   **Root Analysis**: op- (against) + posit (place) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin opponere (to set against) → oppositio → Old French → English "opposition" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a lot of opposition to the new rules.
   - The opposition is gaining support in the polls.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal encountered substantial opposition from stakeholders.
   - Opposition parties articulated concerns regarding policy implementation.

1. **Noun**: resistance (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The refusal to accept or comply with something; active opposition or unwillingness to change
   - *(Sense 2)* The ability to withstand or fight against something harmful or unwanted
   
   **Synonyms**: opposition, refusal, defiance, unwillingness, pushback
   
   **Antonyms**: acceptance, compliance, cooperation, submission, yielding
   
   **When to Use**: Describing opposition to change, policies, or force; medical/scientific contexts for immunity; can be passive or active opposition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When willing cooperation is discussed; context distinguishes psychological vs. physical resistance
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - meet with resistance
   - resistance to change
   - overcome resistance
   - passive resistance
   - build resistance
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back/against) + sist (stand) + -ance (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin resistere (to stand against) → French résistance → English "resistance" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's been some resistance to the new schedule.
   - You need to build up resistance to those germs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizational resistance impeded transformational initiatives.
   - The pathogen demonstrated resistance to conventional treatments.

1. **Noun**: conflict (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A serious disagreement or argument; a state of opposition between persons, groups, or principles
   - *(Sense 2)* Armed fighting or warfare between opposing forces
   
   **Synonyms**: dispute, disagreement, clash, confrontation, discord
   
   **Antonyms**: agreement, harmony, peace, accord, cooperation
   
   **When to Use**: Describing disagreements, competing interests, or active fighting; ranges from interpersonal disputes to armed warfare
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For minor disagreements (too strong); when cooperation is emphasized
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - conflict of interest
   - armed conflict
   - resolve conflict
   - in conflict with
   - conflict resolution
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + flict (strike) → striking together, collision
   
   **Etymology**: Latin confligere (to strike together, fight) → conflictus → English "conflict" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a conflict between what she says and what she does.
   - Try to avoid conflict at the family dinner.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conflict between organizational goals and individual objectives requires resolution.
   - International conflicts necessitate diplomatic intervention.

1. **Noun**: antagonism (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Active hostility or opposition; deep-seated dislike or enmity
   - *(Sense 2)* Biochemical opposition between substances, muscles, or organisms
   
   **Synonyms**: hostility, opposition, animosity, enmity, antipathy
   
   **Antonyms**: friendship, cooperation, harmony, sympathy, alliance
   
   **When to Use**: Describing intense opposition or hostility; scientific contexts for opposing forces or effects; emphasizes active, often emotional opposition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For mild disagreement; when neutrality is appropriate; sounds formal and intense
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deep antagonism
   - mutual antagonism
   - antagonism toward/towards
   - antagonism between
   - sense of antagonism
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + agon (struggle, contest) + -ism (state/condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek antagonisma (competition) → Late Latin antagonismus → English "antagonism" (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's real antagonism between those two.
   - I can sense the antagonism in the room.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Historical antagonism between the groups complicated reconciliation efforts.
   - Pharmacological antagonism reduces therapeutic efficacy.

1. **Noun**: adversary (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - One's opponent in a contest, conflict, or dispute; an enemy or rival
   - Someone or something that opposes or attacks; a hostile force
   
   **Synonyms**: opponent, enemy, rival, foe, antagonist
   
   **Antonyms**: ally, friend, supporter, partner, collaborator
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts describing opponents in competitions, legal disputes, or conflicts; more formal than "opponent"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds formal); friendly competition contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - formidable adversary
   - political adversary
   - face one's adversary
   - worthy adversary
   - business adversary
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to/toward) + vers (turn) + -ary (person who)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin adversarius (opponent, enemy) → Old French adversaire → English "adversary" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Less common casually; prefer "opponent" or "rival")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Legal teams prepared to face formidable adversaries in court.
   - Competitive intelligence helps organizations anticipate adversarial strategies.

1. **Noun**: objection (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An expression of disapproval or opposition; a reason for disagreeing or opposing something
   - *(Legal)* A formal protest raised in court proceedings
   
   **Synonyms**: protest, complaint, opposition, disagreement, reservation
   
   **Antonyms**: approval, agreement, consent, acceptance, endorsement
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts expressing disagreement or protest; legal proceedings; structured decision-making situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts; when acceptance rather than disagreement is discussed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - raise an objection
   - voice objection
   - objection to
   - have no objection
   - overrule the objection
   
   **Root Analysis**: ob- (against) + ject (throw) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin obiectio (throwing against, opposition) → Old French → English "objection" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you have any objections to that plan?
   - I'd like to raise an objection to the proposal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Counsel raised objections to the admissibility of evidence.
   - No substantive objections emerged during the consultation period.

1. **Noun**: defiance (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Bold resistance to authority or opposition; open disobedience or refusal to comply
   - The quality of being boldly challenging or unwilling to submit
   
   **Synonyms**: disobedience, rebellion, insubordination, resistance, boldness
   
   **Antonyms**: obedience, compliance, submission, cooperation, deference
   
   **When to Use**: Describing bold or open resistance to authority; emphasizes courage or stubbornness in opposition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For passive resistance; when describing cooperation; can sound admiring or critical depending on context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - act of defiance
   - in defiance of
   - show defiance
   - spirit of defiance
   - open defiance
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away/down) + fi (trust/faith) + -ance (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French defi (challenge, contempt) → defiance → English "defiance" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He quit in defiance of his parents' wishes.
   - Her actions were pure defiance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The demonstration represented open defiance of governmental authority.
   - Actions taken in defiance of established protocols resulted in sanctions.

1. **Noun**: rivalry (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Competition for the same objective or superiority in the same field; state of being rivals
   - Ongoing competition or opposition between individuals, groups, or organizations
   
   **Synonyms**: competition, contention, contest, opposition, competitiveness
   
   **Antonyms**: cooperation, partnership, alliance, collaboration, friendship
   
   **When to Use**: Describing ongoing competitive relationships; sports, business, or social competitions; can be friendly or hostile
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When cooperation is emphasized; single competitions (use "competition" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fierce rivalry
   - sibling rivalry
   - business rivalry
   - rivalry between/among
   - intense rivalry
   
   **Root Analysis**: rival (competitor) + -ry (state/condition of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin rivalis (using the same stream) → rival → rivalry (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's always been rivalry between those two teams.
   - Sibling rivalry is totally normal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Market rivalry intensified as new competitors entered the sector.
   - Historical rivalries between institutions shaped contemporary relationships.

1. **Noun**: contest (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A competition or struggle for victory, superiority, or a prize; an event in which people compete
   - *(Sense 2)* A dispute or controversy; an act of challenging or disputing something
   
   **Synonyms**: competition, battle, struggle, match, challenge
   
   **Antonyms**: cooperation, collaboration, agreement, harmony, alliance
   
   **When to Use**: Describing competitive events, struggles for dominance, or disputes; ranges from sports to legal challenges
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When cooperation rather than competition is meant; context determines sporting vs. adversarial meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - enter a contest
   - contest between
   - fierce contest
   - no contest (clear winner)
   - contest for power
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together/against) + test (witness, testimony) → testify against
   
   **Etymology**: Latin contestari (to call to witness, challenge) → French conteste → English "contest" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She entered the singing contest.
   - It's no contest—he's way better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The election represented a close contest between competing ideologies.
   - Legal contests over intellectual property rights are increasingly common.

1. **Noun**: counteraction (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Action taken to oppose or neutralize another action; the act of working against something to reduce its effect
   - Force or influence that acts in opposition to another force
   
   **Synonyms**: opposition, countermeasure, neutralization, offset, resistance
   
   **Antonyms**: reinforcement, support, amplification, assistance, cooperation
   
   **When to Use**: Technical, medical, or strategic contexts describing opposing forces or corrective actions; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too technical); when simpler terms like "response" or "opposition" suffice
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - counteraction to/against
   - require counteraction
   - effective counteraction
   - counteraction of effects
   - mutual counteraction
   
   **Root Analysis**: counter- (against/opposite) + action (doing, activity)
   
   **Etymology**: Counter (Latin contra = against) + action → "counteraction" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used casually; more natural: "response" or "opposing action")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Effective counteraction requires understanding of underlying mechanisms.
   - Pharmacological counteraction mitigated adverse effects.

1. **Noun**: dissent (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The expression or holding of opinions at variance with those commonly or officially held; disagreement with majority or authority
   - Refusal to accept or comply with prevailing opinions, practices, or decisions
   
   **Synonyms**: disagreement, opposition, nonconformity, objection, protest
   
   **Antonyms**: agreement, consent, conformity, compliance, assent
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts describing disagreement with authority or majority opinion; political, judicial, or academic contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual disagreement; when consensus is discussed; very formal register
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - voice dissent
   - dissent from
   - political dissent
   - suppress dissent
   - dissenting opinion
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart/away) + sent (feel, think) → feel differently
   
   **Etymology**: Latin dissentire (to disagree, differ in sentiment) → English "dissent" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Less common casually; prefer "disagreement")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The judge filed a dissenting opinion regarding the majority decision.
   - Political dissent faced increasing governmental suppression.
