1. **Verb**: combat
   **Meaning**: 
   - To fight against or take strong action to reduce, prevent, or eliminate something harmful.
   - Often used for organised efforts *against* crime, disease, corruption, or misinformation.
   
   **Synonyms**: fight, battle, tackle, counter, oppose
   
   **Antonyms**: tolerate, ignore, enable, encourage, foster
   
   **When to Use**: Describing deliberate action taken *against* a problem (combat corruption, combat racism, combat climate change, combat misinformation).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simple disagreement ("disagree" or "question" may be better); for literal fighting in small personal conflicts ("argue", "quarrel").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - combat corruption
   - combat racism
   - combat misinformation
   - combat disease
   - combat climate change
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + bat (beat, fight)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *combatre* ('to fight') → English 'combat', now widely used metaphorically for organised efforts against harms.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're trying to combat online hate speech.
   - We need better tools to combat spam.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agency implemented new policies to combat systemic corruption.
   - Coordinated international action is required to combat emerging pandemics.

1. **Verb**: counteract
   **Meaning**: 
   - To act *against* something so as to reduce its force or neutralise its effect.
   - Often used for chemical, medical, or strategic responses.
   
   **Synonyms**: neutralise, offset, counter, mitigate, balance out
   
   **Antonyms**: reinforce, amplify, exacerbate, intensify, worsen
   
   **When to Use**: Technical and formal contexts for measures that work directly against undesirable effects (counteract side effects, counteract bias, counteract acid).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general disagreement or criticism; this verb implies direct, often practical, opposition to an effect.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - counteract the effects
   - counteract bias
   - counteract inflation
   - counteract a poison
   - designed to counteract
   
   **Root Analysis**: counter- (against, opposite) + act (do, perform)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin-based *counter-* ('against') + 'act'; used in science, medicine, and policy to describe actions that go directly against other forces.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Drink some water to counteract the coffee.
   - We need something to counteract all this negativity.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention seeks to counteract long-standing structural disadvantages.
   - Buffer solutions are used to counteract rapid pH changes.

1. **Verb**: boycott
   **Meaning**: 
   - To refuse to buy, use, or take part in something as a way of protesting or putting pressure on someone.
   - A collective or individual act *against* a company, event, or policy.
   
   **Synonyms**: refuse to support, avoid, shun, embargo, stage a boycott
   
   **Antonyms**: support, patronise, endorse, back, participate in
   
   **When to Use**: Social, political, or consumer contexts where people organise against something by withdrawing support (boycott a brand, boycott an event).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simple dislike without organised refusal; for formal diplomatic sanctions (which may need more specific terms).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - call for a boycott
   - boycott a company
   - boycott an event
   - join the boycott
   - nationwide boycott
   
   **Root Analysis**: Named after Captain Charles Boycott, an Irish land agent who was socially ostracised.
   
   **Etymology**: From the 19th‑century surname 'Boycott'; his treatment gave rise to the verb meaning to act collectively against someone by withholding cooperation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Some customers decided to boycott the company.
   - People are boycotting the app after the new policy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Activists urged consumers to boycott products linked to forced labour.
   - The campaign explored boycotting the event as a form of protest.

1. **Verb**: neutralise
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make something ineffective or harmless; to cancel out or balance opposing forces.
   - In security and military contexts, to remove a threat.
   
   **Synonyms**: deactivate, offset, nullify, counter, disarm
   
   **Antonyms**: activate, strengthen, empower, intensify, reinforce
   
   **When to Use**: Describing measures that work *against* a threat or effect until it no longer causes harm (neutralise a virus, neutralise opposition, neutralise an attack).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual disagreement; the verb carries technical or serious connotations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - neutralise a threat
   - neutralise an effect
   - neutralise opposition
   - chemically neutralise
   - attempts to neutralise criticism
   
   **Root Analysis**: neutral (neither side) + -ise (make) → make neither side dominant; cancel out
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *neuter* ('neither of two') → 'neutral' → 'neutralise' meaning to make ineffective or remove advantage.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A bit of lemon juice will neutralise the smell.
   - They tried to neutralise the backlash with an apology.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The buffer solution neutralises excess acid in the system.
   - Countermeasures were deployed to neutralise potential cyberattacks.

1. **Verb**: undermine
   **Meaning**: 
   - To gradually weaken, damage, or work *against* something such as authority, confidence, or stability.
   
   **Synonyms**: weaken, erode, sabotage, subvert, corrode
   
   **Antonyms**: strengthen, support, reinforce, bolster, uphold
   
   **When to Use**: Describing subtle or indirect actions that act against trust, systems, or agreements (undermine trust, undermine efforts, undermine authority).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For open, direct opposition ("oppose" or "challenge" may fit better); for physical destruction of structures where "destroy" or "damage" is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - undermine trust
   - undermine efforts
   - undermine confidence
   - undermine authority
   - undermine progress
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (beneath) + mine (dig) → literally, to dig under foundations so they collapse
   
   **Etymology**: Originally a military term for tunnelling under walls; now widely used metaphorically for actions that work secretly or gradually against something.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Constant criticism can really undermine someone's confidence.
   - Leaks like that undermine the team's work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Inconsistent messaging may undermine public trust in institutions.
   - These practices systematically undermine anti-corruption efforts.

1. **Verb**: dismantle
   **Meaning**: 
   - To take something apart, often carefully; to remove the structure that supports a system.
   - Figuratively, to break down systems or practices that are harmful or unjust (dismantle racist policies).
   
   **Synonyms**: take apart, deconstruct, disassemble, break down, abolish
   
   **Antonyms**: build, assemble, construct, reinforce, maintain
   
   **When to Use**: Describing deliberate action *against* harmful structures—laws, institutions, stereotypes—as well as physical structures (dismantle a machine, dismantle a regime).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For minor adjustments or reforms; 'dismantle' implies thorough removal.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - dismantle a system
   - dismantle racism
   - dismantle barriers
   - dismantle an organisation
   - dismantle the framework
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (reverse) + mantle (cloak, covering) → remove covering or supporting structure
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *desmanteler* ('to strip of its cloak, defences'); modern figurative sense is widely used in anti-racism and reform discourse.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're trying to dismantle all the old rules.
   - Activists want to dismantle those unfair systems.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Policy reforms aim to dismantle institutional barriers to equal access.
   - The agreement sought to dismantle outdated regulatory structures.

1. **Verb**: campaign
   **Meaning**: 
   - To organise a series of actions (speeches, events, posts) in order to achieve a political or social goal.
   - Often followed by "against" or used in anti‑[issue] phrases (campaign against corruption, campaign for anti-smoking laws).
   
   **Synonyms**: advocate, lobby, organise, crusade, campaign for/against
   
   **Antonyms**: stay passive, ignore, remain silent, acquiesce
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about sustained efforts *against* or *for* a cause (campaign against violence, campaign for anti-racist education).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For one-off complaints or casual opinions; 'campaign' implies planning and repeated action.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - campaign against [issue]
   - campaign for [change]
   - launch an anti-[issue] campaign
   - ongoing campaign
   - grassroots campaign
   
   **Root Analysis**: from "campaign" (military operations in a field) → extended to organised political or social efforts
   
   **Etymology**: From French *campagne* ('field, countryside, military campaign'); in modern usage, it covers political, marketing, and social-justice efforts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're campaigning against single-use plastics.
   - Students started campaigning for anti-bullying policies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several NGOs campaign against discriminatory legislation.
   - The organisation has campaigned for robust anti-corruption safeguards for over a decade.
