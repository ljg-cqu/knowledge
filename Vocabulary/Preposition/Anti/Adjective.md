1. **Adjective**: anti-social (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Describing behaviour that harms or disregards other people or society (vandalism, intimidation, violence, persistent nuisance).
   - *(Sense 2)* Informally, describing someone who avoids social contact or does not enjoy spending time with others.
   
   **Synonyms**: unsociable, hostile, disruptive, withdrawn, unfriendly
   
   **Antonyms**: sociable, friendly, community-minded, pro-social, outgoing
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about behaviour that is clearly against social norms or public order (anti-social behaviour orders, anti-social neighbours), or casually about people who strongly avoid socializing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When someone is just shy or introverted (too strong and often inaccurate); for serious psychiatric diagnoses unless used carefully in clinical contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-social behaviour
   - anti-social neighbour
   - deeply anti-social acts
   - become increasingly anti-social
   - anti-social tendencies
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + social (relating to society or other people)
   
   **Etymology**: From Greek/Latin prefix *anti-* ('against') + 'social'; used in psychology, criminology, and everyday English to describe behaviour that acts against social norms.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been really anti-social lately and just staying at home.
   - Their late-night shouting is seriously anti-social.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The council introduced new measures to address persistent anti-social behaviour.
   - Anti-social conduct undermines community cohesion and public safety.

1. **Adjective**: anti-racist (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Actively opposed to racism and racial discrimination; supporting policies and actions that dismantle racist structures.
   - Describing policies, practices, or people whose explicit purpose is to work against racism.
   
   **Synonyms**: opposed to racism, equality-focused, inclusive, pro-equality, anti-discriminatory
   
   **Antonyms**: racist, discriminatory, prejudiced, supremacist, exclusionary
   
   **When to Use**: Describing individuals, organisations, books, or policies that are clearly positioned *against* racism (anti-racist training, anti-racist curriculum, anti-racist campaign).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For merely neutral or silent positions; anti-racist normally implies active effort, not just 'not racist'.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-racist policy
   - anti-racist education
   - anti-racist stance
   - explicitly anti-racist
   - anti-racist practice
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + racist (supporting racial discrimination or hierarchy)
   
   **Etymology**: Built from 'anti-' plus 'racist'; widely adopted in 20th–21st century social-justice and educational discourse to stress active opposition to racism.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're trying to be more openly anti-racist at work.
   - I want to raise my kids with a strongly anti-racist mindset.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution articulated an anti-racist framework for all future initiatives.
   - Training programmes now integrate explicitly anti-racist learning outcomes.

1. **Adjective**: anti-war (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Opposed to war in general or to a particular war; favouring peaceful or diplomatic solutions instead of military action.
   
   **Synonyms**: pacifist, peace-oriented, dovish, peace-minded, non-militaristic
   
   **Antonyms**: pro-war, hawkish, militaristic, belligerent, warlike
   
   **When to Use**: Describing people, movements, speeches, or art that stand *against* war or military intervention (anti-war protesters, anti-war speech, anti-war songs).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general preference for peace without political meaning ("likes peace" is weaker); when the stance is specific to one policy but not to war more broadly—then be more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-war movement
   - anti-war protest
   - anti-war activist
   - anti-war rally
   - strongly anti-war
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + war (armed conflict between groups or states)
   
   **Etymology**: Popularised in 20th‑century political discourse, especially during major conflicts (World Wars, Vietnam War) to label movements openly opposed to war.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My grandparents were very anti-war after what they saw.
   - A lot of students here are strongly anti-war.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Anti-war coalitions coordinated demonstrations across multiple capitals.
   - The manifesto reflects a consistently anti-war foreign-policy philosophy.

1. **Adjective**: anti-corruption (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Designed to prevent, detect, or punish corruption in government, business, or other organisations.
   - Describing measures, laws, or institutions that stand *against* corrupt practices.
   
   **Synonyms**: integrity-focused, transparency-focused, clean-governance, corruption-fighting
   
   **Antonyms**: corrupt, compromised, bribery-tolerant, opaque, graft-ridden
   
   **When to Use**: Labelling laws, agencies, campaigns, and tools whose explicit aim is to reduce or oppose corruption (anti-corruption law, anti-corruption bureau, anti-corruption drive).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For generic 'ethical' or 'good' behaviour without specific focus on corruption; when the measures are weak or symbolic only—then the label may be misleading.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-corruption campaign
   - anti-corruption agency
   - anti-corruption measures
   - anti-corruption framework
   - national anti-corruption strategy
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + corruption (abuse of power for private gain)
   
   **Etymology**: Became widespread in late 20th‑century governance and development vocabulary alongside international efforts against bribery and fraud.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new government promised serious anti-corruption action.
   - People are sceptical about yet another anti-corruption campaign.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The country implemented comprehensive anti-corruption legislation aligned with international standards.
   - Independent oversight bodies form a core component of the anti-corruption architecture.

1. **Adjective**: anti-competitive (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Restricting or harming fair competition in a market (for example, through cartels, price-fixing, or exclusive deals).
   - Contrary to the principles of open, competitive markets; often used in legal or economic contexts.
   
   **Synonyms**: monopolistic, restrictive, unfair, market-distorting, exclusionary
   
   **Antonyms**: pro-competitive, open, fair, market-friendly, liberalising
   
   **When to Use**: Describing practices, agreements, or regulations that work *against* competition (anti-competitive practices, anti-competitive behaviour, anti-competitive merger).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simple strong competition ("aggressive" is different); in everyday conversation where technical legal meaning is not intended.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-competitive practices
   - anti-competitive behaviour
   - anti-competitive merger
   - deemed anti-competitive
   - anti-competitive effect
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + competitive (relating to rivalry or competition)
   
   **Etymology**: Developed in competition law and economics to label behaviour that undermines competitive markets (20th century onwards).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That kind of secret price-fixing is totally anti-competitive.
   - Some people think the new rules are actually anti-competitive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulator ruled that the agreement had a significant anti-competitive effect.
   - Anti-competitive conduct may trigger substantial penalties under antitrust law.

1. **Adjective**: anti-inflammatory (non-gradable in core sense)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Reducing or preventing inflammation in the body (swelling, pain, redness).
   - Describing medicines, foods, or treatments that act *against* inflammatory processes.
   
   **Synonyms**: inflammation-reducing, soothing, calming, therapeutic (in this context)
   
   **Antonyms**: pro-inflammatory, irritating, inflammatory (when describing substances or responses)
   
   **When to Use**: Medical, nutritional, and wellness contexts for drugs, diets, or therapies whose function is to act against inflammation (anti-inflammatory drugs, anti-inflammatory diet).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general pain relief that does not specifically affect inflammation (e.g., some painkillers are not anti-inflammatory); outside health contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-inflammatory drug
   - anti-inflammatory cream
   - anti-inflammatory effect
   - anti-inflammatory diet
   - non-steroidal anti-inflammatory (NSAID)
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + inflammatory (causing inflammation)
   
   **Etymology**: Formed within medical terminology, especially 20th‑century pharmacology, to describe drugs counteracting inflammatory responses.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The doctor gave me an anti-inflammatory cream for my ankle.
   - Some people try to follow an anti-inflammatory diet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients received standard non-steroidal anti-inflammatory medication post-operatively.
   - The compound exhibits strong anti-inflammatory properties in clinical trials.

1. **Adjective**: anti-establishment (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Strongly opposed to the dominant political, social, or cultural institutions and elites.
   - Favouring change that goes against the existing establishment or status quo.
   
   **Synonyms**: rebellious, countercultural, anti-system, subversive, anti-status-quo
   
   **Antonyms**: establishment, mainstream, conformist, pro-establishment, institution-friendly
   
   **When to Use**: Describing political movements, parties, art, or attitudes that define themselves mainly by opposition to the establishment (anti-establishment party, anti-establishment sentiment).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For mild criticism of current leaders; when views are mainstream within existing institutions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-establishment sentiment
   - anti-establishment party
   - anti-establishment rhetoric
   - strongly anti-establishment
   - anti-establishment artist
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + establishment (dominant institutions, authorities, and norms)
   
   **Etymology**: Popularised in 1960s political and cultural movements to describe positions defined by opposition to mainstream institutions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His views are very anti-establishment.
   - The band has a really anti-establishment vibe.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Anti-establishment discourse gained traction during periods of economic instability.
   - The election revealed deep anti-establishment feeling among younger voters.
