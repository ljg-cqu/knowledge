1. **Noun**: anti-hero (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A central character in a story who lacks traditional heroic qualities such as bravery, moral purity, or selflessness, yet still functions as the protagonist.
   - Often morally ambiguous, flawed, or reluctant, standing in contrast to the conventional 'ideal' hero.
   
   **Synonyms**: flawed hero, reluctant hero, complex protagonist, unconventional hero, morally ambiguous lead
   
   **Antonyms**: hero, traditional hero, role model, paragon, idealized protagonist
   
   **When to Use**: In literature, film, and media discussions when describing protagonists who stand against stereotypical heroic ideals—cynical detectives, vigilantes, morally grey leaders.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For outright villains with no redeeming qualities; for minor characters who are simply unlikeable but not central to the story.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - modern anti-hero
   - morally ambiguous anti-hero
   - reluctant anti-hero
   - anti-hero archetype
   - dark anti-hero
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against, opposite) + hero (protagonist admired for courage or noble qualities)
   
   **Etymology**: From French 'anti-héros', formed with Greek-derived prefix 'anti-' ('against, opposite') + 'hero'; gained prominence in 20th‑century criticism describing protagonists who reject or subvert heroic norms.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I love shows where the main character is a bit of an anti-hero.
   - He's more of an anti-hero than a classic good guy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The novel centers on an anti-hero whose actions consistently undermine conventional moral expectations.
   - Contemporary cinema frequently elevates the anti-hero as a vehicle for exploring ethical ambiguity.

1. **Noun**: antidote (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A substance that counteracts a poison or harmful effect in the body.
   - *(Sense 2)* Anything that works against or neutralizes an undesirable situation, feeling, or influence.
   
   **Synonyms**: remedy, countermeasure, cure, corrective, counteragent
   
   **Antonyms**: poison, toxin, cause, aggravator, exacerbating factor
   
   **When to Use**: In medical, scientific, and metaphorical contexts when something directly works against or neutralizes harm (antidote to boredom, antidote for anxiety, antidote to corruption).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general treatments that do not directly counteract a specific harm; for vague 'solutions' where direct opposition to a problem is not implied.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - antidote to boredom
   - antidote for anxiety
   - effective antidote
   - seek an antidote
   - no known antidote
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + dote (from Greek 'didonai' = to give) → 'given against' (what is given against a poison)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek 'antidoton' ('given against') → Latin 'antidotum' → Old French → English 'antidote'; originally a specific medical remedy administered against poison, later extended metaphorically.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A good walk outside is the perfect antidote to a stressful day.
   - Laughter can be a real antidote for worry.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Prompt administration of the appropriate antidote is critical in cases of toxic exposure.
   - Community engagement initiatives function as a partial antidote to social isolation.

1. **Noun**: antibody (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A specialized protein produced by the immune system that recognizes and helps neutralize foreign substances such as bacteria and viruses.
   - Often used collectively to refer to the immune response against specific pathogens (antibodies against influenza, antibodies to SARS‑CoV‑2).
   
   **Synonyms**: immunoglobulin, defensive protein, immune protein, protective factor
   
   **Antonyms**: antigen (triggering substance), pathogen, infectious agent
   
   **When to Use**: In medical, biological, and public-health contexts when describing the body's defense 'against' infection (antibodies against measles, antibody levels, antibody response).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In everyday explanations where simpler wording ('protective proteins in your blood') is clearer; when referring to general immunity rather than specific molecules.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - antibodies against
   - antibody response
   - high/low antibody levels
   - antibody test
   - neutralizing antibody
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + body (here: 'foreign body' or organism) → 'against the body/agent'
   
   **Etymology**: Coined in late 19th‑century immunology from 'anti-' ('against') + 'body', reflecting the idea of a substance acting against invading bodies or toxins.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the vaccine, your body makes antibodies against the virus.
   - The test checks whether you already have antibodies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants developed robust antibodies against the target antigen within two weeks.
   - Longitudinal data show declining antibody titres over a twelve‑month period.

1. **Noun**: antithesis (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Direct opposite or complete contrast of something ("the antithesis of fairness").
   - *(Rhetoric)* A figure of speech in which sharply contrasting ideas are placed side by side in balanced clauses.
   
   **Synonyms**: opposite, reverse, counterpoint, contrast, contradiction
   
   **Antonyms**: equivalent, counterpart, parallel, similarity, likeness
   
   **When to Use**: In analytical, literary, and philosophical writing when highlighting something that stands directly 'against' or in sharp contrast to another idea, quality, or position.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For mild or partial differences; everyday conversation often prefers simpler words like 'the opposite'.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the antithesis of
   - direct antithesis
   - perfect antithesis
   - thesis and antithesis
   - rhetorical antithesis
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against, opposite) + thesis (proposition, statement)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek 'antithesis' ('opposition, contrast') → Latin → English; long used in rhetoric and philosophy to mark propositions set against each other.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His relaxed style is the antithesis of my stressy approach.
   - That behaviour is the antithesis of what the company says it stands for.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy represents the antithesis of evidence‑based decision‑making.
   - Classical rhetoric frequently relies on antithesis to sharpen conceptual distinctions.

1. **Noun**: anti-vaxxer (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who is strongly opposed to vaccines, vaccination policies, or both, often on ideological, political, or conspiratorial grounds.
   - Frequently used in media and public-health discussions to label groups defined by their opposition to vaccination.
   
   **Synonyms**: vaccine opponent, anti-vaccination activist, vaccine skeptic (softer), anti-vaccine campaigner
   
   **Antonyms**: pro-vaxxer (informal), vaccine advocate, pro-vaccination supporter
   
   **When to Use**: In discussions of public health, misinformation, and social movements when you need a concise label for groups or individuals who are 'anti' vaccines.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral scientific writing about attitudes (prefer 'individuals with vaccine hesitancy'); when the label could be perceived as dismissive in sensitive dialogue.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hard-core anti-vaxxers
   - anti-vaxxer movement
   - online anti-vaxxer communities
   - debates between pro‑vaxxers and anti‑vaxxers
   - anti-vaxxer rhetoric
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + vax/vaxxer (informal shortening of 'vaccination'/'vaccinator')
   
   **Etymology**: Emerged in late 20th–early 21st century English with rising public debate over vaccination; 'anti‑vax' and 'anti‑vaxxer' became common in media and online discourse.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Some of my relatives are hardcore anti-vaxxers.
   - The comments section is full of anti-vaxxers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Campaigns must address misinformation commonly circulated in anti-vaxxer networks.
   - The study analyzed narratives shared by prominent anti-vaxxer influencers.

1. **Noun**: anti-racism (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Active policies, attitudes, and practices aimed at opposing racism and dismantling racist structures, rather than simply being 'not racist'.
   - A framework that positions itself explicitly 'against' racial discrimination and inequality.
   
   **Synonyms**: opposition to racism, racial justice activism, anti-racist practice, equality work
   
   **Antonyms**: racism, racial discrimination, white supremacy, racial prejudice
   
   **When to Use**: In social-justice, educational, and organizational contexts when referring to deliberate efforts that go beyond neutrality to actively counter racism (anti-racism training, anti-racism policies).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general diversity initiatives not specifically focused on race; when describing personal attitudes without action (prefer 'opposed to racism').
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anti-racism training
   - anti-racism policy
   - commitment to anti-racism
   - anti-racism work
   - anti-racism framework
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (against) + racism (belief or system that treats some races as superior/inferior)
   
   **Etymology**: Built from 'anti-' plus 'racism'; widely adopted in late 20th‑century and especially 21st‑century discourse to emphasize active resistance rather than passive disapproval.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our school is trying to do more serious anti-racism work.
   - They went to an anti-racism workshop last weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization articulated a long-term anti-racism strategy with measurable goals.
   - Anti-racism initiatives must address both individual bias and structural inequality.
