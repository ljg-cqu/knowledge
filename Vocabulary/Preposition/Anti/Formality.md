1. **Original Expression**: I'm anti this idea 
   **Context**: Informally stating that you are against a proposal in a discussion
   
   **Core Intent**: To express personal opposition to a specific idea in a direct, compact way
   
   **Casual**: I'm really anti this idea 
   *Characteristics*: Intensifier "really", informal use of free-standing "anti" before "this idea"; direct, conversational tone  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends or close colleagues in relaxed settings, informal chats, internal team conversations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'm strongly opposed to this idea  
   *Characteristics*: More standard verb phrase "strongly opposed to"; clear stance; neutral-professional tone  
   *Use when*: Team meetings, internal emails, professional discussions where clear disagreement is acceptable
   
   **Formal**: I must express my opposition to this proposal  
   *Characteristics*: Formal phrase "express my opposition", "proposal" instead of "idea"; respectful but firm  
   *Use when*: Board meetings, official minutes, formal feedback documents, communication with senior stakeholders
   
   **Humorous**: My inner self is screaming "hard anti" on this one  
   *Characteristics*: Internet-influenced slang "hard anti"; playful exaggeration; personification of "inner self"  
   *Use when*: Lightening tension among peers, friendly design critiques, informal retrospectives  
   *Avoid when*: High‑stakes decisions, cross‑cultural calls, situations where humour could be misread as disrespect
   
   **Lively**: I'm firmly on the anti side here  
   *Characteristics*: Metaphor of "sides" (pro vs anti), adverb "firmly" for emphasis; energetic but not rude  
   *Use when*: Debates, workshops, strategy discussions where strong positions are welcome
   
   **Empathetic**: I see why it's attractive, but I'm currently leaning anti on this  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges others' viewpoint ("I see why it's attractive"), softens stance with "leaning" and "currently"  
   *Use when*: You want to disagree while preserving relationships, 1:1s, feedback sessions, cross‑team collaboration
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: With senior leaders, prefer semi‑formal or formal variants; with peers, casual or lively can be acceptable.
   - *Social Distance*: With unfamiliar stakeholders or external partners, avoid slangy "anti"; use semi‑formal or formal.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: For major strategic decisions, prefer clear, professional wording; informal "I'm anti" can sound flippant.
   - *Cultural Context*: In high power‑distance cultures, direct opposition may be softened with empathy and hedging.
   - *Medium*: Written documents and emails require clearer, more formal phrases; spoken discussions allow more casual variants.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "I'm anti this" in formal documents—can sound vague or childish.
   - Jumping straight to strong opposition without acknowledging others' reasoning—can trigger defensiveness.
   - Confusing "I'm anti X" (value stance) with specific, actionable objections.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some cultures, strongly labelling yourself "anti" can be perceived as ideological or inflexible; softer expressions focusing on concerns or risks may be better received.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Internal design review with peers → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively ("I'm firmly on the anti side here because...")
   - **Situation 2**: Email to director about roadmap change → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi‑formal ("I'm strongly opposed to this change at this time.")
   - **Situation 3**: Workshop with cross‑functional team → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("I understand the benefits, but I'm leaning anti because of the risks.")

1. **Original Expression**: There's a lot of anti‑[group] feeling right now  
   **Context**: Describing a negative social or political climate towards a particular group
   
   **Core Intent**: To point out that attitudes have turned *against* a group and that this opposition matters
   
   **Casual**: People are really anti‑[group] at the moment  
   *Characteristics*: Informal "really" for emphasis; short, direct structure; uses bound "anti-" before group label  
   *Use when*: Conversations with friends or colleagues, discussing social trends, debriefing news
   
   **Semi-Formal**: There's significant anti‑[group] sentiment right now  
   *Characteristics*: Noun "sentiment" for neutral description; suitable for commentary or internal reports  
   *Use when*: Presentations, internal analyses, professional discussions about public opinion
   
   **Formal**: Recent events have intensified anti‑[group] sentiment in public discourse  
   *Characteristics*: More complex clause; precise verbs ("intensified"), formal phrase "public discourse"  
   *Use when*: Reports, policy analysis, academic writing, formal briefings
   
   **Humorous**: It feels like the algorithm has gone full anti‑[group] mode  
   *Characteristics*: Hyperbolic "full ... mode"; frames social bias through tech metaphor  
   *Use when*: Commenting lightly with peers, social‑media style discussions  
   *Avoid when*: Talking directly to members of the affected group, or in serious DEI contexts
   
   **Lively**: You can almost feel the wave of anti‑[group] energy in the air  
   *Characteristics*: Vivid metaphor "wave of energy"; sensory language ("feel in the air")  
   *Use when*: Storytelling, talks, workshops about social change
   
   **Empathetic**: Many people are experiencing hurt because of rising anti‑[group] attitudes  
   *Characteristics*: Focuses on impact ("experiencing hurt"), explicit naming of attitudes rather than people  
   *Use when*: Facilitating sensitive conversations, writing support materials, discussing impact with affected communities
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: When speaking to or about affected communities, choose empathetic, precise language; avoid jokes.
   - *Social Distance*: Public writing calls for careful, respectful wording; private chats with close friends allow more spontaneity.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious harm or violence requires neutral, accurate framing, not humour or exaggeration.
   - *Cultural Context*: Labelling attitudes as "anti‑X" may be politically sensitive; in some contexts, indirect descriptions are preferred.
   - *Medium*: Reports and articles should avoid vague "people are really anti..." and specify evidence instead.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual or humorous variants when describing serious discrimination.
   - Treating "anti‑[group] feeling" as abstract instead of connecting it to real consequences.
   - Failing to distinguish between criticism of a policy and hostility toward a whole group.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Terms like "anti‑immigrant", "anti‑refugee", or "anti‑Asian" have strong historical and emotional weight; careless use can trivialise lived experience.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Policy memo on hate incidents → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("intensified anti‑[group] sentiment")
   - **Situation 2**: Podcast conversation → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi‑formal ("significant anti‑[group] sentiment")
   - **Situation 3**: Private chat with affected friend → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("people are being hurt by rising anti‑[group] attitudes")

1. **Original Expression**: Anti‑[issue] campaign  
   **Context**: Describing an organised effort against a problem, policy, or practice (anti‑corruption, anti‑bullying, anti‑smoking)
   
   **Core Intent**: To highlight a structured set of actions explicitly aimed at working *against* something
   
   **Casual**: They're running an anti‑[issue] campaign  
   *Characteristics*: Simple present progressive; everyday vocabulary; neutral‑casual description  
   *Use when*: Describing NGOs, schools, or online communities to friends or colleagues
   
   **Semi-Formal**: They've launched a campaign against [issue]  
   *Characteristics*: Uses full "campaign against" phrase; slightly more formal; avoids heavy prefix stacking  
   *Use when*: Internal updates, presentations, stakeholder meetings
   
   **Formal**: The organisation has initiated a comprehensive anti‑[issue] campaign  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verbs ("initiated"), adjective "comprehensive"; fits reports and grant proposals  
   *Use when*: Official documents, policy briefs, funding applications
   
   **Humorous**: They're on a personal anti‑[issue] crusade  
   *Characteristics*: Word "crusade" for dramatic effect, slightly tongue‑in‑cheek; can sound exaggerated  
   *Use when*: Light commentary among peers, not in sensitive or religiously diverse contexts  
   *Avoid when*: Discussing topics where "crusade" can be offensive or historically loaded
   
   **Lively**: They've kicked off a loud, visible anti‑[issue] campaign  
   *Characteristics*: Dynamic verbs ("kicked off"), adjectives ("loud, visible"); conveys momentum  
   *Use when*: Motivating teams, summarising activism, writing engaging overviews
   
   **Empathetic**: They're leading an anti‑[issue] campaign to protect people who are being harmed  
   *Characteristics*: Centres affected people, clarifies protective goal, softens abstract "anti" with human impact  
   *Use when*: Explaining purpose to wider audiences, sensitive stakeholder communication
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: For senior or external stakeholders, semi‑formal and formal variants signal professionalism.
   - *Social Distance*: With supporters and volunteers, lively or empathetic language can build engagement.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: For serious issues (abuse, discrimination), avoid humorous "crusade" framing.
   - *Cultural Context*: Some communities prefer "for" framing ("campaign for safety") over "against" framing; consider both.
   - *Medium*: Slogans may favour short "anti‑[issue]" labels; detailed writing should spell out goals and methods.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Overusing negative "anti‑" framing without explaining positive goals.
   - Using dramatic metaphors that trivialise serious harm.
   - Assuming everyone reads "anti‑[issue]" the same way across cultures.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some languages, "anti‑" campaigns can sound confrontational or politically charged; neutral alternatives like "preventing X" or "promoting Y" may work better.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Grant application → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("comprehensive anti‑[issue] campaign")
   - **Situation 2**: Internal newsletter → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi‑formal or lively ("kicked off an anti‑[issue] campaign")
   - **Situation 3**: Conversation with affected group → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("campaign to protect people who are being harmed")
