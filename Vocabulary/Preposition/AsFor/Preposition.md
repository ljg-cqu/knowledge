1. **Preposition**: as (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In the role, capacity, or function of; indicating the way in which someone or something is regarded or the position they hold.
   - *(Abstract)* During the time when; while; used to show that two events happen at the same time.
   - *(Abstract)* In the manner that; in the same way that; to the same degree or extent that.
   
   **Synonyms**: like (in comparison context), while (in temporal context), in the capacity of (in role context), in the same way as (in manner context)
   
   **Antonyms**: unlike (in comparison), differently from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "as" to indicate someone's role or function (working as a teacher), to make comparisons (as tall as), to show simultaneous actions (as I was leaving), to indicate manner or method (do as I say), or to mean "because" in formal contexts (as you know).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "as" with "like" in formal writing (use "as" for clauses, "like" for nouns/pronouns), avoid using "as" when "because" or "since" would be clearer, don't use "as" for future roles (use "will be" instead).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as a result, as usual, as always, as expected, as soon as, as well as, as for, as of, as to
   - **Verb + as**: work as, serve as, act as, function as, regard as, see as, describe as, define as
   - **Adjective + as**: same as, such as, known as, regarded as
   - **Noun + as**: role as, position as, job as, work as
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "eallswā" (all so, entirely so)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "eallswā" → Middle English "als" → Modern English "as"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's working as a bartender while he finishes college.
   - Just do it as I showed you earlier.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Dr. Smith served as the committee chairperson for three consecutive terms.
   - As indicated in the previous report, revenue has increased significantly.

1. **Preposition**: as (comparison) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Used to indicate that something happens to the same degree or in the same manner as something else; introducing a comparison of equality.
   
   **Synonyms**: equally, to the same extent as, just like
   
   **Antonyms**: more than, less than, unlike
   
   **When to Use**: Use in "as...as" constructions for comparisons of equality (as fast as), with adjectives and adverbs to show equal degree, in similes (white as snow), or to indicate that something matches a standard or expectation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when making unequal comparisons (use "more than" or "less than"), avoid in informal contexts where "like" is preferred, don't confuse with "like" when introducing clauses.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as much as, as many as, as far as, as long as, as good as, as well as
   - **Comparison structures**: as [adjective] as, as [adverb] as, not as...as
   - **Similes**: as white as snow, as light as a feather, as clear as day
   - **Fixed expressions**: as such, as it were, as you wish
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "eallswā" (all so, entirely so)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "eallswā" → developed comparison function in Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's just as smart as her sister, maybe even smarter.
   - I can't run as fast as I used to.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results were as conclusive as those obtained in previous studies.
   - This methodology is as reliable as any currently available alternative.

1. **Preposition**: as for (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; with regard to; regarding. Used to introduce a new topic or to shift attention to a different subject, often contrasting with what was previously mentioned.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, with regard to, in relation to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (no direct opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "as for" at the beginning of a sentence to introduce a new topic or person, to shift the conversation to a different subject, or to contrast with what was previously discussed. Common in both spoken and written English.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overusing in academic writing where "regarding" or "concerning" might be more appropriate, don't use when simple topic continuation would suffice without marking a shift.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as for me, as for the rest, as for now, as for the future, as for that matter
   - **Sentence-initial position**: As for [topic], [statement about that topic]
   - **Topic shift**: [Statement 1]. As for [new topic], [statement 2].
   - **Contrast**: Some agree, but as for me, I disagree.
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the manner of) + for (concerning)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound preposition formed from combining "as" and "for" to create topic-marking function
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to the party. As for John, I have no idea what he's doing.
   - As for dinner, let's just order pizza.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The budget has been approved. As for the implementation timeline, further discussion is required.
   - As for the remaining items on the agenda, they will be addressed in the next meeting.

1. **Preposition**: for (purpose) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* With the purpose of; intended to; in order to achieve or obtain. Indicates the aim, goal, or intended result of an action.
   
   **Synonyms**: in order to, for the purpose of, intended for, aimed at
   
   **Antonyms**: against (in opposition to)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "for" before a noun to indicate purpose (studying for an exam), before -ing forms in some expressions (equipment for testing), to show intended use or benefit, or to indicate what something is meant to achieve.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "for" before an infinitive verb (use "to" instead: "to study," not "for study" in purpose clauses), avoid when "to" would create clearer meaning, don't confuse with "for" indicating recipient.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for the purpose of, for the sake of, for a reason, for this reason
   - **Verb + for**: prepare for, plan for, train for, study for, hope for, wish for
   - **Adjective + for**: ready for, prepared for, suitable for, fit for, designed for
   - **Noun + for**: need for, reason for, cause for, basis for, grounds for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (before, in favor of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → from Proto-Germanic *fura → developed purpose meaning from spatial "before"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm saving up for a new car.
   - What are you planning for the weekend?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee convened for the purpose of reviewing the proposal.
   - This framework was designed for assessing organizational performance.

1. **Preposition**: for (duration) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the duration of; during the continuance of a period of time. Indicates how long an action or state lasts or continues.
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, during (with distinction), over, spanning
   
   **Antonyms**: since (indicates starting point, not duration)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "for" with time periods to express duration (for three hours, for years, for a moment), in perfect tenses to show how long something has lasted, with future time to indicate planned duration.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use with specific time points (use "at," "on," or "in"), don't confuse with "since" (which marks a starting point), avoid using with "during" simultaneously, don't use for instantaneous actions without duration.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for a while, for a long time, for ages, for hours, for years, for the time being
   - **Verb + for**: last for, continue for, persist for, endure for, remain for, stay for
   - **With perfect tenses**: have worked for, has lived for, had studied for, will have been for
   - **Time expressions**: for two weeks, for several months, for a few minutes, for decades
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (duration from spatial "through")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → semantic extension from spatial to temporal meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been waiting for you for like an hour!
   - They've been friends for years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project has been under development for approximately eighteen months.
   - The policy remained in effect for a period of five years.

1. **Preposition**: as (causal) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Because; since; for the reason that. Used to introduce a clause giving the reason for something (more common in formal British English).
   
   **Synonyms**: because, since, seeing that, given that, inasmuch as
   
   **Antonyms**: although, despite, even though
   
   **When to Use**: Use "as" in formal writing to introduce reasons or explanations, particularly at the beginning of sentences, in academic or professional contexts, or in British English where it's more common than American English.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in informal conversation where "because" is clearer and more common, don't use when "since" or "because" would be less ambiguous, avoid if the causal meaning might be confused with temporal "as" (while).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as mentioned, as stated, as indicated, as shown, as noted, as expected
   - **Clause-initial position**: As [reason clause], [main clause]
   - **Academic writing**: As demonstrated in the study, as the data suggests
   - **Formal reasoning**: As we can see, as you know, as is evident
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "eallswā" (all so)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "eallswā" → developed causal meaning through logical connection sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As you're already here, why don't you stay for dinner?
   - I didn't go, as I was feeling sick.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As indicated in the previous section, the methodology requires further refinement.
   - As the evidence suggests, the hypothesis requires reconsideration.

1. **Preposition**: for (in favor of) (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In support of; in favor of; on the side of. Indicates support, approval, or alignment with a person, idea, or cause.
   
   **Synonyms**: in favor of, in support of, on the side of, pro
   
   **Antonyms**: against, opposed to, anti
   
   **When to Use**: Use "for" to express support (vote for, argue for), to indicate being on someone's side, to show agreement or approval, or to express advocacy for a cause or position.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when expressing opposition (use "against"), avoid when neutrality needs to be emphasized, don't confuse with "for" indicating purpose or duration.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for or against, for the motion, for the proposal
   - **Verb + for**: vote for, argue for, fight for, stand for, campaign for, advocate for
   - **Adjective + for**: all for (in favor of), not for (opposed to)
   - **Noun + for**: support for, case for, argument for, reason for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (in favor of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" → from meaning "before" to "on behalf of" to "in favor of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally for the idea of having a party.
   - Are you for or against the new rules?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board voted unanimously for the proposed merger.
   - Several committee members argued for extending the deadline.

1. **Preposition**: as to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; with regard to; about. Used to introduce the subject matter being discussed or to indicate uncertainty about something.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, about, with regard to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (no direct opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "as to" in formal writing to introduce topics, to express uncertainty (as to whether/why/how), in legal or academic contexts, or to indicate the subject matter under consideration.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where simpler alternatives like "about" work better, don't overuse in one document, avoid when direct phrasing would be clearer, less common in American English than British English.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as to whether, as to why, as to how, as to what, as to the nature of
   - **Expressing uncertainty**: uncertainty as to, unclear as to, questions as to, doubts as to
   - **Formal introduction**: as to the matter of, as to the question of, as to the issue of
   - **Legal/academic**: as to the validity, as to the interpretation, as to the meaning
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the manner of) + to (toward, concerning)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound preposition combining "as" and "to" for formal topic introduction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not sure as to why he left early.
   - There's some confusion as to what time we're meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - There remains considerable debate as to the validity of these findings.
   - The committee has expressed concerns as to whether the timeline is realistic.
