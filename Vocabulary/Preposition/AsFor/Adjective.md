1. **Adjective**: equal
   **Meaning**: 
   - The same in quantity, size, degree, or value; having the same amount, number, or level as something else. (gradable in some contexts, non-gradable in mathematical contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: equivalent, identical, same, matching, comparable
   
   **Antonyms**: unequal, different, disparate, varied
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making comparisons with "as...as" constructions (as equal as), when describing fairness or balance, in mathematical or measurement contexts, or when emphasizing sameness in quality or quantity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing things that are similar but not exactly the same (use "similar" instead), don't use when emphasizing difference, inappropriate when discussing inherent superiority or inferiority.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - equal opportunity
   - equal rights
   - equal footing
   - equal measure
   - on equal terms
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (from ex-, out) + -qual (from Latin aequalis, level)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "aequalis" (uniform, equal) → Old French "egal" → Middle English "equal"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should split the bill so everyone pays an equal amount.
   - The teams are pretty equal in skill level.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All participants were given equal access to the research materials.
   - The distribution of resources must be equal across all departments.

1. **Adjective**: suitable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Right or appropriate for a particular person, purpose, or situation; having the necessary qualities or characteristics for something. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: appropriate, fitting, proper, apt, adequate
   
   **Antonyms**: unsuitable, inappropriate, unfit, improper
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" to indicate purpose or person (suitable for beginners), when discussing appropriateness or fitness for a role, when evaluating compatibility, particularly common in formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when something is merely acceptable but not ideal (use "adequate"), don't use when emphasizing perfection (use "ideal" or "perfect"), inappropriate when describing total incompatibility.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - suitable for
   - suitable candidate
   - suitable accommodation
   - suitable position
   - deemed suitable
   
   **Root Analysis**: suit (from Latin sequi, follow) + -able (capable of)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle French "suitable" → from "suit" (to follow, be appropriate) + "-able" suffix
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This movie isn't really suitable for kids.
   - Is Friday a suitable time for everyone?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate possesses qualifications suitable for this senior position.
   - Appropriate measures must be taken to ensure suitable working conditions.

1. **Adjective**: responsible
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the duty to deal with something or take care of someone; being the cause of something and able to be blamed or credited for it. (non-gradable when meaning "having duty," gradable when meaning "trustworthy")
   
   **Synonyms**: accountable, liable, answerable, in charge
   
   **Antonyms**: irresponsible, unaccountable, blameless (in different contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" to indicate duty or causation (responsible for the project), when assigning accountability, when describing mature or trustworthy behavior, in professional or formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing joint accountability without specifying (use "collectively responsible"), don't use when legal liability is the focus (use "liable"), inappropriate when discussing minor contributions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - responsible for
   - responsible party
   - responsible behavior
   - hold responsible
   - socially responsible
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + spond (promise) + -ible (capable of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "respondere" (to respond, promise in return) → French "responsable" → English "responsible"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Who's responsible for bringing the drinks?
   - You need to be more responsible with your money.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The department head is responsible for overseeing all operational activities.
   - Organizations must be held responsible for their environmental impact.

1. **Adjective**: ready
   **Meaning**: 
   - Fully prepared or in a suitable state for an action or situation; willing or eager to do something. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: prepared, set, willing, eager, primed
   
   **Antonyms**: unprepared, unready, reluctant, unwilling
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" to indicate what one is prepared for (ready for the test), use with "to" before infinitives (ready to go), when describing a state of preparation or willingness, in both casual and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when emphasizing incomplete preparation (use "getting ready"), don't use when discussing theoretical capability without current preparedness, inappropriate when unwillingness is the focus.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ready for
   - ready to go
   - ready and willing
   - get ready
   - ready for action
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "ræde" (prepared, prompt)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "geræde" (prepared, ready) → Middle English "redy" → Modern English "ready"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you ready for the party tonight?
   - I'm so ready for this week to be over.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The team is ready for the implementation phase of the project.
   - Participants must be ready for the examination at the scheduled time.

1. **Adjective**: famous
   **Meaning**: 
   - Known about by many people; having widespread recognition or renown, typically for a particular quality or achievement. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: renowned, celebrated, well-known, distinguished, prominent
   
   **Antonyms**: unknown, obscure, anonymous, unrecognized
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" to indicate what someone/something is known for (famous for acting), when describing celebrity or widespread recognition, in both positive and neutral contexts, appropriate across all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing negative notoriety (use "infamous" or "notorious"), don't use for merely recognizable but not celebrated (use "well-known"), inappropriate when emphasizing respect over recognition (use "respected").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - famous for
   - world famous
   - famous landmark
   - become famous
   - famous quote
   
   **Root Analysis**: fame (reputation) + -ous (full of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "fama" (reputation, report) → Old French "fameus" → Middle English "famous"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That restaurant is famous for its amazing burgers.
   - She became famous overnight after her video went viral.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The scientist is famous for his groundbreaking research in quantum physics.
   - This institution has become famous for its innovative educational approaches.

1. **Adjective**: eager
   **Meaning**: 
   - Strongly wanting to do or have something; showing keen interest or enthusiasm. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: enthusiastic, keen, anxious (in positive sense), avid, ardent
   
   **Antonyms**: reluctant, unwilling, indifferent, apathetic
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" before nouns (eager for success) or "to" before infinitives (eager to learn), when expressing enthusiasm or strong desire, in both formal and informal contexts to show motivation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing desperation (use "desperate"), don't use when mild interest is meant (use "interested"), inappropriate when emphasizing obligation over desire.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - eager for
   - eager to please
   - eager anticipation
   - eager learner
   - eager beaver
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old French "aigre" (sharp, keen)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "acer" (sharp, keen) → Old French "aigre" → Middle English "eger" → "eager"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm so eager for summer vacation to start!
   - The kids were eager to open their presents.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate demonstrated an eager willingness to learn new skills.
   - Researchers are eager for opportunities to test their hypotheses.

1. **Adjective**: grateful
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling or showing appreciation for something done or received; thankful and pleased about a benefit or kindness. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: thankful, appreciative, obliged, indebted, beholden
   
   **Antonyms**: ungrateful, unappreciative, thankless
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" to indicate what one appreciates (grateful for help), in expressions of thanks, when acknowledging kindness or benefits received, appropriate in both formal and informal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when expressing mere acknowledgment without appreciation, don't use when resentment is present (use "obliged" in negative sense), inappropriate when demanding rather than appreciating.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - grateful for
   - deeply grateful
   - grateful to have
   - grateful acknowledgment
   - eternally grateful
   
   **Root Analysis**: grate (pleasing) + -ful (full of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "gratus" (pleasing, thankful) → Old French "grat" → "grateful"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really grateful for all your help with the move.
   - We're so grateful to have such great neighbors.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is grateful for the detailed feedback provided by stakeholders.
   - We remain grateful for your continued support and cooperation.

1. **Adjective**: eligible
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the right to do or receive something; satisfying the appropriate conditions or requirements. (non-gradable in formal contexts, sometimes gradable informally)
   
   **Synonyms**: qualified, entitled, suitable, acceptable, fit
   
   **Antonyms**: ineligible, unqualified, unsuitable, disqualified
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" to indicate what one qualifies for (eligible for benefits), in contexts involving qualifications or requirements, in administrative or legal contexts, when discussing candidacy or qualification.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing suitability without requirement (use "suitable"), don't use when qualification is certain without conditions, inappropriate when disqualification has occurred.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - eligible for
   - eligible bachelor
   - eligible to vote
   - eligible candidate
   - become eligible
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (out) + -lig (choose, from Latin legere) + -ible (capable of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "eligere" (to choose out) → Late Latin "eligibilis" → Middle French "eligible"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you eligible for the student discount?
   - He's one of the most eligible bachelors in town.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applicants must be eligible for the scholarship based on academic merit.
   - Employees become eligible for benefits after completing a probationary period.

1. **Adjective**: typical
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the distinctive qualities of a particular type of person or thing; showing the characteristics expected of or popularly associated with something. (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: characteristic, representative, archetypal, standard, normal
   
   **Antonyms**: atypical, unusual, exceptional, abnormal
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "of" more commonly, but can appear in "as" constructions meaning "characteristically," when describing something representative of a category, when discussing expected or normal patterns.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when emphasizing uniqueness (use "unique" or "distinctive"), don't use when describing exact conformity (use "standard"), inappropriate when discussing ideals rather than norms.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - typical of
   - typical example
   - typical behavior
   - typical day
   - anything but typical
   
   **Root Analysis**: typ(e) (from Greek typos, impression) + -ical (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "typos" (impression, figure) → Latin "typicus" → Late Latin "typicalis" → "typical"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's so typical of him to forget his keys.
   - It's just a typical Monday morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This case is typical of the patterns observed in the dataset.
   - The symptoms are typical of patients experiencing this condition.

1. **Adjective**: long (temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Lasting for a considerable amount of time; extended in duration. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: extended, lengthy, prolonged, protracted, drawn-out
   
   **Antonyms**: short, brief, quick, momentary
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "for" to indicate duration (for a long time), when describing temporal extension, in contexts discussing patience or endurance, appropriate across all registers to describe time periods.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing spatial length (use different context), don't use when brevity is the focus, inappropriate when discussing instant duration.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - long time
   - long period
   - long duration
   - as long as (duration condition)
   - for as long as
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "lang" (having considerable linear extent)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "lang" → Proto-Germanic "*langaz" → extended to temporal meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been waiting for a long time.
   - It's been a really long day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research spanned a long period of thirty-five years.
   - Participants were observed for a long duration to ensure data accuracy.
