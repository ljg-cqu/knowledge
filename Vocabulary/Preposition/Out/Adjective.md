1. **Adjective**: out (predicative only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not at home, work, or the usual place; absent
   - No longer burning or shining; extinguished
   - *(Sense 3)* Not possible or acceptable; ruled out
   - *(Sense 4)* No longer in fashion or popular; outdated
   - *(Sense 5)* Unconscious or deeply asleep
   
   **Synonyms**: absent, away, extinguished, unacceptable, outdated, unconscious (context-dependent)
   
   **Antonyms**: in, present, lit, acceptable, fashionable, conscious
   
   **When to Use**: Use as a predicate adjective (after "be," "seem," etc.) to describe absence, extinction, unacceptability, or unconsciousness. Not typically used attributively before nouns.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used before nouns (~~"the out person"~~ is incorrect). Don't confuse with adverbial uses.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - be out
   - lights are out
   - that's out
   - out of it (idiom)
   - out cold
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English ūt (outside, external)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ūt → adjective use meaning "external, absent, extinguished" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry, Sarah's out right now.
   - That idea is completely out—we need something new.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manager is currently out of the office.
   - Such practices are out in contemporary business ethics.

1. **Adjective**: out-of-date (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - No longer current or valid; obsolete or expired
   - Not reflecting current information, styles, or standards
   
   **Synonyms**: outdated, obsolete, old-fashioned, expired, antiquated
   
   **Antonyms**: current, up-to-date, modern, valid, contemporary
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe information, technology, fashion, or documents that are no longer current. Can be used before nouns or after linking verbs.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Hyphenate when used attributively (before noun); can be written as three words when predicative.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - out-of-date information
   - out-of-date technology
   - out-of-date passport
   - become out-of-date
   - hopelessly out-of-date
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + of + date (lacking current temporal validity)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound adjective emphasizing temporal invalidity (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This map is totally out-of-date—half these roads don't exist anymore.
   - Your software is out-of-date; you should update it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulations were deemed out-of-date and no longer enforceable.
   - Out-of-date methodologies were replaced with contemporary approaches.

1. **Adjective**: out-of-the-way (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Remote or difficult to access; located away from main routes
   - Unusual or not commonly encountered
   
   **Synonyms**: remote, isolated, secluded, off-the-beaten-path, obscure
   
   **Antonyms**: central, accessible, convenient, mainstream
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe locations that are distant or hard to reach, or things that are unusual or rare. Hyphenate when used attributively.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can have negative connotations of inconvenience; use "secluded" or "private" for positive spin.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - out-of-the-way place
   - out-of-the-way location
   - out-of-the-way restaurant
   - out-of-the-way village
   - rather out-of-the-way
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + of + the + way (removed from the main path)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound adjective for remote location (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We found this amazing out-of-the-way café in the mountains.
   - It's a bit out-of-the-way, but worth the drive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research facility is located in an out-of-the-way region.
   - Out-of-the-way communities often lack access to essential services.

1. **Adjective**: out-and-out (attributive only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Complete, absolute, or thorough; without qualification
   - Used for emphasis to indicate something is extreme or total
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, absolute, total, utter, thorough, outright
   
   **Antonyms**: partial, moderate, qualified
   
   **When to Use**: Use before nouns for emphatic effect, especially with negative qualities. Always hyphenated when used attributively.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used predicatively (~~"the lie was out-and-out"~~). Reserved for strong emphasis.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - out-and-out lie
   - out-and-out disaster
   - out-and-out fraud
   - out-and-out victory
   - out-and-out refusal
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + and + out (completely through and through)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound emphasizing totality (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's an out-and-out lie!
   - It was an out-and-out disaster from start to finish.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The scheme was determined to be out-and-out fraud.
   - This represents an out-and-out violation of protocol.

1. **Adjective**: outdated (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - No longer in fashion or use; obsolete
   - Not incorporating or reflecting recent developments
   
   **Synonyms**: obsolete, old-fashioned, antiquated, outmoded, passé
   
   **Antonyms**: current, modern, contemporary, up-to-date, cutting-edge
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe ideas, methods, technology, or styles that have been superseded. More concise than "out-of-date."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be pejorative; use "traditional" or "classic" if neutral or positive connotation desired.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - outdated technology
   - outdated thinking
   - outdated methods
   - become outdated
   - hopelessly outdated
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + date + -ed (moved beyond its time)
   
   **Etymology**: out + dated → adjective for temporal obsolescence (early 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your phone is so outdated—time for an upgrade!
   - Those ideas are completely outdated.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The curriculum was criticized as outdated and irrelevant.
   - Outdated infrastructure hampers economic development.

1. **Adjective**: outstanding (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Exceptionally good; excellent or distinguished
   - Not yet paid, resolved, or completed; remaining
   
   **Synonyms**: excellent, exceptional, superb (sense 1); unpaid, unresolved, pending (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: mediocre, poor (sense 1); paid, resolved, completed (sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for high praise or to describe unfinished business. Context usually clarifies which meaning. Both senses appropriate in formal and informal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ambiguity is possible; clarify which sense when context doesn't make it obvious.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - outstanding performance
   - outstanding achievement
   - outstanding debt
   - outstanding issues
   - outstanding balance
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + standing (standing out from others OR standing out unpaid)
   
   **Etymology**: out + standing → "projecting, prominent" (15th c.) → "excellent" (20th c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You did an outstanding job on this project!
   - I still have some outstanding bills to pay.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate demonstrated outstanding qualifications.
   - Several outstanding matters require immediate attention.

1. **Adjective**: outgoing (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Sociable, friendly, and confident in social situations
   - Leaving a position or office; departing
   
   **Synonyms**: sociable, extroverted, friendly (sense 1); departing, retiring (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: introverted, shy, reserved (sense 1); incoming, arriving (sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for personality descriptions or to describe someone leaving a position. Context clarifies meaning.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For the "departing" sense, "outgoing" precedes the noun (outgoing president); for personality, it can be either position.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - outgoing personality
   - outgoing person
   - outgoing president
   - outgoing mail
   - outgoing administration
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + going (going out socially OR going out from position)
   
   **Etymology**: out + going → "departing" (17th c.) → "sociable" (20th c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's really outgoing—makes friends easily.
   - The outgoing manager gave a farewell speech.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate's outgoing nature is an asset in client relations.
   - The outgoing board submitted its final report.

1. **Adjective**: outspoken (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Frank and honest in stating opinions, especially controversial ones
   - Speaking without reserve or fear of consequences
   
   **Synonyms**: frank, candid, forthright, blunt, direct, vocal
   
   **Antonyms**: reserved, reticent, diplomatic, cautious, tactful
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe people who speak candidly, often about difficult topics. Can be positive (honest) or negative (tactless) depending on context.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be perceived as criticism if the frankness is unwelcome; consider audience.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - outspoken critic
   - outspoken advocate
   - outspoken opinions
   - outspoken nature
   - outspoken about something
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + spoken (speaking out openly)
   
   **Etymology**: out + spoken → adjective for frank speech (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's very outspoken—says exactly what he thinks.
   - She's an outspoken advocate for animal rights.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The senator is an outspoken critic of the proposed legislation.
   - Outspoken dissent is essential in democratic discourse.

   - outlying areas

   - outlying districts
   - outlying islands
   - outlying communities
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + lying (lying outside the main area)
   
   **Etymology**: out + lying → adjective for peripheral location (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:

   - Services are limited in outlying areas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Outlying regions face significant infrastructure challenges.
   - The census included data from outlying territories.

   - outright rejection
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + right (completely and directly)

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:

   - outer regions

1. **Adjective**: outward (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to the outside or exterior; external
   - Apparent or visible, as opposed to internal or genuine
   - *(Sense 3)* Directed toward the outside
   
   **Synonyms**: external, exterior, visible, apparent, surface (for senses 1-2); outgoing (for sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: inward, internal, interior, genuine, deep
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe external appearance, direction away from center, or superficial qualities. Also exists as an adverb ("outward" or "outwards").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply superficiality; clarify if describing genuine external features vs. deceptive appearances.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - outward appearance
   - outward sign
   - outward journey
   - outward direction
   - outward behavior
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + -ward (direction suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ūtweard (toward the outside) → Modern English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His outward calm hid his inner anxiety.
   - The outward journey took six hours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Outward indicators suggested stability, but internal analysis revealed issues.
   - The organization maintained an outward focus on community engagement.

1. **Adjective**: outed (predicative, informal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Publicly revealed or exposed, especially regarding previously hidden identity or information
   - No longer concealed or secret
   
   **Synonyms**: exposed, revealed, disclosed, uncovered
   
   **Antonyms**: hidden, concealed, closeted, secret
   
   **When to Use**: Use particularly for revelation of sexual orientation or other personal information against someone's wishes. Can also apply to other secrets revealed.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sensitive term, especially regarding LGBTQ+ contexts; use respectfully. Can imply violation of privacy.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - was outed
   - got outed
   - being outed
   - outed as (something)
   - publicly outed
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + -ed (past participle, forced into visibility)
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "to out" (reveal publicly) → past participle as adjective (late 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He was outed by a tabloid newspaper.
   - She's worried about being outed at work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several officials were outed in the corruption investigation.
   - The whistleblower was outed despite protection guarantees.

1. **Adjective**: out-of-pocket (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Paid for directly by an individual rather than being reimbursed or covered
   - *(Informal)* Out of line or behaving inappropriately
   
   **Synonyms**: self-funded, direct payment (sense 1); inappropriate, out of line (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: reimbursed, covered, paid for (sense 1); appropriate, proper (sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Primarily financial/medical contexts for direct costs. The "inappropriate" sense is slang. Hyphenate when used attributively.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: The slang sense (inappropriate) is very informal and may not be widely understood.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - out-of-pocket expenses
   - out-of-pocket costs
   - out-of-pocket maximum
   - pay out-of-pocket
   - out-of-pocket behavior (slang)
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + of + pocket (payment from one's own resources)
   
   **Etymology**: Financial term (early 20th century) → slang for inappropriate (late 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I had to pay $500 out-of-pocket before insurance kicked in.
   - That comment was really out-of-pocket! (slang)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Out-of-pocket expenses must be documented for reimbursement.
   - The policy reduced out-of-pocket costs for patients.
