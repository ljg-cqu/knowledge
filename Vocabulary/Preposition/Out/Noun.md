1. **Noun**: out (C, pl: outs)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A means of escape or excuse; a way out of a difficult situation
   - *(Baseball)* An instance in which a batter or runner is eliminated from play
   - *(Informal)* A person who is openly LGBTQ+
   
   **Synonyms**: escape, excuse, way out, exit, loophole
   
   **Antonyms**: trap, obligation, dead end
   
   **When to Use**: Use in contexts of escape routes, baseball terminology, or LGBTQ+ identity. Context determines meaning.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: The LGBTQ+ sense can be considered dated or offensive by some; use respectfully and with awareness.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - give someone an out
   - look for an out
   - three outs
   - have an out
   - the ins and outs
   
   **Root Analysis**: out (Old English ūt) used nominally
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ūt → nominal use emphasizing escape or exit (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He gave me an out when he canceled the meeting.
   - That's two outs in the inning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract provided several outs for either party.
   - The team recorded three consecutive outs to end the inning.

1. **Noun**: outcome (C, pl: outcomes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The result or consequence of an action, process, or situation
   - The final product or end state
   
   **Synonyms**: result, consequence, effect, upshot, conclusion
   
   **Antonyms**: cause, origin, input, beginning
   
   **When to Use**: Use in academic, professional, and analytical contexts to describe results. Very common in research, planning, and evaluation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal; in casual contexts, "result" may be more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - desired outcome
   - positive outcome
   - outcome of the meeting
   - measure outcomes
   - outcome-based
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + come (what comes out of a process)
   
   **Etymology**: out + come → compound noun for result (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What was the outcome of your interview?
   - I'm hoping for a positive outcome.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined outcomes over a five-year period.
   - Policy decisions should be evaluated based on measurable outcomes.

1. **Noun**: outing (C, pl: outings)

   - forced outing

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're planning a family outing to the beach.
   - The kids loved their school outing to the museum.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company organized team-building outings quarterly.
   - The outing of public figures raises ethical questions about privacy.

1. **Noun**: outline (C, pl: outlines)

   - rough outline
   - outline of a figure
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + line (outer defining line)
   
   **Etymology**: out + line → compound for boundary or summary (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you give me a quick outline of what happened?
   - I could see the outline of mountains in the distance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The document provides an outline of the proposed methodology.
   - Participants received a course outline at orientation.

1. **Noun**: outrage (C/U, pl: outrages)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An extremely strong reaction of anger or indignation
   - An action or event that causes such a reaction; a violation or offense
   
   **Synonyms**: fury, indignation, anger (sense 1); atrocity, scandal, violation (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: calm, indifference, acceptance
   
   **When to Use**: Use for strong moral anger or offensive acts. Can be countable (specific acts) or uncountable (emotional state).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Strong word; don't use for mild annoyances or disagreements.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - public outrage
   - moral outrage
   - cause outrage
   - expression of outrage
   - outrage at/over something
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + rage (rage beyond normal bounds)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French outrage (excess) → English (violent offense, excessive anger) (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There was public outrage over the decision.
   - That's an outrage! How could they do that?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy generated widespread outrage among stakeholders.
   - Such violations constitute an outrage against human rights.
