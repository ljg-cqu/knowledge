1. **Verb**: subtract
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to take away a number or amount from another number or amount; to deduct one quantity from another
   - (intransitive) to perform the mathematical operation of subtraction
   
   **Synonyms**: deduct, take away, remove, minus, take from
   
   **Antonyms**: add, sum, plus, augment, include
   
   **When to Use**: Use in mathematical contexts when removing one quantity from another; in accounting and finance for deductions; in general contexts when emphasizing removal of quantities; in educational settings when teaching arithmetic
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "remove" would be clearer for physical objects; avoid when "deduct" would be more appropriate in financial contexts; don't use for logical reasoning (use "infer" or "deduce")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - subtract from
   - subtract one number from another
   - subtract expenses
   - subtract the cost
   - subtract and divide
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under, away) + tract (draw, pull)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin subtrahere (to draw away) → subtract (16th century); entered English from Latin through mathematical texts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just subtract 10 from 50 and you'll get the answer.
   - If you subtract the taxes, I'm not making much at all.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - To calculate net income, subtract total expenses from gross revenue.
   - The control group values were subtracted from experimental results to isolate treatment effects.

2. **Verb**: deduct
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to subtract or take away an amount or part from a total; to withhold a portion, especially from wages or payments
   - (transitive - Logic) to infer by deductive reasoning; to derive as a conclusion from something known or assumed
   
   **Synonyms**: subtract, take off, withhold, dock, remove
   
   **Antonyms**: add, include, supplement, augment
   
   **When to Use**: Use in financial contexts for taking amounts from payments or bills; in tax contexts for allowable subtractions; in payroll for withholdings; in logic for reasoning from general to specific; when emphasizing authorized or legitimate removal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "infer" would be clearer in reasoning contexts; avoid when unauthorized taking is meant (use "withhold illegally"); don't use for simple arithmetic (use "subtract")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deduct from salary
   - deduct expenses
   - tax deductible
   - deduct the cost
   - deduct points
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away from) + duct (lead, bring)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deducere (to lead away) → deduct (15th century); originally meant "to lead away," developed financial and logical senses
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They deduct the insurance premium straight from my paycheck.
   - Can I deduct that business lunch from my taxes?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vendor will deduct shipping costs from the final invoice.
   - Taxpayers may deduct qualified medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of adjusted gross income.

3. **Verb**: reduce
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to make something smaller or less in amount, degree, size, or importance; to diminish
   - (intransitive) to become smaller or less; to lose weight
   - (transitive - Chemistry) to cause a substance to undergo reduction; to add electrons to
   
   **Synonyms**: decrease, diminish, lessen, lower, cut
   
   **Antonyms**: increase, enlarge, expand, augment, raise
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making something smaller or less; in commercial contexts for price decreases; in chemistry for reduction reactions; in cooking for liquid concentration; when emphasizing downsizing or cutting; in weight loss contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "simplify" would be clearer for complexity; don't use when "compress" is meant for physical squeezing; avoid when "summarize" would be more precise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reduce costs
   - reduce to tears
   - reduce the risk
   - reduce speed
   - reduce by half
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + duce (lead, bring)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reducere (to bring back) → Old French reduire → reduce (14th century); originally meant "bring back to original state," evolved to "make smaller"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to reduce our spending or we'll go broke.
   - Can you reduce the volume? It's way too loud.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention successfully reduced readmission rates by 35%.
   - Manufacturers must reduce emissions to comply with environmental regulations.

4. **Verb**: diminish
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive/intransitive) to make or become less; to reduce or be reduced in size, importance, or value
   - (transitive) to cause to seem less impressive or valuable; to belittle
   
   **Synonyms**: decrease, lessen, reduce, decline, dwindle
   
   **Antonyms**: increase, enhance, magnify, amplify, strengthen
   
   **When to Use**: Use when something becomes gradually less in size or importance; when describing declining influence or capacity; in music for diminished intervals; when emphasizing loss of status or quality; in formal or literary contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when sudden or complete loss is meant; don't use when "reduce" would be simpler in casual contexts; avoid when "belittle" would be more direct for intentional undermining
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - diminish in value
   - diminish returns
   - diminish the impact
   - gradually diminish
   - diminish responsibility
   
   **Root Analysis**: di- (away) + minus (less) + -ish (make)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deminuere (to make smaller) → Old French diminuer → diminish (15th century); variant of "minish" with added prefix
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My interest in the show has really diminished over the seasons.
   - The pain should gradually diminish over the next few days.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Prolonged stress diminishes cognitive performance and decision-making capacity.
   - The law of diminishing returns applies to marginal productivity increases.

5. **Verb**: deplete
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to use up the supply or resources of something; to reduce something significantly or exhaust it
   - (transitive) to decrease seriously or exhaust the abundance of something
   
   **Synonyms**: exhaust, drain, use up, consume, empty
   
   **Antonyms**: replenish, fill, restore, stock, supply
   
   **When to Use**: Use when resources are being used up or severely reduced; in environmental contexts (deplete natural resources); in medical contexts (deplete energy reserves); when emphasizing near-total consumption or exhaustion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple reduction is meant (use "reduce"); don't use when something can be easily restored; avoid when "empty" would be simpler
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deplete resources
   - deplete reserves
   - deplete energy
   - severely deplete
   - deplete stocks
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (completely) + plete (fill)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deplere (to empty out) → deplete (early 19th century); from Latin "de-" (completely) + "plere" (to fill)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That workout completely depleted my energy.
   - We've pretty much depleted our snack supplies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Overfishing has severely depleted marine ecosystems worldwide.
   - The prolonged campaign depleted financial reserves significantly.

6. **Verb**: remove
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to take something or someone away from a place or position; to eliminate or get rid of
   - (transitive) to dismiss someone from a position or office
   - (transitive) to cause something to no longer exist; to delete or erase
   
   **Synonyms**: take away, eliminate, extract, withdraw, delete
   
   **Antonyms**: add, insert, include, place, install
   
   **When to Use**: Use when taking something away from its location; in medical contexts for surgical extraction; in administrative contexts for dismissals; when emphasizing complete elimination; in editing for deletion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "subtract" would be more appropriate for quantities; avoid when "move" alone would be clearer; don't use for temporary displacement (use "move temporarily")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - remove from office
   - remove obstacles
   - remove doubt
   - surgically remove
   - remove stains
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + move (change position)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin removere (to move back) → Old French → remove (14th century); originally meant "move away," expanded to "take away entirely"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you remove your stuff from the table?
   - I need to remove this app—it's taking up too much space.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee voted to remove the chairman from his position.
   - Surgical intervention was required to remove the obstruction.

7. **Verb**: withdraw
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive/intransitive) to remove or take away something, especially money from a bank account; to extract
   - (intransitive) to leave or retire from a place, situation, or activity
   - (transitive) to take back or retract a statement, offer, or accusation
   
   **Synonyms**: remove, extract, retract, pull out, take back
   
   **Antonyms**: deposit, insert, advance, offer, present
   
   **When to Use**: Use in banking contexts for taking out money; in military contexts for retreating; when retracting statements or offers; in medical contexts for drug cessation; when emphasizing removal from participation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "retreat" would be clearer for physical movement; avoid when "cancel" would be more appropriate; don't use when permanent removal isn't meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - withdraw money
   - withdraw from competition
   - withdraw support
   - withdraw consent
   - withdraw troops
   
   **Root Analysis**: with- (away, back) + draw (pull)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English withdrawen (13th century); from "with-" (against, back) + "draw" (pull)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to withdraw some cash from the ATM.
   - He decided to withdraw from the race due to injury.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants may withdraw consent at any point during the study.
   - The company announced its decision to withdraw the controversial product from the market.
