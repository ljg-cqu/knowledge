1. **Original Expression**: "It's 50 dollars minus tax"  
   **Context**: Discussing prices and costs with subtraction or exclusion
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that a stated amount does not include a particular deduction or component
   
   **Casual**: "It's 50 bucks, but that's before tax."  
   *Characteristics*: Conversational vocabulary ("bucks"), explanatory phrasing ("before tax"), direct structure  
   *Use when*: Shopping with friends, informal price discussions, casual estimates
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "The price is $50, excluding tax."  
   *Characteristics*: Professional vocabulary ("excluding"), precise phrasing, standard business English  
   *Use when*: Business transactions, professional emails, customer service interactions
   
   **Formal**: "The stated price of $50 does not include applicable taxes."  
   *Characteristics*: Complete sentences, precise legal language ("applicable"), formal negation structure  
   *Use when*: Contracts, legal documents, formal business correspondence, official pricing
   
   **Humorous**: "That'll be 50 bucks—the government's cut comes later!"  
   *Characteristics*: Playful reference to government ("government's cut"), casual tone with irony  
   *Use when*: Casual retail settings with friendly rapport, relaxed business environments  
   *Avoid when*: Formal business contexts, international clients unfamiliar with colloquialisms
   
   **Lively**: "Just $50—and hey, that's before they add on the tax!"  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic markers ("Just," "hey"), exclamatory energy  
   *Use when*: Sales presentations, enthusiastic discussions, marketing contexts
   
   **Empathetic**: "The base cost is $50, though please note there will be tax added at checkout."  
   *Characteristics*: Considerate phrasing ("please note"), advance warning, gentle language  
   *Use when*: Customer service, helping confused customers, situations requiring transparency
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher formality for legal/contractual contexts; casual acceptable between peers
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers require semi-formal or formal; friends allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Large purchases or contracts require formal; small transactions allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect very explicit tax disclosure; adjust formality accordingly
   - *Medium*: Written contracts require formal; verbal quotes allow semi-formal or casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "minus" in formal legal documents instead of "excluding" or "not including"—sounds too casual
   - Forgetting to specify what's excluded, leading to confusion—always be explicit
   - Using overly casual language in professional price quotes—undermines credibility
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some countries, prices must legally include all taxes (VAT inclusive), while in others (like the US), prices typically exclude sales tax. The expression "minus tax" is more common in tax-exclusive pricing cultures.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Legal contract for services → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("does not include applicable taxes")
   - **Situation 2**: Texting a friend about splitting dinner bill → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("50 bucks before tax")
   - **Situation 3**: Email quote to business client → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("$50, excluding tax")

2. **Original Expression**: "The team minus their star player"

   *Use when*: Sports discussions with friends, informal analysis, casual observations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "The team, lacking their star player"  
   *Characteristics*: Participial phrase ("lacking"), professional vocabulary, balanced structure  
   *Use when*: Sports journalism, professional analysis, workplace discussions
   
   **Formal**: "The team in the absence of their star player"  
   *Characteristics*: Prepositional phrase structure, formal vocabulary ("in the absence of"), sophisticated syntax  
   *Use when*: Academic sports analysis, formal reports, official statements
   
   **Humorous**: "The team without their superstar—basically lost sheep!"  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor ("lost sheep"), dramatic exaggeration, casual tone  
   *Use when*: Among fans with shared context, friendly sports banter, humorous commentary  
   *Avoid when*: Professional sports journalism, formal analysis, sensitive loss situations
   
   **Lively**: "The team's out there without their star player—it's a whole different game!"  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic exclamation, dramatic emphasis, engaging language  
   *Use when*: Sports commentary, exciting discussions, storytelling
   
   **Empathetic**: "The team is facing challenges without their key player to lead them."  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledging difficulty ("facing challenges"), understanding tone, supportive language  
   *Use when*: Discussing struggling teams, sympathetic analysis, supportive commentary
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Formal for official sports reports; casual for fan discussions
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/public require semi-formal; friends allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious injury context requires empathetic or formal; routine absence allows casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Individual-focused cultures may emphasize star player more than collective cultures
   - *Medium*: Published articles require semi-formal or formal; conversations allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "minus" in formal writing—sounds too mathematical or casual for descriptive prose
   - Overemphasizing individual absence in team-oriented cultures—may seem disrespectful to other players
   - Being flippant about serious injuries or losses—use empathetic register instead
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Team-oriented cultures (many Asian cultures) may prefer emphasizing "the team adjusting" over "missing the star," while individualistic cultures (US, Western Europe) often highlight star player impact.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Sports journalism article → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("lacking their star player")
   - **Situation 2**: Chatting with friends watching a game → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("without their best player")
   - **Situation 3**: Academic paper on team performance → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("in the absence of")

3. **Original Expression**: "10 minus 3 equals 7"  
   **Context**: Expressing mathematical subtraction operations
   
   **Core Intent**: To communicate a basic subtraction calculation
   
   **Casual**: "10 take away 3 is 7"  
   *Characteristics*: Conversational phrase ("take away"), informal verb choice ("is"), simple structure  
   *Use when*: Teaching young children, informal calculations, everyday math discussions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "10 minus 3 equals 7"  
   *Characteristics*: Standard mathematical terminology, clear operation symbols, precise verb ("equals")  
   *Use when*: Middle school and high school mathematics, general educational contexts, business calculations
   
   **Formal**: "The difference between 10 and 3 is 7" OR "Subtracting 3 from 10 yields 7"  
   *Characteristics*: Technical vocabulary ("difference," "yields"), complete sentence structure, formal verbs  
   *Use when*: Academic papers, formal mathematics education, professional technical writing
   
   **Humorous**: "If you've got 10 cookies and you eat 3, you've got 7 left—do the math!"  
   *Characteristics*: Real-world analogy (cookies), playful instruction ("do the math"), colloquial expression  
   *Use when*: Making math relatable, teaching with humor, casual explanations  
   *Avoid when*: Formal education assessments, serious mathematical proofs, professional contexts
   
   **Lively**: "Take 10, knock off 3, and boom—you've got 7!"  
   *Characteristics*: Dynamic verbs ("knock off"), interjection ("boom"), energetic delivery  
   *Use when*: Engaging explanations, enthusiastic teaching, motivational math contexts
   
   **Empathetic**: "Let's work through this together: we start with 10, subtract 3, and we're left with 7."  
   *Characteristics*: Inclusive language ("let's," "we"), gentle pacing, supportive framing ("work through")  
   *Use when*: Tutoring struggling students, patient instruction, building confidence in math
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Formal for academic publications; casual for peer learning
   - *Social Distance*: Professional teaching contexts use semi-formal; family tutoring allows casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes testing requires formal; homework help allows casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Different cultures have different mathematical notation preferences
   - *Medium*: Published textbooks use semi-formal; verbal explanations allow more flexibility
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "take away" in formal mathematical writing—too casual for academic contexts
   - Mixing symbolic notation with casual language—creates inconsistency in formal documents
   - Using overly technical language with young learners—causes confusion and anxiety
   
   **Cultural Notes**: While "minus" is internationally understood, some cultures use different terminology or symbols. British English often uses "take away," while American English prefers "minus" in educational contexts.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: University mathematics textbook → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Subtracting 3 from 10 yields 7")
   - **Situation 2**: Helping a 6-year-old with homework → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("10 take away 3 is 7")
   - **Situation 3**: Middle school classroom instruction → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("10 minus 3 equals 7")

4. **Original Expression**: "That's the price minus the discount"  
   **Context**: Explaining net prices after deductions
   
   **Core Intent**: To clarify that a final price reflects a discount or reduction from an original amount
   
   **Casual**: "That's what you pay after the discount"  
   *Characteristics*: Simple explanation ("after"), everyday language, direct focus on result  
   *Use when*: Retail interactions with customers, friendly shopping contexts, casual explanations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "That's the price after applying the discount"  
   *Characteristics*: Gerund phrase ("applying"), professional vocabulary, clear process description  
   *Use when*: Business communications, customer service emails, professional retail settings
   
   **Formal**: "The stated price reflects the discount deduction" OR "The price net of the discount"  
   *Characteristics*: Financial terminology ("net of," "reflects"), formal structure, technical precision  
   *Use when*: Financial documents, invoices, formal business correspondence, accounting contexts
   
   **Humorous**: "That's your lucky price after we knocked some off the top!"  
   *Characteristics*: Playful language ("lucky price," "knocked off"), conversational idiom, friendly tone  
   *Use when*: Building rapport in retail, friendly sales environments, casual business  
   *Avoid when*: Formal contracts, serious negotiations, professional financial communications
   
   **Lively**: "That's your final price—and yes, the discount is already in there!"  
   *Characteristics*: Emphatic confirmation ("yes"), exclamatory energy, reassuring tone  
   *Use when*: Sales presentations, enthusiastic customer service, clarifying good deals
   
   **Empathetic**: "Just to confirm, this price already includes your discount, so there's nothing more to subtract."  
   *Characteristics*: Reassuring language ("just to confirm"), explicit clarification, considerate tone  
   *Use when*: Helping confused customers, preventing misunderstandings, customer service recovery
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Formal for contracts and official documents; casual for everyday retail
   - *Social Distance*: New customers require semi-formal; regular customers allow more casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Large purchases require formal clarity; small transactions allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Price transparency expectations vary; some cultures require very explicit explanation
   - *Medium*: Written invoices use formal or semi-formal; verbal explanations allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being unclear about whether discount has been applied—always explicitly state this
   - Using "minus" in formal contracts—use "net of" or "less" instead for professional documents
   - Assuming customers understand discount application—many need explicit confirmation
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Discount terminology varies globally. Some cultures expect haggling and informal pricing, while others expect fixed, formal price statements. Adjust register accordingly.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Invoice to business client → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("price net of discount")
   - **Situation 2**: Checkout conversation with customer → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("after the discount")
   - **Situation 3**: Email confirming order to customer → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("after applying the discount")

5. **Original Expression**: "The temperature is minus 10 degrees"  
   **Context**: Describing temperatures below zero
   
   **Core Intent**: To communicate that the temperature is below the freezing point of water
   
   **Casual**: "It's 10 below outside" OR "It's negative 10 out there"  
   *Characteristics*: Abbreviated phrasing ("10 below"), implied context ("outside"), conversational structure  
   *Use when*: Weather conversations with locals, informal discussions, everyday talk about cold
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "The temperature is minus 10 degrees" OR "It's negative 10 degrees"  
   *Characteristics*: Complete temperature statement, standard terminology, clear unit specification  
   *Use when*: Weather reports, professional communications, general public announcements
   
   **Formal**: "The temperature measures negative 10 degrees Celsius" OR "Ambient temperature: -10°C"  
   *Characteristics*: Precise scientific terminology, full unit specification, technical phrasing  
   *Use when*: Scientific reports, technical documentation, formal weather analysis, academic papers
   
   **Humorous**: "It's a crispy minus 10 out there—practically Antarctica!"  
   *Characteristics*: Playful adjective ("crispy"), exaggerated comparison, humorous tone  
   *Use when*: Commiserating about cold with friends, social media posts, casual weather complaints  
   *Avoid when*: Professional weather reporting, serious cold weather warnings, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: "Wow, we're at minus 10 degrees today—break out the winter gear!"  
   *Characteristics*: Exclamatory opening ("Wow"), enthusiastic instruction, engaging delivery  
   *Use when*: Morning announcements, social posts, energetic weather updates
   
   **Empathetic**: "Please be careful—it's minus 10 degrees, so make sure to bundle up warmly."  
   *Characteristics*: Caring instruction ("please be careful"), health-focused advice, gentle warning  
   *Use when*: Public safety announcements, caring for vulnerable populations, responsible weather alerts
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Formal for official meteorological reports; casual for social conversations
   - *Social Distance*: Public announcements use semi-formal; friend conversations allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Dangerous cold requires empathetic or formal warnings; normal cold allows casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Celsius vs. Fahrenheit varies by region; always specify units in international contexts
   - *Medium*: Official weather services use formal; social media allows casual or lively
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Not specifying Celsius vs. Fahrenheit—critical in international contexts (-10°C ≠ -10°F)
   - Using overly casual language in safety warnings—dangerous cold requires serious tone
   - Saying "minus" in some scientific contexts where "negative" is standard—check field conventions
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Temperature scales vary globally (Celsius in most countries, Fahrenheit in US). "Below zero" is universally understood but requires context about which scale. Different cultures have different cold tolerances and appropriate responses.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: National weather service bulletin → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Temperature: -10°C")
   - **Situation 2**: Texting friends about going out → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("It's 10 below!")
   - **Situation 3**: Local news weather report → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("minus 10 degrees")
