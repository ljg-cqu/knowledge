1. **Noun**: subtraction (U/C, pl: subtractions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (U) the mathematical operation of taking one number or amount away from another; the process of calculating the difference between two numbers
   - (C) an instance of subtracting; a particular calculation involving subtraction
   
   **Synonyms**: deduction, taking away, removal, minus operation, difference calculation
   
   **Antonyms**: addition, sum, augmentation, increase
   
   **When to Use**: Use in mathematical contexts when discussing the operation of taking away; in educational settings when teaching arithmetic; in accounting or finance when describing deductions; in general contexts when emphasizing removal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "deduction" in the logical reasoning sense is meant; avoid when "removal" would be clearer in non-mathematical contexts; don't use casually when "taking away" would be simpler
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - simple subtraction
   - subtraction problem
   - addition and subtraction
   - subtraction method
   - perform subtraction
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under, away) + tract (draw, pull) + -ion (action, process)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin subtrahere (to draw away) → subtraction (15th century); entered English through French soustraction, later influenced by Latin spelling
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Kids learn addition before subtraction in school.
   - Just do a quick subtraction and you'll see the difference.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The algorithm employs both addition and subtraction operations sequentially.
   - Subtraction of baseline values revealed significant treatment effects.

2. **Noun**: deficit (C, pl: deficits)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C - Financial) the amount by which something, especially a sum of money, is too small; a shortfall in finances
   - (C - General) a lack or shortage of something considered necessary or desirable
   - (C - Economics) an excess of expenditure over income in a budget period
   
   **Synonyms**: shortfall, shortage, deficiency, gap, loss
   
   **Antonyms**: surplus, excess, profit, abundance, plenty
   
   **When to Use**: Use in financial and economic contexts for negative balances; in medical contexts (attention deficit, immune deficit); in sports for score differences; when discussing any measured shortfall; in policy discussions about government spending
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "debt" would be more accurate (deficit is a flow, debt is a stock); don't use for absolute poverty or complete absence; avoid when "difference" would be neutral and clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - budget deficit
   - trade deficit
   - attention deficit
   - deficit spending
   - run a deficit
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away from) + fic (make, do) + -it (it lacks)
   
   **Etymology**: French déficit → Latin deficit (it is lacking) (18th century); originally used in accounting, expanded to broader contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The team needs to overcome a 10-point deficit in the second half.
   - We're running a serious deficit in the snack department.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The federal budget deficit reached unprecedented levels in the fiscal year.
   - Cognitive deficits were observed in participants with chronic sleep deprivation.

3. **Noun**: deduction (C/U, pl: deductions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) an amount that is or may be deducted from something, especially from taxable income or a sum paid
   - (U) the action of deducting or subtracting something
   - (U/C - Logic) the inference of particular instances by reference to a general law or principle; logical reasoning from general to specific
   
   **Synonyms**: subtraction, reduction, discount, abatement, allowance
   
   **Antonyms**: addition, increase, surcharge, supplement
   
   **When to Use**: Use in tax and payroll contexts for amounts subtracted; in accounting for expense reductions; in logic and reasoning for inferential processes; in retail for discounts; when emphasizing legitimate removal of amounts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse the mathematical and logical senses; avoid when "inference" alone would be clearer in reasoning contexts; don't use when "deduction" might be confused with "detection" (Sherlock Holmes's reasoning)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tax deduction
   - payroll deduction
   - make a deduction
   - standard deduction
   - itemized deductions
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away from) + duct (lead, bring) + -ion (action, process)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deducere (to lead away) → deduction (15th century); initially meant "leading away," developed both mathematical and logical senses
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There are a bunch of deductions taken out of my paycheck every month.
   - I made a simple deduction based on what you told me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Charitable contributions qualify as tax deductions under current regulations.
   - The conclusion follows through logical deduction from established premises.

4. **Noun**: loss (C/U, pl: losses)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C/U) the fact or process of losing something or someone; the state of no longer having something
   - (C - Financial) money that is lost by a business or organization; a deficit in accounts
   - (C - Sport) a defeat in a game or contest
   
   **Synonyms**: deprivation, forfeiture, deficit, shortfall, defeat
   
   **Antonyms**: gain, profit, acquisition, win, surplus
   
   **When to Use**: Use when something valuable is no longer possessed; in business contexts for financial losses; in sports for defeats; in emotional contexts for grief; when emphasizing permanent removal or reduction
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "misplacement" (temporary) is meant; don't use when "change" would be more neutral; avoid in contexts where grief terminology might be insensitive
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at a loss
   - cut one's losses
   - total loss
   - weight loss
   - suffer a loss
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English los (destruction, ruin)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English los → Middle English loss (before 12th century); originally meant "destruction," expanded to "act of losing"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The team suffered another loss last night—that's five in a row.
   - I'm at a total loss about what to do next.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company reported quarterly losses exceeding analyst expectations.
   - Participants experienced significant weight loss during the intervention period.

5. **Noun**: reduction (C/U, pl: reductions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (U) the action or fact of making something smaller or less in amount, degree, or size
   - (C) an instance of reducing something; a discount or price decrease
   - (C - Chemistry) a reaction in which electrons are gained and valence is reduced
   
   **Synonyms**: decrease, diminution, cutback, discount, lessening
   
   **Antonyms**: increase, enlargement, expansion, augmentation, growth
   
   **When to Use**: Use when something is made smaller or less; in retail for price discounts; in chemistry for electron-gaining reactions; in policy contexts for cutbacks; when emphasizing intentional downsizing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "compression" (physical squeezing) is meant; don't use when increase-in-reverse isn't the focus; avoid when "simplification" might be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - price reduction
   - cost reduction
   - reduction in force
   - risk reduction
   - substantial reduction
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + duct (lead, bring) + -ion (action, process)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reducere (to bring back) → reduction (14th century); originally meant "bringing back to normal," evolved to "making smaller"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a huge reduction on winter clothes right now.
   - We've seen a massive reduction in traffic since the new road opened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention achieved a 40% reduction in infection rates.
   - Cost reduction measures were implemented across all operational divisions.

6. **Noun**: absence (C/U, pl: absences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C/U) the state of being away from a place or person; the fact of not being present
   - (U) the non-existence or lack of something; the state of not having a particular quality
   - (C) an occasion or period of being away from a place, especially work or school
   
   **Synonyms**: non-attendance, lack, want, deficiency, non-presence
   
   **Antonyms**: presence, attendance, existence, availability
   
   **When to Use**: Use when someone or something is not present when expected; in attendance contexts; when emphasizing lack or non-existence; in medical contexts (absence seizure); when discussing gaps or missing elements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when temporary misplacement is meant; avoid when "lack" would be less formal and clearer; don't use for intentional exclusion (use "exclusion" or "omission")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the absence of
   - conspicuous by one's absence
   - leave of absence
   - absence makes the heart grow fonder
   - unauthorized absence
   
   **Root Analysis**: ab- (away from) + -ence (state of being)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin abesse (to be away) → absentia → Old French → absence (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your absence was really noticed at the party last night.
   - In the absence of any better ideas, let's just go with this one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Frequent absences from class negatively impact academic performance.
   - The absence of clear guidelines resulted in inconsistent implementation.

7. **Noun**: shortfall (C, pl: shortfalls)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) a deficit of something required or expected; an amount by which something falls short of what is needed or expected
   - (C - Financial) a failure to reach a required or expected amount of money
   
   **Synonyms**: deficit, shortage, deficiency, insufficiency, gap
   
   **Antonyms**: surplus, excess, abundance, overage
   
   **When to Use**: Use in financial contexts when funds are insufficient; in supply contexts when quantity is inadequate; in performance contexts when targets aren't met; when emphasizing the measured difference between expected and actual
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when complete absence is meant (use "absence" or "lack"); don't use when "difference" would be more neutral; avoid when excess or surplus is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - budget shortfall
   - revenue shortfall
   - make up the shortfall
   - funding shortfall
   - projected shortfall
   
   **Root Analysis**: short (not reaching a standard) + fall (decrease, drop)
   
   **Etymology**: short + fall (19th century); compound word combining "short" (inadequate) with "fall" (deficiency)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We have a serious shortfall in volunteers for the event.
   - There's a shortfall of about $500 in our fundraising goal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The district faces a projected budget shortfall of $2.3 million.
   - A significant shortfall in qualified applicants delayed the hiring process.
