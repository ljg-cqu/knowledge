1. **Noun**: regard (C/U, pl: regards)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* Attention, consideration, or concern for something or someone
   - *(Sense 2)* Respect or admiration for someone
   - *(Sense 3)* (Usually plural) Good wishes or polite greetings sent to someone
   
   **Synonyms**: consideration, attention, concern, respect, esteem
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, neglect, contempt, disrespect
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts when discussing consideration, respect, or sending greetings; common in business correspondence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual settings where simpler terms like "care" or "respect" are more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with regard to, in this regard, in regard to
   - hold someone in high regard, have regard for
   - send regards, give my regards, kind regards
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + gard (guard, watch)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French regarder (to look at, watch) → Middle English regarden → noun form "regard" (consideration, attention)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He has no regard for other people's feelings.
   - Give her my regards when you see her.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee made its decision with due regard to all stakeholder interests.
   - Please extend my warmest regards to your colleagues.

1. **Noun**: consideration (C/U, pl: considerations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* Careful thought about something before making a decision
   - *(Sense 2)* A fact or circumstance that needs to be taken into account
   - *(Sense 3)* Thoughtfulness and sensitivity toward others
   
   **Synonyms**: thought, deliberation, reflection, factor, concern
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, neglect, thoughtlessness
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when discussing decision-making, factors affecting choices, or thoughtful treatment of others
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simpler terms like "thinking" or "factor" would be clearer in casual conversation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take into consideration, under consideration, give consideration to
   - key consideration, primary consideration, important consideration
   - out of consideration for
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + sider (star, heavenly body) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin considerare (to examine, observe stars) → Old French consideration → English (careful thought, deliberation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for taking my schedule into consideration.
   - Money isn't the only consideration here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal is currently under consideration by the executive committee.
   - Several considerations must be addressed before implementation can proceed.

1. **Noun**: respect (C/U, pl: respects)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A feeling of admiration for someone or something because of their qualities or achievements
   - *(Sense 2)* Due regard for the feelings, wishes, rights, or traditions of others
   - *(Sense 3)* A particular aspect, point, or detail (usually with "in")
   
   **Synonyms**: esteem, admiration, regard, deference, consideration
   
   **Antonyms**: disrespect, contempt, disdain, scorn
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when discussing admiration, consideration for others, or specific aspects of something
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When casual terms like "liking" might be more appropriate (respect implies deeper admiration)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with respect to, in respect of, in this respect
   - show respect, earn respect, command respect
   - pay respects, out of respect for
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + spect (look, see)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin respectus (regard, consideration) → Old French respect → English (admiration, consideration)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a lot of respect for what she's accomplished.
   - In some respects, you're right about that.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy demonstrates respect for individual privacy rights.
   - With respect to the financial analysis, several points require clarification.

1. **Noun**: reference (C/U, pl: references)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* The act of mentioning or alluding to something
   - *(Sense 2)* A source of information consulted or cited
   - *(Sense 3)* A statement about someone's character or qualifications
   
   **Synonyms**: mention, allusion, citation, source, recommendation
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Academic, professional contexts when discussing citations, mentions, or recommendations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation unless specifically discussing sources or recommendations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with reference to, in reference to, make reference to
   - reference material, reference point, reference book
   - provide a reference, check references
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + fer (carry, bear) + -ence (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin referre (to carry back, report) → Middle French référence → English (mention, citation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Was that a reference to what happened yesterday?
   - Can you give me a reference for the job?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With reference to your letter of March 10, we are pleased to respond.
   - The report makes several references to previous research findings.

1. **Noun**: concern (C/U, pl: concerns)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A matter that causes worry or anxiety
   - *(Sense 2)* A feeling of interest or worry about something or someone
   - *(Sense 3)* A business or company (somewhat formal)
   
   **Synonyms**: worry, anxiety, matter, issue, interest
   
   **Antonyms**: unconcern, indifference, apathy
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when discussing worries, interests, or matters requiring attention
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "worry" alone would be clearer and more direct
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cause for concern, express concern, raise concerns
   - of concern, growing concern, major concern
   - concerning this matter, concerns regarding
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + cern (separate, sift)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin concernere (to sift together, involve) → Late Latin concernere → Middle French concerner → English noun "concern"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My main concern is whether we'll finish on time.
   - Thanks for your concern, but I'm fine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board has expressed concerns regarding the proposed expansion.
   - Environmental concerns must be addressed in the impact assessment.

1. **Noun**: matter (C, pl: matters)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A subject or situation under consideration or being dealt with
   - *(Sense 2)* An affair or situation requiring attention or action
   - *(Sense 3)* The reason for distress or a problem
   
   **Synonyms**: issue, subject, topic, affair, concern
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts when referring to subjects, issues, or affairs requiring attention
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very technical contexts where "issue" or "topic" might be more precise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - regarding this matter, in this matter, matter of concern
   - urgent matter, serious matter, delicate matter
   - as a matter of fact, no matter
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin materia (substance, timber, subject)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin materia (substance, subject) → Old French matere → Middle English matere → "matter" (subject, affair)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's talk about this matter later.
   - What's the matter? You look upset.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will address this matter at the next meeting.
   - Regarding the matter of compensation, further negotiation is required.

1. **Noun**: subject (C, pl: subjects)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A person or thing that is being discussed, described, or dealt with
   - *(Sense 2)* An area of knowledge studied in school or university
   - *(Sense 3)* A person who is under the authority of a monarch or government
   
   **Synonyms**: topic, theme, matter, issue (for sense 1)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts when referring to topics of discussion, study, or consideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "topic" would be simpler and clearer in casual conversation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - regarding the subject, on the subject of, subject matter
   - change the subject, touchy subject, favorite subject
   - subject to approval, subject to change
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under) + ject (throw, place)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin subjectus (placed under, subjected) → Old French suget → Middle English subject (topic, matter under discussion)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's change the subject to something more cheerful.
   - What's your favorite subject in school?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regarding the subject of your inquiry, we require additional information.
   - The subject of climate change demands immediate attention.

1. **Noun**: topic (C, pl: topics)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A subject or theme of a conversation, discussion, or piece of writing
   - *(Sense 2)* A matter dealt with in a text, speech, or discussion
   
   **Synonyms**: subject, theme, matter, issue, point
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when referring to subjects of conversation, writing, or discussion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Highly formal legal or administrative documents where "matter" or "subject" may be preferred
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - on topic, off topic, hot topic
   - discuss a topic, address a topic, cover a topic
   - topic of conversation, topic of discussion
   
   **Root Analysis**: Greek topos (place, commonplace) → topikos (concerning commonplaces)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek topikos (of a place) → Latin topica → French topique → English "topic" (subject for discussion)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's a sensitive topic, so let's avoid it.
   - What topic should we choose for our presentation?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conference will address topics regarding sustainable development.
   - This section explores topics concerning regulatory compliance.

1. **Noun**: issue (C, pl: issues)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* An important topic or problem for debate or discussion
   - *(Sense 2)* A personal problem or difficulty
   - *(Sense 3)* Each of a regular series of publications
   
   **Synonyms**: matter, problem, question, concern, subject
   
   **Antonyms**: solution, resolution, answer
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and formal contexts when discussing problems, topics, or matters requiring resolution
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "problem" would be more direct and clear
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - address an issue, raise an issue, contentious issue
   - regarding the issue, issue at hand, key issue
   - make an issue of, take issue with
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ex- (out) + ire (go)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin exitus (exit, outcome) → Old French issue → Middle English issue (outcome, offspring) → modern "issue" (matter for discussion)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to talk about some issues regarding the roommate situation.
   - I have issues with how this was handled.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several issues regarding implementation remain unresolved.
   - The committee will examine issues concerning data privacy.

1. **Noun**: question (C, pl: questions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A sentence or phrase used to find out information
   - *(Sense 2)* A matter requiring resolution or discussion; an issue
   - *(Sense 3)* Doubt or uncertainty about something
   
   **Synonyms**: query, inquiry, issue, matter, problem (for sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: answer, solution, certainty
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts when referring to inquiries, matters for discussion, or doubts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When more specific terms like "inquiry" or "problem" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - question regarding, raise a question, answer a question
   - in question, without question, call into question
   - burning question, open question
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin quaest- (seek, ask) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin quaestionem (a seeking, inquiry) → Old French question → Middle English questioun → "question"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a quick question regarding the assignment.
   - That raises an interesting question.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several questions regarding the methodology have been raised.
   - The question concerning jurisdiction requires further legal analysis.
