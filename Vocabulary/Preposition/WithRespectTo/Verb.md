1. **Verb**: relate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To show or describe how two or more things are connected; to show the relationship between them (usually "relate A to B" or "relate to").
   
   **Synonyms**: connect, link, associate, tie, correlate
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, disconnect, isolate, distinguish (in contrastive sense)
   
   **When to Use**: When explaining how one quantity or idea stands with respect to another ("relate cost to benefit," "relate results to theory").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual speech where "connect" or "has to do with" is clearer; avoid overusing as a vague academic verb.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - relate A to B
   - relate to (be connected with)
   - results can be related to
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + lat (carry) → "carry back, connect back"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *referre* and *relata* (things told) → French *relater* → English "relate" (tell, connect).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you relate these numbers to our actual costs?
   - Im trying to relate this idea to what we did last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We relate performance metrics to input size with respect to computational complexity.
   - These findings can be related to prior work with respect to underlying assumptions.

1. **Verb**: refer (to)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To mention or allude to something; to direct attention to a source or topic ("refer to X").
   
   **Synonyms**: mention, allude to, cite, point to, invoke
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, omit, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: When explicitly pointing the reader or listener to the thing you are talking about with respect to your current point ("refer to Figure 2," "refer to your question").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk where "talk about" or "bring up" is simpler; dont confuse with "prefer".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - refer to
   - the term refers to
   - as referred to above
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + fer (carry) → "carry back, direct back"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *referre* (to carry back) → Old French *referer* → English "refer".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When I say "baseline," Im referring to where we started.
   - In that email, I was referring to your previous question with respect to timing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The notation refers to the parameter defined with respect to time.
   - As referred to in Section 3, we evaluate the model with respect to three criteria.

1. **Verb**: depend (on)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be affected or decided by something else; to vary as a function of another variable ("depend on X").
   
   **Synonyms**: hinge on, be determined by, be conditioned by, rest on
   
   **Antonyms**: be independent of, be unrelated to
   
   **When to Use**: When describing variables or outcomes that change with respect to another factor ("result depends on sample size," "decision depends on budget").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Where absolute statements are intended; avoid as vague answer ("it depends") without naming what it depends on.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - depend on
   - depend heavily on
   - depend with respect to a parameter
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (from) + pend (hang) → "hang from"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *dependere* (to hang down from) → Old French *dependre* → English "depend".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The result really depends on how you set this parameter.
   - Our plan depends on the clients feedback with respect to scope.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The convergence rate depends on step size with respect to the chosen norm.
   - System behavior depends sensitively on initial conditions with respect to long-term dynamics.

1. **Verb**: vary
   **Meaning**: 
   - To change or differ; to take on different values at different times or in different conditions.
   
   **Synonyms**: change, fluctuate, differ, range, shift
   
   **Antonyms**: remain constant, stay the same, stabilize
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about how a quantity changes with respect to another variable ("varies with time," "varies with respect to temperature").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For one-time changes ("switch," "become" may be clearer); avoid in vague statements without naming what it varies with respect to.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vary with
   - vary as a function of
   - vary with respect to
   
   **Root Analysis**: vari (change) + -y (verb)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *variare* (to change) → Old French *varier* → English "vary".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The load varies a lot with the time of day.
   - My energy really varies with respect to how much I sleep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The response varies with respect to input intensity and duration.
   - Error magnitude varies as a function of sample size with respect to each parameter.

1. **Verb**: evaluate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To judge or calculate the value, quality, or importance of something, often using explicit criteria.
   
   **Synonyms**: assess, appraise, judge, measure, rate
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, guess (without criteria)
   
   **When to Use**: When systematically judging options or results with respect to named criteria ("evaluate with respect to cost and risk").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk where "judge" or "look at" is enough; avoid when no clear standard is defined.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - evaluate with respect to
   - evaluate X against Y
   - evaluate performance
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (out) + value (worth) + -ate (verb)
   
   **Etymology**: From French *évaluer* (assess value) → English "evaluate".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Lets evaluate this plan with respect to how much stress it adds.
   - We need to evaluate the options with respect to cost and time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We evaluate each method with respect to accuracy, robustness, and efficiency.
   - Proposals were evaluated with respect to the criteria outlined in Section 2.

1. **Verb**: compare
   **Meaning**: 
   - To examine two or more things to see how they are similar or different ("compare A with/to B").
   
   **Synonyms**: contrast, set side by side, measure against, juxtapose
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore differences, consider in isolation
   
   **When to Use**: When explicitly looking at one thing with respect to another ("compare results to baseline," "compare models with respect to accuracy").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no clear reference exists; when you are only describing one item without any point of comparison.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - compare A with/to B
   - compare to baseline
   - compare with respect to
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + par (equal) → "place together as equals"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *comparare* (match, couple) → Old French *comparer* → English "compare".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Try to compare this result to what we got last time.
   - How does this feel compared to your old workflow, with respect to effort?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We compare the two algorithms with respect to both accuracy and runtime.
   - Outcomes were compared to the control group with respect to key health indicators.

1. **Verb**: optimize
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make something as good, effective, or functional as possible, often with respect to a chosen objective.
   
   **Synonyms**: improve, fine-tune, refine, maximize, streamline
   
   **Antonyms**: degrade, worsen, neglect, ignore trade-offs
   
   **When to Use**: In technical, business, or planning contexts when trying to choose settings or designs that are best with respect to specific objectives ("optimize with respect to cost," "optimize with respect to latency").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk where "make better" is enough; avoid implying mathematical precision where there is only rough improvement.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - optimize with respect to
   - optimize X for Y
   - optimize parameters
   
   **Root Analysis**: optim(us) (best) + -ize (make)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *optimus* (best) → English "optimum" → verb "optimize" (make best).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Were trying to optimize our schedule with respect to everyones time.
   - Lets optimize this trip with respect to cost and fun.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Parameters are optimized with respect to cross-entropy loss on the validation set.
   - Release cadence should be optimized with respect to both risk and expected value.
