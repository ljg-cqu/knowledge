1. **Verb**: substitute (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To use or put someone or something in place of another person or thing; to replace X with Y
   - (I) To act or serve as a substitute for someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: replace, exchange, swap, switch, trade
   
   **Antonyms**: maintain, keep, retain, preserve
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts and registers; when describing replacement actions; sports, cooking, work contexts; emphasizes putting one thing instead of another
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When both items coexist; when describing additions rather than replacements; preposition usage can confuse ("substitute X for Y" means replace Y with X)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - substitute X for Y (replace Y with X)
   - substitute for someone
   - substitute one thing for another
   - substitute teacher
   - can be substituted
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under, in place of) + -stitute (to place, set)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin substituere (to put in place of another) → English "substitute" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can substitute honey for sugar in this recipe instead.
   - Can you substitute for me at work tomorrow instead of Thursday?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers substituted digital surveys for paper questionnaires instead of maintaining both formats.
   - Renewable materials may be substituted for conventional resources instead of depleting finite supplies.

1. **Verb**: replace (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To take the place of; to provide or find a substitute for something; to put something new instead of something old or broken
   - (T) To succeed or take over from someone in a position or role
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute, supplant, supersede, displace, exchange
   
   **Antonyms**: maintain, keep, retain, preserve
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts; emphasizing removal of old and installation of new; when something is removed and another takes its place instead
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the original continues to exist alongside the new; when describing additions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - replace X with Y
   - replace old with new
   - need to replace
   - replace someone
   - eventually replace
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + place (to put, position)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French replacer (to put back) → English "replace" (16th century, meaning "put back in place," later "substitute")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to replace my phone instead of getting it repaired.
   - They replaced the manager instead of giving her another chance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Traditional methodologies were replaced with innovative approaches instead of incremental modifications.
   - Manual processes have been replaced by automated systems instead of retaining legacy procedures.

1. **Verb**: choose (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To select from a number of possibilities; to decide on something instead of alternatives
   - (T) To prefer or decide to do something rather than something else
   
   **Synonyms**: select, pick, opt for, decide on, prefer
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, refuse, decline, avoid
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts; when making selections or decisions; emphasizes the act of selection between options where one is picked instead of others
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no alternatives exist; when both/all options are selected
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - choose X over Y
   - choose instead of
   - choose to do X
   - choose between X and Y
   - choose wisely
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English ceosan (to choose, select)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ceosan → Middle English chosen → Modern English "choose"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I chose the blue one instead of the red.
   - She chose to stay home instead of going to the party.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee chose strategic focus instead of diversification.
   - Participants predominantly chose online participation instead of in-person attendance.

1. **Verb**: prefer (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To like one thing or person better than another; to choose something instead of an alternative because you like it more
   - (T) To submit or present formally (rare, formal meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: favor, like better, choose, opt for, would rather have
   
   **Antonyms**: dislike, reject, avoid, disfavor
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts; expressing preferences and likes; when one option is more appealing; implies choosing one instead of another based on preference
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no preference exists; when both options are equally liked; when choice is forced rather than preferential
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prefer X to Y
   - prefer X over Y
   - prefer to do X
   - would prefer
   - prefer instead
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + fer (to carry, bear)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praeferre (to set before, esteem more) → French préférer → English "prefer" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I prefer tea instead of coffee in the morning.
   - She prefers walking instead of driving when possible.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations increasingly prefer digital documentation instead of paper-based systems.
   - Researchers prefer qualitative methods instead of quantitative approaches for exploratory studies.

1. **Verb**: opt (intransitive, usually "opt for")
   **Meaning**: 
   - (I) To make a choice or decision from a range of possibilities; to choose to do something instead of alternatives (typically followed by "for" or infinitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: choose, select, decide on, go for, pick
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, refuse, decline, avoid
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral to formal contexts; when describing deliberate choices; business and academic contexts; emphasizes decision-making process of selecting one instead of others
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts (use "choose" or "pick"); when choice is not deliberate or conscious
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - opt for X instead of Y
   - opt to do X
   - opt for X over Y
   - opt out (choose not to participate)
   - decide to opt for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin optare (to choose, wish)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin optare (to choose, desire) → French opter → English "opt" (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I opted for the salad instead of the burger.
   - They opted to stay home instead of traveling.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The majority of participants opted for digital delivery instead of physical materials.
   - Management opted for gradual implementation instead of comprehensive restructuring.

1. **Verb**: select (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To carefully choose someone or something from a group as being the best or most suitable instead of alternatives
   - (T) To pick out with care from a number of options
   
   **Synonyms**: choose, pick, opt for, single out, elect
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, exclude, eliminate, discard
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral to formal contexts; when emphasizing careful or deliberate choice; professional and academic settings; implies thoughtful process of choosing one instead of others
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts (use "pick" or "choose"); when choice is random or careless
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - select X over Y
   - select from options
   - select instead of
   - carefully select
   - select for quality
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin selectus (past participle of seligere, to choose out)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin seligere (to choose out) → English "select" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I selected the chicken instead of the fish.
   - She selected the morning class instead of afternoon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee selected qualitative methodologies instead of quantitative approaches.
   - Participants were selected based on experience instead of credentials alone.

1. **Verb**: pick (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To choose someone or something from a group; to select informally (less formal than "choose" or "select")
   - (T) To take or gather (has other meanings: pick fruit, pick a lock)
   
   **Synonyms**: choose, select, go for, opt for, decide on
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, pass over, ignore, avoid
   
   **When to Use**: Casual to neutral contexts; everyday choices and decisions; when tone should be conversational; emphasizes choosing one instead of alternatives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal contexts (use "select"); academic or technical writing; when more precise language is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pick X over Y
   - pick instead of
   - pick and choose
   - pick out
   - pick one
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English pikken (to pick, peck)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English pikken → Modern English "pick" (meaning "choose" developed in 15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I picked the blue shirt instead of the red one.
   - Let's pick the movie theater instead of staying home.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consumers increasingly pick sustainable options instead of conventional products.
   - Employees picked flexible schedules instead of traditional nine-to-five arrangements.

1. **Verb**: swap (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To exchange one thing for another; to give something and receive something in return; to substitute
   - (T) To replace one thing with another, especially temporarily
   
   **Synonyms**: exchange, trade, switch, substitute, replace
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, retain, maintain, hold onto
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to neutral contexts; when emphasizing mutual or reciprocal exchange; everyday transactions; using one instead of another, often temporarily
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal contexts (use "exchange" or "substitute"); when replacement is permanent or one-directional
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - swap X for Y
   - swap out X
   - swap places
   - swap shifts
   - swap instead
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English swappen (to strike, probably imitative)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English swappen (to strike a bargain) → "swap" (meaning exchange, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's swap seats instead of staying where we are.
   - I swapped my Tuesday shift for Wednesday instead.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Institutions swapped traditional currencies for digital assets instead of maintaining conventional holdings.
   - Organizations swapped hierarchical structures for networked models instead of incremental reforms.

1. **Verb**: exchange (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To give something and receive something else in return; to replace one thing with another of the same kind
   - (T) To substitute one thing for another, often implying mutual replacement
   
   **Synonyms**: swap, trade, switch, substitute, replace
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, retain, hold, maintain
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral to formal contexts; when emphasizing mutual or reciprocal replacement; business, diplomatic, academic contexts; using one instead of another
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When replacement is one-directional without reciprocity; very casual contexts (use "swap")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exchange X for Y
   - exchange instead
   - exchange ideas
   - exchange goods
   - in exchange
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + change (French changer, to change)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French eschangier → English "exchange" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I exchange this shirt for a different size instead?
   - They exchanged gifts instead of buying new ones.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations exchanged traditional practices for contemporary methodologies instead of maintaining status quo.
   - Participants exchanged paper documentation for digital records instead of duplicating formats.

1. **Verb**: switch (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T/I) To change from one thing to another; to exchange one thing for another; to replace with something different
   - (T) To substitute one for another, often quickly or suddenly
   
   **Synonyms**: change, swap, exchange, shift, replace
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, maintain, stay with, continue
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to neutral contexts; when emphasizing change or shift; often implies relatively quick substitution of one instead of another
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing (use "transition" or "change"); when emphasizing careful or gradual change
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - switch to X from Y
   - switch instead
   - switch places
   - switch gears
   - switch over
   
   **Root Analysis**: Related to Low German switsen (to whip, beat)
   
   **Etymology**: Originally meant "to strike" or "whip," later developed meaning "to shift" or "exchange" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm switching to tea instead of coffee.
   - Let's switch to Plan B instead of sticking with this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization switched to remote operations instead of maintaining physical offices.
   - Consumers switched to digital platforms instead of traditional retail channels.

1. **Verb**: adopt (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To choose to take up, follow, or use something; to take on or accept something instead of previous practices
   - (T) To formally approve or accept a proposal, policy, or course of action (also: to legally take another's child as one's own)
   
   **Synonyms**: embrace, take up, accept, implement, choose
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, abandon, discard, discontinue
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral to formal contexts; when describing acceptance of new methods, practices, or policies instead of old ones; emphasizes deliberate selection
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts; when change is forced rather than chosen; when referring to temporary changes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - adopt a new approach
   - adopt instead of
   - adopt measures
   - adopt a policy
   - adopt a strategy
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ad- (to) + optare (to choose)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin adoptare (to choose for oneself) → English "adopt" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should adopt the new system instead of the old one.
   - They adopted a vegetarian diet instead of eating meat.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board adopted revised protocols instead of maintaining legacy procedures.
   - Organizations increasingly adopt agile methodologies instead of waterfall approaches.

1. **Verb**: favor (transitive, American spelling) / favour (British spelling)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) To prefer or support one thing instead of another; to show preference for
   - (T) To treat with partiality; to give advantage to one option over alternatives
   
   **Synonyms**: prefer, support, choose, back, approve
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, disfavor, reject, disadvantage
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral to formal contexts; when expressing preference or support; when one option is given advantage instead of others
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When impartiality is important; very casual contexts (use "prefer" or "like better")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - favor X over Y
   - favor instead of
   - favor one option
   - tend to favor
   - favor an approach
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin favor (goodwill, support)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin favor (goodwill) → Old French favor → English "favor/favour" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I favor the simpler option instead of the complicated one.
   - She favors texting over calling instead.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Current research favors preventive interventions instead of reactive treatments.
   - Policymakers favor incremental reforms instead of comprehensive restructuring.

1. **Verb**: trade (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T/I) To exchange something for something else; to give something in return for something else; to substitute through exchange
   - (T) To swap or replace one thing with another through exchange
   
   **Synonyms**: exchange, swap, switch, substitute, barter
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, retain, hold, maintain
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to neutral contexts; when emphasizing exchange or reciprocal substitution; business, commerce, personal exchanges; using one instead of another through trade
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing; when replacement is not reciprocal; when monetary purchase is involved (unless explicitly a trade)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - trade X for Y
   - trade in X
   - trade instead
   - trade up/down
   - trade places
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle Low German (origin obscure, possibly related to "tread")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English trade (path, course) → developed meaning "exchange" (15th-16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll trade you my sandwich for your chips instead.
   - He traded his old car for a new one instead of selling it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations traded centralized control for distributed decision-making instead of maintaining hierarchies.
   - Investors traded short-term gains for long-term stability instead of pursuing aggressive strategies.
