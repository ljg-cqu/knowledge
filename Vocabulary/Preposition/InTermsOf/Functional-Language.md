1. **Functional Expression**: In terms of [noun], ... (function: framing/contextualizing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to specify the particular aspect, criterion, or dimension being considered in an evaluation or discussion
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (frames rather than directly states)
   
   **Synonyms**: as regards [noun], with respect to [noun], regarding [noun], concerning [noun], as far as [noun] is concerned
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (functional expression without clear opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, professional reports, business discussions, formal presentations when specifying evaluation criteria or analytical dimensions; helps organize complex comparisons
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds overly formal); avoid overuse as it can become wordy filler; not suitable when simpler direct statements work better
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in terms of [gerund] (in terms of achieving goals)
   - in [adjective] terms (in financial terms, in practical terms)
   - in terms of what/how/whether... (subordinate clause)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Serves as hedge/softener in some contexts; allows speaker to compartmentalize complex topics; can signal analytical thinking; overuse may appear evasive or non-committal
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, and in terms of [another aspect]... (adding dimension)
   - That's true in terms of [aspect], but... (partial agreement with qualification)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In terms of movies, I prefer comedies over dramas.
   - What are you looking for in terms of price range?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In terms of economic impact, the policy demonstrates significant positive externalities.
   - The framework can be evaluated in terms of three primary criteria: efficiency, equity, and sustainability.

1. **Functional Expression**: When it comes to [noun/gerund], ... (function: topic introduction/framing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a specific topic or aspect for discussion; signals transition to particular subject matter
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (preparatory phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: as for [noun], regarding [noun], in terms of [noun], as far as [noun] is concerned, concerning [noun]
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to neutral contexts; conversation, presentations, general business communication; effective for topic shifts or highlighting specific aspects
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing (prefer "regarding" or "with respect to"); avoid when direct statements are clearer
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - when it comes to [gerund] (when it comes to choosing)
   - when it comes down to [noun] (emphasizing ultimate factor)
   - when it came to [noun] (past tense narrative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: More conversational than "in terms of"; creates engagement by signaling topic importance; can emphasize speaker's expertise or opinion follows
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I agree, and when it comes to... (extending discussion)
   - True, but when it comes to... (contrasting aspect)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When it comes to cooking, I'm completely hopeless!
   - When it comes to reliability, this brand is the best.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When it comes to regulatory compliance, the organization maintains rigorous standards.
   - When it comes to implementing the strategy, careful planning is essential.

1. **Functional Expression**: As far as [noun] is concerned, ... (function: topic specification/limitation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to specify the particular aspect or topic being addressed; often signals limiting scope to one dimension
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (delimiting phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding [noun], concerning [noun], in terms of [noun], with respect to [noun], as regards [noun]
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Formal business communication, professional writing, when clarifying scope or boundaries of discussion; emphasizes focusing on one specific aspect
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal); avoid when making universal claims without limitation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as far as [noun] goes (slightly less formal)
   - as far as I'm concerned (expressing personal opinion - different function)
   - as far as [clause] is concerned
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals speaker is delimiting scope; often precedes statement that may not apply universally; helps manage expectations about claim boundaries
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That may be true as far as [aspect] is concerned, but... (acknowledging limited scope)
   - As far as [different aspect] is concerned... (contrasting dimension)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As far as the schedule goes, we should be fine.
   - As far as money is concerned, we're doing okay.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As far as technical feasibility is concerned, the proposal appears viable.
   - As far as legal liability is concerned, the contract provides adequate protections.

1. **Functional Expression**: From a [noun] perspective, ... (function: framing viewpoint)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce an analytical viewpoint or evaluative framework; signals which lens or criterion is being applied
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (framing device)
   
   **Synonyms**: from a [noun] standpoint, from a [noun] point of view, in [adjective] terms, with respect to [noun]
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Formal analysis, academic writing, professional presentations, strategic planning; particularly useful when comparing multiple perspectives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal); avoid overuse in single document (becomes repetitive)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - from a [adjective] perspective (from a strategic perspective)
   - from my/our/their perspective (subjective viewpoint)
   - from the perspective of [entity] (stakeholder viewpoint)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals analytical thinking; implies other perspectives exist; useful for hedging or softening claims; shows consideration of multiple viewpoints
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's valid from that perspective, but from a [different] perspective... (contrasting viewpoints)
   - I can see it from that perspective too. (acknowledging validity)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - From a student perspective, the policy is pretty unfair.
   - From a health perspective, you should probably cut back.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - From a financial perspective, the investment demonstrates favorable risk-return characteristics.
   - From an environmental sustainability perspective, the initiative represents significant progress.

1. **Functional Expression**: [Noun]-wise, ... (function: aspect specification)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Informal way to specify which aspect or dimension is being discussed; attaches to noun to create aspectual adverb
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct (concise framing)
   
   **Synonyms**: in terms of [noun], regarding [noun], as for [noun], concerning [noun]
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Casual conversation, informal workplace communication, quick evaluations; efficient for rapid aspect specification
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal writing, academic papers, official reports, professional presentations to senior stakeholders; considered too colloquial
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - price-wise, quality-wise, time-wise (most common)
   - flexibility-wise, design-wise, performance-wise
   - [any noun]-wise (productive suffix)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Highly efficient and common in American English spoken discourse; less favored in British English formal contexts; shows informal register competence
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yeah, but design-wise, it could be better. (contrastive addition)
   - True, and price-wise it's a great deal! (agreement extension)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Price-wise, this is the best deal you'll find.
   - Time-wise, we're actually ahead of schedule.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically avoided in formal contexts; use "in terms of" or "with respect to" instead*

1. **Functional Expression**: With respect to [noun], ... (function: formal topic introduction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Formal expression used to introduce specific topic or aspect for consideration; highly professional tone
   - *Formality*: formal to very formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (preparatory phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding [noun], concerning [noun], in terms of [noun], as regards [noun], with regard to [noun]
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Legal documents, formal business correspondence, academic writing, technical reports; conveys professionalism and precision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (overly formal); informal emails to colleagues; may sound stiff in everyday contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - with respect to [gerund] (with respect to implementing)
   - with respect to the [noun phrase] (with respect to the aforementioned issue)
   - with all due respect (different function - polite disagreement)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conveys respect and formality; useful in hierarchical or formal contexts; helps maintain professional distance; common in legal and technical writing
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - With respect to that point, I concur. (formal agreement)
   - With respect to your concerns, we have addressed... (formal acknowledgment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *Rarely used; would sound overly formal: "With respect to the party, what time should I arrive?" sounds awkward*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With respect to the proposed amendments, the committee recommends approval.
   - With respect to data security protocols, compliance has been verified.

1. **Functional Expression**: In [adjective] terms, ... (function: specifying evaluative dimension)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to specify the type of analysis or evaluation being applied; converts adjective to analytical lens
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (framing device)
   
   **Synonyms**: from a [adjective] perspective, in terms of [noun], regarding [noun]
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, business analysis, professional presentations when specifying evaluation type (financial, practical, strategic, etc.)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation; when simpler direct statement would suffice
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in financial terms, in practical terms, in strategic terms
   - in simple/plain terms (meaning: explained simply - different function)
   - in absolute/relative terms
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Allows compact specification of analytical dimension; common in business and academic discourse; helps organize multi-faceted analysis
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's true in practical terms, but in strategic terms... (contrasting dimensions)
   - In financial terms, yes, but consider... (acknowledging then extending)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In simple terms, we're basically out of money.
   - In practical terms, this won't work at all.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In economic terms, the intervention demonstrates significant cost-effectiveness.
   - In strategic terms, the partnership represents a critical competitive advantage.

1. **Functional Expression**: How does this look/work/compare in terms of [noun]? (function: requesting specific evaluation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Question form requesting evaluation or comparison using specific criterion or dimension
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct question with indirect framing
   
   **Synonyms**: What about [noun]?, How is the [noun]?, What's the [noun] like?, How does it fare regarding [noun]?
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Business meetings, planning sessions, evaluative discussions when seeking specific dimensional analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simpler questions work ("How much does it cost?" vs. "How does it look in terms of cost?"); avoid in very casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How are we doing in terms of [noun]?
   - How does X compare to Y in terms of [noun]?
   - What does this mean in terms of [noun]?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals analytical thinking; helps focus discussion on specific criteria; useful for structured evaluation processes
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - In terms of [noun], it looks good/problematic/... (direct answer)
   - Well, in terms of [noun], we're seeing... (elaborated response)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How does this look in terms of our budget?
   - How are we doing in terms of time?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - How does the proposal compare to alternatives in terms of cost-effectiveness?
   - How does the intervention perform in terms of measurable outcomes?

1. **Functional Expression**: Let me put this in terms of [noun/analogy] (function: translating/simplifying)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used when reformulating complex information using different framework or simpler language; signals translation or reframing
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (preparatory phrase for reformulation)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let me explain this using [noun], To put it simply, Let me frame this as [noun], In other words
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Teaching contexts, presentations, when bridging technical and non-technical audiences; useful for ensuring understanding
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When audience already understands; may seem condescending if overused; avoid when precision of original formulation is essential
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let me put it in simple terms
   - To put it in terms of [familiar concept]
   - If I put this in terms you understand
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows speaker awareness of audience needs; useful pedagogical device; can build rapport by showing concern for comprehension; potential face-threat if audience feels patronized
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Ah, I see now. (understanding achieved)
   - That makes sense in those terms. (acknowledgment of reframing)

   - Let me explain this in terms you'll understand...
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:
