1. **Adjective**: measurable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Able to be measured or quantified; capable of being assessed in numerical or objective terms (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: quantifiable, assessable, calculable, determinable, computable
   
   **Antonyms**: immeasurable, unquantifiable, intangible, indefinite
   
   **When to Use**: Use "measurable" when discussing outcomes, results, or qualities that can be expressed in terms of specific metrics or units; suitable for formal, technical, and business contexts; emphasizes objectivity and precision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing subjective experiences or emotions that resist quantification; less appropriate for casual conversation about personal feelings
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - measurable results
   - measurable outcomes
   - measurable progress
   - measurable impact
   - clearly measurable
   
   **Root Analysis**: measure (verb/noun) + -able (suffix meaning "capable of being")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin mensura (measure) → Old French mesure → Middle English "measure" → "measurable" (14th century), emphasizing capability of quantification
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We've seen measurable improvements in his grades this semester.
   - The difference is measurable—you can actually see it in the numbers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project objectives must be defined in measurable terms to facilitate evaluation.
   - Measurable outcomes serve as critical indicators of program effectiveness.

   **Synonyms**: numerical, statistical, mathematical, metric, measurable
   
   **Antonyms**: qualitative, descriptive, subjective, non-numerical
   
   **When to Use**: Use "quantitative" in academic, scientific, or business contexts when referring to data, analysis, or measurements expressed in numbers; emphasizes objective measurement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing subjective qualities, experiences, or descriptive characteristics; inappropriate for casual conversation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - quantitative analysis
   - quantitative data
   - quantitative research
   - quantitative methods
   - quantitative assessment
   
   **Root Analysis**: quant (how much) + -itative (suffix forming adjectives from nouns ending in -ity)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin quantitas (quantity) → Medieval Latin quantitativus → English "quantitative" (17th century), emphasizing numerical measurement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're looking at it from a more quantitative angle now, with actual numbers.
   - The quantitative stuff shows we're doing better than last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study employs quantitative methods to assess the relationship between variables.
   - Quantitative indicators provide objective evidence of performance trends.
