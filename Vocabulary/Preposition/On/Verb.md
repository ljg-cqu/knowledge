1. **Verb**: rely on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To need and trust someone or something to provide support, help, or perform a necessary function; to depend on for success or survival
   
   **Synonyms**: depend on, count on, bank on, trust in, lean on
   
   **Antonyms**: distrust, doubt, be independent of, manage without
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage in all contexts—personal relationships, professional settings, technical descriptions, academic writing. Essential for expressing dependency and trust relationships.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot separate the verb from "on" (*rely something is incorrect; must be rely on something)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rely heavily on
   - rely entirely on
   - rely solely on
   - can always rely on
   - rely on someone to do something
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (intensive prefix) + -ly (from Latin "ligare" = bind)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "religare" (to bind fast, tie back) → Old French "relier" → English "rely". The preposition "on" became fixed with this verb in Middle English, creating the sense of being bound to or supported by something.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I rely on my alarm clock to wake me up every morning.
   - You can rely on Tom—he never lets anyone down.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The success of this initiative relies on the cooperation of all stakeholders involved.
   - Modern infrastructure increasingly relies on digital technologies for operational efficiency.

1. **Verb**: focus on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To concentrate attention, effort, or resources on a particular subject, activity, or goal; to make something the center of one's attention
   
   **Synonyms**: concentrate on, center on, zero in on, pay attention to, prioritize
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, neglect, lose focus, be distracted from
   
   **When to Use**: Essential in academic, professional, and instructional contexts. Used for directing attention, setting priorities, describing scope of work, or discussing mental concentration.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot use without "on" when indicating what is being focused on (*focus the problem is incorrect; must be focus on the problem)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - focus on the task
   - focus on what matters
   - need to focus on
   - focus on the positive
   - sharply focused on
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "focus" (hearth, fireplace, center point)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "focus" (hearth) → English "focus" (1600s, originally optical term for point of convergence). Extended to mental concentration by 1800s. "On" indicates the target or point where attention converges.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you please focus on the road while you're driving?
   - I need to focus on studying for my exam this weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This research focuses on the environmental impact of industrial agriculture.
   - The committee has decided to focus on three primary objectives for the fiscal year.

1. **Verb**: depend on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To need something or someone in order to survive, function, or succeed; to be determined or influenced by something; to trust or have confidence in
   - *(Sense 2)* To be contingent upon; to vary according to circumstances
   
   **Synonyms**: rely on, rest on, hinge on, be contingent on, be determined by
   
   **Antonyms**: be independent of, be unaffected by, stand alone
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage across all registers. Essential for expressing necessity, conditional relationships, trust, and causal dependencies.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot separate verb from "on" in this meaning (*depend something is incorrect)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - depend heavily on
   - depend entirely on
   - it depends on
   - depend on someone/something for
   - whether...depends on
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down, from) + -pend (from Latin "pendere" = hang)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "dependere" (to hang from, hang down) → Old French "dependre" → Middle English "depend". The metaphor of hanging from something created the sense of being supported by or relying on it. "On" has been the fixed preposition since Middle English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My weekend plans depend on the weather forecast.
   - Kids depend on their parents for food, shelter, and love.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The viability of the project depends on securing adequate funding from investors.
   - Whether the policy succeeds depends on multiple socioeconomic factors.

1. **Verb**: insist on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To demand something firmly and refuse to accept any alternative; to state or assert something forcefully despite opposition; to require as a necessary condition
   
   **Synonyms**: demand, require, stipulate, maintain firmly, stand firm on
   
   **Antonyms**: yield, compromise, accept alternatives, be flexible about
   
   **When to Use**: Used when someone makes a firm demand or requirement, refuses alternatives, or maintains a position strongly. Common in negotiations, personal boundaries, and formal requirements.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May sound inflexible or stubborn in contexts requiring diplomacy. Cannot use different prepositions with this meaning (*insist about/at are incorrect)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - insist on doing something
   - insist on the truth
   - insist on quality
   - continue to insist on
   - absolutely insist on
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (on, upon) + -sist (from Latin "sistere" = stand, take a stand)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "insistere" (to stand on, persist, urge) → French "insister" → English "insist" (1500s). The "on" preposition reinforces the Latin prefix "in-" (on, upon), emphasizing firmness and persistence.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My mom always insists on paying when we go out to eat.
   - I insist on seeing the manager right now!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legal team insists on reviewing all contracts before execution.
   - The committee insists on maintaining the highest ethical standards throughout the investigation.

1. **Verb**: work on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To devote time and effort to doing, making, or improving something; to attempt to repair, develop, or complete something
   - *(Sense 2)* To attempt to persuade or influence someone
   
   **Synonyms**: labor on, toil at, develop, improve, refine, tackle
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, abandon, ignore, leave unfinished
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage for describing ongoing tasks, projects, self-improvement, repairs, or persuasion efforts. Essential in professional and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: The phrasal verb "work on" changes meaning without "on" ("work" alone means to have employment)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - work on a project
   - work on oneself/one's skills
   - work on fixing something
   - currently working on
   - need to work on
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "wyrcan" (to create, perform)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wyrcan/worc" → Middle English "worken" → Modern English "work". The addition of "on" creates a phrasal verb indicating directed effort toward a specific task or object.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm working on my presentation for tomorrow's meeting.
   - You really need to work on your listening skills!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team is currently working on developing a new vaccine.
   - The diplomat worked on building consensus among the opposing parties.

1. **Verb**: comment on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To make remarks or express opinions about something; to provide explanation or critical notes on a text, situation, or event
   
   **Synonyms**: remark on, observe about, note, discuss, offer an opinion on
   
   **Antonyms**: remain silent about, ignore, refrain from discussing
   
   **When to Use**: Common in journalism, academic discourse, social media, and everyday conversation when expressing opinions or reactions to events, statements, or situations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot use different prepositions (*comment about is less standard; comment on is the fixed form)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - refuse to comment on
   - declined to comment on
   - comment on the situation
   - feel free to comment on
   - invited to comment on
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (with, together) + Latin "mentum" (from "mens" = mind)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "commentari" (to interpret, annotate, meditate on) → French "commenter" → English "comment" (1500s). "On" indicates the target or subject of the commentary.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you see how many people commented on my Instagram post?
   - I don't want to comment on the rumors until I know the facts.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The CEO declined to comment on the ongoing merger negotiations.
   - Several experts were invited to comment on the implications of the new policy.

1. **Verb**: decide on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To make a choice or selection from available options; to reach a conclusion about which option to choose
   
   **Synonyms**: choose, select, settle on, opt for, determine, pick
   
   **Antonyms**: be undecided about, defer, postpone decision, remain uncertain
   
   **When to Use**: Common when discussing choice-making processes, particularly when there are multiple options. Used in personal decisions and professional contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Different from "decide to do something" (which doesn't use "on"). "Decide on" specifically requires an object representing the chosen option.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - decide on a plan
   - can't decide on
   - finally decided on
   - need to decide on
   - have you decided on
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (off, away) + -cide (from Latin "caedere" = cut)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "decidere" (to cut off, determine) → Old French "decider" → English "decide" (1400s). "On" indicates the specific option that is chosen or settled upon.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We couldn't decide on a restaurant, so we just ordered pizza.
   - Have you decided on a name for your baby yet?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board of directors has decided on a new strategic direction for the company.
   - After extensive deliberation, the committee decided on implementing a phased approach.

1. **Verb**: congratulate on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To express pleasure or approval to someone for an achievement, good fortune, or happy event; to offer felicitations
   
   **Synonyms**: praise for, commend on, compliment on, felicitate on, applaud for
   
   **Antonyms**: criticize for, condemn for, disparage
   
   **When to Use**: Used in social interactions, professional contexts, and formal ceremonies when acknowledging someone's success, achievement, or good news.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot use different prepositions (*congratulate for is incorrect in standard English; must use "on")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - congratulate someone on their success
   - congratulated him on winning
   - would like to congratulate you on
   - congratulate yourself on
   - warmly congratulated on
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (with, together) + gratul- (from Latin "gratus" = pleasing, thankful) + -ate
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "congratulari" (to wish joy together) → English "congratulate" (1500s). "On" indicates the specific achievement or event being celebrated.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I want to congratulate you on your new job—that's awesome!
   - Everyone congratulated them on their engagement.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The President congratulated the team on their outstanding scientific achievement.
   - We would like to congratulate the recipients on receiving this prestigious award.

1. **Verb**: build on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To use something as a foundation or starting point for further development; to expand or develop from existing work, knowledge, or success
   
   **Synonyms**: expand on, develop from, extend, elaborate on, grow from
   
   **Antonyms**: start from scratch, discard, abandon, ignore previous work
   
   **When to Use**: Common in academic research, business development, project management, and any context involving incremental progress or development. Essential for describing evolutionary processes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Different from "build" alone (physical construction). "Build on" emphasizes using something existing as a foundation.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - build on previous work
   - build on success
   - build on the foundation of
   - continue to build on
   - build on existing knowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "byldan" (to construct)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "byldan" → Middle English "bilden" → Modern English "build". The phrasal verb "build on" emphasizes using something as a base or foundation, with "on" indicating the supporting structure.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's build on the ideas we discussed last week and take them further.
   - The sequel builds on the success of the original movie.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This research builds on the groundbreaking work of previous scholars in the field.
   - The strategic plan builds on the organization's core strengths and market position.

1. **Verb**: reflect on
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To think carefully and deeply about something, especially past experiences, situations, or ideas; to contemplate or consider thoughtfully
   
   **Synonyms**: contemplate, ponder, think about, meditate on, consider, mull over
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, dismiss, act without thinking, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Common in personal development, academic writing, philosophical discussions, and professional self-assessment. Essential for expressing introspection and thoughtful consideration.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Different from "reflect" alone which means to mirror/show an image. "Reflect on" specifically means to think about something.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take time to reflect on
   - reflect on one's experiences
   - reflect on the meaning of
   - pause to reflect on
   - reflect deeply on
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + -flect (from Latin "flectere" = bend)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "reflectere" (to bend back, turn back) → Old French "reflecter" → English "reflect" (1400s). The metaphor of bending back one's thoughts creates the sense of contemplation. "On" indicates the subject of contemplation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been reflecting on our conversation and I think you were right.
   - Take some time to reflect on what you really want in life.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study participants were asked to reflect on their decision-making processes.
   - This anniversary provides an opportunity to reflect on the organization's achievements and challenges.
