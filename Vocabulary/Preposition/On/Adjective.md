1. **Adjective**: based on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Founded on or derived from something; using something as evidence, foundation, or source; established according to particular criteria or information (predicative use with "on")
   
   **Synonyms**: founded on, grounded in, derived from, built on, established on
   
   **Antonyms**: unrelated to, independent of, disconnected from, baseless
   
   **When to Use**: Universal usage across all registers. Essential in academic writing, analysis, decision-making, and explaining rationale. Extremely common structure in English.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used without "on" in this meaning (must specify what something is based on)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - based on evidence
   - based on the fact that
   - decision based on
   - argument based on
   - policy based on
   
   **Root Analysis**: base (Greek "basis" through Latin) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "basis" (foundation) → Latin "basis" → French "base" → English "base" (1300s). The past participle "based" with "on" creates the sense of something being positioned or founded on a particular foundation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My opinion is based on my own personal experience, not just hearsay.
   - The movie is based on a novel I read in high school.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conclusions are based on rigorous empirical analysis of the data.
   - Admission decisions are based on a holistic review of each applicant's qualifications.

1. **Adjective**: dependent on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relying on or determined by something or someone else; needing something in order to exist, function, or succeed; contingent upon (gradable, predicative use with "on")
   
   **Synonyms**: reliant on, contingent on, conditional on, subject to, hinging on
   
   **Antonyms**: independent of, autonomous from, self-sufficient, unaffected by
   
   **When to Use**: Common in academic, scientific, and technical contexts for expressing causal relationships and dependencies. Essential for describing conditional situations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used without specifying what something is dependent on
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - heavily dependent on
   - entirely dependent on
   - dependent on circumstances
   - dependent on external factors
   - economically dependent on
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down, from) + -pend (Latin "pendere" = hang) + -ent
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "dependens" (hanging from) → Old French "dépendant" → English "dependent" (1400s). The "on" preposition completes the sense of reliance or support.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Whether we go to the beach is dependent on the weather forecast.
   - I'm still financially dependent on my parents while I'm in college.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project's success is heavily dependent on securing adequate funding.
   - Agricultural productivity is critically dependent on climatic conditions and water availability.

1. **Adjective**: focused on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Concentrating attention, effort, or resources on something; directed toward a particular goal or subject; giving priority or emphasis to (non-gradable in this collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: concentrated on, centered on, dedicated to, committed to, intent on
   
   **Antonyms**: distracted from, unfocused, scattered, inattentive to
   
   **When to Use**: Common in professional contexts, goal-setting, strategic planning, and describing priorities. Essential for expressing dedication and concentration.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used without specifying what one is focused on
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sharply focused on
   - intensely focused on
   - remain focused on
   - stay focused on
   - laser-focused on (intensive)
   
   **Root Analysis**: focus (Latin "focus" = hearth) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "focus" (hearth, fireplace) → English "focus" (1600s as optical term). The adjective form "focused" with "on" emerged to describe directed attention or effort.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really focused on improving my grades this semester.
   - The team needs to stay focused on what they can control.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization remains focused on achieving its core mission and strategic objectives.
   - Research efforts are primarily focused on developing sustainable energy solutions.

1. **Adjective**: keen on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very interested in or enthusiastic about something; eager to do something; having a strong liking for (gradable, primarily British English, predicative use with "on")
   
   **Synonyms**: enthusiastic about, eager for, interested in, fond of, partial to
   
   **Antonyms**: uninterested in, averse to, reluctant about, disinclined toward
   
   **When to Use**: Primarily British English; less common in American English. Used in informal to semi-formal contexts to express interest, enthusiasm, or willingness.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May sound overly British in American English contexts. Cannot be used without "on" to express enthusiasm for something specific.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very keen on
   - not keen on
   - particularly keen on
   - always been keen on
   - not too keen on
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "cēne" (brave, bold) → Middle English "kene" → Modern English "keen"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "cēne" (brave, fierce, bold) → Middle English (extended to "sharp, acute") → Modern English "keen" (eager, enthusiastic). The use with "on" to indicate enthusiasm became established in British English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not really keen on going out tonight—I'm too tired.
   - She's always been keen on outdoor activities like hiking and camping.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee members were particularly keen on implementing sustainable practices.
   - Management is keen on exploring innovative approaches to employee development.

1. **Adjective**: intent on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Determined to do something; firmly resolved or set on achieving a goal; having one's attention or mind firmly fixed on (non-gradable, predicative use with "on")
   
   **Synonyms**: determined to, resolved to, set on, bent on, committed to
   
   **Antonyms**: indifferent to, undecided about, wavering on, hesitant about
   
   **When to Use**: Used across registers to describe strong determination or purpose. Common in both narrative and analytical contexts. Emphasizes firmness of purpose.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used without specifying what someone is intent on
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - seems intent on
   - intent on achieving
   - intent on proving
   - intent on destruction (negative)
   - clearly intent on
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (on, upon) + -tent (from Latin "tendere" = stretch, direct)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "intentus" (attentive, eager, stretched toward) → Old French "intent" → English "intent" (1300s). The use with "on" emphasizes the object or goal of the intention.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He seems intent on making everyone's life miserable today.
   - I'm intent on finishing this project before the deadline, no matter what.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization appears intent on pursuing aggressive expansion strategies.
   - Opposition forces remain intent on disrupting the peace negotiations.

1. **Adjective**: bent on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Determined or resolved to do something, especially something harmful or undesirable; having a strong fixed intention (non-gradable, predicative use with "on", often negative connotation)
   
   **Synonyms**: determined to, intent on, resolved to, set on, hell-bent on (intensive)
   
   **Antonyms**: dissuaded from, reluctant to, opposed to, unwilling to
   
   **When to Use**: Often used with negative or disapproving connotations (bent on destruction, bent on revenge). Can be neutral in some contexts. More common in literary or journalistic writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Carries stronger, sometimes negative connotations than "intent on." Avoid when neutral description is needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bent on revenge
   - bent on destruction
   - bent on proving
   - hell-bent on (intensive)
   - seems bent on
   
   **Root Analysis**: bend (Old English "bendan") + past participle "bent"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bendan" (to bend, curve) → Middle English "bent" (past participle). Extended metaphorically to mean "inclined toward" or "determined on" by 1500s, with "on" indicating the object of determination.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My little brother seems bent on annoying me every chance he gets.
   - They're bent on winning this game no matter what it takes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The opposition appears bent on obstructing any legislative progress.
   - Extremist factions remain bent on undermining the democratic process.

1. **Adjective**: reliant on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Depending on someone or something for support, help, or to function; needing something to exist or operate (gradable, predicative use with "on")
   
   **Synonyms**: dependent on, contingent on, counting on, banking on, trusting in
   
   **Antonyms**: independent of, self-sufficient, autonomous from, free from
   
   **When to Use**: Common in economic, technical, and relationship contexts. Used to describe dependencies and necessary conditions. Essential for discussing support structures.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used without specifying what something is reliant on
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - heavily reliant on
   - increasingly reliant on
   - too reliant on
   - less reliant on
   - overly reliant on
   
   **Root Analysis**: rely (from Latin "religare" = bind) + -ant (forming adjectives)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "religare" (to bind) → Old French "relier" → English "rely" + "-ant" suffix → "reliant" (1600s). The use with "on" specifies the source of dependency or support.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm way too reliant on my phone for everything these days.
   - We're heavily reliant on good weather for the outdoor event.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The economy remains heavily reliant on fossil fuel exports for revenue.
   - Modern communication systems are increasingly reliant on satellite technology.

1. **Adjective**: contingent on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Dependent on or subject to certain conditions; occurring or existing only if something else happens; conditional (non-gradable, formal, predicative use with "on")
   
   **Synonyms**: conditional on, dependent on, subject to, hinging on, predicated on
   
   **Antonyms**: unconditional, guaranteed, independent of, certain regardless
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts, legal documents, business negotiations, scientific writing. Essential for expressing conditional relationships and dependencies.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation. Cannot be used without specifying the condition.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contingent on approval
   - contingent on circumstances
   - entirely contingent on
   - contingent on the outcome
   - offer contingent on
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (with, together) + -ting (from Latin "tangere" = touch) + -ent
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "contingens" (touching, happening by chance) from "contingere" (to touch, befall) → English "contingent" (1300s). The use with "on" specifies the condition upon which something depends.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My acceptance of the job offer is contingent on them matching my salary requirements.
   - Our plans are contingent on whether my parents can watch the kids.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Final approval is contingent on successful completion of the environmental impact assessment.
   - The proposed merger is contingent on regulatory approval from multiple jurisdictions.

1. **Adjective**: hooked on (informal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Addicted to or very enthusiastic about something; unable to stop doing, using, or enjoying something (gradable, informal, predicative use with "on")
   
   **Synonyms**: addicted to, obsessed with, crazy about, into, fascinated by
   
   **Antonyms**: indifferent to, uninterested in, turned off by, averse to
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts, casual conversation. Can refer to positive enthusiasm (hooked on a TV show) or negative addiction (hooked on drugs). Very common in everyday speech.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for academic or professional writing. Avoid in formal contexts where "addicted" or "enthusiastic about" would be more appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - totally hooked on
   - get hooked on
   - got hooked on
   - hooked on drugs (negative)
   - hooked on a TV show (positive)
   
   **Root Analysis**: hook (catching device) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: "Hook" (Old English "hoc") → metaphorical use of being "caught" like a fish → "hooked on" emerged in 1920s American English, originally referring to drug addiction, later extended to any strong attachment or enthusiasm.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally hooked on that new Netflix series—I binged the whole thing!
   - My dad got hooked on crossword puzzles during retirement.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts*
   - Alternative: Individuals may develop dependencies on various substances or behaviors.

1. **Adjective**: fixated on
   **Meaning**: 
   - Obsessively focused on or preoccupied with something; unable to stop thinking about something; having an abnormal or unhealthy attachment to (gradable, often negative connotation)
   
   **Synonyms**: obsessed with, preoccupied with, fixated upon, consumed by, hung up on
   
   **Antonyms**: indifferent to, flexible about, open-minded about, unconcerned with
   
   **When to Use**: Used in psychological contexts, criticism, or describing excessive focus. Often carries negative connotations of obsession or inflexibility. Common in both informal and academic psychology.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies excessive or unhealthy focus; avoid when describing positive dedication (use "focused on" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overly fixated on
   - become fixated on
   - seem fixated on
   - unhealthily fixated on
   - fixated on the past
   
   **Root Analysis**: fix (Latin "fixus" = fastened) + -ate (verb suffix) + -ed
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "fixus" (fastened, fixed) → "fixate" (1880s, psychological term) → "fixated" (past participle used as adjective). Popularized by Freudian psychology. "On" indicates the object of obsessive focus.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's way too fixated on what other people think of him.
   - Why are you so fixated on that one negative comment? There were hundreds of positive ones!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The patient appears fixated on perceived slights from colleagues.
   - Critics argue the policy debate has become fixated on minor technical details rather than fundamental principles.
