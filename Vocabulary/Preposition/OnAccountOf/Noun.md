1. **Noun**: account (C/U, pl: accounts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A written or spoken description of events; a report or narrative of something that happened
   - *(Sense 2)* (C) A record of money received, paid, or owed; a financial arrangement with a bank or business
   - *(Sense 3)* (U) Consideration or regard; importance given to something (formal usage: "take account of," "on account of")
   
   **Synonyms**: report, narrative, description, record, statement, explanation
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A for most senses (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability; narrative contexts (account of events); financial contexts (bank account); formal causal contexts ("on account of"); professional and academic settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When more specific terms are needed (e.g., "story" vs "account" in casual storytelling)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - give an account of
   - take into account
   - on account of
   - bank account
   - by all accounts
   - call to account
   - settle accounts
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to, toward) + count (compute, reckon) + -t (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "aconter" (to count, reckon) → Latin "computare" (to calculate) → Middle English "account" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She gave us a detailed account of what happened at the party.
   - I need to check my bank account on account of some suspicious charges.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The witness provided a comprehensive account of the incident on account of direct observation.
   - Financial accounts must be reconciled on account of regulatory compliance requirements.

1. **Noun**: reason (C/U, pl: reasons)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A cause, explanation, or justification for an action, belief, or event; the motive behind something
   - *(Sense 2)* (U) The power of logical thinking and understanding; rational thought
   - *(Sense 3)* (U) Good judgment or common sense
   
   **Synonyms**: cause, explanation, justification, motive, rationale, grounds
   
   **Antonyms**: result, effect, consequence, outcome
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability across all registers; explaining causation; providing justification; logical discourse; everyday and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing effects rather than causes; highly technical causal analysis requiring more specific terminology
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - for this reason
   - the reason why
   - by reason of
   - good reason
   - stands to reason
   - within reason
   - no reason to
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, back) + -son (from Latin "ratio" meaning reckoning, calculation)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ratio" (reckoning, calculation, reason) → Old French "raison" → Middle English "reason" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the reason you're late? Traffic again?
   - She left on account of personal reasons she didn't want to discuss.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal was rejected on account of reasons articulated in the comprehensive evaluation report.
   - By reason of statutory requirements, compliance measures must be implemented systematically.

1. **Noun**: cause (C/U, pl: causes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A person, thing, or event that makes something happen; the reason for an action or condition
   - *(Sense 2)* (C) A principle, ideal, or movement that people support or fight for
   - *(Sense 3)* (U) Reasonable grounds for complaint, action, or feeling
   
   **Synonyms**: reason, origin, source, root, basis, grounds
   
   **Antonyms**: effect, result, consequence, outcome
   
   **When to Use**: All registers when discussing origins of events or conditions; philosophical and scientific discourse about causation; contexts involving principles or movements; legal contexts ("just cause")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing effects or results; when "reason" is more natural in context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the root cause
   - just cause
   - cause and effect
   - lost cause
   - common cause
   - for a good cause
   - cause for concern
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "causa" (reason, purpose, lawsuit)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "causa" (reason, purpose, legal case) → Old French "cause" → Middle English "cause" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What was the cause of the accident? Was it the weather?
   - The event was cancelled on account of causes beyond their control.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation identified multiple causal factors on account of which the system failure occurred.
   - Policy modifications are warranted on account of causes systematically documented through empirical research.

1. **Noun**: accountability (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The fact or condition of being accountable; responsibility for actions and decisions, with obligation to explain and justify them
   - *Used in contexts of governance, management, ethics, and professional responsibility*
   
   **Synonyms**: responsibility, answerability, liability, obligation
   
   **Antonyms**: irresponsibility, unaccountability, immunity, exemption
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and organizational contexts; governance discussions; ethical frameworks; performance management; compliance and regulatory contexts; leadership discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual everyday contexts about minor responsibilities; when simpler "responsibility" suffices
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ensure accountability
   - lack of accountability
   - accountability framework
   - accountability measures
   - demand accountability
   - personal accountability
   - financial accountability
   
   **Root Analysis**: account (explanation, reckoning) + -ability (capability, quality of being)
   
   **Etymology**: "Account" (13th century) + "-able" (15th century) → "accountable" → "accountability" (19th century, modern sense developed with organizational management theory)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There needs to be more accountability in how they spend the budget.
   - People want accountability on account of the mistakes that were made.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizational accountability frameworks must be strengthened on account of governance failures identified during the audit.
   - Public sector accountability is essential on account of fiduciary responsibilities to taxpayers and citizens.

1. **Noun**: consequence (C/U, pl: consequences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A result or effect of an action or condition, especially an unwanted or unpleasant one
   - *(Sense 2)* (U) Importance or significance (formal: "of consequence" = important)
   - *Often implies negative outcomes, though can be neutral*
   
   **Synonyms**: result, outcome, effect, repercussion, ramification, upshot
   
   **Antonyms**: cause, reason, origin, source
   
   **When to Use**: All registers when discussing results of actions; formal contexts emphasizing impacts; contexts involving responsibility for outcomes; when emphasizing seriousness of results
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing causes rather than effects; when neutral "result" is more appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - face the consequences
   - as a consequence of
   - suffer consequences
   - unintended consequences
   - serious consequences
   - of no consequence (unimportant)
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (with, together) + sequ (follow) + -ence (state, quality)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "consequentia" (that which follows) → Old French "consequence" → Middle English "consequence" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to face the consequences of your actions.
   - The delay had serious consequences on account of tight deadlines.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Policy decisions carry significant consequences on account of far-reaching implications for stakeholder interests.
   - The organization must address unintended consequences on account of inadequate impact assessment protocols.

1. **Noun**: justification (C/U, pl: justifications)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C/U) A good reason or explanation for something; the action of showing something to be right or reasonable
   - *Implies providing rationale or defense for actions, beliefs, or decisions*
   
   **Synonyms**: reason, grounds, rationale, explanation, defense, vindication
   
   **Antonyms**: condemnation, criticism, blame, accusation
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts requiring explanation of decisions; ethical and philosophical discussions; professional contexts defending choices; academic argumentation; legal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts where "reason" or "excuse" are more natural; when acknowledging wrong without defending it
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - without justification
   - provide justification
   - adequate justification
   - lack justification
   - moral justification
   - budgetary justification
   
   **Root Analysis**: justify (make just/right) + -ation (action, process)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "justificare" (make just) → Old French "justification" → Middle English "justification" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's your justification for being so late? Traffic isn't enough.
   - He provided justification on account of unexpected circumstances.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal requires comprehensive justification on account of significant budgetary implications.
   - Adequate justification must be provided on account of deviation from established protocols.

1. **Noun**: liability (C/U, pl: liabilities)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (U) The state of being legally responsible for something; obligation to pay or do something
   - *(Sense 2)* (C) A person or thing causing disadvantage or risk; a burden or handicap
   - *(Sense 3)* (C, usually plural) Financial debts or obligations
   
   **Synonyms**: responsibility, obligation, debt, burden, handicap, accountability
   
   **Antonyms**: asset, advantage, benefit, immunity, exemption
   
   **When to Use**: Legal contexts discussing responsibility; financial contexts (balance sheets); professional discussions of risk; contexts evaluating disadvantages; insurance and regulatory contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual everyday contexts about simple responsibilities; when describing assets or advantages
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - legal liability
   - financial liability
   - limited liability
   - potential liability
   - liability insurance
   - admit liability
   - liability issue
   
   **Root Analysis**: liable (legally bound) + -ity (state, quality)
   
   **Etymology**: French "liable" → "liability" (state of being liable) → English "liability" (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Having him on the team is becoming a liability on account of constant mistakes.
   - The company faces liability on account of the safety violations.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization incurred significant liability on account of contractual breaches documented during litigation.
   - Financial liabilities increased substantially on account of expanded operational commitments and market conditions.

1. **Noun**: rationale (C, pl: rationales)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A set of reasons or logical basis for a course of action or belief; the underlying reasoning or justification
   - *Emphasizes logical, systematic reasoning rather than simple reasons*
   
   **Synonyms**: reasoning, logic, grounds, basis, justification, explanation
   
   **Antonyms**: irrationality, unreason, arbitrariness
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts explaining systematic reasoning; professional decision-making contexts; policy discussions; contexts requiring articulation of logical foundations; strategic planning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual everyday explanations; when simple "reason" is more appropriate; highly emotional contexts where logic isn't primary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the rationale behind
   - provide a rationale
   - strategic rationale
   - theoretical rationale
   - clear rationale
   - underlying rationale
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "rationalis" (of reason, rational)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ratio" (reason, reckoning) → "rationalis" (reasonable) → French "rationale" → English "rationale" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the rationale for changing the schedule? Seems unnecessary.
   - She explained the rationale on account of efficiency concerns.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The strategic rationale must be articulated comprehensively on account of stakeholder approval requirements.
   - Research methodology requires clear rationale on account of peer review standards and disciplinary expectations.
