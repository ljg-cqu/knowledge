1. **Adjective**: accountable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Required to explain actions or decisions to someone; responsible for something and expected to justify it
   - *(Gradable, predicative preferred)* Someone who is accountable for something is held responsible for it and must be able to give a satisfactory explanation of it
   
   **Synonyms**: responsible, answerable, liable, obligated, bound
   
   **Antonyms**: unaccountable, irresponsible, exempt, free
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts when discussing responsibility, oversight, or obligation; formal situations involving authority relationships; legal and governance contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations about minor everyday responsibilities; contexts where "responsible" would be more natural; situations requiring informal tone
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - be held accountable
   - accountable to someone
   - accountable for actions
   - fully accountable
   - directly accountable
   
   **Root Analysis**: account (reckoning, explanation) + -able (capable of, subject to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → French → English. From Latin "computare" (to count) → Old French "aconter" → Middle English "accounten" + suffix "-able" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You're accountable for what happened on account of your decision to skip the meeting.
   - She's accountable to her boss, so she can't just do whatever she wants.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The director is accountable to the board on account of fiduciary responsibilities.
   - Public officials must remain accountable for their actions, particularly on account of taxpayer trust.

1. **Adjective**: causal
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to or acting as a cause; expressing or indicating a cause or reason
   - *(Non-gradable, typically attributive)* Used to describe relationships, factors, or explanations that show one thing causes another
   
   **Synonyms**: causative, contributory, responsible, explanatory, etiological
   
   **Antonyms**: resultant, consequential, effect-related, incidental
   
   **When to Use**: Academic and scientific writing when discussing cause-effect relationships; formal analysis of reasons and outcomes; philosophical or logical discourse
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Everyday casual conversation; when simpler terms like "causing" would suffice; informal explanations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - causal relationship
   - causal factor
   - causal link
   - causal explanation
   - causal analysis
   
   **Root Analysis**: caus(e) (that which produces an effect) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "causa" (reason, purpose, cause) → Medieval Latin "causalis" → English "causal" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's no clear causal connection between the two events, though they happened on account of similar circumstances.
   - Scientists are still looking for the causal factors on account of the complexity involved.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study establishes a causal relationship between exposure and outcomes on account of rigorous methodology.
   - Researchers must distinguish causal factors from correlational patterns on account of scientific validity requirements.

1. **Adjective**: consequential
   **Meaning**: 
   - Following as a result or consequence; important and significant with notable effects
   - *(Sense 2)* Having important consequences or effects; significant in impact or influence
   - *(Gradable, both attributive and predicative)*
   
   **Synonyms**: significant, important, momentous, weighty, substantial
   
   **Antonyms**: inconsequential, trivial, insignificant, negligible, unimportant
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts discussing important outcomes or effects; professional analysis of impacts; academic writing about significant results; situations emphasizing the gravity of consequences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual everyday conversation about minor matters; when discussing things with no notable impact; informal settings where "important" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - consequential decision
   - highly consequential
   - consequential impact
   - consequential damages
   - consequential changes
   
   **Root Analysis**: consequence (result) + -al (relating to) → consequent + -ial (characterized by)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "consequentia" (logical conclusion) → French "conséquent" → English "consequential" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is a pretty consequential choice on account of how much it'll affect your future.
   - The meeting turned out to be consequential on account of the decisions made there.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy changes are consequential on account of their far-reaching implications for stakeholders.
   - Such decisions are consequential on account of their potential to reshape institutional frameworks.

1. **Adjective**: attributable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Able to be regarded as caused by or resulting from something; capable of being ascribed or credited to a particular source or cause
   - *(Non-gradable, typically predicative)* Something is attributable to a cause when it can be explained as resulting from that cause
   
   **Synonyms**: ascribable, assignable, traceable, imputable, due to
   
   **Antonyms**: inexplicable, random, unattributable, unaccountable
   
   **When to Use**: Academic and analytical contexts when identifying causes; formal discussions of responsibility or credit; scientific and research writing establishing causation; professional analysis of factors
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation about obvious causes; when simpler phrasing like "caused by" is more appropriate; informal storytelling
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - directly attributable to
   - largely attributable to
   - attributable causes
   - attributable risk
   - primarily attributable
   
   **Root Analysis**: attribute (assign to a cause) + -able (capable of being)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "attributus" (assigned, bestowed) → "attribute" + "-able" → English "attributable" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The delay was attributable to bad weather on account of the storm system moving through.
   - Her success is largely attributable to hard work on account of her dedication.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The variance is statistically attributable to external factors on account of regression analysis findings.
   - Performance improvements are directly attributable to process changes on account of controlled experimental conditions.

1. **Adjective**: resultant
   **Meaning**: 
   - Occurring or produced as a result or consequence of something; arising from a particular cause
   - *(Non-gradable, typically attributive)* Describes something that comes about as an effect or outcome
   
   **Synonyms**: resulting, consequent, ensuing, following, derivative
   
   **Antonyms**: causal, causative, originating, initial, primary
   
   **When to Use**: Technical and academic writing describing outcomes; formal analysis of cause-effect relationships; scientific contexts discussing effects; professional discussions of consequences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Everyday informal conversation; when "resulting" is simpler and adequate; casual explanations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - resultant effect
   - resultant damage
   - resultant force
   - resultant consequences
   - resultant changes
   
   **Root Analysis**: result (outcome) + -ant (characterized by, performing action)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "resultare" (to spring back, rebound) → French "résultant" → English "resultant" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The resultant chaos happened on account of poor planning and communication.
   - They had to deal with the resultant problems on account of their earlier mistakes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The resultant structural modifications were necessary on account of engineering requirements.
   - Researchers observed resultant behavioral changes on account of environmental interventions.

1. **Adjective**: explanatory
   **Meaning**: 
   - Serving to explain or make something clear; providing clarification or reasons
   - *(Non-gradable, typically attributive)* Intended to describe, clarify, or account for something
   
   **Synonyms**: clarifying, illustrative, elucidatory, interpretive, informative
   
   **Antonyms**: confusing, obscuring, complicating, ambiguous
   
   **When to Use**: Academic and educational contexts; formal writing providing analysis or reasoning; professional communications offering justification; contexts requiring detailed clarification
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple casual conversation; when direct explanation without meta-description suffices; informal contexts where "explaining" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - explanatory note
   - explanatory power
   - explanatory framework
   - explanatory variable
   - explanatory statement
   
   **Root Analysis**: explain (make clear) + -atory (serving to, relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "explanare" (to make flat/clear) → "explain" + "-atory" → English "explanatory" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check the explanatory notes on account of some confusing terms in the document.
   - She added an explanatory paragraph on account of reader questions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The model demonstrates significant explanatory power on account of its comprehensive variable inclusion.
   - Appendix A provides explanatory materials on account of methodological complexity.

1. **Adjective**: liable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Legally responsible for something; required to do something or pay for damages
   - *(Sense 2)* Likely to experience or do something (usually undesirable)
   - *(Non-gradable in legal sense, gradable in probability sense)*
   
   **Synonyms**: responsible, accountable, answerable, obligated, susceptible
   
   **Antonyms**: exempt, immune, free, protected, safe
   
   **When to Use**: Legal and formal contexts discussing responsibility; professional situations involving obligations; formal discussions of probability or risk; contractual and regulatory contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual everyday situations about simple responsibilities; informal predictions; contexts where simpler terms like "likely" or "responsible" are more appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - held liable
   - liable for damages
   - liable to prosecution
   - strictly liable
   - jointly liable
   
   **Root Analysis**: From French "liable" → "lier" (to bind)
   
   **Etymology**: Anglo-French "liable" (bound) → Old French "lier" → Latin "ligare" (to bind) → English "liable" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You're liable to get in trouble on account of skipping class so often.
   - The company could be held liable on account of safety violations.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant is liable for compensatory damages on account of contractual breach.
   - Organizations are liable to regulatory sanctions on account of non-compliance with statutory requirements.

1. **Adjective**: responsible
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having a duty to deal with something or having control over someone; being the primary cause of something
   - *(Sense 2)* Capable of being trusted; showing good judgment and reliability
   - *(Gradable, both attributive and predicative)*
   
   **Synonyms**: accountable, liable, answerable, dependable, reliable
   
   **Antonyms**: irresponsible, unaccountable, unreliable, careless, reckless
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability across all registers; professional contexts discussing duties and obligations; casual contexts about everyday accountability; situations involving trustworthiness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When more specific legal terms like "liable" are required; overly formal contexts where "accountable" might be more precise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - responsible for
   - hold responsible
   - responsible party
   - socially responsible
   - responsible behavior
   
   **Root Analysis**: response (answer, reply) + -ible (capable of being)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "respondere" (to answer, pledge) → Old French "responsible" → English "responsible" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's responsible for the mistake on account of being the team lead.
   - You need to be more responsible, especially on account of having others depend on you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The department is responsible for policy implementation on account of its regulatory mandate.
   - Senior management remains responsible for strategic decisions on account of fiduciary duties.
