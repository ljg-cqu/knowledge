1. **Preposition**: on account of (type: phrasal preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Because of; due to; by reason of; used to introduce the cause or reason for something (formal register)
   
   **Synonyms**: because of (in context of causation), due to (in formal contexts), owing to (in equally formal contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal written communication (reports, official letters, academic writing); professional contexts requiring elevated register; official announcements and statements; contexts where formal causation needs precise expression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual everyday conversation (sounds overly formal or pretentious); informal emails among colleagues; spoken English except very formal speeches; modern casual business contexts where "because of" or "due to" are more appropriate
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on account of + [noun phrase indicating reason]
   - **Verb + prep**: cancelled on account of, delayed on account of, rejected on account of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A (not typically used with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: decision on account of, change on account of, delay on account of
   
   **Root Analysis**: on (prep: indicating basis/position) + account (n: reckoning, explanation) + of (prep: indicating source/origin)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English combination of "on" + "acount" (from Old French "aconter" - to explain, count) + "of" → developed meaning "by reason of" in formal English (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We cancelled on account of the weather. (sounds formal for casual speech; better: "because of the weather")
   - He's absent on account of illness. (acceptable but formal)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The merger was postponed on account of regulatory concerns requiring comprehensive due diligence.
   - Admission was denied on account of incomplete documentation submitted beyond established deadlines.

1. **Preposition**: of (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Expressing the relationship between a part and a whole; indicating belonging, origin, or material
   - *(Abstract)* Expressing the relationship between a cause and effect when combined with "on account" to form "on account of"
   - *(Abstract)* Used to indicate reference, concern, or connection
   
   **Synonyms**: from (in context of origin), about (in context of reference), concerning (in context of topic)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (prepositions typically don't have direct antonyms)
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability across all contexts; essential structural preposition; expressing possession, origin, material, reference, and relationship
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent; some constructions require different prepositions in different varieties of English
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: account of [events], because of, out of, on account of
   - **Verb + prep**: think of, consist of, approve of, dispose of
   - **Adjective + prep**: aware of, capable of, typical of, independent of
   - **Noun + prep**: account of, risk of, cause of, result of
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "of" (away, from)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "of" (away from, from) → Middle English "of" (expanded uses including possession, material, reference) → Modern English "of" (before 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Tell me about the events of yesterday.
   - I'm thinking of going on account of the invitation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation revealed patterns of systematic irregularities on account of which remedial measures are required.
   - Analysis of preliminary data necessitates revision of hypotheses on account of unexpected correlations.

1. **Preposition**: because of (type: compound preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* For the reason that; on account of; used to introduce cause or reason at all formality levels
   
   **Synonyms**: on account of (more formal), due to (semi-formal), owing to (formal), as a result of, thanks to
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability across all registers; both spoken and written English; clear causal explanations at any formality level; everyday reasoning and formal analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When extremely formal register specifically requires "on account of" or "owing to" for stylistic reasons; legal documents preferring more elevated language
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: because of + [noun phrase indicating cause]
   - **Verb + prep**: happened because of, cancelled because of, succeeded because of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A (not typically used with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: delay because of, problem because of, success because of
   
   **Root Analysis**: because (for the reason that) + of (indicating source/cause)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "bi cause" (by reason) + "of" → merged into compound preposition (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm late because of traffic—sorry about that!
   - The party got cancelled because of rain.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project timeline was extended because of unforeseen technical challenges requiring additional resources.
   - Operational efficiency declined because of resource constraints, which is further explained on account of budgetary limitations.

1. **Preposition**: due to (type: compound preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Caused by; attributable to; because of; used to indicate direct causal relationship (semi-formal to formal)
   
   **Synonyms**: because of (less formal), on account of (more formal), owing to (equally formal), caused by, attributable to
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and business writing; semi-formal to formal contexts; reports, announcements, and professional communications; when causation needs clear statement with professional tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (though increasingly common); some strict formal academic contexts prefer alternatives; after linking verbs in traditional formal writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: due to + [noun phrase indicating cause]
   - **Verb + prep**: postponed due to, cancelled due to, occurred due to
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A (not typically used with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: delay due to, change due to, failure due to
   
   **Root Analysis**: due (owed, attributable) + to (indicating direction/attribution)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "deu" (owed) from Latin "debere" (to owe) → English "due to" with causal meaning (15th century origin, causal sense 19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The flight's delayed due to mechanical issues.
   - We're staying home due to the weather.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Performance improvements are due to systematic process optimization implemented on account of strategic initiatives.
   - The variance is statistically significant due to methodological differences, which emerged on account of divergent sampling protocols.

1. **Preposition**: owing to (type: compound preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Because of; on account of; due to; used to introduce cause or reason in formal register
   
   **Synonyms**: because of (less formal), due to (equally formal), on account of (equally formal), by reason of (more formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal written English; official reports and documents; academic writing; British English formal contexts more than American; when formal causal explanation is stylistically appropriate
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds archaic or overly formal); informal writing; American English casual contexts; when contemporary alternatives like "because of" or "due to" are more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: owing to + [noun phrase indicating cause]
   - **Verb + prep**: cancelled owing to, postponed owing to, occurred owing to
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A (not typically used with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: delay owing to, change owing to, success owing to
   
   **Root Analysis**: owing (being indebted/attributable) + to (indicating attribution)
   
   **Etymology**: "Owe" from Old English "āgan" (to possess, own) → "owing to" meaning "attributable to" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The event was postponed owing to weather. (too formal for casual speech)
   - Better casual: The event was postponed because of weather.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation was extended owing to the complexity of financial irregularities, which necessitated scrutiny on account of regulatory requirements.
   - Access restrictions remain in effect owing to ongoing security assessments conducted on account of threat intelligence.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the reason, cause, or purpose; because of; in support of
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating duration or extent of time
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the beneficiary or recipient of something
   
   **Synonyms**: because of (in causal contexts), during (in temporal contexts), in favor of (in support contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: against (in context of support), despite (in context of reason)
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability; expressing purpose, reason, duration, benefit; all registers and contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent; some constructions require different prepositions
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for the reason, for account of (archaic), for this purpose
   - **Verb + prep**: account for, ask for, wait for, responsible for
   - **Adjective + prep**: responsible for, famous for, sorry for, accountable for
   - **Noun + prep**: reason for, need for, basis for, grounds for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (before, in front of, because of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" (before, on account of, in exchange for) → expanded meanings in Middle English (before 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I did it for you, on account of our friendship.
   - We waited for hours because of the delay.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology was selected for its rigor, on account of which results demonstrate enhanced validity.
   - Accountability mechanisms exist for ensuring compliance, particularly on account of regulatory mandates.

1. **Preposition**: by (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the agent or means of an action; through the agency or action of
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the method or manner of doing something
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating proximity or position near something
   
   **Synonyms**: through (in context of means), near (in spatial context), according to (in context of standard)
   
   **Antonyms**: without (in context of means), far from (in spatial context)
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability; expressing agency, means, method, proximity; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent; some agency constructions require different prepositions
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by reason of, by means of, by virtue of, by account
   - **Verb + prep**: caused by, affected by, influenced by, determined by
   - **Adjective + prep**: caused by, influenced by, affected by
   - **Noun + prep**: action by, decision by, judgment by
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "bī" (near, by, around)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bī" (near, around, through the agency of) → Middle English "by" (before 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The decision was made by the committee on account of budget concerns.
   - I learned about it by accident.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conclusion is supported by empirical evidence, particularly on account of methodological rigor demonstrated throughout the study.
   - Determinations made by the board reflect strategic priorities established on account of stakeholder consultations.

1. **Preposition**: from (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating the starting point of movement or location; away from
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating the starting point in time
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating source, origin, or cause of something
   
   **Synonyms**: out of (in context of source), since (in temporal context), because of (in causal context)
   
   **Antonyms**: to (in directional context), toward (in movement context)
   
   **When to Use**: Universal applicability; expressing origin, source, starting point, separation; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent; direction toward requires "to" instead
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from account of (rare), from the standpoint of, from the perspective of
   - **Verb + prep**: result from, stem from, derive from, suffer from
   - **Adjective + prep**: different from, distinct from, separate from, free from
   - **Noun + prep**: departure from, deviation from, distinction from
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "fram" (forward, from, away)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "fram" (forward, away from) → Middle English "from" (before 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The problem came from poor planning, which happened on account of rushed timelines.
   - She's from Boston originally.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings derive from comprehensive analysis conducted on account of methodological requirements specified in research protocols.
   - Deviation from established procedures occurred on account of exigent circumstances requiring immediate operational response.
