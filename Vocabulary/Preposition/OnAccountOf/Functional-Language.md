1. **Functional Expression**: On account of [reason], [result/action] (function: explaining/justifying)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to give a formal explanation or reason for something; indicates causation or justification for an action, decision, or outcome
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, because of, owing to, by reason of, as a result of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal written communication (reports, official letters, academic writing); professional explanations requiring elevated register; official announcements; contexts requiring precise causal language
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual everyday conversation (sounds overly formal); informal emails or messages; contexts where "because of" or "due to" are more appropriate; when speaking rather than writing in most contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - due to (less formal, more common)
   - because of (casual, universal)
   - owing to (equally formal, slightly archaic)
   - by reason of (very formal, legal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "On account of" adds formality and often appears in written rather than spoken English. It can sound pretentious in casual settings but demonstrates appropriate register awareness in formal contexts. Positions typically before the reason being explained.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acknowledgment: "I understand" / "That's understandable"
   - Further inquiry: "What specifically caused...?" / "Can you elaborate on...?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I couldn't go on account of being sick." (sounds overly formal for casual speech)
   - "We're late on account of traffic." (better: "because of traffic")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project timeline was extended on account of unforeseen regulatory requirements.
   - Admission was denied on account of incomplete documentation submitted beyond the deadline.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm afraid [negative statement] on account of [reason] (function: refusing/declining politely)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Softens bad news or refusal by expressing regret, then provides formal justification using "on account of"
   - *Formality*: formal to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (face-saving)
   
   **Synonyms**: Unfortunately [statement] because of, Regrettably [statement] due to, I'm sorry but [statement] owing to
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm pleased to confirm, I'm happy to say, Fortunately
   
   **When to Use**: Professional refusals or declining requests; delivering disappointing news in business contexts; maintaining politeness while explaining negative outcomes; formal customer service responses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Among close friends (too formal); when direct refusal is more appropriate; casual contexts; emergency situations requiring immediate clarity
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I regret to inform you that... on account of
   - Unfortunately... due to
   - I'm sorry, but... because of
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines softening device "I'm afraid" with formal causal explanation "on account of" to maintain professional tone while declining. The formality signals respect for the requester despite refusing.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting decline: "I understand, thank you for letting me know"
   - Negotiating: "Is there any alternative?" / "What if we...?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'm afraid I can't come on account of other plans." (too formal for friends)
   - "Sorry, I can't make it because I have other stuff going on." (better casual alternative)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'm afraid we cannot approve your request on account of current budget constraints.
   - I'm afraid the position has been filled on account of the extended candidate evaluation process.

1. **Functional Expression**: This is attributable to / This can be attributed to (function: explaining cause academically)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Formal way of stating that something is caused by or results from specific factors; often used with "on account of" style reasoning
   - *Formality*: very formal/academic
   - *Directness*: direct but academic
   
   **Synonyms**: This results from, This is due to, This stems from, This is caused by
   
   **Antonyms**: This causes, This leads to, This produces
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing and research papers; formal analysis and reports; scientific explanations; professional presentations discussing causation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; informal writing; when simpler causal language suffices; creative writing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - may be attributed to
   - largely attributable to
   - partially attributed to
   - can be traced to
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Attributable to" serves similar function to "on account of" in establishing formal causal relationships. Common in academic discourse where precise attribution of causes is essential. Often followed by detailed explanation.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "That's a valid attribution" / "The evidence supports that"
   - Challenge: "Have you considered alternative explanations?" / "What about...?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The problem is attributable to bad planning." (too formal for casual speech)
   - "It happened because we didn't plan well." (better casual version)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The observed variance is largely attributable to methodological differences, on account of divergent sampling protocols.
   - Performance improvements are directly attributable to training interventions implemented on account of identified skill gaps.

1. **Functional Expression**: Given that [situation], [consequence] on account of [reason] (function: reasoning/explaining with context)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Presents context or conditions, then explains consequence with formal justification; multi-layered causal explanation
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct but elaborated
   
   **Synonyms**: Considering that... due to, Since... because of, In light of the fact that... owing to
   
   **Antonyms**: Despite the fact that, Even though, Regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Complex professional explanations requiring context; formal analytical writing; business cases requiring layered reasoning; academic arguments
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple explanations; casual contexts; when brevity is needed; informal communication
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Considering that... on account of
   - In light of... due to
   - Taking into account that... because of
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines conditional/contextual framing with formal causal explanation. Useful when multiple factors or layers of reasoning need to be expressed. Shows sophisticated logical thinking.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "That follows logically" / "I see the connection"
   - Questioning: "But what about...?" / "Does that necessarily follow?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Given that it's raining, we're staying home on account of the roads being bad." (overly complex for casual)
   - "It's raining and the roads are bad, so we're staying home." (better casual version)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given that market conditions have deteriorated significantly, strategic repositioning is essential on account of competitive pressures.
   - Given that regulatory frameworks have evolved, compliance protocols must be updated on account of statutory obligations.

1. **Functional Expression**: We must/need to [action] on account of [compelling reason] (function: justifying necessary action)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expresses obligation or necessity, providing formal justification; emphasizes that action is required because of stated reason
   - *Formality*: formal to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly states necessity)
   
   **Synonyms**: We are required to... due to, It is necessary to... because of, We are obligated to... owing to
   
   **Antonyms**: We may choose to, We have the option to, It would be nice to
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts establishing requirements; business communications explaining mandatory actions; policy announcements; formal directives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Suggestions or recommendations (too strong); casual requests; contexts where collaborative language is better; when discussing options
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It is necessary to... on account of
   - We are required to... due to
   - must... because of (less formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines obligation modal with formal justification to establish non-negotiable requirements. The formality of "on account of" reinforces the seriousness and legitimacy of the obligation.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Compliance: "Understood, we'll proceed accordingly"
   - Clarification: "What specifically do we need to do?" / "By when?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We need to leave now on account of the traffic." (acceptable but slightly formal)
   - "We should go now because of traffic." (more natural casual)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We must suspend operations temporarily on account of mandatory safety inspections required by regulatory authorities.
   - Organizations need to implement enhanced protocols on account of updated compliance requirements.

1. **Functional Expression**: Regrettably/Unfortunately, [negative outcome] on account of [reason] (function: apologizing/expressing regret formally)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expresses formal regret about negative situation, then provides formal explanation; combines sympathy with justification
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (softened with regret expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: I regret that... due to, Sadly... because of, It is unfortunate that... owing to
   
   **Antonyms**: Happily, Fortunately, We're pleased that, Thankfully
   
   **When to Use**: Formal apologies and explanations; professional communications about negative outcomes; customer service responses to complaints; official bad news delivery
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual apologies to friends; when taking personal responsibility without deflecting; situations requiring immediate problem-solving rather than explanation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I regret to inform you that... on account of
   - We apologize that... due to
   - Sadly... because of (less formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Sentence adverb + formal causal explanation creates appropriate register for delivering bad news in professional contexts. Shows empathy while maintaining professional distance.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acceptance: "Thank you for explaining" / "I appreciate your transparency"
   - Disappointment: "This is very disappointing" / "What can be done about this?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Unfortunately, we can't go on account of the weather." (acceptable but formal)
   - "Sorry, we can't go because of the weather." (more natural casual)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regrettably, your application cannot be approved on account of incomplete documentation submitted beyond established deadlines.
   - Unfortunately, the event has been cancelled on account of unforeseen circumstances beyond our control.

1. **Functional Expression**: [Someone] was held accountable on account of [actions/decisions] (function: assigning responsibility formally)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Formally states that someone was made responsible or faced consequences, with explanation using elevated register
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicitly assigns accountability)
   
   **Synonyms**: was held responsible because of, was deemed liable due to, faced consequences owing to
   
   **Antonyms**: was excused despite, was exempted from, was forgiven for
   
   **When to Use**: Formal accountability discussions; professional disciplinary contexts; official reports on responsibility; legal and governance communications
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations about minor mistakes; when discussing shared responsibility; contexts requiring collaborative rather than punitive tone
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - held responsible due to
   - deemed accountable because of
   - faced consequences on account of
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Held accountable" + "on account of" creates formal, serious tone appropriate for official responsibility assignment. Often appears in formal reports, legal documents, or official statements.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acknowledgment: "That's appropriate given the circumstances"
   - Defense: "But what about mitigating factors?" / "Were all facts considered?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "He got in trouble on account of missing the deadline." (slightly formal)
   - "He got in trouble for missing the deadline." (more natural)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The executive was held accountable on account of fiduciary breaches discovered during the audit process.
   - Leadership faced consequences on account of systematic failures in oversight and governance protocols.
