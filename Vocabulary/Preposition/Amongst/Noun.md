1. **Noun**: consensus (U, pl: consensuses - though typically uncountable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - General agreement among a group of people; the collective opinion reached by a group as a whole; widespread agreement on a particular issue or matter
   - *Grammatical info*: primarily uncountable, though "consensuses" exists for multiple instances of agreement
   
   **Synonyms**: agreement, accord, harmony, unity, concurrence, unanimity
   
   **Antonyms**: disagreement, discord, dissent, conflict, division
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal contexts discussing group decision-making, collective opinion, or shared understanding. Common in academic, political, organizational contexts. Appropriate when emphasizing collective judgment.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to imposed or forced agreement (use "compliance"). Don't use when referring to only two parties (use "agreement"). Avoid claiming consensus when significant disagreement exists.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reach/achieve/build consensus
   - consensus amongst stakeholders/members
   - broad/strong/emerging consensus
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + *sensus* (Latin: feeling, perception)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *consensus* (agreement, accord) from *consentire* (feel together, agree) → English *consensus* (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I think we've reached consensus amongst everyone here about what to do next."
   - "There's a pretty strong consensus amongst the team that we need more time."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The committee achieved consensus amongst all stakeholders regarding the implementation framework."
   - "Broad consensus exists amongst scholars concerning the theoretical foundations of this approach."

2. **Noun**: diversity (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being diverse; variety; the inclusion of people from different social, ethnic, cultural, or other backgrounds; a range of different things
   - *Grammatical info*: uncountable
   
   **Synonyms**: variety, heterogeneity, multiplicity, range, assortment, pluralism
   
   **Antonyms**: uniformity, homogeneity, sameness, similarity, monotony
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal contexts discussing variation in populations, perspectives, or options. Common in academic, organizational, social contexts. Appropriate when discussing representation and inclusion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using as euphemism without specificity. Don't use when "variety" or "difference" would be clearer. Avoid when specific demographic categories should be named directly.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - diversity of perspectives/opinions/backgrounds
   - diversity amongst the population/membership
   - cultural/ethnic/gender diversity
   
   **Root Analysis**: di- (apart) + *versus* (Latin: turned) + -ity (noun suffix indicating state/quality)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *diversitas* (difference, contradiction) → Old French *diversité* → English *diversity* (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "There's a lot of diversity amongst the people in our program."
   - "The committee values diversity of opinions and backgrounds."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The study examined diversity amongst participants in terms of socioeconomic and educational backgrounds."
   - "The institution prioritizes diversity in faculty recruitment and student admissions."

3. **Noun**: disagreement (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Lack of consensus or approval; a situation in which people have different opinions and often argue; the state of differing or being at variance
   - *Grammatical info*: countable when referring to specific instances, uncountable when referring to the general state
   
   **Synonyms**: discord, dissent, dispute, conflict, difference of opinion, controversy
   
   **Antonyms**: agreement, consensus, accord, harmony, concurrence
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers to describe conflicting opinions or positions. Common in conflict discussions, mediation, debate contexts. Appropriate when acknowledging differing views.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "discussion" or "debate" would be less negative. Don't use when consensus actually exists. Avoid when specific nature of conflict should be specified.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disagreement amongst members/stakeholders
   - express/voice disagreement
   - point of disagreement/area of disagreement
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart, not) + agree (Old French *agreer*) + -ment (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: French *désagrément* → English *disagreement* (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "There's some disagreement amongst the group about which option to choose."
   - "We had a pretty big disagreement about how to handle the situation."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Significant disagreement persists amongst committee members regarding the proposed methodology."
   - "The analysis revealed substantial disagreement amongst experts concerning optimal intervention strategies."

4. **Noun**: cohort (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A group of people with a shared characteristic, especially age; a group of people banded together or treated as a group; in research, a group of subjects who share a defining characteristic
   - *Grammatical info*: countable
   
   **Synonyms**: group, batch, class, generation, peer group, contingent
   
   **Antonyms**: individual, singleton, outlier
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal academic, medical, or demographic contexts to describe groups sharing characteristics. Common in research, education, generational studies. Appropriate when emphasizing shared experience or timing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "group" or "class" is more natural. Don't use when precise group definition isn't relevant. Avoid when historical military meaning might cause confusion.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - age cohort, birth cohort
   - cohort study/analysis
   - amongst one's cohort/peers
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *cohors* (enclosed yard, company of soldiers)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *cohortem* (courtyard, retinue, company of soldiers) → French *cohorte* → English *cohort* (15th century, academic/research meaning 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "She's definitely one of the smartest amongst her cohort at the university."
   - "My cohort graduated last year during the pandemic."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "This participant ranks amongst the highest performers within the experimental cohort."
   - "The longitudinal study tracked three cohorts across a fifteen-year period."

5. **Noun**: peer (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person of the same age, status, or ability as another specified person; someone equal in standing or belonging to the same social group
   - *Grammatical info*: countable, commonly used in plural "peers"
   
   **Synonyms**: equal, contemporary, colleague, counterpart, fellow, compeer
   
   **Antonyms**: superior, subordinate, junior, senior, unequal
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers to describe people of similar status, age, or position. Common in educational, professional, social contexts. Appropriate when emphasizing equality or shared standing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when hierarchical relationship should be emphasized. Don't use when "colleague" or "classmate" would be more specific. Avoid when age/status similarity isn't relevant.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - peer group/network/support
   - amongst one's peers/colleagues
   - peer review/evaluation/pressure
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *par* (equal)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *par* (equal) → Old French *per* → Anglo-Norman *peer* → English *peer* (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "He's really respected amongst his peers at work."
   - "She tends to be shy around her peers but confident with younger kids."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Dr. Johnson is widely recognized amongst her peers as a leading authority in the field."
   - "The evaluation incorporates both self-assessment and peer review amongst faculty members."

6. **Noun**: collective (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A group of people or entities that work together or are considered as a single entity; an organization owned and controlled by the people who work in it or use its services
   - *Grammatical info*: countable
   
   **Synonyms**: group, cooperative, consortium, association, union, commune
   
   **Antonyms**: individual, corporation, hierarchy
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal contexts to describe collaborative groups or cooperative organizations. Common in discussions of organizational structure, social movements, or cooperative businesses.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to traditional hierarchical organizations. Don't use when "group" or "organization" would be clearer. Avoid when political connotations of "collective" might be problematic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - artist/worker/farming collective
   - function as a collective
   - members of the collective
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *col-* (together) + *legere* (gather) + -ive (adjective/noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *collectivus* → French *collectif* → English *collective* (noun usage 17th century, popular 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "They formed a collective to share studio space and resources."
   - "The artist collective has been really successful in promoting local work."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The research collective comprises scholars from diverse disciplinary backgrounds."
   - "Decision-making authority is distributed equally amongst members of the collective."

7. **Noun**: majority (C/U, pl: majorities)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The greater number or part; a number greater than half of a total; the group constituting more than half of a population
   - *Grammatical info*: countable for specific instances, uncountable for general concept
   
   **Synonyms**: bulk, preponderance, most, greater part, lion's share
   
   **Antonyms**: minority, few, lesser part, fraction
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers to describe the larger portion or group. Common in voting, demographic, statistical contexts. Appropriate when contrasting with minority or emphasizing dominant portion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to pluralities (largest group but not >50%). Don't use when precise numbers would be more appropriate. Avoid when all or nearly all is meant (use "almost all" or "nearly all").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the majority of people/members
   - vast/overwhelming/significant majority
   - majority amongst the population/voters
   
   **Root Analysis**: major (Latin *major*, greater) + -ity (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *major* (greater) → French *majorité* → English *majority* (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The majority amongst our group voted to go with option A."
   - "I think the majority of people would agree with that statement."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The majority of participants demonstrated significant improvement in measured outcomes."
   - "A substantial majority amongst surveyed stakeholders expressed support for the proposed changes."

8. **Noun**: stakeholder (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or group with an interest or concern in something, especially a business; anyone affected by or able to affect an organization's actions
   - *Grammatical info*: countable
   
   **Synonyms**: interested party, shareholder, participant, investor, constituent
   
   **Antonyms**: outsider, non-participant, disinterested party
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal professional, business, and organizational contexts to describe parties with vested interests. Common in project management, corporate governance, policy development. Appropriate when emphasizing multiple interested parties.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "people involved" is clearer. Don't use when "shareholder" or more specific term is meant. Avoid overuse as jargon in contexts not requiring technical precision.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - key/primary/external stakeholders
   - stakeholder engagement/consultation/buy-in
   - consensus amongst stakeholders
   
   **Root Analysis**: stake (Old English *staca*, post, wager) + holder (one who holds)
   
   **Etymology**: *Stake* (wager, investment) + *holder* → *stakeholder* (17th century in legal contexts, 20th century in business contexts)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We need to make sure all the stakeholders are happy with the decision."
   - "There's been a lot of discussion amongst the various stakeholders involved."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The initiative requires consensus amongst all stakeholder groups to proceed successfully."
   - "Comprehensive stakeholder analysis identified divergent priorities amongst key organizational constituencies."
