1. **Verb**: rank
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To give someone or something a position on a scale according to quality, importance, or success; to classify or organize in ranks
   - (intransitive) To have a particular position on a scale of quality, importance, or success; to be considered as having a particular status
   - *Grammatical info*: transitive and intransitive
   
   **Synonyms**: classify, rate, grade, position, place, order, class
   
   **Antonyms**: disorganize, scatter, randomize
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal contexts when describing hierarchical positioning, evaluation, or classification. Common in academic, professional, competitive contexts. Appropriate for both active ranking and stating position.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "put" or "place" would be more natural. Don't use when informal assessment is meant. Avoid when ranking implies inappropriate comparison.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rank amongst the best/top/highest
   - rank highly/poorly
   - rank someone/something as/by
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old French *ranc* (row, line, rank)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *renc* (row, line) → *rank* (noun) → *rank* (verb, to arrange in rows, 16th century; to assign relative position, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'd definitely rank her amongst the best teachers I've ever had."
   - "This restaurant ranks pretty high on my list of favorites."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The institution ranks amongst the top ten nationally in research productivity and graduate outcomes."
   - "Participants were ranked according to their performance on standardized assessment measures."

2. **Verb**: distribute
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To give shares of something to each person in a group; to spread something over an area; to supply goods to stores and businesses
   - *Grammatical info*: transitive
   
   **Synonyms**: allocate, disperse, divide, share out, apportion, disseminate
   
   **Antonyms**: collect, gather, centralize, consolidate, hoard
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal contexts when describing systematic allocation or spreading. Common in logistics, resource allocation, information dissemination contexts. Appropriate for physical and abstract distribution.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "hand out" or "share" would be more natural. Don't use when centralization is meant. Avoid when "give" would be simpler.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - distribute amongst members/participants
   - distribute evenly/equally/fairly
   - distribute resources/materials/information
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart, away) + *tribuere* (Latin: assign, allot)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *distribuere* (to divide, allot) → Old French *distribuer* → English *distribute* (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Let's distribute these tasks amongst the whole team so nobody gets overwhelmed."
   - "We need to distribute the supplies evenly amongst all the classrooms."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Resources were distributed equitably amongst participating departments based on documented need."
   - "The survey instrument was distributed amongst all eligible participants via electronic communication."

3. **Verb**: divide
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To separate or split into parts; to share or distribute among a number of recipients; to cause disagreement or disunity
   - (intransitive) To separate into parts; to split
   - *Grammatical info*: transitive and intransitive
   
   **Synonyms**: split, separate, partition, share, apportion, break up
   
   **Antonyms**: unite, combine, join, merge, unify
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers when describing physical separation, mathematical division, or distribution. Common in mathematical, resource allocation, conflict contexts. Appropriate for both physical and abstract division.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when unity should be emphasized. Don't use when "separate" or "split" would be clearer. Avoid when negative connotations of division are inappropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - divide amongst members/participants
   - divide equally/evenly/fairly
   - divide and conquer, divided opinion
   
   **Root Analysis**: di- (apart) + *videre* (Latin: separate)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *dividere* (to force apart, divide) → Old French *divider* → English *divide* (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Let's just divide the bill equally amongst all of us."
   - "The inheritance was divided amongst the three siblings."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Responsibilities were divided systematically amongst existing subcommittees based on jurisdictional mandates."
   - "The sample was divided into three cohorts for comparative analysis purposes."

4. **Verb**: share
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To use, do, or have something at the same time as someone else; to divide something between people; to tell someone about something
   - (intransitive) To have or use something with others
   - *Grammatical info*: transitive and intransitive
   
   **Synonyms**: divide, split, distribute, apportion, partake, communicate
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, withhold, monopolize, hoard
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers to describe distribution, joint use, or information exchange. Common in everyday, collaborative, social contexts. Appropriate for both physical items and abstract concepts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when individual ownership should be emphasized. Don't use when confidentiality is meant. Avoid when "tell" or "give" would be more specific.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - share amongst the group/team
   - share knowledge/information/resources
   - share equally/fairly, share out
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *scearu* (division, part)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *scearu* (cutting, division) → *scieran* (to cut, divide) → Middle English *share* (portion, verb: to divide, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We'll share the costs equally amongst everyone who's going."
   - "She's really good at sharing her expertise with others on the team."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Knowledge transfer mechanisms facilitate systematic sharing of expertise amongst organizational members."
   - "Best practices should be shared proactively amongst all participating institutions."

5. **Verb**: circulate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To send something to all members of a group; to spread or distribute something
   - (intransitive) To move around continuously; to spread or be distributed among people
   - *Grammatical info*: transitive and intransitive
   
   **Synonyms**: distribute, disseminate, spread, pass around, propagate
   
   **Antonyms**: confine, restrict, contain, suppress, withhold
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal contexts to describe movement through a system or distribution among people. Common in organizational, medical, academic contexts. Appropriate for both physical circulation and information spread.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "pass around" or "send out" would be more natural. Don't use when controlled distribution is meant (use "distribute"). Avoid when source should be specified.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - circulate amongst members/staff
   - circulate widely/freely
   - circulate documents/information/rumors
   
   **Root Analysis**: circul- (Latin: circle) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *circulare* (to form a circle, move in a circle) → *circulatus* → English *circulate* (15th century, medical; 17th century general usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "That rumor has been circulating amongst the staff all week."
   - "Can you circulate this document amongst everyone on the team?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Draft guidelines circulated amongst committee members prior to formal review."
   - "Preliminary findings were circulated amongst stakeholders for comment and feedback."

6. **Verb**: spread
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To distribute over an area or among people; to cause to reach more people; to extend over a period of time
   - (intransitive) To extend over a larger area; to become known by more people; to be distributed
   - *Grammatical info*: transitive and intransitive
   
   **Synonyms**: disperse, disseminate, scatter, propagate, diffuse, extend
   
   **Antonyms**: concentrate, gather, collect, contain, confine
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers to describe expansion, distribution, or dissemination. Common in disease, information, physical distribution contexts. Appropriate for both intentional and unintentional spread.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when containment is meant. Don't use when controlled distribution is appropriate (use "distribute"). Avoid when negative connotations of "spread" (disease, rumors) are inappropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - spread amongst the population/community
   - spread rapidly/quickly/widely
   - spread information/news/rumors
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *sprǣdan* (to spread out, stretch)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *sprǣdan* → Middle English *spreden* → English *spread* (verb and noun, extent and distribution meanings developed over time)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The news spread quickly amongst everyone in the office."
   - "Let's spread out these responsibilities amongst the whole team."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Misinformation spread rapidly amongst social media users despite fact-checking efforts."
   - "The intervention aims to prevent disease spread amongst vulnerable population segments."

7. **Verb**: distinguish
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To recognize or treat as different; to see or hear clearly; to make oneself notable
   - (intransitive, with "between") To recognize a difference between things
   - *Grammatical info*: transitive and intransitive
   
   **Synonyms**: differentiate, discern, discriminate, identify, separate, tell apart
   
   **Antonyms**: confuse, conflate, mix up, equate
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal contexts when describing recognition of differences, notable achievement, or clear perception. Common in academic, evaluative, descriptive contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "tell apart" or "see the difference" would be more natural. Don't use when similarity should be emphasized. Avoid when "recognize" would be simpler.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - distinguish oneself/itself amongst peers
   - distinguish between/from
   - distinguished by/for
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart) + *stinguere* (Latin: to prick, mark)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *distinguere* (to separate by marking) → Old French *distinguer* → English *distinguish* (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "She really distinguished herself amongst all the candidates with that presentation."
   - "It's hard to distinguish between the two options – they seem pretty similar."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "This methodology distinguishes itself amongst existing approaches through its innovative integration."
   - "The research distinguishes clearly between correlation and causation in observed relationships."

8. **Verb**: stand out
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive, phrasal verb) To be much better or more important than other similar people or things; to be very noticeable or conspicuous
   - *Grammatical info*: intransitive phrasal verb
   
   **Synonyms**: excel, distinguish oneself, be prominent, be conspicuous, shine
   
   **Antonyms**: blend in, conform, be ordinary, be unremarkable
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers to describe noticeability or exceptional quality. Common in evaluative, competitive, descriptive contexts. Appropriate for both positive distinction and simple visibility.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when blending in is meant. Don't use when negative visibility is described (use "stick out" with negative connotation). Avoid when "excel" would be more specific.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stand out amongst peers/competitors
   - stand out from the crowd
   - really stand out, clearly stand out
   
   **Root Analysis**: stand (Old English *standan*) + out (Old English *ūt*, outward)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *standan* + *ūt* → phrasal verb *stand out* (project, protrude, 15th century; be conspicuous/prominent, 19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Her application really stood out amongst all the others we received."
   - "That restaurant definitely stands out as the best option in the area."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "This candidate's qualifications stand out prominently amongst a highly competitive applicant pool."
   - "The methodology stands out amongst contemporary approaches for its rigor and innovation."
