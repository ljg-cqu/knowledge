1. **Adjective**: collective (comp: more collective, sup: most collective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Shared by or involving all members of a group; done by people acting as a group rather than individually; relating to or characteristic of a group taken together
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: communal, shared, joint, common, group, unified
   
   **Antonyms**: individual, personal, separate, independent, private
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing actions, decisions, properties, or responsibilities shared by a group. Common in discussions of organizations, communities, social movements, and collaborative work. Appropriate in formal and semi-formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when individual agency or responsibility should be emphasized. Don't use when referring to physical gathering (use "gathered" or "assembled"). Avoid when "collaborative" more precisely describes cooperative effort.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - collective decision/agreement/effort
   - collective responsibility/action/wisdom
   - collective bargaining/consciousness
   
   **Root Analysis**: collect (Latin *col-* together + *legere* gather) + *-ive* (adjective suffix indicating tendency or quality)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *collectivus* (gathered together) → French *collectif* → English *collective* (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The whole team made a collective decision to postpone the deadline."
   - "There's a collective sigh of relief whenever he finally leaves the room."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The committee reached a collective agreement regarding the implementation framework."
   - "This approach emphasizes collective responsibility rather than individual accountability."

2. **Adjective**: distinguished (comp: more distinguished, sup: most distinguished)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Successful, authoritative, and respected in one's field; having an air of distinction or excellence; marked by eminence, dignity, or excellence
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, primarily attributive but can be predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: eminent, prominent, renowned, prestigious, illustrious, notable
   
   **Antonyms**: undistinguished, obscure, unknown, unremarkable, ordinary
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to describe people, institutions, or achievements of high status or recognition. Common in academic contexts, professional biographies, awards citations. Appropriate when showing respect or acknowledging achievement.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where it sounds overly formal or pompous. Don't use ironically unless context is very clear. Avoid when simpler descriptors ("successful," "well-known") would suffice.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - distinguished professor/scholar/scientist
   - distinguished career/service/record
   - distinguished member/guest/alumnus
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart, away) + *stinguere* (Latin: mark, separate) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *distinguere* (to separate by marking) → Old French *distinguer* → English *distinguish* + *-ed* (set apart by excellence, 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "He had a really distinguished career before retiring last year."
   - "She comes from a pretty distinguished family of academics."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Professor Williams ranks amongst the most distinguished researchers in the field of molecular biology."
   - "The institution has maintained its distinguished reputation for over a century."

3. **Adjective**: prominent (comp: more prominent, sup: most prominent)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Important and well-known; standing out from others; conspicuous or noticeable; projecting or standing out from something
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: notable, leading, eminent, distinguished, conspicuous, outstanding
   
   **Antonyms**: obscure, unknown, inconspicuous, minor, insignificant
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe people, features, or issues that are important, noticeable, or standing out. Appropriate across registers from semi-formal to formal. Common in academic, journalistic, and professional contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "important" or "noticeable" would be clearer. Don't use when describing physical protrusion unless that meaning is clearly intended. Avoid overuse which dilutes impact.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prominent figure/member/role
   - prominent feature/position/place
   - prominent amongst peers/colleagues
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + *minere* (Latin: jut, project) + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *prominere* (to jut out, project forward) → *prominentem* → English *prominent* (15th century, originally physical; figurative meaning developed 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "She's become a pretty prominent voice in the environmental movement."
   - "The issue has been really prominent in recent discussions."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Dr. Chen occupies a prominent position amongst contemporary cognitive scientists."
   - "This concern features prominently amongst the challenges identified in the assessment."

4. **Adjective**: diverse (comp: more diverse, sup: most diverse)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Showing a great deal of variety; very different from each other; including or involving people from a range of different social and ethnic backgrounds
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: varied, various, different, heterogeneous, assorted, miscellaneous
   
   **Antonyms**: homogeneous, uniform, similar, identical, monotonous
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe variety within groups, populations, collections, or perspectives. Common in discussions of demographics, opinions, options, or representation. Appropriate across all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using as euphemism for racial or ethnic minorities (use specific, direct language). Don't use when "different" would be clearer. Avoid when uniformity is actually the point.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - diverse perspectives/opinions/views
   - diverse group/population/community
   - diverse backgrounds/experiences
   
   **Root Analysis**: di- (apart) + *versus* (Latin: turned) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *diversus* (turned different ways, various) → Old French *divers* → English *diverse* (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Our friend group is pretty diverse – we all come from different backgrounds."
   - "There are diverse opinions amongst the team about which approach to take."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The study sample comprised participants from diverse socioeconomic and cultural backgrounds."
   - "The committee values diverse perspectives amongst its members to ensure comprehensive evaluation."

5. **Adjective**: widespread (comp: more widespread, sup: most widespread)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Found or distributed over a large area or number of people; existing or happening in many places or situations, or among many people
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: prevalent, common, extensive, pervasive, ubiquitous, general
   
   **Antonyms**: rare, isolated, localized, uncommon, limited
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe phenomena, practices, beliefs, or conditions that exist across broad populations or areas. Common in academic writing, journalism, research. Appropriate in formal and semi-formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to small or localized occurrences. Don't use when "common" would be simpler and clearer. Avoid when precise statistics would be more appropriate than vague frequency claims.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - widespread support/belief/concern
   - widespread practice/occurrence/use
   - widespread amongst the population/community
   
   **Root Analysis**: wide (Old English *wīd*, extensive) + spread (Old English *sprǣdan*, to scatter)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound *wide* + *spread* (16th century, originally describing physical extension; figurative meaning developed later)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "There's pretty widespread agreement amongst everyone that we need to make changes."
   - "The practice is widespread amongst companies in this industry."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "These concerns remain widespread amongst stakeholders despite recent policy interventions."
   - "The phenomenon demonstrates widespread prevalence across diverse demographic groups."

6. **Adjective**: prevalent (comp: more prevalent, sup: most prevalent)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Widespread in a particular area or at a particular time; generally existing, occurring, or accepted
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, primarily predicative but can be attributive
   
   **Synonyms**: common, widespread, frequent, predominant, current, general
   
   **Antonyms**: rare, uncommon, infrequent, unusual, exceptional
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to describe frequency or commonness of conditions, beliefs, or practices. Common in academic writing, research, medical contexts. Appropriate when making evidence-based frequency claims.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "common" is more natural. Don't use without evidence in academic contexts. Avoid when "widespread" or "common" would be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prevalent belief/view/opinion
   - prevalent condition/disease/practice
   - prevalent amongst certain groups/populations
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + *valere* (Latin: be strong) + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *praevalentem* (very strong, having superior force) → English *prevalent* (16th century, meaning shifted from "powerful" to "widespread")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "That attitude is still pretty prevalent amongst people in that generation."
   - "The problem seems more prevalent amongst younger users."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Such attitudes remain prevalent amongst early-career professionals in the sector."
   - "The condition demonstrates higher prevalence rates amongst populations with specific risk factors."

7. **Adjective**: unique (comp: more unique, sup: most unique - though traditionally considered absolute)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Being the only one of its kind; unlike anything else; particularly remarkable, special, or unusual
   - *Grammatical info*: traditionally non-gradable (absolute), but colloquially often gradable; both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: singular, distinctive, one-of-a-kind, unparalleled, exceptional, incomparable
   
   **Antonyms**: common, typical, ordinary, standard, conventional
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe true singularity or exceptional distinctiveness. Common across all registers. In formal contexts, use without degree modifiers; in casual contexts, "more unique" is accepted though prescriptively incorrect.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid with intensifiers like "very" or "extremely" in formal writing (use "truly unique" if emphasis needed). Don't use when "unusual" or "distinctive" would be more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unique perspective/approach/solution
   - unique position/opportunity/challenge
   - unique amongst one's peers/contemporaries
   
   **Root Analysis**: uni- (one) + -que (Latin adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *unicus* (single, sole, only one) → French *unique* → English *unique* (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Her approach is pretty unique amongst everyone else working on this problem."
   - "This place has a really unique atmosphere – nothing else like it around here."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "This methodology is unique amongst existing approaches in its integration of multiple theoretical frameworks."
   - "The institution's governance model stands unique amongst peer organizations in the sector."

8. **Adjective**: equal (comp: more equal, sup: most equal - though traditionally considered absolute)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The same in quantity, size, degree, or value; having the same status, rights, or opportunities; uniform in application or effect
   - *Grammatical info*: traditionally non-gradable, but "more equal" used colloquially; both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: equivalent, identical, the same, uniform, even, balanced
   
   **Antonyms**: unequal, different, disparate, uneven, inequitable
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe equivalence in quantity, status, rights, or treatment. Common in discussions of fairness, mathematics, social justice. Appropriate across all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when emphasizing inequality or difference. Don't use with degree modifiers in formal writing (though Orwell's "more equal" is deliberately ironic). Avoid when "fair" or "equitable" better captures the meaning.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - equal distribution/division amongst
   - equal status/standing/footing
   - equal opportunity/treatment/representation
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (variant of ex-, out) + *aequus* (Latin: level, even) + -al (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *aequalis* (equal, like, uniform) → Old French *egal* → English *equal* (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We should split the costs on an equal basis amongst all of us."
   - "Everyone should have an equal say amongst the group members."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Resources were distributed on an equal basis amongst all participating institutions."
   - "The policy ensures equal representation amongst stakeholder groups in decision-making processes."
