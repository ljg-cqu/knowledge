1. **Adverb**: collectively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - As a group; together as a whole rather than individually; in a way that involves all members of a group
   - *Grammatical info*: can appear in mid-position (after auxiliary) or end-position; modifies verbs or entire clauses
   
   **Synonyms**: together, jointly, as a group, communally, in concert, as one
   
   **Antonyms**: individually, separately, independently, alone, singly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize group action or shared responsibility. Common in formal and semi-formal contexts discussing organizational behavior, decision-making, or collaborative work. Appropriate when contrasting group versus individual action.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when individual contributions should be highlighted. Don't use when physical gathering is meant (use "together"). Avoid when "jointly" or "together" would be simpler and clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - collectively decide/agree/determine
   - collectively responsible/accountable
   - work collectively, act collectively
   
   **Root Analysis**: collective (Latin *col-* together + *legere* gather + *-ive*) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *collectivus* → collective + *-ly* → *collectively* (forming adverb of manner)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We all collectively decided to postpone the meeting until next week."
   - "The team collectively breathed a sigh of relief when the project was done."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The committee members collectively agreed upon the implementation framework."
   - "These institutions collectively represent the leading voices in the field."

2. **Adverb**: equally (type: manner/degree)

   **Antonyms**: unequally, disproportionately, unevenly, differently, unfairly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize fairness, uniformity, or equivalent degree. Common in discussions of distribution, comparison, rights, or treatment. Appropriate across all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when inequality or differentiation is the point. Don't use when "the same" would be clearer. Avoid when emphasizing one thing over another.
   
   **Common Phrases**:

   - treat equally, share equally
   
   **Root Analysis**: equal (Latin *aequalis*) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *aequalis* → equal + *-ly* → *equally* (adverb of manner/degree)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:

3. **Adverb**: particularly (type: degree/sentence adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a higher degree than is usual or average; especially; used to single out one person, thing, or situation above all others
   - *Grammatical info*: can appear in various positions; as sentence adverb modifies entire clause; as degree adverb modifies adjectives/verbs
   
   **Synonyms**: especially, specifically, notably, in particular, above all, chiefly
   
   **Antonyms**: generally, broadly, universally, ordinarily
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize or single out specific cases from a larger set. Common in formal and semi-formal writing to add precision or emphasis. Appropriate when making nuanced claims about degree or specificity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse which dilutes emphasis. Don't use when all cases are equal. Avoid in casual speech where "especially" is more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - particularly important/significant/relevant
   - particularly amongst certain groups
   - particularly true/evident/noticeable
   
   **Root Analysis**: particular (Latin *particularis* from *particula*, small part) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *particularis* → particular + *-ly* → *particularly* (emphasizing specificity)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "This issue is particularly important amongst younger voters."
   - "The problem is particularly bad during the summer months."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "These findings are particularly relevant amongst populations with elevated baseline risk."
   - "The phenomenon demonstrates particular salience amongst early-career professionals."

5. **Adverb**: prominently (type: manner)

   **Synonyms**: conspicuously, notably, markedly, strikingly, significantly, visibly
   
   **Antonyms**: obscurely, inconspicuously, minimally, slightly, barely
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize visibility, importance, or noticeability of something. Common in formal contexts describing positioning, features, or status. Appropriate in academic and professional writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when subtle or understated presence is meant. Don't use when "noticeably" or "importantly" would be simpler. Avoid when the subject isn't actually prominent.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - feature prominently, figure prominently
   - prominently displayed/featured/mentioned
   - stand prominently, appear prominently
   
   **Root Analysis**: prominent (Latin *pro-* forward + *minere* jut) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *prominere* → prominent + *-ly* → *prominently* (adverb of manner)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:

   - "Her work features prominently in the exhibition."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "This concern features prominently amongst the challenges identified in stakeholder consultations."
   - "The methodology is prominently discussed amongst contemporary theoretical frameworks."

8. **Adverb**: predominantly (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Mainly; for the most part; in a way that is most noticeable or largest in number or amount
   - *Grammatical info*: typically sentence-initial or mid-position; modifies adjectives, verbs, or clauses
   
   **Synonyms**: mainly, primarily, chiefly, mostly, largely, principally
   
   **Antonyms**: minimally, slightly, rarely, secondarily, marginally
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate that something is true for the majority but allows for exceptions. Common in formal writing describing demographics, characteristics, or patterns. Appropriate in academic and professional contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to equal distribution or minority cases. Don't use when "mostly" would be simpler. Avoid when precise percentages would be more appropriate than vague majority claims.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - predominantly found/seen amongst
   - predominantly male/female/young
   - predominantly used/employed/adopted
   
   **Root Analysis**: predominant (Latin *prae-* before + *dominari* dominate) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *praedominari* → predominant + *-ly* → *predominantly* (adverb of degree/manner)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The practice is predominantly found amongst older generations."
   - "The audience was predominantly made up of students."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The sample comprised predominantly female participants from urban settings."
   - "These attitudes are predominantly observed amongst early-career professionals in the sector."
