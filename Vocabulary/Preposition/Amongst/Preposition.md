1. **Preposition**: amongst (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the middle of or surrounded by a group of people or things; within a particular group or collection
   - *(Abstract)* As part of or included in a particular group; used to show a relationship of being one member within a larger set
   
   **Synonyms**: among (neutral, more common), amid (formal, literary), amidst (formal, literary), in the midst of (formal), surrounded by (casual)
   
   **Antonyms**: outside of, separate from, apart from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "amongst" in formal or literary contexts when describing physical or abstract positioning within a group or collection. Common in British English and in contexts requiring elevated register. Use when emphasizing membership or inclusion within a category or community.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual American English conversation where "among" is strongly preferred. Do not use when referring to only two items (use "between" instead). Avoid in technical writing where precision is required over stylistic flourish.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: amongst friends, amongst colleagues, amongst the ruins, amongst other things, amongst the crowd
   - **Verb + prep**: distribute amongst, divide amongst, share amongst, hidden amongst, scattered amongst
   - **Adjective + prep**: lost amongst, buried amongst, prominent amongst
   - **Noun + prep**: discussion amongst (colleagues), consensus amongst (experts), division amongst (members)
   
   **Root Analysis**: among + -st (archaic English suffix indicating superlative or emphasis)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *onmang* (in the crowd of) → Middle English *amonges* → Modern English *amongst* (variant of "among" with added -st suffix common in Middle English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She felt comfortable amongst her old friends from university.
   - The keys were hidden amongst all the papers on his desk.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manuscript was discovered amongst the private collection of a renowned scholar.
   - This phenomenon remains one of the most debated topics amongst contemporary researchers.

2. **Preposition**: among (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the company of; surrounded by a group of people or things
   - *(Abstract)* Being part of or included in a particular group; used to indicate distribution or relationship within a set
   - *(Comparative)* Used to show something is true for multiple members of a group
   
   **Synonyms**: amongst (formal, British), amid (formal), within (context-dependent), between (for specific pairs)
   
   **Antonyms**: outside, beyond, separate from, excluding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "among" as the standard, neutral form in both American and British English. Appropriate in all registers from casual to formal. Use when referring to more than two items or people, when showing distribution, or when indicating membership in a category.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use when referring to exactly two items (use "between"). Avoid when spatial precision is required (prefer "in the middle of" or "surrounded by"). Do not use when exclusion is meant rather than inclusion.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: among friends, among others, among the best, among them, among us
   - **Verb + prep**: count among, number among, rank among, choose among, distribute among
   - **Adjective + prep**: popular among, common among, rare among, widespread among
   - **Noun + prep**: differences among, relationships among, discussion among, consensus among
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (prefix: on, in) + mong (crowd, throng)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *ongemang* (in the throng/crowd) → *among* (simplified form, lost the -st variant over time in American English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's really popular among his classmates.
   - Let's split the bill among the four of us.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings were consistent among all participants in the longitudinal study.
   - This approach is widely recognized among leading economists.

3. **Preposition**: between (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the space separating two objects, places, or people; in the interval separating two points
   - *(Temporal)* In the time interval separating two events or time points
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating a relationship, connection, or choice involving two (or sometimes more) distinct entities
   
   **Synonyms**: betwixt (archaic), amid (less specific), in the middle of (spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: beyond, outside, excluding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "between" for two distinct entities or for pairs within a larger group. Use when emphasizing individual relationships (between A and B and C). Appropriate when showing spatial, temporal, or abstract separation or connection.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Traditionally avoid when referring to more than two items as a collective group (use "among"), though modern usage accepts "between" for multiple items when emphasizing individual relationships. Do not use when "within" or "inside" is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: between times, between meetings, between you and me, between the lines
   - **Verb + prep**: choose between, distinguish between, differentiate between, divide between, alternate between
   - **Adjective + prep**: torn between, caught between, stuck between
   - **Noun + prep**: difference between, relationship between, connection between, gap between, choice between
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (prefix: by, near) + twēon (two)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *betwēonum* (by two, in the space of two) → Middle English *bitwene* → Modern English *between*
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you keep this between us? I don't want anyone else to know.
   - She's trying to choose between the two job offers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The correlation between socioeconomic status and educational outcomes has been well documented.
   - Negotiations between the two parties are scheduled to resume next week.

4. **Preposition**: amid (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the middle of; surrounded by (especially in a literary or formal context)
   - *(Abstract)* During; while something else is happening (often used to describe circumstances or conditions)
   
   **Synonyms**: amidst (equally formal), among (more common), amongst (formal), in the midst of (formal), surrounded by
   
   **Antonyms**: outside, beyond, away from, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "amid" in formal writing, journalism, or literary contexts to describe situations, events, or circumstances. Common in news reporting to set context or describe conditions surrounding an event. Preferred in American formal writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where it sounds overly formal or pretentious. Do not use in technical writing where precise spatial descriptions are needed. Generally less common than "among" in everyday speech.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: amid concerns, amid speculation, amid controversy, amid chaos, amid celebrations
   - **Verb + prep**: occur amid, arise amid, happen amid, emerge amid
   - **Adjective + prep**: lost amid, visible amid, prominent amid
   - **Noun + prep**: statement amid (controversy), decision amid (uncertainty)
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (prefix: on, in) + mid (middle)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *on middan* (in the middle) → Middle English *amidde* → Modern English *amid* (simplified from *amidst*)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They found some hope amid all the difficulties they'd been facing.
   - He remained calm amid the chaos of the moving day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company announced layoffs amid declining revenues and increased competition.
   - The treaty was signed amid growing international pressure for diplomatic resolution.

5. **Preposition**: amidst (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the middle of; surrounded by (literary or formal variant of "amid")
   - *(Abstract)* During; in the context of ongoing circumstances or events
   
   **Synonyms**: amid (more common in American English), among, amongst (formal), in the midst of, surrounded by
   
   **Antonyms**: outside, beyond, apart from, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "amidst" in literary, poetic, or highly formal contexts, particularly in British English. Choose when aiming for an elevated or dramatic tone. Common in historical writing or formal narratives.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in American English where "amid" is strongly preferred. Do not use in casual conversation or informal writing. Avoid in business or technical writing where "amid" or "during" would be more direct.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: amidst the turmoil, amidst the ruins, amidst uncertainty, amidst the crowd
   - **Verb + prep**: stand amidst, sit amidst, found amidst, discovered amidst
   - **Adjective + prep**: alone amidst, lost amidst, visible amidst
   - **Noun + prep**: silence amidst (noise), calm amidst (storm)
   
   **Root Analysis**: amid + -st (Middle English suffix for emphasis or formality)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English *amiddes* → *amidst* (formed by adding -st suffix to "amid", parallel to "amongst" from "among")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She felt utterly alone amidst the bustling city streets.
   - They discovered beauty amidst the decay of the abandoned building.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The poet's voice emerged amidst a tumultuous period of social transformation.
   - The manuscript was preserved amidst the ravages of time and war.

6. **Preposition**: within (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Inside the bounds or limits of a place, area, or object; not beyond the boundaries of
   - *(Temporal)* Before a period of time has elapsed; not later than
   - *(Abstract)* In the scope, range, or limits of something; as part of a group or organization
   
   **Synonyms**: inside (spatial), in (less formal), before (temporal), among (abstract membership)
   
   **Antonyms**: outside, beyond, exceeding, past
   
   **When to Use**: Use "within" to emphasize boundaries, limits, or containment (spatial, temporal, or abstract). Appropriate in all registers when precision about limitations or membership is important. Common in formal instructions, deadlines, and organizational contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple "in" would be clearer and less formal. Do not use when "by" is more appropriate for deadlines. Avoid overuse in casual speech where it may sound stiff.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: within reach, within limits, within reason, within the organization, within 24 hours
   - **Verb + prep**: stay within, remain within, fall within, operate within, come within
   - **Adjective + prep**: contained within, included within, situated within
   - **Noun + prep**: changes within (the system), divisions within (the company)
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (preposition: against, toward) + -in (inside)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *wiðinnan* (with-inside, from inside) → Middle English *withinne* → Modern English *within*
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The store is within walking distance from here.
   - I'll get back to you within a couple of days.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All applications must be submitted within the specified timeframe.
   - The decision falls within the purview of the ethics committee.
