1. **Adverb**: formally (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an official, ceremonial, or legally recognized way; according to established rules and procedures
   - From the standpoint of recognized roles or institutions rather than personal relationships
   
   **Synonyms**: officially, ceremonially, institutionally, outwardly, ostensibly
   
   **Antonyms**: informally, casually, personally, unofficially
   
   **When to Use**: Use when contrasting someone's behavior or status qua office-holder with their behavior qua private individual ("Formally, she reports to the board"). Common in legal, organizational, and academic writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you mean "politely" or "with good manners" (use "politely" or "respectfully" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - formally recognized
   - formally responsible
   - formally speaking
   - formally identical qua structure
   - formally distinct but materially similar
   
   **Root Analysis**: formal (relating to official form) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "formal" (Latin *formalis*) + -ly; used especially in legal and philosophical English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Formally, he's my boss, but qua friend he's very easy-going.
   - Formally speaking, you're a tenant; informally, you're almost family.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Formally, the state qua sovereign is distinct from the government of the day.
   - Two systems may be formally equivalent qua structure while differing empirically.

1. **Adverb**: legally (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is connected with the law or recognized by law
   - From the perspective of legal status or rights rather than moral or social views
   
   **Synonyms**: lawfully, juridically, in legal terms, de jure (Lat., formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: illegally, unlawfully, extra-legally, de facto (when opposed to de jure)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when distinguishing a person or entity qua legal subject ("legally, the corporation is a person") from the same entity qua moral or social agent. Frequent in jurisprudence and policy writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when talking about moral permissibility (use "morally"); do not confuse with "rightfully" in everyday disputes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - legally responsible
   - legally entitled
   - legally recognized qua person
   - legally distinct
   - legally speaking
   
   **Root Analysis**: legal (relating to law) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "legal" (Latin *legalis*, from *lex* "law") + -ly.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Legally he's an adult, but qua student he still depends on his parents.
   - Legally speaking, the company qua employer has to provide insurance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Legally, the state qua regulator is constrained by constitutional rights.
   - Legally and formally, the board acts qua collective body rather than as individuals.

1. **Adverb**: conceptually (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - From the standpoint of concepts or abstract ideas rather than concrete details
   - In terms of how something is thought about or classified
   
   **Synonyms**: theoretically, abstractly, in conceptual terms, notionally
   
   **Antonyms**: empirically, concretely, practically, experientially
   
   **When to Use**: Use when you treat something qua instance of a concept ("conceptually, the agent qua chooser differs from the agent qua observer"). Common in philosophy, cognitive science, and theoretical writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing implementation details; then "practically" or "in practice" is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - conceptually distinct
   - conceptually separate
   - conceptually simple but practically complex
   - conceptually viewing X qua Y
   - conceptually speaking
   
   **Root Analysis**: concept (idea) + -ual (relating to) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "concept" (Latin *conceptus*) → "conceptual" → "conceptually"; used widely in analytic writing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Conceptually, think of yourself qua user, not qua developer.
   - Conceptually it's simple, but qua implementation it's tricky.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Conceptually, the self qua subject differs from the self qua object of reflection.
   - The model treats the firm conceptually qua nexus of contracts.

1. **Adverb**: strictly (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that follows rules or definitions exactly; with narrow interpretation
   - Used to signal that a statement holds only in a precise sense or aspect
   
   **Synonyms**: exactly, narrowly, rigorously, precisely, technically
   
   **Antonyms**: loosely, broadly, approximately, informally
   
   **When to Use**: Use in "Strictly speaking" to specify the role or standpoint ("Strictly speaking, qua employee you're bound by this policy"). Helpful when drawing fine distinctions among aspects of the same thing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in empathetic or diplomatic communication where rigid precision feels harsh.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strictly speaking
   - strictly legal sense
   - strictly qua
   - strictly internal perspective
   - strictly limited to X qua Y
   
   **Root Analysis**: strict (tight, rigid) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *strictus* (drawn tight) → strict → strictly.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Strictly speaking, qua guest you shouldn't move the furniture.
   - Strictly, I'm talking about you qua student, not qua friend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Strictly speaking, the theory applies only to the agent qua rational chooser.
   - The obligation arises strictly for the company qua employer, not for directors personally.

1. **Adverb**: normatively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - From the standpoint of norms, values, or what ought to be, rather than what *is*
   - In terms of standards that guide evaluation or behavior
   
   **Synonyms**: evaluatively, from a normative standpoint, morally (in many contexts), prescriptively
   
   **Antonyms**: descriptively, factually, empirically, value-neutrally
   
   **When to Use**: Use when distinguishing an agent qua bearer of moral or social obligations ("normatively, the manager qua leader") from the same agent qua causal factor. Common in ethics and political philosophy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when merely reporting facts; then "descriptively" is better.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - normatively speaking
   - normatively significant role
   - normatively loaded description
   - normatively assessing X qua Y
   
   **Root Analysis**: norm (standard) + -ative (relating to) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "normative" (19th century, from Latin *norma* "carpenter's square, rule") + -ly.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Normatively, I'm talking about you qua friend who owes support.
   - Normatively it's different: the company qua employer should protect staff.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Normatively, citizens qua voters have duties that go beyond self-interest.
   - The paper analyzes the state normatively qua guarantor of basic rights.

1. **Adverb**: descriptively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that merely describes facts without evaluating them
   - From the standpoint of how things are rather than how they should be
   
   **Synonyms**: factually, non-normatively, empirically (in some contexts), neutrally
   
   **Antonyms**: normatively, evaluatively, prescriptively, judgmentally
   
   **When to Use**: Use when you describe an entity qua occupant of a role without endorsing or criticizing it ("descriptively, he qua CEO controls resources"). Helpful for separating analysis from evaluation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you intend to recommend or criticize; then "normatively" is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - descriptively speaking
   - descriptively accurate but normatively troubling
   - descriptively true of X qua Y
   - purely descriptively
   
   **Root Analysis**: descript(ion) (a writing down) + -ive + -ly
   
   **Etymology**: From "descriptive" (Latin *describere* "to write down") + -ly; widely used in philosophy and social science.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Descriptively, you're the owner qua signer on the contract.
   - I'm speaking descriptively, not saying it's good—just that you qua parent decide.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Descriptively, the model treats agents qua preference-maximizers.
   - The institution is descriptively powerful qua agenda-setter, whatever we think of it normatively.
