1. **Noun**: capacity (C, pl: capacities)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The official position, function, or role in which a person acts ("in her capacity as/qua director")
   - The ability or power to do, experience, or understand something
   
   **Synonyms**: role, function, office, position, remit
   
   **Antonyms**: powerlessness (for ability sense), lay status, outsider status (for role sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when you want to highlight *which role* a person is acting in—"in my capacity qua teacher" vs "in my capacity qua parent". Very common in legal, bureaucratic, and academic contexts where one person holds multiple roles.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in everyday conversation where simple "as" is enough ("as your teacher"); don't use when you just mean physical size (use "volume" or "size").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in my capacity as/qua [role]
   - acting in his official capacity
   - capacity qua chair of the committee
   - capacity as legal representative
   - institutional capacity
   
   **Root Analysis**: capac- (able to hold, take in) + -ity (state, quality)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *capacitas* (ability, breadth, power) → Old French *capacité* → English "capacity"; extended from physical "room" to abstract "role" and "legal standing".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not speaking in my capacity as manager now, just as a friend.
   - In your capacity as class rep, can you talk to the teacher?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The CEO, in her capacity qua director, signed the agreement.
   - Members, acting in their capacity qua trustees, owe fiduciary duties to the charity.

1. **Noun**: role (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The function, job, or part played by a person or thing in a particular situation
   - A set of expectations attached to a social or professional position (e.g., parent, citizen, judge)
   
   **Synonyms**: function, position, duty, office, part
   
   **Antonyms**: non-role, bystander, outsider, spectator (in some contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when contrasting what someone does *qua* holder of one role versus another—"role qua citizen" versus "role qua employee". Central in sociology, organizational theory, ethics, and political philosophy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing a specific task (use "task" or "job"); don't confuse with "rule" (spelling).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - social role
   - professional role
   - role conflict between A qua parent and B qua worker
   - role-specific obligations
   - occupy the role of [X]
   
   **Root Analysis**: role (from French *rôle* = part in a play)
   
   **Etymology**: French *rôle* (roll of paper listing an actor's part) → English "role"; shifted from theatre to any patterned set of expectations.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm trying to balance my roles as a parent and as a manager.
   - In your role as captain, you speak for the team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The paper distinguishes the individual qua private person from the same individual qua role-bearer.
   - Citizens occupy multiple roles, including their role qua voter and qua taxpayer.

1. **Noun**: standpoint (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A particular position or point of view from which something is considered
   - The perspective associated with a role, identity, or set of experiences
   
   **Synonyms**: perspective, viewpoint, angle, position, vantage point
   
   **Antonyms**: neutrality, view from nowhere, non-position
   
   **When to Use**: Use when you want to be precise about the *standpoint qua which* something is judged—"from the standpoint of the worker qua union member". Common in philosophy, critical theory, and social sciences.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very simple descriptions where "view" or "opinion" is enough; don't use if no particular role or identity is relevant.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - from the standpoint of [role]
   - theoretical standpoint
   - moral standpoint qua citizen
   - feminist standpoint
   - standpoint epistemology
   
   **Root Analysis**: stand (to be positioned) + point (specific spot or place)
   
   **Etymology**: Formed in English from "stand" + "point" in the 18th century; extended metaphorically to mental point of view.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - From my standpoint as a parent, that rule makes sense.
   - From your standpoint qua student, the policy feels unfair.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - From the standpoint of justice, citizens qua equals must be treated the same.
   - The analysis is conducted from the standpoint of the firm qua profit‑seeking entity.

1. **Noun**: aspect (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A particular part, feature, or side of something
   - One of the ways in which something can be regarded or understood
   
   **Synonyms**: facet, dimension, side, feature, angle
   
   **Antonyms**: whole, totality, entirety, unity
   
   **When to Use**: Use when distinguishing different *aspects qua which* the same thing can be analyzed—"the self qua body" vs "the self qua mind"; widely used in philosophy, linguistics, and general analytic writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the focus is on the whole rather than parts; don't use where "problem" or "issue" is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - legal aspect qua contract
   - moral aspect qua obligation
   - social aspect of X qua institution
   - examine one aspect at a time
   - multiple aspects of the same agent
   
   **Root Analysis**: a(d)- (toward) + spect (look) → "what is looked at"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *aspectus* (appearance, look) → Old French → Middle English "aspect"; now widely used for figurative "angle".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm talking about one aspect of you—your role qua teacher.
   - Let's look at the financial aspect of this decision.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theory isolates the agent under different aspects: qua chooser, qua sufferer, and qua bystander.
   - Each institution is analyzed under its aspect qua producer of public goods.

1. **Noun**: status (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C/U) A person's legal, social, or professional position in relation to others
   - (C) A particular classification or condition assigned by law or custom (e.g., marital status, resident status)
   
   **Synonyms**: standing, position, rank, legal status, station
   
   **Antonyms**: low status, marginality, lack of status, equality (when status differences disappear)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing someone *qua holder of a status*—"status qua citizen", "status qua shareholder". Frequent in law, sociology, and immigration or tax contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you just mean "update" (as in social media status); don't use where "condition" or "state" would be more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - legal status qua person
   - status as/qua employee
   - immigration status
   - change of status
   - higher/lower status in a role hierarchy
   
   **Root Analysis**: stat- (stand) + -us (noun ending: state, condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *status* (state, condition, position) → Old French → English; used for both legal categories and social rank.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's your status here—are you speaking qua manager or qua friend?
   - Her status as a tenant gives her some rights.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis turns on the individual's status qua citizen under constitutional law.
   - Directors incur additional duties by virtue of their status qua fiduciaries.
