1. **Verb**: worth (be worth)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have a value of; to deserve or merit; to be equal in value to; to have a particular financial value or equivalent.
   - *(Modal-like use)* To be advisable or beneficial (worth doing/having).
   
   **Synonyms**: merit, deserve, be valued at, equal, justify
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms as verb)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express value, merit, or deserving; in contexts of financial valuation; when recommending actions; typically with gerund "worth doing" or noun "worth X dollars"; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "worthy" (adjective); avoid using as main verb without subject "be" ("it worths" is incorrect—must be "it is worth"); don't use in progressive form ("is worthing" is incorrect).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - be worth [amount]
   - worth doing/having/seeing
   - worth one's while
   - worth the effort/trouble
   - worth mentioning/noting
   
   **Root Analysis**: worth (Old English "weorþ" - value, price)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "weorþ" (having value) → used predicatively with "be"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How much is this old guitar worth?
   - That movie is definitely worth watching.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The property is worth approximately three million dollars.
   - This matter is worth our careful consideration.

1. **Verb**: value
   **Meaning**: 
   - To estimate the monetary worth of something; to consider someone or something to be important or beneficial; to appreciate or hold in high regard.
   
   **Synonyms**: appraise, prize, appreciate, treasure, esteem
   
   **Antonyms**: undervalue, depreciate, disregard, devalue
   
   **When to Use**: Use when assessing or assigning worth; when expressing appreciation; in contexts of estimation and appraisal; appropriate in all registers; transitive verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with noun "value" vs. verb "value"; avoid when simple "like" is more natural; don't use when financial precision requires "appraise" or "assess."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - value at [amount]
   - highly value/valued
   - value someone's opinion
   - value for money
   - undervalue/overvalue
   
   **Root Analysis**: value (from Latin "valere" - be strong, be worth)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "valere" → Old French "value" → English verb usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I really value your friendship.
   - The antique dealer valued the painting at $50,000.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization values diversity and inclusion.
   - Experts valued the estate at substantially more than anticipated.

1. **Verb**: appreciate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To recognize the full worth of something; to be grateful for something; to understand fully; (financial) to increase in value over time.
   
   **Synonyms**: value, treasure, prize, recognize, understand, increase in value
   
   **Antonyms**: depreciate, undervalue, ignore, diminish
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing gratitude or recognition of worth; in contexts of understanding value; when discussing increasing value; appropriate in all registers; can be transitive or intransitive.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse financial sense (increase in value) with recognition sense (recognize value); avoid overuse in formal requests which can sound presumptuous; distinguish "appreciate" (understand) from "appreciate" (be grateful).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - appreciate the worth/value
   - greatly/highly appreciate
   - appreciate in value
   - appreciate the opportunity
   - fail to appreciate
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to) + preci(um) (price, value) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "appretiare" (set a price on) → Late Latin "appretiatus" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I appreciate everything you've done for me.
   - Real estate typically appreciates over time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We appreciate the significance of this contribution.
   - The investment has appreciated considerably in recent years.

1. **Verb**: evaluate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To form an idea of the amount, number, or value of something; to assess; to appraise or determine the worth or quality of something systematically.
   
   **Synonyms**: assess, appraise, judge, estimate, measure
   
   **Antonyms**: guess, ignore, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts of assessment; when systematic judgment of worth is involved; in academic and professional evaluation contexts; appropriate in formal registers; transitive verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when casual opinion rather than systematic assessment is meant; avoid when "assess" or "judge" is simpler and clearer; don't use when "calculate" (precise measurement) is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - evaluate the worth/value
   - evaluate effectiveness/performance
   - critically evaluate
   - evaluate against criteria
   - systematically evaluate
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (out) + value + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: French "évaluer" (determine value) → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to evaluate whether this plan is worth pursuing.
   - How do you evaluate the worth of experience versus salary?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will evaluate proposals based on their merit.
   - Researchers evaluated the program's cost-effectiveness and overall worth.

1. **Verb**: assess
   **Meaning**: 
   - To evaluate or estimate the nature, ability, or quality of something; to calculate or estimate the value or amount of something; to determine worth through judgment.
   
   **Synonyms**: evaluate, appraise, estimate, judge, gauge
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, guess, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making judgments about worth or quality; in contexts of estimation and evaluation; when determining value systematically; appropriate in formal and professional contexts; transitive verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when precise measurement rather than judgment is involved; avoid when "estimate" is clearer for approximate values; don't confuse "assess" (evaluate) with "access" (gain entry).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - assess the worth/value
   - assess the situation
   - difficult to assess
   - assess against standards
   - comprehensively assess
   
   **Root Analysis**: as- (to) + sess (from Latin "sedere" - sit, settle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "assidere" (sit beside as advisor) → Old French "assesser" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's hard to assess how much this old comic book is worth.
   - Let's assess whether the upgrade is worth the cost.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Auditors will assess the company's asset worth.
   - The study assesses the relative worth of various intervention strategies.

1. **Verb**: treasure
   **Meaning**: 
   - To keep or regard as precious; to value highly; to cherish and hold dear; to preserve carefully something of great worth.
   
   **Synonyms**: cherish, prize, value, appreciate, hold dear
   
   **Antonyms**: discard, neglect, disregard, waste
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing deep appreciation or emotional value; in contexts emphasizing care and preservation; when describing personal or sentimental worth; appropriate in emotional and emphatic contexts; transitive verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing financial value objectively; don't use when emotional emphasis is inappropriate or excessive; don't overuse which can sound insincere or melodramatic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - treasure the memory/moment
   - dearly/deeply treasure
   - treasure above all else
   - treasure one's time with
   - treasure every moment
   
   **Root Analysis**: treasure (from Latin "thesaurus" - storehouse, treasure)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "thesauros" → Latin "thesaurus" → Old French "tresor" → English verb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I treasure our friendship more than anything.
   - She treasures those letters from her grandmother.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We treasure the contributions made by distinguished alumni.
   - The institution treasures its long tradition of academic excellence.

1. **Verb**: merit
   **Meaning**: 
   - To deserve or be worthy of something (reward, punishment, attention); to earn or be entitled to because of worth or quality.
   
   **Synonyms**: deserve, warrant, earn, justify, be worthy of
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms, but: not deserve, fail to warrant)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing deserving quality or attention; in formal contexts of judgment; when emphasizing that something is worth receiving; appropriate in formal registers; transitive verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when simple "deserve" is clearer; avoid when discussing inherent quality rather than earned worth; don't confuse verb "merit" with noun "merit" (quality vs. deserve).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - merit consideration/attention
   - merit recognition/praise
   - merit further investigation
   - merit the effort
   - scarcely merit
   
   **Root Analysis**: merit (from Latin "merere" - earn, deserve)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "meritum" (that which is deserved) → Old French "meriter" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That excuse doesn't merit a response.
   - Does this issue really merit all this attention?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal merits careful consideration given its potential worth.
   - Research findings merit publication in a peer-reviewed journal.

1. **Verb**: justify
   **Meaning**: 
   - To show or prove to be right or reasonable; to demonstrate that something is worth doing or having; to provide adequate grounds for an action or belief.
   
   **Synonyms**: warrant, vindicate, validate, substantiate, defend
   
   **Antonyms**: condemn, discredit, invalidate, refute
   
   **When to Use**: Use when providing reasons for worth or correctness; in contexts of defense or validation; when showing something merits its cost or effort; appropriate in argumentative and analytical contexts; transitive verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when simple "explain" suffices without defense implication; avoid when moral justification vs. practical justification distinction matters; don't use when "excuse" (seeking forgiveness) vs. "justify" (proving rightness) is appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - justify the cost/expense
   - justify the effort/investment
   - hard to justify
   - fully justified
   - justify one's actions
   
   **Root Analysis**: just (right, fair) + -ify (make, cause to be)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "justificare" (act justly toward) → Old French "justifier" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you justify spending that much on shoes?
   - The results don't justify all the hype.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The benefits justify the substantial investment required.
   - Current evidence does not justify the proposed intervention's cost.

1. **Verb**: warrant
   **Meaning**: 
   - To justify or necessitate a course of action; to deserve or merit; to guarantee or affirm; to serve as adequate grounds for.
   
   **Synonyms**: justify, merit, deserve, necessitate, call for
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms, but: not warrant, fail to justify)
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts when something merits attention or action; when discussing whether circumstances justify effort; when emphasizing deservingness; appropriate in formal and legal contexts; transitive verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with noun "warrant" (legal document); avoid when simpler "deserve" or "need" is clearer; don't use when "guarantee" (promise) vs. "warrant" (deserve/justify) is meant.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - warrant attention/consideration
   - warrant the expense/effort
   - warrant further investigation
   - circumstances warrant
   - hardly warrant
   
   **Root Analysis**: warrant (from Germanic "warant" - guarantor)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "warant" (guarantor) → "warantir" (guarantee) → English usage evolved to "justify/merit"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Does this problem really warrant calling a plumber?
   - I don't think the situation warrants that kind of response.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The severity of the situation warrants immediate intervention.
   - These preliminary findings warrant further investigation.

1. **Verb**: depreciate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To diminish in value over time; to reduce the recorded value of an asset; to disparage or belittle; to become less valuable.
   
   **Synonyms**: decrease, decline, devalue, diminish, belittle
   
   **Antonyms**: appreciate, increase, value, praise
   
   **When to Use**: Use in financial contexts for declining value; when discussing reduction in worth; in accounting for asset valuation; can be transitive or intransitive; appropriate in financial and technical contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "deprecate" (express disapproval vs. lose value); avoid when "criticize" is clearer than "depreciate" (belittle); distinguish financial depreciation from disparagement.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - depreciate in value/worth
   - rapidly depreciate
   - depreciate assets
   - depreciate over time
   - fully depreciated
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down, away) + preci(um) (price, value) + -ate
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "de" + "pretium" (price) → Late Latin "depretiare" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - New cars depreciate in value as soon as you drive them off the lot.
   - Don't depreciate your own achievements!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Equipment depreciates according to standard accounting schedules.
   - The currency has depreciated significantly against the dollar.
