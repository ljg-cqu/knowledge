1. **Preposition**: of (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Possessive/Belonging)* Expressing the relationship between a part and a whole; belonging to, relating to, or connected with.
   - *(Specification)* Used to indicate the relationship between a noun and what it quantifies, measures, or characterizes.
   - *(Worth context)* Used to specify the value, quality, or amount of something worth having or considering.
   
   **Synonyms**: (context-dependent: belonging to, consisting of, relating to)
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms as preposition)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to connect "worth" with what specifies value; in phrases like "of value," "of worth," "of merit"; to indicate possession or connection; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "off" (away from vs. belonging to); avoid overuse in noun strings where clearer constructions exist; don't use when direct possession ("'s") is clearer for specific owners.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: of value, of worth, of merit, of consequence, of importance
   - **Noun + of**: something of value, items of worth, matters of consequence
   - **Adjective + of**: worthy of, deserving of, capable of
   
   **Root Analysis**: of (Old English preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "of" (away from, from) → evolved to express possession and relationship
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is there anything of value in that old box?
   - She's a friend of great worth to me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The collection contains items of considerable historical worth.
   - This represents a contribution of significant value to the discipline.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Purpose/Benefit)* In support of; on behalf of; to the advantage of.
   - *(Exchange)* In return for; as the price or equivalent of.
   - *(Duration)* Throughout the period of; during.
   - *(Worth context)* Indicating price, value, or exchange equivalent.
   
   **Synonyms**: (context-dependent: in exchange for, worth, at the price of)
   
   **Antonyms**: (context-dependent: against, despite)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate exchange value or price; in contexts of equivalence; when discussing what something can be traded for; in "worth X dollars" contexts implicitly; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "four" (number); avoid when "because of" (reason) vs. "for" (exchange/benefit) is meant; don't use when directional "to" is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for sale, for a price, for nothing, for a fortune
   - **Verb + for**: sell for, trade for, exchange for, valued for
   - **Worth + for**: worth [amount] for [item], worth fighting for
   
   **Root Analysis**: for (Old English preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "for" (before, on account of, for the purpose of)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I sold it for way less than it's worth.
   - Is this really worth fighting for?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The property sold for substantially more than its assessed worth.
   - This principle is worth advocating for in policy discussions.

1. **Preposition**: at (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Location/Point)* Expressing location in space or time; indicating a particular point or level.
   - *(Price/Value)* Indicating the price, rate, or value of something.
   - *(Worth context)* Specifying the exact value or price point.
   
   **Synonyms**: (context-dependent: valued at, priced at, selling for)
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms as preposition)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate specific price or value points; in contexts of valuation and assessment; when stating exact worth; appropriate in financial and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "to" (direction vs. point); avoid when range rather than specific point is meant; don't use when "for" (in exchange) is more natural than "at" (at a value).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at a price, at great cost, at full value
   - **Verb + at**: valued at, assessed at, priced at, estimated at
   - **Worth + at**: worth at least, worth at most
   
   **Root Analysis**: at (Old English preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "æt" (at, near, by)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're selling the house at way below its worth.
   - That painting is worth at least a thousand dollars.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The estate was valued at three million dollars.
   - Assets are recorded at historical cost or fair market value.

   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Direction/Recipient)* Expressing direction, movement toward, or recipient.
   - *(Relation)* Indicating the relationship between one thing and another.
   - *(Worth context)* Used in comparison phrases or to indicate recipient of value.
   
   **Synonyms**: (context-dependent: toward, for, until)
   
   **Antonyms**: from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use in comparative worth expressions; when indicating recipients of value; in phrases about worth relative to someone; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "too" (also/excessively) or "two" (number); avoid when "for" (exchange) is more appropriate than "to" (direction/relation); don't use when static relationship rather than directional relationship is meant.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: of value to, of worth to, worth to [someone]
   - **Adjective + to**: valuable to, precious to, worthwhile to
   - **Verb + to**: mean something to, matter to
   
   **Root Analysis**: to (Old English preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "tō" (in the direction of, for the purpose of)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This bracelet is worth a lot to me emotionally.
   - What's it worth to you?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Preposition**: beyond (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Location/Limit)* At or to the further side of; outside the physical limits of.
   - *(Degree)* To a degree or extent greater than; surpassing.
   - *(Worth context)* Exceeding in value; surpassing in worth or importance.
   
   **Synonyms**: exceeding, surpassing, above, more than, outside
   
   **Antonyms**: within, below, under, less than
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing that worth exceeds limits or expectations; in contexts of surpassing value; when indicating immeasurable worth; appropriate in emphatic and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when precise quantification is needed; avoid when "more than" is clearer for simple comparisons; don't use when literal spatial meaning might be confused with figurative value meaning.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: beyond value, beyond worth, beyond price
   - **Worth/Value + beyond**: worth beyond measure, value beyond calculation
   - **Adjective + beyond**: valuable beyond, precious beyond
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + yond (Old English "geond" - over, through)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "begeondan" (on the far side of) → "beyond"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These memories are worth more to me than you can imagine—beyond any price.
   - Her help was valuable beyond words.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contribution's worth extends beyond immediate financial returns.
   - The artifact possesses cultural value beyond monetary assessment.

1. **Preposition**: within (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Location/Boundary)* Inside the limits or bounds of; not exceeding.
   - *(Time)* Before the end of a period of time; during.
   - *(Worth context)* Not exceeding in value; contained in the limits of worth.
   
   **Synonyms**: inside, in, not exceeding, not beyond
   
   **Antonyms**: beyond, outside, exceeding
   
   **When to Use**: Use when worth falls within specified limits; in contexts of bounded value; when indicating value that doesn't exceed limits; appropriate in precise and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "with" (accompaniment vs. within bounds); avoid when external rather than internal boundaries are meant; don't use when "inside" (physical) vs. "within" (limits/bounds) distinction matters.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: within value range, within worth limits
   - **Worth/Value + within**: worth within budget, value within parameters
   - **Verb + within**: fall within, remain within, stay within
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + in (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wiþ" (with, against) + "innan" (inside) → "withinne" → "within"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need something within our budget—what's worth buying for under $100?
   - Is there anything of value within this price range?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All proposals must fall within the specified budget parameters.
   - The assessment must remain within established valuation guidelines.

1. **Preposition**: without (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Absence/Lack)* In the absence of; not having; lacking; excluding.
   - *(Outside)* On or at the outside of (archaic).
   - *(Worth context)* Lacking value or merit; having no worth in certain aspects.
   
   **Synonyms**: lacking, devoid of, absent, minus, excluding
   
   **Antonyms**: with, having, including, possessing
   
   **When to Use**: Use when indicating absence of worth or value; in contexts emphasizing lack; when discussing worthlessness in certain aspects; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse archaic "without" (outside) with modern "without" (lacking); avoid when "unless" (conditional) is clearer than "without" (absence); don't use when positive framing is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: without value, without worth, without merit
   - **Adjective + without**: worthless without, meaningless without, valueless without
   - **Verb + without**: exist without, remain without
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + out (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wiþūtan" (outside, externally) → evolved to mean "lacking"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That certificate is worthless without proper documentation.
   - Experience without skill isn't worth much.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Assets without proper title documentation have diminished worth.
   - Research without methodological rigor lacks academic value.
