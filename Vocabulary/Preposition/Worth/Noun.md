1. **Noun**: value (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The regard that something is held to deserve; importance or usefulness; material or monetary worth; the numerical amount denoted by an algebraic term.
   
   **Synonyms**: worth, merit, importance, usefulness, significance
   
   **Antonyms**: worthlessness, insignificance, unimportance
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing worth in quantifiable or qualitative terms; in contexts of assessment and evaluation; when discussing economic worth or personal principles; appropriate in all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse countable (specific attribute) vs. uncountable (general worth) senses; avoid when "price" (cost) vs. "value" (worth) distinction matters; don't use "values" (principles) when "value" (worth) is meant.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - of value/worth
   - great/little value
   - monetary/market value
   - assess the value
   - add value
   
   **Root Analysis**: value (from Latin "valere" - be strong, be worth)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "valere" (be strong, be worth) → Old French "value" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the value of this old watch?
   - These skills have real value in the job market.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research adds significant value to existing scholarship.
   - Asset valuation must account for both tangible and intangible value.

1. **Noun**: merit (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The quality of being good and deserving praise or reward; excellence or worth; a good point or feature; advantage.
   
   **Synonyms**: worth, value, quality, virtue, excellence
   
   **Antonyms**: demerit, fault, defect, disadvantage
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing deserved recognition or inherent quality; in contexts of evaluation and judgment; when discussing competitive worth; often in formal contexts of assessment; appropriate in formal registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use casually where "worth" or "value" is clearer; avoid when discussing price rather than quality; don't confuse "merit" (inherent quality) with "merits" (advantages/benefits).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - of merit/worth
   - judge on merit
   - merit consideration/attention
   - without merit
   - of questionable merit
   
   **Root Analysis**: merit (from Latin "merere" - earn, deserve)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "meritum" (that which is deserved) → Old French "merite" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That idea has some merit—let's explore it further.
   - The plan lacks merit in my opinion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Each proposal will be evaluated on its individual merit.
   - The research demonstrates considerable scholarly merit.

1. **Noun**: wealth (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An abundance of valuable possessions or money; the state of being rich; plentiful supplies of a particular resource; prosperity.
   
   **Synonyms**: riches, fortune, affluence, prosperity, assets
   
   **Antonyms**: poverty, destitution, lack, scarcity
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing accumulated worth or abundant resources; in economic contexts; when describing prosperity or resource abundance; appropriate in economic and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "income" (flow vs. stock); avoid when "money" (general) is clearer than "wealth" (accumulated); don't use for non-material abundance where "abundance" is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accumulate wealth
   - wealth of experience/information
   - wealth creation/distribution
   - personal/national wealth
   - net worth and wealth
   
   **Root Analysis**: wealth (from Old English "wela" - well-being, prosperity)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wela" (welfare) → Middle English "welthe" → "wealth"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They've accumulated considerable wealth over the years.
   - There's a wealth of information available online.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines patterns of wealth distribution across generations.
   - Intangible assets contribute significantly to organizational wealth.

1. **Noun**: asset (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A useful or valuable thing, person, or quality; a property or item of value owned by a person or company; resources with economic value.
   
   **Synonyms**: resource, possession, property, holding, advantage
   
   **Antonyms**: liability, burden, disadvantage, debt
   
   **When to Use**: Use in financial contexts for owned resources; when describing valuable qualities or people; in discussions of worth and resources; appropriate in business and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "liability" (obligation/debt); avoid when general "resource" is clearer; don't use when human dignity concerns make objectification problematic ("human asset" can be offensive).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - valuable asset
   - asset worth/value
   - tangible/intangible asset
   - asset management
   - strategic asset
   
   **Root Analysis**: asset (from Old French "asez" - enough, sufficient)
   
   **Etymology**: Anglo-Norman "asetz" (sufficient property) → "asset" (property having value)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her language skills are a real asset.
   - That old house has become quite an asset.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company's intellectual property represents its most valuable asset.
   - Human capital is increasingly recognized as a critical organizational asset.

1. **Noun**: investment (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action or process of investing money for profit; a thing worth buying because it will be useful or profitable; money invested; time or effort devoted to achieving a result.
   
   **Synonyms**: venture, expenditure, stake, outlay, allocation
   
   **Antonyms**: withdrawal, divestment, expenditure (consumptive)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing allocation of resources for future returns; in financial contexts; when discussing time/effort devoted to worthwhile pursuits; appropriate in business and planning contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when immediate consumption rather than future return is meant; avoid when "expense" (cost with no expected return) is more accurate; don't confuse financial investment with emotional investment loosely.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - worthwhile investment
   - investment worth/value
   - return on investment
   - make an investment
   - sound/wise investment
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + vest (clothe) + -ment (action/result)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "investire" (clothe in, surround) → Italian financial usage → English (putting money into ventures)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Education is a worthwhile investment in your future.
   - That car has proven to be a terrible investment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project represents a significant investment of institutional resources.
   - Return on investment analysis indicates the initiative's worth.

1. **Noun**: benefit (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An advantage or profit gained from something; a payment made by the state or an insurance scheme; a public performance to raise money for a charity.
   
   **Synonyms**: advantage, gain, profit, value, merit
   
   **Antonyms**: disadvantage, cost, harm, detriment
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing advantages or positive returns; in cost-benefit analyses; when describing worth in terms of outcomes; appropriate in analytical and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "cost" (what is paid vs. what is gained); avoid when "advantage" is clearer in non-economic contexts; distinguish financial benefits from non-material benefits when precision matters.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - worth the benefit
   - benefit worth having
   - cost-benefit analysis
   - derive benefit from
   - tangible/intangible benefit
   
   **Root Analysis**: bene- (well, good) + fact (do) + -it
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "benefactum" (good deed) → Old French "bienfait" → "benefit"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the benefit of upgrading to the premium version?
   - The benefits hardly seem worth the effort.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Cost-benefit analysis reveals the program's net worth to stakeholders.
   - Participants reported multiple benefits from the intervention.

1. **Noun**: utility (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being useful, profitable, or beneficial; usefulness; (economics) the satisfaction or benefit derived from consuming goods or services.
   
   **Synonyms**: usefulness, value, benefit, practicality, functionality
   
   **Antonyms**: uselessness, futility, impracticality
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or technical contexts discussing usefulness; in economic discussions of value; when emphasizing practical worth over aesthetic value; appropriate in academic and technical contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when aesthetic or intrinsic value is meant; avoid when "usefulness" is clearer in non-technical contexts; don't confuse with "utilities" (services like water/electricity).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - utility and worth
   - practical utility
   - marginal utility
   - utility value
   - maximize utility
   
   **Root Analysis**: util(is) (useful) + -ity (state of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "utilitas" (usefulness) → Old French "utilite" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I question the utility of buying such an expensive phone.
   - What's the actual utility of this gadget?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The economic concept of utility measures subjective value.
   - Consumer behavior theory assumes individuals maximize utility.

1. **Noun**: appraisal (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An act of assessing something or someone; an expert estimate of value; a formal assessment of performance; evaluation.
   
   **Synonyms**: assessment, evaluation, valuation, estimation, judgment
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms, but: neglect, disregard)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing formal evaluation of worth; in contexts of performance review or property valuation; when assessing value systematically; appropriate in professional and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when informal opinion rather than systematic assessment is meant; avoid when "evaluation" or "assessment" is clearer and less formal; distinguish performance appraisal from property appraisal.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - appraisal of worth/value
   - conduct an appraisal
   - property appraisal
   - performance appraisal
   - fair/accurate appraisal
   
   **Root Analysis**: appraise (set a price on) + -al (action/process)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "aprisier" (set value) → "appraise" → "appraisal"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to get an appraisal to know what this ring is worth.
   - My performance appraisal is coming up next week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The appraisal determined the property's fair market value.
   - Critical appraisal of the literature reveals significant gaps in research.

1. **Noun**: premium (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An amount paid for insurance; an amount above the normal price or cost; something of superior quality; a bonus or reward.
   
   **Synonyms**: bonus, surcharge, extra, supplement, markup
   
   **Antonyms**: discount, markdown, deduction, standard price
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing additional payment for higher value; in contexts of superior quality commanding higher worth; in insurance contexts; appropriate in business and economic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse insurance premium (periodic payment) with price premium (additional cost); avoid when "bonus" or "surcharge" is clearer; distinguish adjective "premium" (superior) from noun "premium" (extra payment).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - premium worth paying
   - at a premium (scarce and valuable)
   - premium value
   - pay a premium
   - command a premium
   
   **Root Analysis**: premium (from Latin "praemium" - reward, prize)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praemium" (reward) → English commercial usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is the premium version worth the extra cost?
   - Organic food commands a premium at the grocery store.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Sustainable products command a premium in contemporary markets.
   - The premium reflects the elevated risk associated with the policy.

1. **Noun**: treasure (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A quantity of precious metals, gems, or other valuable objects; a very valuable object or person; something highly valued.
   
   **Synonyms**: riches, valuables, prize, gem, fortune
   
   **Antonyms**: junk, trash, worthless item
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing items of great worth; in contexts of emotional or historical value; when emphasizing preciousness; appropriate in emotional and emphatic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing ordinary valuable items; don't use when financial precision is needed ("asset" or "valuable" more appropriate); be careful with metaphorical overuse.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - treasure worth having/keeping
   - priceless treasure
   - national treasure
   - hidden treasure
   - treasure trove
   
   **Root Analysis**: treasure (from Latin "thesaurus" - storehouse, treasure)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "thesauros" → Latin "thesaurus" → Old French "tresor" → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These old photos are treasures to me—worth more than money.
   - Grandma's recipes are family treasures.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The museum houses national treasures of inestimable worth.
   - Archaeological treasures provide invaluable insights into ancient civilizations.
