1. **Expression**: worth one's while (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Valuable or useful enough to justify the time and effort someone spends on it; deserving of one's time and attention; providing sufficient benefit to make effort worthwhile.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: worthwhile, worth the effort, worth doing, beneficial, rewarding
   
   **Antonyms**: waste of time, not worth it, pointless
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing whether an activity justifies time investment; in contexts of persuasion or recommendation; when evaluating cost-benefit of time expenditure; appropriate across all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing monetary value rather than time value; when simpler "worthwhile" suffices without emphasis on personal investment; when formality requires more explicit benefit-cost language.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "make it worth someone's while" (offer incentive)
   - "worth your while" (second person)
   - "worth my while" (first person)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Historically, "while" referred to period of time; "worth one's while" means valuable enough to compensate for time spent; dates to 16th century; emphasizes personal time as valuable resource
   
   **Root Analysis**: worth (value) + possessive + while (period of time)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Trust me, taking that class is worth your while.
   - Is it worth my while to wait for the sale, or should I buy now?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants found the training worth their while despite the time commitment.
   - The committee must determine whether pursuing this avenue is worth stakeholders' while.

1. **Expression**: more trouble than it's worth (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Requiring more effort, difficulty, or problems than the value or benefit gained; not worthwhile because complications outweigh benefits.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed comparative idiom)
   
   **Synonyms**: not worth the hassle, not worth the effort, more problem than value, too difficult to justify
   
   **Antonyms**: worth the effort, worthwhile, worth the trouble, beneficial
   
   **When to Use**: When rejecting or discouraging something due to excessive complications; when explaining why something isn't worthwhile; in contexts where effort-benefit ratio is unfavorable; appropriate across registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to reject without criticizing; when "not worth it" is sufficient without emphasizing trouble; when you might want to reconsider later (this phrase is quite final).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "more hassle than it's worth"
   - "more problem than it's worth"
   - "more bother than it's worth"
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Compares two abstract quantities (trouble vs. worth) in fixed pattern; "more X than Y" structure conventionalized for this specific comparison; emphasizes excessive difficulty as reason for rejection
   
   **Root Analysis**: comparative structure + trouble (difficulty) + than + worth (value)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Fixing that old car is more trouble than it's worth—just sell it.
   - Don't bother complaining—it's more trouble than it's worth.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee determined that regulatory compliance would be more trouble than it's worth.
   - Pursuing litigation in this case is more trouble than the settlement is worth.

1. **Expression**: worth the price of admission (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Valuable or entertaining enough to justify the cost or effort of experiencing it; so good that it alone makes the entire experience worthwhile; especially used for highlights of an experience.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (theatrical/entertainment origin)
   
   **Synonyms**: worth the ticket price, worth the cost, worth paying for, justifies the expense
   
   **Antonyms**: not worth the money, disappointing, overpriced, waste of money
   
   **When to Use**: When highlighting exceptional value or standout feature; in reviews and recommendations; when emphasizing that one aspect alone justifies entire cost; somewhat casual register; often in entertainment contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal financial analysis (too colloquial); when discussing non-entertainment value; when overall experience matters more than highlights; in contexts without clear "admission" parallel.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "worth the price of admission alone" (emphasizing single feature)
   - "worth the admission price"
   - "worth the ticket price"
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originates from theater/entertainment where admission price was barrier to entry; metaphorically extended to any situation with entry cost; "admission" signals entertainment/experience context; emphasizes getting money's worth
   
   **Root Analysis**: worth + price + of + admission (entry fee)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That concert's final song alone was worth the price of admission!
   - His jokes are worth the price of admission—the rest is just a bonus.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Reviewers noted that the keynote speaker alone was worth the conference admission price.
   - The networking opportunities proved worth the price of admission despite limited session value.

1. **Expression**: net worth (type: collocation/technical term)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The total value of someone's assets minus their liabilities; the difference between what someone owns and what they owe; overall financial value.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed financial terminology)
   
   **Synonyms**: total assets, overall wealth, financial worth, equity value
   
   **Antonyms**: debt, liabilities, what one owes
   
   **When to Use**: In financial contexts discussing overall wealth; when calculating or reporting financial position; in contexts of financial planning and assessment; primarily formal and technical usage.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing income rather than accumulated wealth; when "assets" or "wealth" alone is clearer; in casual contexts where technical financial term seems overly formal; don't confuse with "net income" (earnings).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "personal net worth"
   - "calculate net worth"
   - "net worth statement"
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Net" (after deductions) naturally pairs with "worth" (total value) in financial contexts; accounting convention distinguishes gross (before) from net (after deductions); universally understood in finance as assets minus liabilities
   
   **Root Analysis**: net (clear of deductions) + worth (total value)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After paying off student loans, my net worth finally went positive!
   - What's your net worth now that you've sold the house?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The individual's net worth is estimated at $12 million.
   - Net worth calculations must account for both tangible and intangible assets.

1. **Expression**: for what it's worth (type: idiom/discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to present information or an opinion while suggesting it may not be valuable or relevant; acknowledging that what follows may or may not be helpful; hedging device showing uncertainty about value of contribution.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed hedging expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: take it or leave it, in my opinion, for all the good it may do, FWIW (internet abbreviation)
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms, but more assertive: "definitely," "I'm certain")
   
   **When to Use**: When offering potentially unwelcome or uncertain advice; when giving opinions you're not confident about; when showing deference to recipient's judgment; appropriate across most registers; very common discourse marker.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need to be assertive and confident; when expertise should be foregrounded not minimized; in formal reports where authority shouldn't be undermined; when hedging seems insincere or manipulative.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "for whatever it's worth" (slightly more dismissive)
   - "FWIW" (internet/text abbreviation)
   - "for what it may be worth" (more formal)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Grammaticalized phrase that became discourse marker; "what it's worth" refers to value of coming statement; functions as extreme politeness/hedging device; shows humility about one's contributions; conventionalized as single chunk
   
   **Root Analysis**: for + what + it + is + worth (relative clause structure conventionalized as idiom)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - For what it's worth, I think you made the right choice.
   - FWIW, that restaurant has terrible reviews.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - For what it's worth, preliminary data suggests a different interpretation.
   - The consultant offered, for what it may be worth, an alternative framework for analysis.

1. **Expression**: worth its weight in gold (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Extremely valuable or useful; having great worth far exceeding apparent or monetary value; invaluable.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (metaphorical comparison to gold)
   
   **Synonyms**: invaluable, priceless, extremely valuable, worth a fortune, precious
   
   **Antonyms**: worthless, useless, valueless, not worth much
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing exceptional value; in contexts of strong recommendation; when something proves more valuable than expected; appropriate in emphatic and emotional contexts; somewhat casual to neutral register.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In financial reports requiring precise valuation; when moderate rather than hyperbolic praise is appropriate; when gold reference might seem culturally inappropriate or confusing.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "worth her weight in gold" (personal reference)
   - "worth their weight in gold"
   - "pure gold" (shortened metaphor)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Gold as universal standard of high value; "weight in gold" creates vivid calculation of worth; ancient expression reflecting gold's historical role as wealth standard; hyperbolic comparison emphasizes extraordinary value
   
   **Root Analysis**: worth + possessive + weight + in + gold (measurement metaphor)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That employee is worth her weight in gold—don't let her leave!
   - This tool has been worth its weight in gold for my projects.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The consultant's expertise proved worth its weight in gold during the crisis.
   - Access to this database is worth its weight in gold for researchers in the field.

1. **Expression**: get one's money's worth (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To receive good value in return for money spent; to receive benefits equal to or exceeding what was paid; to fully utilize or benefit from something purchased.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (possessive structure conventionalized)
   
   **Synonyms**: receive good value, get full value, maximize benefit, make the most of
   
   **Antonyms**: waste money, not worth it, overpay, be cheated
   
   **When to Use**: In consumer contexts discussing value received; when evaluating purchases or expenditures; when advising how to maximize value; appropriate in casual and semi-formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal financial reports (too colloquial); when discussing non-monetary exchanges; when "value" or "return on investment" is more precise for business contexts.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "get your money's worth"
   - "getting full money's worth"
   - "got my money's worth"
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Double possessive structure ("one's money's worth") is unusual and marks this as fixed expression; "worth" specifically refers to value equivalent to money paid; consumer-oriented idiom about value-for-money
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (obtain) + possessive + money + possessive + worth (value equivalent)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You'll definitely get your money's worth with that buffet—eat up!
   - I made sure to use all the amenities to get my money's worth.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Survey results indicate that 87% of participants felt they got their money's worth.
   - To ensure customers get their money's worth, we've enhanced the service package.

1. **Expression**: know one's worth (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have accurate understanding of one's value, abilities, or deserving; to recognize one's own capabilities and not undervalue oneself; self-awareness of personal value.
   - *Fixedness*: medium (common pattern with variations)
   
   **Synonyms**: recognize one's value, appreciate one's abilities, understand one's value, self-worth awareness
   
   **Antonyms**: undervalue oneself, lack confidence, sell oneself short
   
   **When to Use**: In contexts of self-advocacy and empowerment; when discussing confidence and self-valuation; in negotiations and professional development; appropriate across registers with empowerment emphasis.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing arrogance or overconfidence (this phrase has positive connotation); when humility rather than self-assertion is valued; in cultures that discourage self-promotion.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "know your worth"
   - "knows his/her worth"
   - "recognize one's worth"
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Know" (awareness) naturally partners with "worth" (value) in contexts of self-valuation; empowerment discourse emphasizes accurate self-assessment; "one's own worth" is possessed/inherent quality to be recognized
   
   **Root Analysis**: know (be aware of) + possessive + worth (value, merit)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't let them lowball you—know your worth!
   - She knows her worth and won't settle for less.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Professional development programs help employees recognize their worth in the marketplace.
   - Knowing one's worth is essential for effective salary negotiation.

1. **Expression**: prove one's worth (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To demonstrate one's value, ability, or usefulness through actions or results; to show that one deserves recognition, position, or reward; to validate one's value.
   - *Fixedness*: medium (common collocation with variations)
   
   **Synonyms**: demonstrate value, show one's ability, establish credibility, validate oneself, earn respect
   
   **Antonyms**: fail to deliver, disappoint expectations, prove worthless
   
   **When to Use**: In contexts of testing or evaluation; when discussing opportunity to demonstrate capability; in probationary or competitive situations; appropriate in professional and achievement contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When worth shouldn't require proving (inherent dignity contexts); when phrase might suggest current doubt about someone's value; in non-competitive or non-evaluative contexts.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "prove your worth"
   - "proves his/her worth"
   - "proven one's worth"
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Prove" (demonstrate through evidence) naturally collocates with "worth" (value) in achievement contexts; worth as something demonstrated through performance; common in competitive/evaluative discourse
   
   **Root Analysis**: prove (demonstrate, establish) + possessive + worth (value, capability)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is your chance to prove your worth to the team!
   - He's proven his worth time and time again.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - New employees are given six months to prove their worth to the organization.
   - The technology has proven its worth through consistent performance improvements.

1. **Expression**: of little/great worth (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having minimal or substantial value; used to characterize the degree of value something possesses; formal evaluation of worth.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (conventional value assessment pattern)
   
   **Synonyms**: of little/great value, minimally/highly valuable, insignificant/significant, worthless/precious
   
   **Antonyms**: (internal: "little worth" ↔ "great worth")
   
   **When to Use**: In formal evaluations and assessments; when making judgments about worth in measured language; in academic and professional contexts; more formal register than simple "worthless" or "valuable."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where simpler alternatives work better; when precise quantification rather than general assessment is needed; when "value" is clearer than "worth."
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - "of considerable worth"
   - "of negligible worth"
   - "of substantial worth"
   - "of no worth"
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Of" + abstract noun is formal assessment pattern; "little/great" are conventional quantifiers for abstract qualities; "worth" functions as uncountable noun in this construction; common in evaluative academic/formal discourse
   
   **Root Analysis**: of (possessing) + quantifier (little/great) + worth (value)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That advice was of little worth to me, honestly.
   - Her contributions have been of great worth to this project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee judged the proposal to be of little worth given current priorities.
   - Historical documents of great worth have been preserved in the archive.
