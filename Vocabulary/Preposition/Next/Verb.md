1. **Verb**: follow (T/I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To come after in time, order, or position; to be the next thing
   - To go or come after a person or thing proceeding ahead
   - To happen or occur as a consequence or result
   
   **Synonyms**: succeed, come after, pursue, ensue, result
   
   **Antonyms**: precede, lead, anticipate, come before
   
   **When to Use**: Use for temporal, spatial, or logical succession; versatile across all contexts and registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ambiguous between physical pursuit and temporal succession; clarify context when necessary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - follow after
   - follow next
   - follow in sequence
   - what follows
   - as follows
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow (Old English folgian)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English folgian (accompany, pursue) → general succession meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Wednesday follows Tuesday.
   - You go first, I'll follow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results section follows the methodology.
   - A detailed discussion follows in the next chapter.

1. **Verb**: succeed (T/I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To come next after another in order, position, or time
   - To inherit a title, throne, or position from someone
   - To achieve desired aim or result (different meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: follow, come after, replace, inherit, take over
   
   **Antonyms**: precede, come before, fail (for achievement meaning)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for formal succession, especially in organizational or hereditary contexts; more formal than "follow"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be confused with "succeed" meaning achieve success; context must clarify
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - succeed to the throne
   - succeed in office
   - succeed someone
   - be succeeded by
   - rightful successor
   
   **Root Analysis**: suc- (under/up) + ceed (go)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin succedere (come after, follow) → formal succession and achievement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Charles succeeded Elizabeth as king.
   - Who will succeed her as CEO?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vice president will succeed to the office upon vacancy.
   - Each generation succeeds the previous in managing the estate.

1. **Verb**: proceed (I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To go forward or continue, especially after stopping
   - To move to the next stage or step in a sequence
   - To begin or continue a course of action
   
   **Synonyms**: continue, advance, progress, move forward, go on
   
   **Antonyms**: stop, halt, cease, retreat, discontinue
   
   **When to Use**: Use for continuing or advancing to the next stage; formal to neutral tone; common in instructions and processes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in casual speech; prefer "go ahead" or "continue"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proceed with
   - proceed to
   - proceed next
   - proceed as planned
   - before proceeding
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + ceed (go)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin procedere (go forward) → advancement and continuation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's proceed to the next item.
   - You can proceed when ready.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will proceed to the next agenda item.
   - Participants may proceed to the next phase upon completion.

1. **Verb**: continue (T/I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To persist in an activity or process; to keep going
   - To resume an activity after interruption
   - To extend in time or space without interruption
   
   **Synonyms**: persist, carry on, resume, maintain, go on, proceed
   
   **Antonyms**: stop, cease, discontinue, halt, terminate
   
   **When to Use**: Use for ongoing activities or resumption after pause; versatile across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for starting completely new activities; implies connection to prior state
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - continue with
   - continue to
   - continue next
   - to be continued
   - continue on
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together/intensive) + tinu- (hold)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin continuare (join together, make continuous) → ongoing persistence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's continue this discussion later.
   - The story continues next week!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study will continue into the next fiscal year.
   - Participants may continue to the next module.

1. **Verb**: advance (T/I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move forward in position, progress, or time
   - To proceed to the next stage or level
   - To make progress or cause something to progress
   
   **Synonyms**: progress, proceed, move forward, promote, further
   
   **Antonyms**: retreat, withdraw, regress, decline, reverse
   
   **When to Use**: Use for forward movement or progression; implies improvement or development; formal to neutral tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for simple continuation without improvement; implies advancement
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advance to
   - advance next
   - advance forward
   - advance the cause
   - advance in stages
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to/toward) + vance (go)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin abante (from before) → French avancer → forward movement and progression
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Click here to advance to the next level.
   - You've advanced to the finals!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research advances understanding of cellular mechanisms.
   - Candidates who pass will advance to the next selection stage.

1. **Verb**: transition (I/T)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To undergo or cause to undergo a process of change from one state or condition to another
   - To move from one stage, subject, or place to the next
   
   **Synonyms**: shift, move, change, progress, convert
   
   **Antonyms**: remain, stay, persist, maintain status quo
   
   **When to Use**: Use for deliberate movement between distinct states or stages; formal tone; emphasizes the change process
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; sounds like jargon in some contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - transition to
   - transition into
   - transition between
   - transition next
   - smooth transition
   
   **Root Analysis**: trans- (across) + it (go) + -ion
   
   **Etymology**: Latin transitio (passage across) → movement between states (technical use from 1940s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're transitioning to remote work next month.
   - Let's transition to the next topic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization is transitioning to a new operational model.
   - Patients transition from intensive care to standard recovery units.

1. **Verb**: move (T/I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To go from one place, position, or stage to another
   - To progress or advance to the next item or stage
   - To change position or location
   
   **Synonyms**: progress, advance, proceed, shift, go
   
   **Antonyms**: stay, remain, halt, stop, pause
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical or abstract movement to next position; versatile across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be vague; specify direction or destination when clarity is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move on
   - move to the next
   - move forward
   - move along
   - move ahead
   
   **Root Analysis**: move (from Latin movere)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin movere (to move) → Old French → general movement and progression
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's move on to the next question.
   - We should move to the next house now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will move to consider the next proposal.
   - The curriculum moves systematically through progressive levels.

1. **Verb**: approach (T/I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To come near or nearer to something in distance, time, or quality
   - To begin to deal with or consider something; to tackle the next item
   - To come close to or approximate something
   
   **Synonyms**: near, come close to, draw near, tackle, address
   
   **Antonyms**: depart, leave, withdraw, avoid, retreat
   
   **When to Use**: Use for gradual movement toward next stage or item; implies careful or deliberate movement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for sudden or immediate transitions; implies gradual approach
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approach the next
   - approach next
   - approach carefully
   - approaching deadline
   - approach the problem
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to/toward) + proach (near)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin appropiare (come near) → Old French → gradual nearing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're approaching the next exit.
   - Let's approach the next challenge together.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study approaches the question from a novel perspective.
   - As we approach the next fiscal quarter, planning is essential.

1. **Verb**: queue (I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To line up or wait in a line for one's turn; to be next in sequence
   - To arrange or be arranged in a queue
   
   **Synonyms**: line up, wait in line, get in line, form a queue
   
   **Antonyms**: disperse, scatter, leave the line
   
   **When to Use**: Use for orderly waiting systems; British English more common than American (which prefers "line up")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Regional variation; Americans may not recognize verb form
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - queue up
   - queue for
   - queue next
   - queuing system
   - wait in queue
   
   **Root Analysis**: queue (tail, line)
   
   **Etymology**: French queue (tail) → British English line-up system (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's queue up for tickets.
   - Who's queuing next?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Customers queue at designated service points.
   - Requests are processed in the order they queue in the system.

1. **Verb**: ensue (I)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To happen or occur afterward as a result or consequence
   - To follow as the next event; to come next in sequence
   
   **Synonyms**: follow, result, occur subsequently, arise, transpire
   
   **Antonyms**: precede, come before, anticipate
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to indicate consequences or sequential events; literary or formal tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; sounds archaic or literary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - chaos ensued
   - what ensued
   - ensued from
   - immediately ensued
   - subsequently ensued
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (in) + sue (follow)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin insequi (follow after) → Old French ensivre → formal consequence marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used; prefer "what happened next" or "then")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Significant controversy ensued following the announcement.
   - The events that ensued demonstrated the policy's shortcomings.
