1. **Original Expression**: What's next?  
   **Context**: Asking about the following action or event in a sequence
   
   **Core Intent**: To inquire about subsequent steps or events
   
   **Casual**: What's next?  
   *Characteristics*: Contracted form, direct, concise, conversational tone  
   *Use when*: Speaking with friends, colleagues of equal status, informal meetings
   
   **Semi-Formal**: What is the next step?  
   *Characteristics*: Full form, clear structure, neutral professional tone  
   *Use when*: Business meetings, workplace discussions, professional emails
   
   **Formal**: What is the subsequent course of action?  
   *Characteristics*: Sophisticated vocabulary ("subsequent," "course of action"), complete sentences, academic tone  
   *Use when*: Academic presentations, formal reports, executive communications
   
   **Humorous**: What's the next adventure? / What fresh chaos awaits?  
   *Characteristics*: Playful word choice, rhetorical flair, light self-deprecation  
   *Use when*: Lighthearted moments, team bonding, casual presentations  
   *Avoid when*: Serious situations, crisis management, formal evaluations
   
   **Lively**: So what's coming up next?! / What happens now?!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic punctuation, enthusiastic tone, present progressive for immediacy  
   *Use when*: Motivational contexts, excited anticipation, dynamic presentations
   
   **Empathetic**: What feels like the right next step for you? / What would you like to do next?  
   *Characteristics*: Person-centered language, acknowledgment of feelings, open-ended phrasing  
   *Use when*: Coaching conversations, supporting someone through decisions, counseling
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status requires "What is the next step?" or formal variant; equals allow "What's next?"
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/new contacts require semi-formal; close relationships allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Crisis/serious matters require formal; routine activities allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more formal language even in casual settings
   - *Medium*: Written communication (emails) typically more formal than spoken
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual "What's next?" in formal presentations - sounds unprofessional
   - Using overly formal "subsequent course of action" in team meetings - sounds pompous
   - Asking "What's next?" when someone is processing difficult emotions - sounds dismissive
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American English tends toward more casual language than British English or Asian English in professional settings
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Team meeting with peers → **Appropriate Variant**: "What's next?" (casual/semi-formal)
   - **Situation 2**: Presenting to board of directors → **Appropriate Variant**: "What is the subsequent phase?" (formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Coaching someone through career transition → **Appropriate Variant**: "What feels like the right next step for you?" (empathetic)

1. **Original Expression**: You're next  
   **Context**: Informing someone their turn is coming or has arrived
   
   **Core Intent**: To notify someone of their position in sequence
   
   **Casual**: You're next / You're up  
   *Characteristics*: Contracted, brief, directive tone, minimal elaboration  
   *Use when*: Informal queues, among friends, casual service contexts
   
   **Semi-Formal**: You are next in line / It's your turn next  
   *Characteristics*: Full form, complete phrasing, clear and neutral  
   *Use when*: Professional service contexts, organized meetings, workplace rotations
   
   **Formal**: You are next to present / We are prepared to see you now  
   *Characteristics*: Complete sentences, specific verbs, dignified tone, passive constructions  
   *Use when*: Formal proceedings, interviews, medical appointments, official contexts
   
   **Humorous**: You're up, champ! / Your moment of glory awaits!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful nicknames, dramatic language, tongue-in-cheek tone  
   *Use when*: Friendly competitions, casual presentations, rapport-building  
   *Avoid when*: Nervous presenters, high-stakes situations, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: You're on! / Time to shine!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic, encouraging, motivational language  
   *Use when*: Performance contexts, motivational settings, team encouragement
   
   **Empathetic**: Whenever you're ready, it's your turn / Take your time—you're next when you're ready  
   *Characteristics*: Patience markers, acknowledgment of readiness, pressure-reducing language  
   *Use when*: Nervous speakers, stressful situations, supporting anxious individuals
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Authority figures should use complete formal phrases; peers can be casual
   - *Social Distance*: Professional contexts require semi-formal minimum; friends allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes presentations require formal; routine turns allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures see directness as rude; add softening language
   - *Medium*: Face-to-face allows more casualness than written notifications
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Shouting "You're up!" in quiet formal settings - disruptive and inappropriate
   - Using formal "We are prepared to see you now" at casual gatherings - creates awkward distance
   - Using casual "You're next" without allowing preparation time in high-pressure situations
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: DMV counter service → **Appropriate Variant**: "Next, please!" (semi-formal institutional)
   - **Situation 2**: Job interview waiting room → **Appropriate Variant**: "We're ready for you now" (formal, respectful)
   - **Situation 3**: Friends playing games → **Appropriate Variant**: "You're up!" (casual)

1. **Original Expression**: Let's move on to the next topic  
   **Context**: Transitioning between discussion topics or agenda items
   
   **Core Intent**: To signal transition from current topic to following one
   
   **Casual**: Let's move on / Next topic!  
   *Characteristics*: Brief, directive, elliptical structures (omitting words)  
   *Use when*: Informal team meetings, study groups, casual discussions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Let's proceed to the next item / Moving on to the next topic  
   *Characteristics*: Complete professional phrasing, neutral tone, inclusive "let's"  
   *Use when*: Standard business meetings, professional discussions, project reviews
   
   **Formal**: We shall now address the next agenda item / Let us transition to the subsequent topic  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verbs ("shall," "address"), sophisticated vocabulary, complete formal structure  
   *Use when*: Board meetings, academic conferences, official proceedings
   
   **Humorous**: On to bigger and better things! / Let's see what's behind door number two!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphors, game show references, exaggerated enthusiasm  
   *Use when*: Light-hearted meetings, creative brainstorms, team morale building  
   *Avoid when*: Serious topics need closure, sensitive discussions, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: Alright, what's up next?! / Let's dive into the next one!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic tone, action verbs ("dive"), inclusive energy  
   *Use when*: Dynamic presentations, motivational settings, engaging audiences
   
   **Empathetic**: If everyone feels ready, we can move forward / Does anyone need more time before we transition?  
   *Characteristics*: Checks for readiness, acknowledges group needs, permission-seeking  
   *Use when*: Difficult discussions, processing emotional topics, inclusive facilitation
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Meeting leader determines formality; match their level
   - *Social Distance*: New groups require semi-formal; established teams allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious matters require respectful formal transitions
   - *Cultural Context*: High-context cultures may need more explicit closure before transitioning
   - *Medium*: Written agendas should use semi-formal regardless of spoken style
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Abruptly saying "Next topic!" when discussion isn't concluded - appears dismissive
   - Using casual "Let's move on" in formal board meetings - lacks appropriate gravitas
   - Never checking if group is ready to transition - ignores processing needs
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some cultures expect more closure and summary before transitions; others value efficiency and quick movement
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Weekly team sync → **Appropriate Variant**: "Let's move to the next item" (casual-semi-formal)
   - **Situation 2**: Academic committee meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: "We shall now consider the next matter" (formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Grief support group → **Appropriate Variant**: "When everyone's ready, we can continue" (empathetic)

   **Core Intent**: To say goodbye while establishing future connection
   
   **Casual**: See you next week / Catch you next week  
   *Characteristics*: Informal verbs ("catch"), contracted forms, friendly tone  
   *Use when*: Colleagues, friends, regular casual meetings
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'll see you next week / Until next week  
   *Characteristics*: Complete sentences, neutral tone, clear future reference  
   *Use when*: Professional colleagues, regular business meetings, client relationships
   
   **Formal**: I look forward to our next meeting / We shall reconvene next week  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verbs ("reconvene"), complete formal structures, professional distance  
   *Use when*: Executive meetings, formal client relations, official proceedings
   
   **Humorous**: Don't miss me too much! See you next week / Same time, same place, same shenanigans?  
   *Characteristics*: Playful self-reference, rhetorical questions, lighthearted exaggeration  
   *Use when*: Friendly workplace relationships, casual recurring events, humor-appropriate contexts  
   *Avoid when*: First-time meetings, professional distance needed, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: Can't wait for next week! / Looking forward to next time!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic tone, emphasis on anticipation, energetic punctuation  
   *Use when*: Exciting projects, positive team dynamics, motivational contexts
   
   **Empathetic**: Take care until next week / I hope the week goes well for you  
   *Characteristics*: Care expressions, well-wishes, person-focused language  
   *Use when*: Supporting colleagues through challenges, coaching relationships, pastoral contexts
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: With superiors, use formal; with peers, match their style
   - *Social Distance*: New professional relationships require semi-formal; established allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: After serious discussions, empathetic variant shows awareness
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more elaborate farewells; others prefer brevity
   - *Medium*: Email farewells typically semi-formal; spoken allows more variation
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "Catch you next week" with senior executives - too casual, inappropriate
   - Formal "We shall reconvene" with casual team - creates unnecessary distance
   - Generic "See you next week" after someone shared personal struggles - misses emotional context
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Regular team meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: "See you next week" (casual-semi-formal)
   - **Situation 2**: Client concluding consulting session → **Appropriate Variant**: "I look forward to our next meeting" (formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Colleague mentioned upcoming surgery → **Appropriate Variant**: "Take care—thinking of you. See you when you're back" (empathetic)

1. **Original Expression**: What should I do next?  
   **Context**: Asking for guidance about following action
   
   **Core Intent**: To seek direction about subsequent steps
   
   **Casual**: What should I do next? / What's next for me?  
   *Characteristics*: Direct question, conversational tone, first-person focus  
   *Use when*: Asking friends, peers, informal learning contexts
   
   **Semi-Formal**: What is the next step? / How should I proceed?  
   *Characteristics*: Professional phrasing, neutral tone, action-focused  
   *Use when*: Workplace training, professional guidance, standard business contexts
   
   **Formal**: What would be the appropriate next course of action? / How would you recommend I proceed?  
   *Characteristics*: Conditional phrasing ("would be"), sophisticated vocabulary, deferential tone  
   *Use when*: Consulting superiors, formal mentorship, high-stakes decisions
   
   **Humorous**: What's my next move, coach? / Hit me with the next challenge!  
   *Characteristics*: Sports metaphors, playful role assignment, enthusiastic tone  
   *Use when*: Friendly training contexts, game-like learning, rapport-building  
   *Avoid when*: Serious professional guidance, crisis situations, formal mentorship
   
   **Lively**: I'm ready—what's next?! / Bring on the next step!  
   *Characteristics*: Readiness expressions, enthusiastic tone, forward momentum  
   *Use when*: Motivational contexts, expressing eagerness, dynamic learning
   
   **Empathetic**: I'm feeling a bit lost—could you help me understand what comes next? / I'd appreciate guidance on next steps  
   *Characteristics*: Vulnerability expression, explicit help request, gratitude markers  
   *Use when*: Feeling overwhelmed, need emotional support, building trust relationships
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: With authority, use formal with conditional softening; peers allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: Professional contexts minimum semi-formal; close mentors allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Important decisions require formal; routine tasks allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct questioning may seem inappropriate in hierarchical cultures
   - *Medium*: Written requests typically more formal than spoken
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Asking "What should I do next?" repeatedly - appears unable to think independently
   - Using casual "What's next for me?" with senior leadership - too informal
   - Never expressing confusion when lost - leads to errors; empathetic variant shows self-awareness
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some cultures expect learners to figure things out; others expect explicit guidance. Adapt to cultural norms.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Learning new software from peer → **Appropriate Variant**: "What should I do next?" (casual-semi-formal)
   - **Situation 2**: Consulting with executive mentor → **Appropriate Variant**: "What would you recommend as my next step?" (formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Overwhelmed new employee → **Appropriate Variant**: "I'd appreciate guidance on what comes next" (empathetic)
