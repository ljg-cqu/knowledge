1. **Preposition**: next (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Immediately adjacent to or beside something; in the closest position
   - *(Temporal)* Immediately following in time or order; coming after the present or a specified point
   - *(Sequential)* Following in a series or sequence; the one that comes after
   
   **Synonyms**: beside (spatial), following (temporal), subsequent, adjacent, neighboring
   
   **Antonyms**: previous, prior, before, preceding, last
   
   **When to Use**: Use "next" for immediate adjacency in space or time; common in everyday conversation, scheduling, directions, and sequencing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for distant future events (prefer "later" or "in the future"); avoid when specific time/position needs emphasis (use "immediately after" or "directly beside")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: next to, next door, next week, next month, next in line
   - **Verb + prep**: sit next to, stand next to, come next to, move next to
   - **Adjective + prep**: positioned next to, located next to
   - **Noun + prep**: person next to, building next to, day next to
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (superlative form of "nigh" meaning near)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English nēhst (nearest, superlative of nēah "near") → Middle English nexte → unchanged temporal/spatial adjacency meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you sit next to me?
   - Let's meet next week!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is located next to the main campus building.
   - The next quarterly meeting will address strategic initiatives.

1. **Preposition**: next to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Immediately beside or adjacent to something; in the closest position to something
   - *(Comparative)* Almost; nearly; close to a particular state or condition (informal)
   - *(Sequential)* Second in importance or preference after something
   
   **Synonyms**: beside, adjacent to, alongside, by, near to
   
   **Antonyms**: far from, away from, distant from
   
   **When to Use**: Primary use for spatial proximity; also for ranking or comparison ("next to impossible"); versatile in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precise distance measurement is needed; when "beside" sounds more natural in formal writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: right next to, directly next to, next to nothing, next to impossible
   - **Verb + prep**: sit next to, stand next to, place next to, position next to
   - **Adjective + prep**: seated next to, located next to
   - **Noun + prep**: seat next to, house next to, person next to
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (nearest) + to (directional marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound prepositional phrase from Old English nēhst + tō (dative marker)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Put your bag next to mine.
   - That's next to impossible!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The laboratory is positioned next to the research center.
   - This task is next to impossible without additional resources.

1. **Preposition**: next door (type: phrasal - adverbial/prepositional)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In or to the adjacent building, room, or apartment; in the immediately neighboring location
   - *(Abstract)* Very close or immediately adjacent in any context
   
   **Synonyms**: in the adjacent building, in the neighboring house, close by, nearby
   
   **Antonyms**: far away, distant, across town
   
   **When to Use**: Specifically for adjacent buildings, rooms, or properties; can be used as adverb ("lives next door") or with "to" as preposition ("next door to the bank")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate for non-adjacent locations; don't use for temporal relationships
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: next door to, right next door, next door neighbor
   - **Verb + prep**: live next door, move next door, work next door
   - **Adjective + prep**: located next door, situated next door
   - **Noun + prep**: house next door, apartment next door, office next door
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (nearest) + door (entrance/building metonym)
   
   **Etymology**: From architectural/spatial relationship (18th century) → immediate adjacency
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My best friend lives next door!
   - Let's grab coffee next door.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The administrative offices are located next door to the main entrance.
   - The organization operates next door to our headquarters.

1. **Preposition**: the next best thing (type: phrasal - idiomatic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Comparative)* The second-best option or alternative when the preferred choice is unavailable
   
   **Synonyms**: second choice, alternative option, substitute, runner-up
   
   **Antonyms**: the best, first choice, ideal option, preferred option
   
   **When to Use**: When the ideal option isn't available and you need to describe the closest alternative; common in practical decision-making contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate when the alternative is significantly inferior; implies the substitute is nearly as good
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: the next best thing to, the next best thing available
   - **Verb + phrase**: settle for the next best thing, choose the next best thing, accept the next best thing
   - **Noun + phrase**: option as the next best thing
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (following in sequence) + best (optimal) + thing (item/option)
   
   **Etymology**: Idiomatic phrase from comparative ranking (20th century commercial usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't afford the real thing, so this is the next best thing!
   - If you can't come, a video call is the next best thing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When primary data is unavailable, secondary research represents the next best thing.
   - This solution constitutes the next best thing to full system replacement.

1. **Preposition**: next in line (type: phrasal - idiomatic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sequential)* The person or thing that is scheduled or designated to follow; having the next position in a sequence or hierarchy
   - *(Succession)* Having the right or designation to succeed to a position, especially in hereditary succession
   
   **Synonyms**: following in sequence, next to serve, successor, heir apparent
   
   **Antonyms**: first in line, last in line, out of sequence
   
   **When to Use**: For orderly sequences, queues, succession planning, or turn-taking; formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for spatial adjacency; specifically for sequential ordering
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: next in line for, next in line to, first/second next in line
   - **Verb + phrase**: be next in line, stand next in line, wait next in line
   - **Adjective + phrase**: person next in line, candidate next in line
   - **Noun + phrase**: heir next in line, customer next in line
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (following) + in (within) + line (ordered sequence)
   
   **Etymology**: From physical queuing → abstract succession (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You're next in line—get ready!
   - Who's next in line for the promotion?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vice president is next in line for succession.
   - The candidate next in line will be notified immediately.

1. **Preposition**: next of kin (type: phrasal - legal term)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Legal/Administrative)* A person's closest living blood relative or relatives; legally recognized family members for notification or inheritance purposes
   
   **Synonyms**: closest relative, immediate family, blood relations, legal heir
   
   **Antonyms**: distant relative, unrelated person, stranger
   
   **When to Use**: In legal, medical, and administrative contexts requiring family contact information or inheritance matters; formal term
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate for casual family references; specifically legal/administrative terminology
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: contact next of kin, notify next of kin, list next of kin
   - **Verb + phrase**: designate next of kin, name next of kin, identify next of kin
   - **Adjective + phrase**: registered next of kin, designated next of kin
   - **Noun + phrase**: information about next of kin, details of next of kin
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (closest) + of (possessive) + kin (family/relatives)
   
   **Etymology**: Legal terminology from Old English cynn (family/relatives) → formalized legal usage (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They need your next of kin for the form.
   - Put your mom down as next of kin.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please provide contact information for your next of kin.
   - The hospital will notify the patient's next of kin in case of emergency.

1. **Preposition**: the week/month/year next (type: phrasal - temporal postposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* The following week, month, or year; coming after the current one (archaic/formal construction)
   
   **Synonyms**: next week/month/year, the following week/month/year, the coming week/month/year
   
   **Antonyms**: last week/month/year, the previous week/month/year
   
   **When to Use**: Rare archaic or poetic construction; modern usage prefers "next week/month/year" with adjective before noun
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Uncommon in contemporary English; sounds archaic or overly formal; prefer standard "next week" construction
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: the week next, the month next (archaic)
   - **Modern equivalents**: next week, next month, next year (standard)
   
   **Root Analysis**: definite article + time unit + next (following)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English word order with postpositive adjective → modern prenominal position (pre-1500s usage pattern)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used in modern casual speech; prefer "next week")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The assembly is scheduled for the week next. (archaic)
   - Modern: The assembly is scheduled for next week.

1. **Preposition**: next up (type: phrasal - adverbial)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sequential)* Coming immediately after in a sequence; scheduled to appear or happen next
   
   **Synonyms**: coming next, following, up next, scheduled next
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, earlier, before
   
   **When to Use**: Common in presentations, broadcasts, schedules, and informal sequences; functions as discourse marker to introduce next topic/person/item
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate for formal academic writing; casual to semi-formal contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: next up is, next up on the agenda, next up for consideration
   - **Verb + phrase**: come next up, be next up
   - **Discourse marker**: "Next up, we'll discuss..." (introducing new topic)
   - **Noun + phrase**: speaker next up, item next up
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (following) + up (elevated position/attention)
   
   **Etymology**: From broadcasting and performance contexts (mid-20th century) → general sequential marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Next up, we've got pizza for dinner!
   - Who's next up to present?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Next up on the agenda is the budget review.
   - The speaker next up will address sustainability initiatives.

1. **Preposition**: next after (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sequential)* Immediately following a specified person or thing in order or importance
   
   **Synonyms**: following, subsequent to, after, succeeding
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, preceding
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizes sequential position or hierarchy; more formal than simple "after"; clarifies ordering
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Often redundant with "after" alone; can sound overly formal or archaic in casual contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: next after this, next after that, next after the first
   - **Verb + phrase**: come next after, follow next after, rank next after
   - **Adjective + phrase**: positioned next after, scheduled next after
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (following) + after (subsequent to)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic compound emphasizing sequential position (formal usage since 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What comes next after this step?
   - You're next after Sarah.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate ranked next after the winner will be considered.
   - This priority ranks next after safety compliance in our evaluation framework.

1. **Preposition**: next along (type: phrasal - directional)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial-Sequential)* The next item or location as you proceed in a particular direction
   
   **Synonyms**: further along, the following one along, continuing in that direction
   
   **Antonyms**: back along, previous along, earlier along
   
   **When to Use**: When describing progression along a path, route, or linear sequence; combines spatial movement with sequential ordering
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for temporal sequences; specifically for spatial/linear progression
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: next along the road, next along the line, next along the path
   - **Verb + phrase**: continue next along, proceed next along, move next along
   - **Adjective + phrase**: station next along, stop next along
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (following) + along (linear progression marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound prepositional phrase combining sequential and directional meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The shop is next along on the right.
   - Keep going—it's next along the street.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The checkpoint next along the route requires authorization.
   - The station next along the line provides transfer connections.
