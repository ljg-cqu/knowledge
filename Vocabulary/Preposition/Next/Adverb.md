1. **Adverb**: next (type: time/sequence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - On the occasion or at the time immediately following; in the position or place that follows
   - Used to introduce the next item in a sequence or the next step in a process
   
   **Synonyms**: then, subsequently, afterward, following that, after that
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, before, earlier, prior
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate temporal succession or to introduce the next step in instructions, narratives, or sequences; versatile in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous if temporal context isn't clear; specify with time markers when necessary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - what happens next
   - coming up next
   - next we'll discuss
   - try this next
   - see you next time
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (from superlative "nearest")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English nēhst (nearest) → adverbial use for temporal succession
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - First mix the ingredients, then next you bake it.
   - What should I do next?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Next, the committee will review the proposed amendments.
   - The study next examined the correlation between variables.

1. **Adverb**: subsequently (type: time, formal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a later time; afterward; following something in time or order
   - *(More formal synonym for "next" or "later")*
   
   **Synonyms**: afterward, later, next, following that, thereafter, then
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, earlier, before, beforehand, prior
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing, academic contexts, or professional communication; indicates temporal succession with implied causal relationship
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; can sound stilted in everyday speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - subsequently discovered
   - subsequently published
   - subsequently revised
   - and subsequently
   - subsequently determined
   
   **Root Analysis**: subsequent (following) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin subsequens (following) + -ly → formal temporal succession marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used in casual speech; prefer "later" or "then")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hypothesis was tested and subsequently confirmed through replication.
   - The policy was implemented in 2020 and subsequently amended in 2022.

1. **Adverb**: thereafter (type: time, formal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - After that time; from that point onward; subsequently
   - *(Formal synonym for "after that" or "from then on")*
   
   **Synonyms**: afterward, subsequently, from then on, after that, following that
   
   **Antonyms**: before that, prior to that, theretofore
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing, legal documents, or when marking a clear temporal transition point; emphasizes continuity from a specific point forward
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; archaic-sounding in everyday speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - shortly thereafter
   - immediately thereafter
   - soon thereafter
   - and thereafter
   - years thereafter
   
   **Root Analysis**: there (that point) + after (following)
   
   **Etymology**: there (demonstrative) + after → formal temporal marker (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used in casual speech; prefer "after that")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agreement was signed in March, and thereafter all parties complied.
   - The system was installed and thereafter operated without interruption.

1. **Adverb**: afterward/afterwards (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a later or subsequent time; after an event or time mentioned
   - Following something in time (not necessarily immediately)
   
   **Synonyms**: later, subsequently, next, then, thereafter
   
   **Antonyms**: before, beforehand, previously, earlier, prior
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate something happens after a mentioned event; neutral formality; "afterward" (American English) vs. "afterwards" (British English)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less precise than "immediately after" when timing is important
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - shortly afterward
   - soon afterward
   - immediately afterward
   - long afterward
   - years afterward
   
   **Root Analysis**: after (following in time) + -ward(s) (directional suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English æfter + -weard (toward) → temporal direction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's get dinner and go to a movie afterward.
   - I felt much better afterwards.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The presentation was well-received, and afterward participants requested additional data.
   - Subjects were debriefed immediately afterward.

1. **Adverb**: then (type: time/sequence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At that time; next in order of time; after that
   - Used to introduce the next step in a sequence or the consequence of something
   
   **Synonyms**: next, after that, subsequently, at that time, following that
   
   **Antonyms**: now, before, previously, earlier
   
   **When to Use**: Versatile temporal and logical connector; indicates both sequence and consequence; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous between temporal and logical meaning; clarify context when necessary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - and then
   - then what
   - if...then
   - then again
   - just then
   
   **Root Analysis**: then (from demonstrative "that")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English þonne/þanne (at that time) → temporal/logical marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - First we'll eat, then we'll watch a movie.
   - What happened then?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sample was prepared, then analyzed using spectroscopy.
   - If the hypothesis is correct, then the predicted pattern should emerge.

1. **Adverb**: immediately (type: time/manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Without any delay; at once; instantly
   - Directly; with nothing in between (spatial or temporal)
   - *(Can modify "next" to emphasize immediacy)*
   
   **Synonyms**: instantly, at once, right away, straightaway, promptly, directly
   
   **Antonyms**: eventually, later, gradually, slowly, after some time
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize zero delay or direct adjacency; works in all registers; conveys urgency
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not when some delay is expected or acceptable
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immediately after
   - immediately following
   - immediately next to
   - immediately available
   - act immediately
   
   **Root Analysis**: immediate (without interval) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin immediatus (without anything between) + -ly → temporal/spatial directness
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Call me back immediately!
   - The store is immediately next to the bank.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will convene immediately following the presentation.
   - Immediate action is required to address the discrepancy.

1. **Adverb**: presently (type: time, formal/archaic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(British English)* Soon; in a short time; shortly
   - *(American English)* At the present time; currently; now
   - *(Archaic)* Immediately; at once
   
   **Synonyms**: soon (British), shortly, currently (American), at present, now
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, later, eventually (depending on sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use carefully due to different meanings in British vs. American English; formal tone; somewhat archaic
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ambiguous due to transatlantic differences; prefer "soon" or "currently" for clarity
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - presently available
   - presently employed
   - arrive presently
   - presently under review
   
   **Root Analysis**: present (current time) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praesens (being at hand) + -ly → multiple temporal meanings (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll be there presently. (British: soon; American: now)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The matter is presently under consideration.
   - The director will arrive presently.

1. **Adverb**: following (type: time/sequence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Coming next or after; subsequently; as a result
   - Used to introduce what comes after in time or logic
   
   **Synonyms**: next, subsequently, afterward, then, after that
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, before, prior, preceding
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate temporal or logical succession; can function as discourse marker to introduce subsequent information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be unnecessarily formal when "next" is clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immediately following
   - the day following
   - following that
   - in the days following
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow (come after) + -ing (participial/adverbial form)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English folgian (come after) + -ing → temporal succession
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We met on Monday and went out the day following.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The incident occurred on March 15, with investigations commencing immediately following.
   - Following the presentation, participants engaged in discussion.

1. **Adverb**: shortly (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a short time; soon; before long
   - For a short time; briefly
   - In a curt or abrupt manner (less common adverbial sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: soon, presently, before long, in a moment, momentarily
   
   **Antonyms**: much later, eventually, in the distant future
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate near-future timing; implies relatively immediate action; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Imprecise timing; avoid when specific timeframes are important
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - shortly after
   - shortly before
   - arriving shortly
   - will begin shortly
   - shortly thereafter
   
   **Root Analysis**: short (brief duration) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English sceort (brief) + -ly → near-future temporal marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The movie starts shortly, let's hurry!
   - I'll call you back shortly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The meeting will commence shortly.
   - Results will be available shortly after completion.

1. **Adverb**: consecutively (type: manner/sequence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In unbroken succession; following one after another without interruption
   - In a consecutive manner; sequentially
   
   **Synonyms**: successively, sequentially, in succession, one after another, in sequence
   
   **Antonyms**: intermittently, sporadically, randomly, discontinuously
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize unbroken sequence or continuity; common in technical, legal, and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not for simple temporal succession; specifically emphasizes continuity without gaps
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - consecutively numbered
   - for three days consecutively
   - consecutively occurring
   - run consecutively
   - arranged consecutively
   
   **Root Analysis**: consecutive (following in order) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin consecutus (following closely) + -ly → unbroken succession
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He won the championship three years consecutively!
   - Number the pages consecutively.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants completed three trials consecutively without interruption.
   - The data points were recorded consecutively at five-minute intervals.
