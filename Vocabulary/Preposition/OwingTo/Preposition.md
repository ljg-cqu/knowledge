1. **Preposition**: owing to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; as a result of; by reason of
   - Used to introduce the cause or reason for something; formal expression indicating causation
   - More flexible in sentence position than "due to" and commonly appears at the beginning of sentences or after commas
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, because of, on account of, as a result of, by reason of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, notwithstanding, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "owing to" in formal and semi-formal contexts to express causation; particularly common in British English; suitable for academic writing, business communication, and professional reports; can appear at the beginning of sentences or mid-sentence after commas
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual conversation where "because of" is more natural; less common in American English where "due to" or "because of" are often preferred; not appropriate for informal text messages or everyday speech
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: owing to circumstances, owing to the fact that, owing to unforeseen events, owing to delays, owing to changes
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (typically used at beginning of sentence or after comma, not directly after verbs)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: closure owing to, cancellation owing to, postponement owing to, delay owing to, failure owing to
   
   **Root Analysis**: owing (present participle of "owe") + to (preposition indicating direction/attribution)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English agan (to possess, own) → Middle English owen (to owe, be indebted) → "owing to" developed in 16th-17th century as causal expression meaning "attributable to" or "because of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The shop's closed owing to renovations.
   - We're running late owing to traffic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Owing to unforeseen circumstances, the conference has been postponed until further notice.
   - The decline in student enrollment is owing to demographic shifts and economic factors.

1. **Preposition**: due to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; caused by; as a result of
   - Used to introduce the cause or reason, traditionally prescribed after linking verbs (be, seem, appear)
   
   **Synonyms**: owing to, because of, on account of, as a result of, thanks to (positive contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "due to" primarily after linking verbs (is due to, was due to) in formal and semi-formal contexts; common in academic, technical, and business writing; very frequent in both British and American English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Traditional grammar prescribes not using "due to" to begin sentences or replace "because of" in non-linking verb contexts (though modern usage has become more flexible); avoid in very informal conversation
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: due to circumstances, due to the fact that, due to unforeseen events, due to technical difficulties
   - **Verb + prep**: is due to, was due to, seems due to, appears due to
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: delay due to, cancellation due to, failure due to, success due to, problem due to
   
   **Root Analysis**: due (owed, attributable) + to (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin debere (to owe) → Old French deu (owed) → Middle English due (owed, proper, appropriate) → "due to" emerged as causal expression meaning "attributable to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The game was called off due to rain.
   - She's staying home due to a cold.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project delay was due to insufficient resource allocation and planning errors.
   - Market volatility is largely due to geopolitical tensions and regulatory uncertainty.

1. **Preposition**: because of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* By reason of; as a result of; on account of
   - Most versatile causal preposition, works in all sentence positions and after all verb types
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, owing to, on account of, as a result of, thanks to
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "because of" to introduce reasons in all contexts and positions; works after both linking and action verbs; appropriate for all registers from informal to formal; most common causal preposition in everyday English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Before finite clauses (use "because" instead: "because he arrived" not "because of he arrived"); in very formal academic writing where stylistic variation with "owing to" or "due to" might be preferred
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: because of this, because of that, because of circumstances, because of the weather, because of the fact that
   - **Verb + prep**: happened because of, changed because of, left because of, failed because of, succeeded because of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: problem because of, success because of, delay because of, change because of
   
   **Root Analysis**: because (by + cause) + of (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English bi cause (by reason) → "because of" as compound prepositional phrase expressing causation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm exhausted because of the long drive yesterday.
   - We stayed inside all day because of the storm.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative failed because of inadequate funding and insufficient stakeholder engagement.
   - Productivity increased significantly because of the implementation of the new system.

1. **Preposition**: on account of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; by reason of; due to
   - Formal, somewhat old-fashioned expression introducing cause or reason
   
   **Synonyms**: because of, due to, owing to, as a result of, by reason of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "on account of" in formal writing and speech for stylistic variation; common in business, legal, and official contexts; adds formality and gravitas to communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; can sound old-fashioned, stuffy, or overly formal in everyday speech; avoid in modern informal writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on account of circumstances, on account of health reasons, on account of the fact that
   - **Verb + prep**: cancelled on account of, postponed on account of, dismissed on account of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: absence on account of, refusal on account of, dismissal on account of, closure on account of
   
   **Root Analysis**: on + account (reckoning, consideration, reason) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Latin computare → Old French acont → Middle English acount → "on account of" meaning "by reason of" or "for the sake of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The party was cancelled on account of bad weather.
   - He quit on account of the terrible commute.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hearing was adjourned on account of the defendant's illness.
   - Exemptions may be granted on account of extenuating circumstances and documented hardship.

1. **Preposition**: as a result of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; consequently from; due to
   - Explicitly emphasizes the cause-and-effect relationship between events
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, owing to, because of, on account of, consequent upon
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "as a result of" when emphasizing the causal relationship and consequential outcome; common in academic, scientific, analytical writing, and formal reports; useful when cause-effect logic needs to be explicit
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be wordy and verbose in casual conversation where "because of" is more natural and concise; may sound overly formal in everyday speech
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as a result of this, as a result of which, as a result of changes, as a result of factors
   - **Verb + prep**: occurred as a result of, changed as a result of, emerged as a result of, developed as a result of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: outcome as a result of, change as a result of, development as a result of, improvement as a result of
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the manner of) + a + result (outcome, consequence) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Latin resultare (to rebound, spring back) → "as a result of" emphasizing consequential relationship and cause-effect logic
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He lost his job as a result of the company layoffs.
   - The road is completely closed as a result of the accident.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Significant improvements in patient outcomes were observed as a result of the intervention.
   - The economic decline occurred as a result of multiple contributing factors including policy changes and market conditions.

1. **Preposition**: by reason of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* Because of; on account of; owing to
   - Very formal, archaic-sounding expression for causation, primarily used in legal and official contexts
   
   **Synonyms**: because of, owing to, on account of, due to
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, notwithstanding, in spite of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "by reason of" in very formal legal, governmental, or official documents; adds legal or ceremonial weight to statements; common in statutory language and formal declarations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal and archaic for modern everyday use; sounds stilted in casual or even standard business communication; avoid in contemporary writing unless specifically required by legal/official context
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by reason of circumstances, by reason of the fact that, by reason of disability, by reason of incapacity
   - **Verb + prep**: dismissed by reason of, exempted by reason of, excused by reason of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: dismissal by reason of, exemption by reason of, discharge by reason of
   
   **Root Analysis**: by (preposition) + reason (cause, justification) + of (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English resoun → "by reason of" used historically in legal language to mean "because of" or "on account of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - N/A (too formal for casual use)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The employee was dismissed by reason of gross misconduct and breach of contract.
   - Certain individuals may be exempt from service by reason of age, disability, or other qualifying circumstances.

1. **Preposition**: in consequence of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* As a result of; because of; owing to
   - Formal expression emphasizing the consequential nature of causation
   
   **Synonyms**: as a result of, owing to, because of, on account of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, notwithstanding, in spite of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "in consequence of" in formal academic or professional writing to emphasize cause-and-effect relationships; particularly common in British English; adds formality and logical structure to arguments
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for everyday conversation; can sound stilted or pompous in casual contexts; less common in modern American English
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in consequence of this, in consequence of which, in consequence of events, in consequence of actions
   - **Verb + prep**: occurred in consequence of, arose in consequence of, developed in consequence of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: change in consequence of, development in consequence of, outcome in consequence of
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + consequence (result, effect) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Latin consequi (to follow after) → "in consequence of" meaning "as a result of" or "following from"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The store closed in consequence of poor sales. (somewhat formal for casual)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In consequence of these findings, the committee recommends further investigation and policy revision.
   - The organization underwent significant restructuring in consequence of the merger and strategic realignment.

1. **Preposition**: thanks to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal - Positive)* Because of; as a result of (typically for positive outcomes)
   - *(Causal - Ironic)* Because of (used ironically or sarcastically for negative outcomes)
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, owing to, because of (when positive)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use "thanks to" primarily for positive outcomes, achievements, or beneficial results; also used ironically for negative outcomes; common in both formal and informal contexts; expresses attribution with positive or ironic tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for straightforwardly negative causation unless being deliberately ironic; may sound inappropriate or insensitive in serious negative contexts; don't use when neutral tone is required
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: thanks to efforts, thanks to technology, thanks to support, thanks to help, thanks to [person]
   - **Verb + prep**: succeeded thanks to, improved thanks to, achieved thanks to, survived thanks to
   - **Adjective + prep**: possible thanks to, successful thanks to, available thanks to
   - **Noun + prep**: success thanks to, improvement thanks to, achievement thanks to, progress thanks to
   
   **Root Analysis**: thanks (expression of gratitude, plural noun) + to (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English þanc (thought, gratitude) → "thanks to" developed from expression of gratitude to expression of positive causation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We made it on time thanks to your help and directions.
   - Thanks to the rain, our picnic is completely ruined. (ironic)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project succeeded thanks to collaborative efforts and dedicated teamwork across departments.
   - Mortality rates have declined significantly thanks to improved healthcare access and medical advances.
