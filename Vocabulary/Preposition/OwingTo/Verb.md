1. **Verb**: postpone
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To arrange for something to happen at a later time than originally planned; to delay an event or action.
   
   **Synonyms**: delay, defer, put off, reschedule, push back
   
   **Antonyms**: advance, bring forward, schedule, proceed with
   
   **When to Use**: Use when the plan is to carry out the action later, not cancel it entirely: *"The meeting was postponed owing to illness."*
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don’t use when the event is cancelled with no intention to reschedule.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - postpone a meeting
   - postpone the launch
   - postponed owing to [reason]
   - indefinitely postponed
   
   **Root Analysis**: post- (after) + pone (from ponere = to place) → to place after
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *postponere* (to put after), via Late Latin and French, into English with the meaning "to defer."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We postponed the trip owing to the bad weather.
   - The game’s been postponed owing to a problem with the stadium lights.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The review session was postponed owing to key stakeholders being unavailable.
   - Owing to regulatory feedback, the product launch has been postponed until further notice.

1. **Verb**: cancel
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To decide that something planned will not happen at all.
   
   **Synonyms**: call off, abort, scrap, withdraw, terminate
   
   **Antonyms**: confirm, schedule, proceed with, hold (an event)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when the action or event is not merely delayed but will not occur in its original form: *"The workshop was cancelled owing to low registration."*
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don’t use when you intend to rearrange for a later date—then "postpone" or "reschedule" is better.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cancel an order
   - cancel a flight
   - cancel a meeting
   - cancelled owing to [reason]
   
   **Root Analysis**: from Latin *cancellare* (to cross out)
   
   **Etymology**: Entered English via Old French; originally meant "cross out" or "annul," extended to events and arrangements.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They cancelled the show owing to a power cut.
   - Our dinner plans were cancelled owing to a family emergency.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The flight was cancelled owing to adverse weather conditions at the destination.
   - The procurement process was cancelled owing to significant changes in requirements.

1. **Verb**: delay
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive/intransitive) To cause something to happen later than planned, or to be late.
   
   **Synonyms**: slow, hold up, defer, stall, lag
   
   **Antonyms**: expedite, accelerate, fast‑track, advance
   
   **When to Use**: Use when time is pushed back but the action still occurs: *"Delivery was delayed owing to customs checks."*
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the main decision is cancellation; then "cancel" is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - delay shipment
   - delay implementation
   - cause a delay
   - delayed owing to [reason]
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away) + lay (place) → to put off
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *deler* (to put off); "delay" has long meant "postponement" or "slowing."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The train was delayed owing to an earlier incident.
   - My response was delayed owing to a full inbox.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The migration was delayed owing to unresolved defects in the staging environment.
   - Owing to vendor dependencies, several milestones were delayed by two weeks.

1. **Verb**: reschedule
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To change the time of a planned event to a new time.
   
   **Synonyms**: rearrange, move, shift, rebook, set a new date
   
   **Antonyms**: cancel, maintain (the schedule), keep as planned
   
   **When to Use**: Use when you want to emphasize that a new time has already been chosen: *"The meeting was rescheduled owing to a clash."*
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don’t use when you haven’t agreed a new time yet (then "postpone" may be better).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reschedule a meeting
   - reschedule the exam
   - rescheduled owing to [reason]
   - need to reschedule
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again) + schedule (timetable) → to schedule again
   
   **Etymology**: Modern English formation; "reschedule" became common with the rise of formal timetables and booking systems.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had to reschedule the call owing to a clash with another meeting.
   - Can we reschedule owing to something that came up?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audit has been rescheduled owing to the unavailability of key personnel on the original dates.
   - Owing to venue maintenance, the conference has been rescheduled for the following month.

1. **Verb**: suspend
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To temporarily stop something from continuing or being in force, often with the intention to review or resume later.
   
   **Synonyms**: halt, pause, freeze, put on hold, interrupt
   
   **Antonyms**: resume, continue, maintain, reinstate
   
   **When to Use**: Use when an activity or service is paused because of a serious reason: *"Operations were suspended owing to safety concerns."*
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don’t use when cancellation is final; then "terminate" or "cancel" is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - suspend operations
   - suspend service
   - suspend trading
   - suspended owing to [reason]
   
   **Root Analysis**: sus- (from sub- = from below) + pend (hang) → to hang up
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *suspendere* (to hang up), via Old French; metaphorically extended to "pause" or "hold in abeyance."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The class was suspended owing to a power cut.
   - Service was suspended owing to a problem on the line.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Trading was temporarily suspended owing to extreme market volatility.
   - Owing to safety concerns, field activities were suspended pending further investigation.

1. **Verb**: abandon
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To stop doing something before it is finished, especially because it has become impossible or no longer sensible.
   
   **Synonyms**: give up, drop, scrap, discontinue, call off
   
   **Antonyms**: pursue, continue, maintain, persist with
   
   **When to Use**: Use for projects, plans, or attempts that are stopped completely, often with an explanation: *"The route was abandoned owing to unsafe conditions."*
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for minor changes of plan; "adjust" or "revise" may sound less extreme.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - abandon a plan
   - abandon the attempt
   - abandon the route
   - abandoned owing to [reason]
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (to) + bandon (control, jurisdiction) → give up control of
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *abandoner* (to surrender, relinquish), then in English "to give up completely."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We abandoned the hike owing to the storm rolling in.
   - The idea was abandoned owing to the cost.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initial design was abandoned owing to structural constraints identified during feasibility analysis.
   - Owing to escalating risks, the exploration programme was abandoned before field deployment.
