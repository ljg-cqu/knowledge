# Noun: To

1. **Noun**: access to (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The right or opportunity to use, reach, or obtain something; the means or possibility of approaching or entering a place
   
   **Synonyms**: entry to, admittance to, availability of, right to use
   
   **Antonyms**: denial of, exclusion from, restriction from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing rights, opportunities, or physical/digital entry to places, resources, or information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Access" as a verb doesn't take "to" (say "access the database," not "access to the database" when using as verb)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - access to information
   - access to resources
   - gain access to
   - provide access to
   - restrict access to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + cess (Latin "cedere" - to go) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "accessus" (approach, entrance) from "accedere" (approach); developed meanings of opportunity and right to use
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't have access to my work email from home.
   - The park provides access to the beach.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Equitable access to education remains a fundamental policy objective.
   - Participants were granted access to confidential research data.

2. **Noun**: approach to (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A way of dealing with or thinking about something; a method or strategy
   - The action of coming near or nearer to something in distance or time
   
   **Synonyms**: method of, strategy for, way of handling, attitude toward
   
   **Antonyms**: avoidance of, departure from, withdrawal from
   
   **When to Use**: Use for methods, strategies, attitudes, or physical/temporal proximity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple meanings require context; "approach to teaching" (method) vs. "approach to the building" (physical)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approach to problem-solving
   - holistic approach to
   - systematic approach to
   - new approach to
   - traditional approach to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to) + proach (Latin "prope" - near) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "appropiare" (come nearer) from "ad-" + "prope" (near); developed metaphorical meaning of method/strategy
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like your approach to dealing with difficult people.
   - What's your approach to studying for exams?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research employs a mixed-methods approach to data collection.
   - A comprehensive approach to climate policy is urgently needed.

3. **Noun**: solution to (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A means of solving a problem or dealing with a difficult situation; an answer to a question or puzzle
   
   **Synonyms**: answer to, resolution of, remedy for, fix for
   
   **Antonyms**: problem, cause of, source of difficulty
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing answers, fixes, or resolutions to problems, questions, or challenges
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Solution for" can work in some contexts, but "solution to" is more standard for problems/questions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - solution to the problem
   - ideal solution to
   - viable solution to
   - find a solution to
   - propose a solution to
   
   **Root Analysis**: solut- (Latin "solvere" - to loosen, solve) + -ion + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "solutio" (loosening, payment) from "solvere" (loosen, solve); developed meaning of problem-solving answer
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think I found a solution to our parking problem!
   - What's the solution to number 7?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report proposes innovative solutions to urban congestion.
   - No single solution to the crisis exists; multiple interventions are required.

4. **Noun**: alternative to (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - One of two or more available possibilities; something that can be chosen instead of something else
   
   **Synonyms**: substitute for, option instead of, replacement for, choice over
   
   **Antonyms**: only option, sole choice, necessity
   
   **When to Use**: Use when presenting options, substitutes, or different possibilities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Alternative" implies choice between options; use "substitute" if one thing replaces another by necessity
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - alternative to traditional
   - viable alternative to
   - attractive alternative to
   - offer an alternative to
   - seek alternatives to
   
   **Root Analysis**: alter- (Latin "alter" - other) + -native + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "alternare" (do by turns) from "alter" (other); developed meaning of other choice or option
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Biking is a great alternative to driving.
   - Is there an alternative to surgery?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Renewable energy represents a sustainable alternative to fossil fuels.
   - The study examines cost-effective alternatives to conventional treatment protocols.

5. **Noun**: reaction to (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A response or behavior resulting from something that happens or something someone does; a feeling or opinion caused by a situation or event
   
   **Synonyms**: response to, reply to, feedback on, reception of
   
   **Antonyms**: cause of, stimulus for, instigation of
   
   **When to Use**: Use for responses, feedback, chemical processes, or emotional responses to stimuli or events
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Reaction to" (response) vs. "reaction against" (opposition); preposition changes meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reaction to news
   - immediate reaction to
   - positive/negative reaction to
   - chemical reaction to
   - visceral reaction to
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + act (Latin "agere" - to do) + -ion + to
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin "reactio" from "re-" + "actio"; developed meanings of response, opposition, and chemical process
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What was her reaction to the surprise?
   - I had a bad reaction to the medication.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Public reaction to the policy announcement was overwhelmingly negative.
   - The substance undergoes a rapid exothermic reaction to the catalyst.

6. **Noun**: response to (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An answer or reply to a question, request, or situation; a reaction to something
   
   **Synonyms**: answer to, reply to, reaction to, feedback on
   
   **Antonyms**: question, inquiry, silence
   
   **When to Use**: Use for answers, replies, reactions, or adaptive behaviors following stimuli or requests
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Response to" implies something prompted the response; use "unsolicited response" if nothing prompted it
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - response to questions
   - response to criticism
   - in response to
   - prompt response to
   - measured response to
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + spons (Latin "spondere" - to pledge) + -e + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "responsum" (answer) from "respondere" (answer, pledge back); developed meanings of reply and reaction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm still waiting for a response to my email.
   - His response to the complaint was pretty defensive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The government's response to the economic crisis has been criticized.
   - Participants' physiological responses to stress were measured and analyzed.

7. **Noun**: key to (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The crucial factor or solution that achieves or explains something; an essential element for success or understanding
   - A tool for opening locks (literal sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: secret of, crucial factor in, essential element of, answer to
   
   **Antonyms**: obstacle to, barrier to, impediment to
   
   **When to Use**: Use metaphorically for crucial factors or literally for physical keys to locks
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context distinguishes literal (lock) from metaphorical (crucial factor) uses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - key to success
   - key to understanding
   - key to the problem
   - hold the key to
   - key to solving
   
   **Root Analysis**: key (Old English "cǣg") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "cǣg" (key, solution); metaphorical use for crucial factor emerged from literal key that opens locks
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Practice is the key to getting better.
   - Do you have the key to this door?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Innovation is widely regarded as the key to sustained competitive advantage.
   - Education represents a key to social mobility and economic development.

8. **Noun**: reference to (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action of mentioning or alluding to something; a source of information cited in a text
   - A relationship or connection between things
   
   **Synonyms**: mention of, allusion to, citation of, regard to
   
   **Antonyms**: omission of, exclusion of, disregard of
   
   **When to Use**: Use for mentions, citations, allusions, or establishing relationships between concepts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Reference to" (mention) vs. "reference for" (recommendation letter); preposition determines meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - make reference to
   - reference to previous
   - with reference to
   - reference to sources
   - without reference to
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + fer (Latin "ferre" - to carry) + -ence + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "referre" (carry back, report); developed meanings of citation, mention, and relation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She made a reference to that incident from last year.
   - Is this a reference to that movie?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author makes frequent reference to classical philosophy.
   - With reference to your inquiry of March 15, please find our response attached.

9. **Noun**: contribution to (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something given or done to help achieve something or to make something successful; a donation or payment
   - The part played by something in causing a result
   
   **Synonyms**: donation to, input into, role in, participation in
   
   **Antonyms**: detriment to, hindrance to, obstruction of
   
   **When to Use**: Use for donations, participation, causal factors, or roles in outcomes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Contribution to" (what you give to something) vs. "contribution of" (what something provides)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contribution to society
   - valuable contribution to
   - make a contribution to
   - significant contribution to
   - contribution to knowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + tribut (Latin "tribuere" - to give) + -ion + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "contributio" (bringing together) from "contribuere"; developed meanings of donation and causal role
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone made a contribution to the potluck.
   - Your contribution to the project was really important.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research makes a significant contribution to theoretical understanding.
   - We gratefully acknowledge your generous contribution to the scholarship fund.

10. **Noun**: threat to (C)
    **Meaning**: 
    - A person or thing likely to cause damage or danger; a statement of intention to harm or punish
    
    **Synonyms**: danger to, risk to, menace to, hazard to
    
    **Antonyms**: benefit to, protection for, safeguard of, advantage for
    
    **When to Use**: Use for dangers, risks, warnings, or sources of potential harm
    
    **When NOT to Use**: "Threat to" (danger to something) vs. "threat of" (risk that something will happen)
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - threat to security
    - pose a threat to
    - serious threat to
    - perceived threat to
    - under threat
    
    **Root Analysis**: threat (Old English "þreat" - crowd, pressure)
    
    **Etymology**: Old English "þreat" (crowd, oppression) → Middle English "threte"; developed meaning of menace and warning
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Climate change is a real threat to coastal cities.
    - He's not a threat to anyone.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Cybersecurity breaches pose a significant threat to organizational integrity.
    - The invasive species represents an existential threat to native ecosystems.
