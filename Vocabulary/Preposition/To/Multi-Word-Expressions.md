# Multi-Word Expressions: To

1. **Expression**: come to terms with (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To gradually accept a sad or difficult situation; to reach an understanding or agreement about something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: accept, reconcile oneself to, make peace with, learn to live with
   
   **Antonyms**: deny, reject, refuse to accept, struggle against
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing the process of accepting difficult realities, losses, or changes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies emotional processing; too strong for trivial disappointments
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - come to terms with the fact that
   - finally come to terms with
   - struggle to come to terms with
   - never come to terms with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Terms" originally meant conditions of agreement; the phrase evolved from negotiation contexts to emotional acceptance, metaphorically treating difficult reality as something to negotiate with
   
   **Root Analysis**: come + to + terms (Old French "terme" - limit, end) + with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It took me years to come to terms with my parents' divorce.
   - I'm still trying to come to terms with what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Survivors must be given adequate support to come to terms with traumatic experiences.
   - Society has yet to come to terms with the implications of technological disruption.

2. **Expression**: look forward to (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To feel pleased and excited about something that is going to happen; to anticipate with pleasure
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: anticipate, await eagerly, be excited about, can't wait for
   
   **Antonyms**: dread, fear, worry about, not look forward to
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express positive anticipation; common in both personal and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Note: "to" is a preposition here, so follow with noun/gerund (looking forward to seeing you), not infinitive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - really look forward to
   - eagerly look forward to
   - very much look forward to
   - I look forward to hearing from you (formal closing)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Look" (direct attention) combines with "forward" (into future) and "to" (toward object) to create natural expression for future-oriented positive emotion
   
   **Root Analysis**: look + forward + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really looking forward to the concert next week!
   - Looking forward to seeing you soon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We look forward to receiving your application by the deadline.
   - I look forward to the opportunity to discuss this matter further.

3. **Expression**: when it comes to (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With regard to; concerning; in matters involving; as far as something is concerned
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, as for, in terms of
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; it's a topic marker)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to introduce or shift to a specific topic, especially to indicate expertise, preferences, or focus
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be overused; simpler "regarding" or "for" often suffices
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - when it comes to X, [statement]
   - especially when it comes to
   - particularly when it comes to
   - at least when it comes to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Come to" originally meant "arrive at"; the phrase metaphorically treats topics as destinations one arrives at in discussion
   
   **Root Analysis**: when + it + come + -s + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When it comes to cooking, I'm completely hopeless.
   - She's brilliant when it comes to solving puzzles.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When it comes to climate policy, international cooperation is essential.
   - The organization demonstrates exceptional competence when it comes to crisis management.

4. **Expression**: pay attention to (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To watch, listen to, or think about someone or something carefully; to give consideration or notice to
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: focus on, concentrate on, heed, take notice of, give thought to
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, disregard, pay no attention to, tune out
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing need for focus, care, or consideration; common in instructions and criticism
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Pay attention to" is more emphatic than "notice"; choose based on desired emphasis
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - pay close attention to
   - pay careful attention to
   - pay more attention to
   - fail to pay attention to
   - need to pay attention to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Pay" (give, offer) combines with "attention" (mental focus) to create metaphor of attention as valuable resource being given
   
   **Root Analysis**: pay (Old French "paier") + attention (Latin "attendere" - stretch toward) + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to pay more attention to what I'm saying!
   - I wasn't paying attention to where I was going.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Policymakers must pay close attention to stakeholder concerns.
   - The analysis pays particular attention to demographic variations.

5. **Expression**: bring to light (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To reveal or make known something that was previously hidden or unknown; to expose or uncover
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: reveal, expose, uncover, disclose, make known, bring to public attention
   
   **Antonyms**: conceal, hide, cover up, suppress, keep secret
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing revelations, discoveries, or exposures of previously hidden information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies something was deliberately or accidentally hidden; not for ordinary discoveries
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - bring to light the fact that
   - bring new information to light
   - recently brought to light
   - finally brought to light
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Light" metaphorically represents knowledge/visibility; bringing something "to light" treats understanding as illumination of darkness
   
   **Root Analysis**: bring + to + light (Old English "lēoht")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The investigation brought to light some disturbing facts.
   - New evidence has brought the issue to light again.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audit brought to light significant irregularities in financial reporting.
   - Recent scholarship has brought to light previously overlooked historical documents.

6. **Expression**: give rise to (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To cause something to happen or exist; to produce or result in something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: cause, lead to, result in, produce, generate, bring about
   
   **Antonyms**: prevent, suppress, eliminate, end, stop
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to describe causation, especially for abstract phenomena or complex results
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal register; simple "cause" or "create" more natural in casual speech
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - give rise to concerns
   - give rise to questions
   - give rise to speculation
   - likely to give rise to
   - has given rise to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Give rise to" treats emergence as elevation or creation; "rise" metaphorically represents coming into existence
   
   **Root Analysis**: give + rise (Old English "rīsan") + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The announcement gave rise to a lot of rumors.
   - This situation could give rise to serious problems.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy has given rise to significant ethical concerns.
   - Technological advances have given rise to unprecedented opportunities for innovation.

7. **Expression**: fall victim to (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suffer as a result of something bad; to be harmed or adversely affected by something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: be affected by, suffer from, succumb to, be harmed by, be subjected to
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid, escape, resist, overcome, survive
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe negative outcomes, vulnerabilities, or being adversely affected by problems
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies passivity or vulnerability; use "succumb to" for more active failure
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fall victim to fraud
   - fall victim to circumstances
   - fall victim to the same mistake
   - easily fall victim to
   - not fall victim to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Fall" suggests descent or failure; "victim" indicates harm received; natural combination for expressing vulnerability
   
   **Root Analysis**: fall (Old English "feallan") + victim (Latin "victima" - sacrifice) + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't fall victim to those online scams.
   - I fell victim to the same trick last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many organizations fall victim to inadequate cybersecurity measures.
   - Investors frequently fall victim to cognitive biases in decision-making.

8. **Expression**: owing to (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Because of; as a result of; due to; used to introduce a cause or reason
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, because of, as a result of, on account of, thanks to
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to attribute causation; particularly common in British English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal; "because of" or "due to" more common in casual and American English
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - owing to circumstances
   - partly owing to
   - largely owing to
   - owing to the fact that
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Owing" (being indebted) extends financial metaphor to causal relationships; results are "owed to" their causes
   
   **Root Analysis**: ow- (Old English "āgan" - possess, owe) + -ing + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The match was canceled owing to bad weather.
   - Owing to technical difficulties, we'll start late.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delay was owing to unforeseen regulatory complications.
   - Success can be attributed, owing to careful planning and execution.

9. **Expression**: add to (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To increase the size, amount, or degree of something; to make something larger or more intense
   - *Fixedness*: medium (preferred partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: increase, augment, enhance, contribute to, compound
   
   **Antonyms**: subtract from, reduce, diminish, detract from, lessen
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing increases, enhancements, or intensifications; common in both literal and figurative senses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Add to" can be literal (add to the list) or figurative (add to the problem); context clarifies
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - add to the problem
   - add to the confusion
   - add to the total
   - add to existing
   - only add to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Add" naturally combines with "to" for directional increase; semantic and grammatical compatibility
   
   **Root Analysis**: add (Latin "addere" - give to) + to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This just adds to my stress!
   - Can you add this to the shopping list?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These findings add to the growing body of evidence supporting the hypothesis.
   - The new regulations add to the existing compliance burden on small businesses.

10. **Expression**: put an end to (type: collocation)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To cause something to stop happening or existing; to terminate or conclude something
    - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed partnership)
    
    **Synonyms**: stop, end, terminate, halt, cease, eliminate, abolish
    
    **Antonyms**: start, begin, initiate, continue, perpetuate, maintain
    
    **When to Use**: Use when describing deliberate termination or cessation of activities, situations, or problems
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Implies deliberate action; use "come to an end" for natural conclusions
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - put an end to speculation
    - put an end to the practice
    - put a final end to
    - put a stop to (similar expression)
    - time to put an end to
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "Put" (cause/make) combines with "end" (termination) and "to" (toward object) to create action-oriented termination expression
    
    **Root Analysis**: put + an + end (Old English "ende") + to
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - We need to put an end to this nonsense.
    - They finally put an end to their long feud.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The legislation aims to put an end to discriminatory practices.
    - International cooperation is essential to put an end to the humanitarian crisis.
