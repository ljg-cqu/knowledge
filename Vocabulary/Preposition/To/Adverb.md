# Adverb: To

1. **Adverb**: up to (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate a maximum limit or extent of something (position: typically before amount/number)
   
   **Synonyms**: as many as, as much as, a maximum of, not more than
   
   **Antonyms**: at least, no less than, a minimum of
   
   **When to Use**: Use to specify maximum quantities, limits, or capacities; common in descriptions of ranges and capabilities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "up to" meaning "engaged in" (what are you up to?) or "responsible for" (it's up to you)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - up to five people
   - up to 50% off
   - up to date
   - capacity up to
   - speeds up to
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (Old English "upp") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Combines directional "up" with "to" to indicate reaching toward a limit; developed as degree modifier for maximum quantities
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The car can seat up to seven people.
   - You can save up to 30% this weekend!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants may enroll up to three colleagues in the program.
   - The facility can accommodate up to 500 individuals simultaneously.

2. **Adverb**: so as to (type: purpose)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In order to; for the purpose of; with the intention of (position: typically before verb phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: in order to, with the aim of, for the purpose of, to
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; it's a purpose marker)
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to express purpose or intention; emphasizes deliberate action
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal; simple "to" (infinitive of purpose) is more natural in most contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - so as to avoid
   - so as to ensure
   - so as to maintain
   - so as to minimize
   - positioned so as to
   
   **Root Analysis**: so + as + to (combination forming purpose clause)
   
   **Etymology**: Combines "so" (in such a way) with "as to" to create emphatic purpose construction; formal register marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I left early so as to avoid traffic.
   - We whispered so as to not wake the baby.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Procedures were implemented so as to ensure compliance with regulations.
   - The study was designed so as to minimize confounding variables.

3. **Adverb**: only to (type: result)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate a disappointing or unexpected result following an action (position: typically mid-sentence after verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: but then, with the result that, just to, but instead
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; expresses specific narrative function)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe unexpected or disappointing outcomes; creates narrative tension or irony
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Specifically for unexpected negative or ironic results; don't use for expected positive outcomes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - only to find
   - only to discover
   - only to realize
   - only to be disappointed
   - only to fail
   
   **Root Analysis**: only (Old English "ānlic") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Combines "only" (exclusively) with "to" to create construction expressing ironic or disappointing result
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I rushed to the store only to find it was closed.
   - He studied all night only to fail the exam.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative was launched with great fanfare, only to be quietly discontinued months later.
   - Researchers invested years in the project, only to discover fundamental flaws in the methodology.

4. **Adverb**: enough to (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a sufficient degree or extent to achieve something (position: after adjective/adverb, before verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: sufficiently to, adequately to, to the extent needed to
   
   **Antonyms**: insufficiently to, inadequately to, too [adjective] to
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate that a quality or quantity is sufficient to cause or allow something
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Word order matters: "enough to" follows the adjective (big enough to), not precedes it
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - good enough to
   - large enough to
   - strong enough to
   - clever enough to
   - old enough to
   
   **Root Analysis**: enough (Old English "genōg") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "genōg" (sufficient) + "to" creates degree construction indicating sufficiency for purpose
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you strong enough to lift this?
   - He was smart enough to avoid the trap.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sample size was large enough to detect statistically significant differences.
   - The evidence is compelling enough to warrant further investigation.

5. **Adverb**: close to (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Almost; nearly; approaching a particular number, state, or time (position: before amount/number or adjective)
   
   **Synonyms**: nearly, almost, approximately, approaching, around
   
   **Antonyms**: far from, nowhere near, distant from
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate proximity to quantities, states, or completion; emphasizes near achievement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous with adjective "close to"; context must clarify whether adverb (nearly) or adjective (near)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - close to finished
   - close to midnight
   - close to a hundred
   - close to breaking
   - close to tears
   
   **Root Analysis**: close (Latin "clausus") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Extends spatial "close" to temporal and quantitative approximation; developed as degree modifier
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's close to midnight already!
   - I'm close to being done with this assignment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project is close to completion, pending final approval.
   - Temperatures approached close to record highs for the season.

6. **Adverb**: as opposed to (type: contrast)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In contrast with; rather than; used to emphasize difference (position: typically mid-sentence for contrast)
   
   **Synonyms**: rather than, instead of, in contrast to, unlike, as against
   
   **Antonyms**: similar to, like, in the same way as
   
   **When to Use**: Use to highlight contrasts, distinctions, or alternatives between two things
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More formal than simple "rather than" or "instead of"; context determines register appropriateness
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - as opposed to traditional
   - quality as opposed to quantity
   - theory as opposed to practice
   - formal as opposed to informal
   - as opposed to previous
   
   **Root Analysis**: as + opposed (Latin "opponere" - to set against) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Combines "as" with "opposed" (from Latin "opponere") to create emphatic contrast marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I prefer coffee, as opposed to tea.
   - Let's focus on solutions as opposed to problems.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines qualitative data, as opposed to quantitative metrics.
   - The approach emphasizes prevention as opposed to reactive intervention.

7. **Adverb**: according to (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a manner consistent with or proportional to something (position: typically sentence-initial or after verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: in accordance with, in line with, proportionally to, based on
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, in opposition to, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Use for attribution, conformity, or proportional relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can function as preposition (according to the report) or adverb (varies according to); context determines
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vary according to
   - change according to
   - act according to
   - distributed according to
   - arranged according to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + cord (Latin "cor" - heart) + -ing + to
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin "accordare" (bring into agreement); used for conformity and attribution
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We'll divide the work according to everyone's skills.
   - Prices vary according to the season.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Resources were allocated according to demonstrated need.
   - Participants were categorized according to predefined criteria.

8. **Adverb**: prior to (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Before; earlier than; preceding in time (position: typically sentence-initial or before time reference)
   
   **Synonyms**: before, ahead of, earlier than, preceding, in advance of
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following, subsequent to, later than
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to indicate temporal precedence; common in academic, legal, and technical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal; simple "before" is more natural in casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prior to departure
   - prior to implementation
   - prior to the event
   - immediately prior to
   - shortly prior to
   
   **Root Analysis**: prior (Latin "prior" - former) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "prior" (earlier) used as formal temporal marker; emphasizes sequence in formal register
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure to call prior to your visit.
   - Prior to this, I had no experience.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Baseline assessments were conducted prior to intervention implementation.
   - Participants completed informed consent procedures prior to data collection.

9. **Adverb**: thanks to (type: manner/cause)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Because of; as a result of; used to attribute causation (can be positive or ironic) (position: typically sentence-initial)
   
   **Synonyms**: because of, due to, owing to, as a result of, on account of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use to attribute causation with emphasis on agency; can be grateful or sarcastic depending on context
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Tone matters; can sound sarcastic with negative outcomes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - thanks to technology
   - thanks to efforts
   - thanks to support
   - thanks to advances
   - thanks largely to
   
   **Root Analysis**: thanks (plural of "thank") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Extends gratitude expression "thanks" to causal attribution; can be genuine or ironic
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks to your help, we finished on time!
   - Thanks to the delay, we missed our flight. (ironic)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Thanks to recent technological advances, efficiency has improved substantially.
   - The project encountered significant delays, thanks to unforeseen regulatory obstacles.

10. **Adverb**: compared to (type: manner)
    **Meaning**: 
    - In comparison with; when measured against; relative to (position: typically sentence-initial or mid-sentence)
    
    **Synonyms**: in comparison with, relative to, when measured against, versus, against
    
    **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; it's a comparison marker)
    
    **When to Use**: Use to establish comparisons, contrasts, or relative measurements between things
    
    **When NOT to Use**: "Compared to" (similarity/difference) vs. "compared with" (detailed comparison); distinction often subtle
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - compared to previous
    - compared to other
    - when compared to
    - relatively compared to
    - directly compared to
    
    **Root Analysis**: compare (Latin "comparare" - liken) + -ed + to
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "comparare" (make equal, liken) from "com-" + "par" (equal); used for establishing relative measurements
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - This winter is mild compared to last year.
    - You're doing great compared to where you started!
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Treatment group outcomes were significantly improved compared to control conditions.
    - Economic growth remained modest compared to pre-recession levels.
