# Adjective: To

1. **Adjective**: similar to (attributive/predicative, gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having a resemblance or likeness to something else; alike in appearance, nature, or qualities but not identical
   
   **Synonyms**: alike, comparable to, resembling, like, akin to
   
   **Antonyms**: different from, unlike, dissimilar to, distinct from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when comparing things that share common characteristics; common in academic writing, descriptions, and analyses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "same as" (identical); "similar to" implies resemblance, not identity
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very similar to
   - quite similar to
   - strikingly similar to
   - similar to previous
   - not dissimilar to
   
   **Root Analysis**: simil- (Latin "similis" - like) + -ar + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "similis" (like, resembling) → Old French "similier" → English "similar"; phrasal adjective pattern with "to" for comparison
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your car is really similar to mine!
   - This tastes similar to the one we had last time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The observed patterns are strikingly similar to those reported in previous studies.
   - The current economic situation is similar to the conditions preceding the 2008 crisis.

2. **Adjective**: relevant to (predicative, gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Connected with or appropriate to what is being done or considered; pertinent or applicable to a particular matter
   
   **Synonyms**: pertinent to, applicable to, related to, germane to, apropos
   
   **Antonyms**: irrelevant to, unrelated to, inapplicable to, beside the point
   
   **When to Use**: Use when establishing connections between information and topics; essential in academic and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Overuse can sound formal; simpler "related to" works in casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - directly relevant to
   - highly relevant to
   - particularly relevant to
   - not relevant to
   - relevant to the discussion
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (intensive) + lev- (Latin "levare" - to raise) + -ant + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "relevare" (raise up, lighten) → Medieval Latin "relevans" (bearing upon); developed meaning of pertinence through legal usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's not really relevant to what we're talking about.
   - Is this relevant to the project?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings are particularly relevant to policy development in emerging economies.
   - Please include only information directly relevant to the research question.

3. **Adjective**: essential to (predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Absolutely necessary; extremely important; fundamental to the nature or functioning of something
   
   **Synonyms**: crucial to, vital to, indispensable to, fundamental to, critical to
   
   **Antonyms**: unnecessary to, optional for, superfluous to, dispensable to
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize absolute necessity or fundamental importance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: As non-gradable adjective, avoid "very essential" (use "absolutely essential" or "crucial" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - absolutely essential to
   - essential to success
   - essential to survival
   - essential to understanding
   - essential to the process
   
   **Root Analysis**: essent- (Latin "esse" - to be) + -ial + to
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin "essentialis" (pertaining to essence) from "essentia" (being, essence); emphasizes fundamental necessity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Coffee is essential to my morning routine!
   - Good communication is essential to any relationship.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Stakeholder engagement is essential to successful project implementation.
   - Access to clean water is essential to public health outcomes.

4. **Adjective**: close to (predicative, gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Near in space, time, or relationship; almost at a particular state or number
   - Having a strong connection or similar characteristics
   
   **Synonyms**: near to, adjacent to, approximate to, approaching, intimate with
   
   **Antonyms**: far from, distant from, remote from, unrelated to
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical proximity, temporal nearness, numerical approximation, or emotional intimacy
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple meanings require context; "close to tears" vs. "close to the store" vs. "close to completion"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very close to
   - close to home
   - close to completion
   - close to tears
   - close to the truth
   
   **Root Analysis**: close (Latin "clausus" - shut, enclosed) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "clausus" (enclosed) → Old French "clos"; developed meanings of proximity from spatial enclosure
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're pretty close to finished here.
   - I'm really close to my grandmother.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The estimated value is close to the actual measurement.
   - The research brings us close to understanding the underlying mechanisms.

5. **Adjective**: equal to (predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The same in quantity, size, degree, or value; having the ability or resources to deal with a challenge
   
   **Synonyms**: equivalent to, identical to (in value), the same as, tantamount to
   
   **Antonyms**: unequal to, inferior to, superior to, different from
   
   **When to Use**: Use for comparisons of quantity/quality or to express capability to handle challenges
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Non-gradable; don't say "more equal" (except ironically, as in Orwell's "some are more equal than others")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - equal to the task
   - equal to the challenge
   - not equal to
   - equal to or greater than
   - prove equal to
   
   **Root Analysis**: equ- (Latin "aequus" - even, level) + -al + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "aequalis" (equal, uniform) from "aequus" (level, even); developed meanings of sameness and capability
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - One dollar is equal to about 6.5 yuan.
   - I don't think I'm equal to this challenge.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The observed effect is equal to that predicted by the theoretical model.
   - The candidate demonstrated that she is equal to the demands of the position.

6. **Adjective**: contrary to (predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Opposite in nature, direction, or meaning; in conflict with or opposition to
   
   **Synonyms**: opposite to, opposed to, contradictory to, counter to, against
   
   **Antonyms**: consistent with, in accordance with, in agreement with, aligned with
   
   **When to Use**: Use to highlight contradictions, oppositions, or conflicts between ideas, statements, or expectations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More formal than "against" or "opposite"; context determines appropriateness
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contrary to popular belief
   - contrary to expectations
   - contrary to evidence
   - contrary to the rules
   - quite contrary to
   
   **Root Analysis**: contra- (Latin - against) + -ary + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "contrarius" (opposite, opposed) from "contra" (against); emphasizes opposition and contradiction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Contrary to what you think, I actually do care.
   - That's completely contrary to what he told me yesterday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Contrary to the initial hypothesis, no significant correlation was observed.
   - The policy is contrary to international human rights standards.

7. **Adjective**: immune to (predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Protected against or resistant to a particular disease or harmful substance
   - Not affected by or susceptible to something (criticism, pressure, influence)
   
   **Synonyms**: resistant to, protected from, unaffected by, invulnerable to, insusceptible to
   
   **Antonyms**: susceptible to, vulnerable to, prone to, sensitive to
   
   **When to Use**: Use for biological immunity, emotional/psychological resistance, or metaphorical protection from influences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Biological sense requires actual immunity; metaphorical use should be clear from context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immune to disease
   - immune to criticism
   - immune to change
   - immune to pressure
   - not immune to
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (not) + mun (Latin "munis" - serving) + -e + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "immunis" (exempt from service) from "in-" + "munis"; medical sense developed in 19th century, metaphorical uses followed
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He seems immune to embarrassment!
   - I got the vaccine, so I'm immune to that strain.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - No organization is immune to technological disruption.
   - Individuals vaccinated against the virus are immune to severe infection.

8. **Adjective**: subject to (predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Likely to be affected by or to experience something, especially something unwelcome
   - Dependent on or conditional upon something; under the authority or control of
   
   **Synonyms**: liable to, prone to, susceptible to, conditional on, dependent on
   
   **Antonyms**: exempt from, immune to, independent of, free from
   
   **When to Use**: Use for vulnerability, conditionality, or being under authority/rules
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Legal/formal tone; "prone to" or "likely to" may be more natural in casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - subject to change
   - subject to approval
   - subject to conditions
   - subject to interpretation
   - subject to the law
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under) + ject (Latin "jacere" - to throw) + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "subjectus" (placed under) from "subjicere" (place under); developed meanings of subordination and vulnerability
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The schedule is subject to change, so check back later.
   - Everyone's subject to the same rules here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All applications are subject to rigorous review processes.
   - The findings are subject to interpretation within theoretical frameworks.

9. **Adjective**: open to (predicative, gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Willing to consider or accept new ideas, suggestions, or possibilities
   - Vulnerable or exposed to something; susceptible to
   
   **Synonyms**: receptive to, amenable to, willing to consider, susceptible to (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: closed to, opposed to, resistant to, unreceptive to
   
   **When to Use**: Use for willingness to consider ideas (positive) or vulnerability to problems (negative)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context determines whether positive (receptive) or negative (vulnerable); clarify if ambiguous
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - open to suggestions
   - open to criticism
   - open to interpretation
   - open to new ideas
   - open to attack
   
   **Root Analysis**: open (Old English "open") + to
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "open" (not closed); metaphorical use for mental receptiveness emerged in Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm open to trying that new restaurant.
   - She's really open to feedback, which is great.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee remains open to alternative proposals.
   - The methodology is open to criticism regarding sample size limitations.

10. **Adjective**: prior to (predicative, non-gradable)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Existing or occurring before something else in time or order; earlier than
    
    **Synonyms**: before, earlier than, preceding, previous to, anterior to
    
    **Antonyms**: after, following, subsequent to, later than
    
    **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to indicate temporal precedence; common in academic, legal, and professional writing
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Very formal; "before" is simpler and more natural in most casual contexts
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - prior to this
    - prior to the event
    - prior to arrival
    - prior to implementation
    - prior to approval
    
    **Root Analysis**: prior (Latin "prior" - former, earlier) + to
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "prior" (former, earlier) comparative of Old Latin "pri" (before); formal temporal marker in English
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Prior to moving here, I lived in Boston.
    - What did you do prior to this job?
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Prior to initiating the intervention, baseline measurements were obtained.
    - All materials must be submitted prior to the specified deadline.
