# Preposition: To

1. **Preposition**: to (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Expressing direction or movement toward a destination, place, or position
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating a point in time marking the end of a period or range
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the relationship between one thing and another; expressing purpose, result, or function
   
   **Synonyms**: toward (in context of direction), until (in context of time), for (in context of purpose)
   
   **Antonyms**: from (indicating origin/departure), away from (indicating distance)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "to" for direction/destination (go to school), time ranges (from 9 to 5), purpose (to learn), indirect objects (give it to me), comparisons (superior to), infinitives (to run), and ratios (5 to 1)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use "to" with "home" (say "go home," not "go to home"), after modal verbs (can/should/will + verb, not "to"), or when "for" indicates duration (stayed for three days, not "to three days")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: to school, to work, to bed, to the point, to some extent, to date, to the best of my knowledge
   - **Verb + to**: go to, come to, listen to, talk to, speak to, belong to, lead to, contribute to, refer to
   - **Adjective + to**: similar to, close to, next to, related to, relevant to, equal to, kind to, cruel to
   - **Noun + to**: access to, approach to, solution to, answer to, key to, reaction to, response to
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "tō" (unchanged as a simple preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "tō" → Middle English "to" → Modern English "to"; from Proto-Germanic *tō, related to Latin "de-" and Greek "do-" (to, toward)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm heading to the store, want anything?
   - From Monday to Friday, I'm super busy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study aims to investigate the relationship between X and Y.
   - Applications must be submitted to the committee by the specified deadline.

2. **Preposition**: to the extent that (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* To the degree that; insofar as; indicating the degree or limit to which something is true
   
   **Synonyms**: insofar as, to the degree that, inasmuch as, in that
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; it's a qualifier)
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to qualify statements, indicate limits of applicability, or express conditional relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; can sound academic or legalistic
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: to the extent that possible, to the extent that resources allow
   - **Sentence structure**: [Statement], to the extent that [qualification/condition]
   - **Verb + to the extent that**: applies to the extent that, works to the extent that
   
   **Root Analysis**: to + the + extent (from Latin "extentus" - stretched out) + that
   
   **Etymology**: "Extent" from Latin "extentus" (stretched out); phrasal use emphasizing degree/limit emerged in formal register
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll help you out, to the extent that I'm able.
   - It's useful to the extent that you actually use it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy applies to the extent that local regulations permit.
   - Resources will be allocated to the extent that budgetary constraints allow.

3. **Preposition**: to some extent (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Partly; to a limited degree; somewhat
   
   **Synonyms**: partially, partly, somewhat, to a degree, to a certain extent
   
   **Antonyms**: completely, entirely, wholly, not at all
   
   **When to Use**: Use to soften statements, acknowledge partial truth, or indicate limitations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can weaken arguments if overused; avoid in contexts requiring definitive statements
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: true to some extent, valid to some extent, responsible to some extent
   - **Sentence adverbial**: To some extent, ... (hedging statement)
   - **Verb + to some extent**: applies to some extent, depends to some extent, reflects to some extent
   
   **Root Analysis**: to + some + extent (Latin "extentus")
   
   **Etymology**: Combines "extent" with indefinite quantifier "some" to create hedging expression
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I agree with you to some extent, but not completely.
   - To some extent, it's my own fault.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings support the hypothesis to some extent.
   - Success depends to some extent on external market conditions.

4. **Preposition**: to date (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Until now; up to the present time; so far
   
   **Synonyms**: until now, so far, up to now, thus far, hitherto
   
   **Antonyms**: in the future, subsequently, hereafter
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to summarize what has happened up to the current moment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal; in casual speech use "so far" or "until now" instead
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: to date, research to date, efforts to date, results to date
   - **Sentence position**: To date, ... (sentence-initial); ... to date (sentence-final/mid)
   - **Noun + to date**: findings to date, evidence to date, experience to date
   
   **Root Analysis**: to + date (Old French "date" from Latin "data")
   
   **Etymology**: Combines preposition "to" with "date" (Latin "data" - given); formal expression emerged in business/academic contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - To date, we've raised about $5,000.
   - That's the best result we've seen to date.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research to date has yielded inconclusive results.
   - The initiative represents the most comprehensive policy intervention to date.

5. **Preposition**: to the point (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Relevant and appropriate; directly addressing the matter; concise and relevant
   
   **Synonyms**: relevant, pertinent, apt, apropos, germane
   
   **Antonyms**: irrelevant, off-topic, beside the point, rambling
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe communication that is focused, relevant, and concise; can also indicate arriving at the crucial moment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound blunt in some contexts; be careful not to use dismissively
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: straight to the point, brief and to the point, very to the point
   - **Adjective phrase**: is to the point, was to the point
   - **Verb + to the point**: get to the point, come to the point, keep to the point
   
   **Root Analysis**: to + the + point (Old French "point" from Latin "punctum")
   
   **Etymology**: "Point" from Latin "punctum" (something pricked); metaphorical use for main issue emerged in Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just get to the point already!
   - Her comments were short and to the point.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The presentation was concise and to the point.
   - I shall be brief and to the point: we need immediate action.

6. **Preposition**: to be honest (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Used to introduce a frank or candid statement; speaking truthfully
   
   **Synonyms**: frankly, honestly, to tell the truth, in all honesty, to be frank
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; it's a discourse marker)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to introduce sincere opinions, confessions, or frank assessments; signals authenticity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply that previous statements weren't honest; avoid if it suggests deception elsewhere
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Sentence adverbial**: To be honest, ... (sentence-initial)
   - **Parenthetical**: ..., to be honest, ... (mid-sentence)
   - **Clause-final**: ..., to be honest (sentence-final)
   
   **Root Analysis**: to + be + honest (Old French "honeste" from Latin "honestus")
   
   **Etymology**: "Honest" from Latin "honestus" (honorable); phrasal use as discourse marker for introducing frank statements
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - To be honest, I don't really like it.
   - I forgot about it, to be honest.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - To be honest, the initial results were disappointing.
   - The process, to be honest, requires substantial refinement.

7. **Preposition**: according to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* As stated or indicated by; in agreement or conformity with
   
   **Synonyms**: as stated by, as per, in accordance with, based on, per
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, despite, in opposition to
   
   **When to Use**: Use to cite sources, reference authorities, or indicate conformity with rules/standards
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for your own opinions (incorrect: "according to me"); use "in my opinion" instead
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: according to reports, according to the study, according to plan
   - **Citation**: According to [author/source], ... (sentence-initial)
   - **Conformity**: according to regulations, according to protocol, according to tradition
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + cord (Latin "cor" - heart) + -ing + to
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin "accordare" (bring to agreement); "according to" emerged as phrasal preposition for attribution/conformity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to my mom, I need to clean my room.
   - Everything went according to plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to recent census data, population growth has stabilized.
   - The procedure must be conducted according to established protocols.

8. **Preposition**: with regard to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; about; in relation to; regarding
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, in relation to, as regards
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; it's a topic marker)
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to introduce topics or shift focus to specific subjects
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Overly formal for casual contexts; simpler alternatives like "about" often work better
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with regard to your request, with regard to the matter, with regard to policy
   - **Sentence structure**: With regard to [topic], [statement]
   - **Formal transitions**: shifting topics in formal documents
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + regard (Old French "regarder" - to look at) + to
   
   **Etymology**: "Regard" from Old French "regarder" (to look at); phrasal use for topic introduction in formal register
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With regard to your question, I'm not sure.
   - What's the plan with regard to the party?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regard to your inquiry dated June 15, please find our response enclosed.
   - The committee's recommendations with regard to budget allocation are outlined below.

9. **Preposition**: thanks to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Because of; as a result of (can be positive or ironic/negative)
   
   **Synonyms**: because of, due to, owing to, as a result of, on account of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Use to attribute causation, either genuinely grateful (positive) or ironically (negative)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be careful with tone; can sound sarcastic in negative contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: thanks to you, thanks to technology, thanks to efforts
   - **Sentence structure**: Thanks to [cause], [result] (sentence-initial)
   - **Ironic use**: Thanks to [negative cause], [negative result]
   
   **Root Analysis**: thanks (plural of "thank") + to
   
   **Etymology**: "Thank" from Old English "þancian"; "thanks to" developed as causal expression, can be used genuinely or ironically
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks to you, we're late! (ironic)
   - Thanks to your help, I passed the test.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Thanks to advances in technology, efficiency has improved dramatically.
   - The project was delayed, thanks to unforeseen regulatory complications.

10. **Preposition**: in addition to (type: phrasal)
    **Meaning**: 
    - *(Abstract)* As well as; besides; along with; also including
    
    **Synonyms**: besides, as well as, along with, plus, together with
    
    **Antonyms**: excluding, except for, apart from, without
    
    **When to Use**: Use to add information, list additional items, or expand on previous points
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can be wordy; simple "and" or "also" often suffices in casual contexts
    
    **Common Patterns**: 
    - **Prepositional phrases**: in addition to this, in addition to that, in addition to the above
    - **Sentence structure**: In addition to [X], [Y] (sentence-initial or mid-sentence)
    - **Listing**: used to add items to a list or discussion
    
    **Root Analysis**: in + addition (Latin "addere" - to add) + to
    
    **Etymology**: "Addition" from Latin "addere" (to add); phrasal use for expanding lists or adding information
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - In addition to pizza, we're getting wings.
    - She plays guitar, and in addition to that, she sings.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - In addition to the quantitative analysis, qualitative interviews were conducted.
    - The candidate possesses relevant experience in addition to the required qualifications.
