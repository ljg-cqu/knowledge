# Functional Language: To

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd like to... (function: polite requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to politely express a desire or make a request; softer than direct "I want to"
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I would like to, I wish to, I want to (less polite), Could I..., May I...
   
   **Antonyms**: I refuse to, I don't want to, I'd rather not
   
   **When to Use**: Use in polite requests, customer service, formal situations, or when softening demands; universally appropriate
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly formal with close friends; "I want to" more natural in casual settings
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd love to... (more enthusiastic)
   - I'd really like to... (more emphatic)
   - I'd very much like to... (very formal)
   - We'd like to... (group/organizational requests)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses conditional "would" for politeness; distances speaker from direct demand; negative politeness strategy (respects addressee's autonomy)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Of course," "Certainly," "No problem," "Go ahead"
   - Declining: "I'm afraid that's not possible," "Unfortunately, we can't," "I'm sorry, but..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd like to order the chicken, please.
   - I'd like to add something if I may.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would like to express my gratitude for this opportunity.
   - We would like to schedule a meeting at your earliest convenience.

2. **Functional Expression**: I'm going to... (function: stating intention/future plan)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to announce plans, intentions, or predictions about the future; expresses determination or certainty
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I will, I plan to, I intend to, I'm planning to, I'll
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm not going to, I won't, I refuse to, I have no intention of
   
   **When to Use**: Use for announcements of plans, intentions, warnings, or predictions; very common in everyday speech
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less formal than "I will" or "I intend to" for official contexts; sounds less flexible than "I might"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm gonna... (very casual contraction)
   - I'm not going to... (refusal, negative intention)
   - We're going to... (group plans)
   - Are you going to...? (asking about plans)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies speaker has control over action; used for threats/warnings ("I'm going to tell!"); more colloquial than "will" for future
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Supportive: "Good idea," "Go for it," "That sounds great"
   - Discouraging: "Are you sure?", "Maybe reconsider," "I wouldn't if I were you"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to grab some coffee. Want anything?
   - I'm not going to argue about this anymore.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I am going to present the findings at next week's conference.
   - The committee is going to review all applications thoroughly.

3. **Functional Expression**: I want to... (function: expressing desire/intention)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to directly state desires, wishes, or intentions; straightforward expression of what speaker wants
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I'd like to (more polite), I wish to (formal), I desire to (very formal), I intend to
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't want to, I refuse to, I'd rather not
   
   **When to Use**: Use in casual contexts, with close relationships, or when clarity/directness is valued over politeness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound demanding or childish in formal/polite contexts; "I'd like to" is safer for requests
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I really want to... (emphatic)
   - I don't want to... (refusal)
   - I just want to... (minimizing, focus)
   - Do you want to...? (invitation/offer)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Direct expression can seem demanding; appropriate with intimates or for emphasis; children's primary desire expression
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Okay," "Sure," "Go ahead," "That's fine"
   - Declining: "No," "Sorry, you can't," "That's not possible," "Maybe later"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I want to learn how to play guitar.
   - I don't want to go to that party.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I want to emphasize the importance of this initiative. (acceptable for emphasis)
   - I want to clarify my previous statement. (acceptable in presentations)

4. **Functional Expression**: I need to... (function: expressing necessity)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express necessity, obligation, or requirement; indicates something must be done
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I must, I have to, I've got to, It's necessary for me to, I ought to
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't need to, I needn't, There's no need to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing obligations, requirements, or urgent necessities; very common in everyday speech
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound demanding if used for requests from others; "Could you please" softer for requests
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I really need to... (urgent)
   - I need to go (leaving, excuse)
   - We need to... (group obligation/suggestion)
   - You need to... (advice, instruction, or command depending on tone)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Expresses speaker's obligation, not request from listener; when directed at others ("you need to"), can sound commanding
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acknowledging: "Okay," "I understand," "Go ahead," "Do what you need to do"
   - Questioning: "Why?", "Do you really?", "Can it wait?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to finish this by tomorrow.
   - Sorry, I need to take this call.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I need to emphasize the urgency of this matter.
   - We need to address these concerns systematically.

5. **Functional Expression**: I have to... (function: expressing obligation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express external obligation, requirement, or necessity; something imposed rather than chosen
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I must, I need to, I've got to, I'm required to, I'm obliged to
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't have to, I needn't, I'm not required to, I'm free to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing obligations, especially external requirements; common for excuses and explanations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies external requirement; use "I want to" if it's personal choice, "I should" for recommendations
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I've got to... (casual contraction, more urgent feeling)
   - I don't have to... (no obligation)
   - Do I have to...? (questioning necessity, often reluctant)
   - You have to... (instruction, requirement)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies obligation from external source (rules, circumstances); often used to excuse oneself; less personal choice than "want to"
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sympathetic: "I understand," "That's too bad," "Good luck"
   - Practical: "Okay," "When do you have to?", "How long will that take?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have to work late tonight, sorry.
   - Do we have to do this now?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I have to attend a mandatory training session.
   - Participants have to complete informed consent procedures.

6. **Functional Expression**: I'm supposed to... (function: expressing expected action)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express what is expected, required, or planned according to rules, arrangements, or social norms
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm expected to, I'm meant to, I should, I'm scheduled to, It's expected that I
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm not supposed to, I shouldn't, It's not expected
   
   **When to Use**: Use when referencing expectations, rules, or plans; often implies uncertainty or reluctance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound uncommitted; use "I will" or "I'm going to" for firmer commitment
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I was supposed to... (past obligation not fulfilled)
   - I'm not supposed to... (prohibition)
   - Am I supposed to...? (checking expectations)
   - We're supposed to... (group expectations)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies external expectation rather than personal commitment; can distance speaker from obligation; often signals potential non-compliance
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Confirming: "Yes, that's right," "Yes, you are," "According to the plan"
   - Releasing: "Don't worry about it," "It can wait," "Only if you can"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm supposed to meet them at 7, but I might be late.
   - I'm not supposed to tell anyone, but...
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants are supposed to arrive 15 minutes prior to the session.
   - The report is supposed to be submitted by end of day.

7. **Functional Expression**: I tend to... (function: describing habits/patterns)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to describe habitual behavior, typical patterns, or natural inclinations
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I usually, I typically, I have a tendency to, I'm inclined to, I often
   
   **Antonyms**: I never, I rarely, I avoid, I'm unlikely to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing habits, patterns, or general tendencies; softer than absolute statements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for specific future plans; use "I will" or "I'm going to" for definite intentions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I tend not to... (negative pattern)
   - I tend to think/believe... (hedging opinions)
   - People tend to... (general patterns)
   - It tends to... (object tendencies)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedges statements, avoiding absolute claims; useful for expressing opinions without dogmatism; describes patterns, not certainties
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: "Me too," "That makes sense," "That's understandable"
   - Disagreeing: "Really? I'm the opposite," "I tend to do the reverse"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tend to forget names pretty quickly.
   - I tend to avoid crowds when I can.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I tend to think that a multifaceted approach is most appropriate.
   - Research findings tend to support this interpretation.

8. **Functional Expression**: I'm trying to... (function: expressing effort/attempt)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to describe ongoing efforts, attempts, or goals being pursued; emphasizes process over achievement
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm attempting to, I'm working to, I'm endeavoring to, I'm making an effort to
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm not trying, I've given up, I refuse to try
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing effort, excusing imperfect results, or describing ongoing attempts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound like excuse for failure; use "I will" for firmer commitment without hedging
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm really trying to... (emphatic effort)
   - I'm just trying to... (defensive, minimizing)
   - I've been trying to... (extended effort)
   - Are you even trying to...? (criticism)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Emphasizes effort rather than result; can excuse failure ("I tried"); signals ongoing process; can sound defensive
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Encouraging: "Keep at it," "You'll get there," "Good effort"
   - Practical: "Maybe try a different approach," "Do you need help?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm trying to eat healthier these days.
   - I'm just trying to help, okay?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We are trying to implement best practices across all departments.
   - The organization is trying to reduce its environmental impact.

9. **Functional Expression**: I used to... (function: describing past habits/states)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to describe past habits, repeated actions, or states that are no longer true
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I formerly, I previously, In the past I, I would (past habitual)
   
   **Antonyms**: I now, I currently, These days I, I no longer
   
   **When to Use**: Use when contrasting past with present, describing change, or expressing nostalgia
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Only for repeated past actions or states, not single events ("I went" not "I used to go" for one-time action)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I didn't use to... (past negative habit)
   - I never used to... (emphatic past negative)
   - Did you use to...? (asking about past habits)
   - I used to be... (past state)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies change has occurred; often nostalgic or regretful tone; contrasts past and present implicitly
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sympathetic: "What happened?", "Why did you stop?", "I miss those days too"
   - Curious: "When was that?", "For how long?", "What changed?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I used to play basketball every weekend.
   - I used to live in that neighborhood.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization used to maintain a different policy framework.
   - This approach used to be considered best practice.

10. **Functional Expression**: I was wondering if... (function: polite requesting/asking)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Used to make very polite requests or ask sensitive questions; softens directness
    - *Formality*: neutral to formal
    - *Directness*: very indirect
    
    **Synonyms**: Could I possibly..., Would it be possible..., Might I..., I'd like to know if...
    
    **Antonyms**: Tell me..., I demand to know..., Give me... (all much more direct)
    
    **When to Use**: Use for polite requests, sensitive questions, impositions, or formal situations requiring tact
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly indirect or hesitant; too formal for close friends in casual settings
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - I was wondering whether... (more formal)
    - I was just wondering if... (minimizing, even softer)
    - We were wondering if... (group requests)
    - I've been wondering if... (extended curiosity)
    
    **Pragmatic Notes**: Past continuous "was wondering" maximizes distance from request; extreme negative politeness; allows easy refusal; very tentative
    
    **Response Patterns**: 
    - Accepting: "Of course," "Sure, what is it?", "Go ahead," "Absolutely"
    - Declining: "I'm afraid not," "Unfortunately, no," "I'd rather not," "That won't be possible"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - I was wondering if you could help me with something.
    - I was wondering if you're free this weekend.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - I was wondering if we might schedule a meeting to discuss this matter.
    - I was wondering whether you could provide additional clarification regarding the policy.
