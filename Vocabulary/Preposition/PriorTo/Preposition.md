1. **Preposition**: prior to (type: phrasal preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Before a particular time, event, or action; preceding in time
   - Used primarily in formal and academic contexts to express temporal precedence
   
   **Synonyms**: before, in advance of, preceding, ahead of, earlier than
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following, subsequent to, later than
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing (academic papers, business reports, legal documents), official communications, when emphasizing temporal sequence in professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds overly formal), informal writing (use "before" instead), when writing for general audiences who may find it pretentious
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: prior to departure, prior to implementation, prior to the meeting, prior to approval
   - **Verb + prep**: completed prior to, occurred prior to, submitted prior to, reviewed prior to
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rarely combined with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: weeks prior to, days prior to, moments prior to, years prior to
   
   **Root Analysis**: prior (earlier, former) + to (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "prior" (former, earlier, superior) → Middle English → Combined with "to" to form phrasal preposition in 17th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to finish this before Friday. (Note: "prior to" would sound stilted here)
   - Make sure you call me before the meeting starts. (Not: "prior to the meeting")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All participants must register prior to the conference commencement.
   - The document should be reviewed and approved prior to distribution to stakeholders.

2. **Preposition**: before (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* At an earlier time than; preceding in time
   - *(Spatial)* In front of; ahead of in position or order
   - *(Abstract)* In preference to; rather than
   
   **Synonyms**: prior to (formal), ahead of, in advance of, earlier than, preceding
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following, behind, later than, subsequent to
   
   **When to Use**: All registers (casual to formal), general temporal/spatial relationships, everyday communication, when clarity and naturalness are priorities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When excessive formality is required (use "prior to"), though "before" is acceptable in virtually all contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: before lunch, before the deadline, before sunset, before class
   - **Verb + prep**: arrive before, finish before, leave before, happen before
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare combinations)
   - **Noun + prep**: day before, moment before, week before, night before
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by, about) + fore (front, in front of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "beforan" (in front of, earlier) → Middle English "before" → Modern English (both temporal and spatial meanings retained)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you finish this before dinner?
   - I've never been to Paris before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Candidates must submit applications before the specified deadline.
   - The committee convened before the official announcement was released.

3. **Preposition**: in advance of (type: phrasal preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Before a particular time or event, especially with preparation or anticipation
   - Implies forward planning or preparedness
   
   **Synonyms**: before, prior to, ahead of, in preparation for, preceding
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following, in the wake of, subsequent to
   
   **When to Use**: Formal/business contexts emphasizing preparation, when highlighting proactive planning, official schedules and timelines
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "before"), when simple temporal sequence is sufficient without emphasis on preparation
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in advance of the launch, in advance of the deadline, in advance of the event
   - **Verb + prep**: planned in advance of, prepared in advance of, scheduled in advance of
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare)
   - **Noun + prep**: weeks in advance of, days in advance of, months in advance of
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + advance (forward movement) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "abante" → French "avancer" (to move forward) → English "advance" (14th century) → phrasal preposition usage (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You should book your tickets before the concert. (Not: "in advance of")
   - Let me know before you arrive. (More natural than "in advance of")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Strategic resources must be allocated in advance of the fiscal year.
   - Participants will receive briefing materials in advance of the summit.

4. **Preposition**: ahead of (type: compound preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Before in time; earlier than scheduled or expected
   - *(Spatial)* In front of; further forward than
   - *(Abstract)* More advanced or progressive than; surpassing
   
   **Synonyms**: before, in front of, prior to, in advance of, preceding
   
   **Antonyms**: behind, after, following, lagging, trailing
   
   **When to Use**: Both formal and informal contexts, when emphasizing being early or in front, competitive or comparative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Highly formal academic writing where "prior to" may be preferred, simple statements where "before" is clearer
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: ahead of schedule, ahead of time, ahead of the curve, ahead of the competition
   - **Verb + prep**: finish ahead of, arrive ahead of, stay ahead of, move ahead of
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare direct combinations)
   - **Noun + prep**: days ahead of, weeks ahead of, years ahead of
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on, in) + head (forward position)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "on heafde" → Middle English "ahed" → Modern English "ahead" (spatial → temporal metaphorical extension)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We finished the project ahead of schedule!
   - She arrived ahead of everyone else.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization remains ahead of industry standards in sustainability practices.
   - Implementation was completed ahead of the projected timeline.

5. **Preposition**: preceding (type: participial preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Coming before in time or order
   - *(Sequential)* Immediately before in a sequence or series
   
   **Synonyms**: before, prior to, earlier than, previous to, antecedent to
   
   **Antonyms**: following, subsequent, succeeding, after, later
   
   **When to Use**: Formal/academic writing, when referring to items in ordered sequences, technical documentation, when precision about sequence is important
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds academic), simple statements where "before" is clearer and more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: preceding the event, preceding the chapter, preceding the announcement
   - **Verb + prep**: occurred preceding, discussed preceding, mentioned preceding
   - **Adjective + prep**: immediately preceding, directly preceding
   - **Noun + prep**: days preceding, weeks preceding, events preceding
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + ced (go, move) + -ing (participial form)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praecedere" (to go before) → Old French "preceder" → English "precede" → participial form "preceding" used as preposition
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We talked about this before the meeting. (Not: "preceding the meeting")
   - Everything that happened before now is history. (More natural than "preceding")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data from the quarter preceding the merger indicate strong growth.
   - All conditions outlined in the section preceding this clause must be satisfied.

6. **Preposition**: until (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Up to the point in time or the event mentioned; up to the time when
   - Indicates continuation up to a specific endpoint
   
   **Synonyms**: till, up to, up until, as far as, through to
   
   **Antonyms**: from, since, after, starting from
   
   **When to Use**: All registers, when expressing duration up to a point, when indicating a deadline or endpoint, both formal and casual contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When expressing starting points (use "from" or "since"), when the action is completed before the endpoint (use "before" or "by")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: until now, until then, until Monday, until further notice
   - **Verb + prep**: wait until, continue until, stay until, work until
   - **Adjective + prep**: valid until, open until, available until
   - **Noun + prep**: time until, days until, period until
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (as far as) + til (to, towards)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "und" (as far as) + Old English "til" (to) → Middle English "until" (12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll wait here until you get back.
   - The store is open until 9 PM.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The provisional agreement remains in effect until formal ratification occurs.
   - Participants must maintain confidentiality until official publication.

7. **Preposition**: by (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Not later than; at or before a specified time (deadline)
   - *(Agency)* Through the agency or action of
   - *(Spatial)* Near; beside; next to
   
   **Synonyms**: before (for deadlines), no later than, through, via (for agency)
   
   **Antonyms**: after, beyond (temporal), without (agency)
   
   **When to Use**: All registers, when expressing deadlines, when indicating agency or means, spatial proximity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing continuation up to a point (use "until"), when simply indicating precedence without deadline (use "before")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by Friday, by the end, by then, by tomorrow
   - **Verb + prep**: finish by, complete by, arrive by, submit by
   - **Adjective + prep**: due by, required by, expected by
   - **Noun + prep**: deadline by, date by
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form from Old English
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bī" (near, by, about) → Middle English "by" → Modern English (multiple meanings evolved from spatial proximity)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you finish this by tomorrow?
   - I'll be there by 3 o'clock.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All submissions must be received by the specified deadline.
   - The report shall be completed by the end of the fiscal quarter.
