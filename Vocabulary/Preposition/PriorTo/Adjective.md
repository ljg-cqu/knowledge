1. **Adjective**: prior
   **Meaning**: 
   - Existing or coming before in time, order, or importance; earlier or previous
   - *Gradable*: Non-gradable (absolute temporal precedence)
   
   **Synonyms**: previous, earlier, preceding, former, antecedent
   
   **Antonyms**: subsequent, following, later, posterior, ensuing
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts, when describing temporal sequence, legal and academic writing, official documents
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "earlier" or "previous"), informal writing where simpler terms are clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prior experience/knowledge/approval
   - prior to (preposition meaning "before")
   - prior arrangement/agreement
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "prior" (former, earlier, first of two)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "prior" (former, earlier) → Old French → English (17th century, formal register)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Do you have any previous experience with this?"
   - "We talked about this earlier."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Candidates with prior experience in project management will be given preference."
   - "Prior approval from the ethics committee is required before commencing research."

2. **Adjective**: previous
   **Meaning**: 
   - Existing or happening before the present time or before something else; earlier
   - *Gradable*: Generally non-gradable (but can use "immediately previous")
   
   **Synonyms**: prior, earlier, preceding, former, past
   
   **Antonyms**: next, following, subsequent, later, forthcoming
   
   **When to Use**: All registers and contexts, common in both formal and informal language, when referring to earlier times or events
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "prior" is required for extreme formality (legal contexts), when discussing future events
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - previous experience/version/meeting
   - on a previous occasion
   - the previous day/week/year
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + -vious (way, going)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praevius" (going before) → English (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I mentioned this in our previous conversation."
   - "The previous version was better."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "As discussed in the previous meeting, budget allocation remains a critical concern."
   - "The new policy supersedes all previous regulations on this matter."

3. **Adjective**: preceding
   **Meaning**: 
   - Coming immediately before in time or order; just before
   - *Gradable*: Non-gradable (absolute positional/temporal relationship)
   
   **Synonyms**: previous, prior, foregoing, above (in text), earlier
   
   **Antonyms**: following, subsequent, succeeding, next, ensuing
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, academic and technical contexts, when referring to items in ordered lists or sequences, legal documents
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "previous" or "earlier"), when not referring to immediate precedence
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the preceding paragraph/chapter/section
   - in the preceding discussion/analysis
   - days/weeks preceding [event]
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + ced (go) + -ing (participial adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praecedere" (to go before) → participial form used as adjective (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Like I said earlier..." (More natural than "as stated in the preceding")
   - "The day before that..." (Simpler than "the preceding day")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "As outlined in the preceding section, several factors contributed to this outcome."
   - "The weeks preceding the implementation were characterized by intensive preparation."

4. **Adjective**: preliminary
   **Meaning**: 
   - Coming before a more important action or event, especially introducing or preparing for it; initial or preparatory
   - *Gradable*: Generally non-gradable (but "very preliminary" possible)
   
   **Synonyms**: initial, preparatory, introductory, early, provisional
   
   **Antonyms**: final, conclusive, definitive, ultimate, completed
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and academic contexts, when describing early stages or initial versions, preparatory activities, formal reports and research
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing final versions, casual conversation about simple preparations, when "early" or "first" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - preliminary results/findings/data
   - preliminary assessment/investigation
   - preliminary stage/phase
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + limin (threshold) + -ary (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praeliminaris" (before the threshold) → French "préliminaire" → English (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "These are just the early results."
   - "This is a first draft."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Preliminary analysis suggests a significant correlation between the variables."
   - "The committee will conduct a preliminary review prior to the comprehensive assessment."

5. **Adjective**: early
   **Meaning**: 
   - Happening or done before the usual, expected, or planned time; near the beginning of a period or process
   - *Gradable*: Yes (earlier, earliest; very early, quite early)
   
   **Synonyms**: premature, advance, ahead of time, timely (in some contexts), initial
   
   **Antonyms**: late, delayed, tardy, belated, overdue
   
   **When to Use**: All registers and contexts, common in both formal and informal language, when describing timing relative to expectations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When extreme formality requires Latin-derived terms, when describing sequence rather than timing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - early stage/phase
   - arrive/leave early
   - early morning/evening
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "ærlīce" (soon, in the morning)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "ær" (soon, before) + "-ly" → "early" (before expected time)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I got there really early."
   - "It's still pretty early in the project."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Early intervention is critical for optimal outcomes."
   - "The project is in its early stages, with initial results expected next quarter."

6. **Adjective**: advance
   **Meaning**: 
   - Done, sent, or provided before an event or time; ahead of time (attributive only: before noun)
   - *Gradable*: Non-gradable (absolute temporal relationship)
   
   **Synonyms**: prior, early, preliminary, preparatory, beforehand
   
   **Antonyms**: last-minute, delayed, retrospective, subsequent
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts, when describing preparations or early provision, notification contexts, planning situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: As predicative adjective (can't say "the notice was advance"), very casual contexts, when "early" or "prior" would be simpler
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advance notice/warning/payment
   - advance booking/reservation
   - advance preparation/planning
   
   **Root Analysis**: From verb "advance" (Latin "abante," before)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "abante" (before) → Old French "avancer" → English "advance" → attributive adjective use (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Let me know ahead of time."
   - "Did you book early?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Advance notification is required for all schedule changes."
   - "Participants who register with advance payment receive a discounted rate."

7. **Adjective**: preparatory
   **Meaning**: 
   - Serving to prepare for something; done as preparation for something more important
   - *Gradable*: Generally non-gradable (absolute function)
   
   **Synonyms**: preliminary, preparative, introductory, initial, groundwork
   
   **Antonyms**: final, concluding, main, principal, ultimate
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts, when describing activities or stages that prepare for main events, educational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "prep" or "preparation"), when simpler language suffices, informal writing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - preparatory work/activities/measures
   - preparatory stage/phase
   - preparatory school (private school preparing for university)
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + par (make ready) + -atory (serving for, relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praeparatorius" (serving to prepare) → English (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We did some prep work first."
   - "This is just to get ready for the main event."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Comprehensive preparatory measures were undertaken prior to the system migration."
   - "The preparatory phase of the research involved extensive literature review."

8. **Adjective**: anticipated
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expected or predicted; regarded as likely to happen in the future
   - *Gradable*: Yes (can be modified: highly anticipated, much anticipated)
   
   **Synonyms**: expected, predicted, foreseen, awaited, projected
   
   **Antonyms**: unexpected, unanticipated, surprising, unforeseen, spontaneous
   
   **When to Use**: All registers, when describing expected future events or outcomes, planning and forecasting contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing actual past events, when unpredictability is emphasized
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - much/highly anticipated
   - anticipated results/outcomes
   - anticipated arrival/completion
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (before) + cip (take) + -ated (past participle used as adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "anticipatus" (taken beforehand) → English participle → adjective use (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Everyone's been waiting for this movie!"
   - "The party is tomorrow—so exciting!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The anticipated completion date for the project is December 31st."
   - "Results significantly exceeded anticipated performance metrics for the quarter."

9. **Adjective**: proactive
   **Meaning**: 
   - Acting in anticipation of future problems, needs, or changes; taking initiative before situations require action
   - *Gradable*: Yes (more proactive, highly proactive, very proactive)
   
   **Synonyms**: anticipatory, forward-thinking, preventive, preemptive, initiative-taking
   
   **Antonyms**: reactive, passive, responsive, inactive, after-the-fact
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and business contexts, when describing forward planning and initiative, management and leadership discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing responsive or reactive actions, casual contexts where simpler terms suffice, when criticizing excessive anticipation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proactive approach/measures/strategy
   - proactive rather than reactive
   - take a proactive stance
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward, before) + active (taking action)
   
   **Etymology**: Modern formation (1930s): "pro-" (forward) + "active" → popularized in business literature (1980s-1990s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We should get ahead of this problem."
   - "Let's deal with it before it becomes an issue."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Organizations must adopt proactive strategies to remain competitive in rapidly evolving markets."
   - "A proactive approach to risk management can prevent costly disruptions."

10. **Adjective**: prerequisite
    **Meaning**: 
    - Required as a prior condition for something else; necessary beforehand (attributive use as adjective)
    - *Gradable*: Generally non-gradable (absolute requirement)
    
    **Synonyms**: required, necessary, essential, mandatory, obligatory
    
    **Antonyms**: optional, unnecessary, subsequent, consequent, elective
    
    **When to Use**: Academic and professional contexts, when describing requirements that must be met first, course planning, technical specifications
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "required first"), when conditions are optional, when simpler language is preferred
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - prerequisite course/requirement/condition
    - prerequisite knowledge/skills
    - prerequisite for [something]
    
    **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + requisite (required, necessary)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "requisitus" (required) with prefix "pre-" → English formation (17th century)
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - "You need to take the intro class first."
    - "Do I need anything before I start?"
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - "Completion of the prerequisite courses is mandatory for enrollment in advanced seminars."
    - "Meeting prerequisite qualifications is essential prior to submitting an application."
