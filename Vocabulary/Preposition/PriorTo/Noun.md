1. **Noun**: precedence (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The condition of being considered more important than something else; priority in importance, order, or rank
   - *(Sense 2)* The right to come before others in order or time
   
   **Synonyms**: priority, preference, superiority, preeminence, primacy
   
   **Antonyms**: subordination, inferiority, lower priority, secondary status
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts, when discussing importance or order of priorities, technical and legal contexts, formal decision-making
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "priority"), informal contexts, when simpler terms would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take precedence over (be more important than)
   - give precedence to (prioritize)
   - in order of precedence
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + ced (go) + -ence (noun suffix, state of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praecedentia" (act of going before) → Old French → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Family comes first for me." (More natural than "takes precedence")
   - "Safety is our top priority." (Simpler than "precedence")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "In emergency situations, patient safety takes precedence over all other considerations."
   - "The constitutional provisions have precedence over statutory regulations."

2. **Noun**: priority (C/U, pl: priorities)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that is more important than other things and that needs to be done or dealt with first
   - *(Sense 2)* The right to have or do something before others; precedence
   
   **Synonyms**: precedence, preference, urgency, importance, first concern
   
   **Antonyms**: afterthought, low priority, secondary concern, triviality
   
   **When to Use**: All registers and contexts, planning and decision-making, time management, both formal and informal situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing exact temporal sequence rather than importance (use "order" or "sequence")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - top/high/low priority
   - set priorities
   - make something a priority
   
   **Root Analysis**: pri- (first, before) + -ority (noun suffix, state of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "prior" (first, former) → Medieval Latin "prioritas" → Old French "priorité" → English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Getting this done is my top priority right now."
   - "We need to set some priorities for the project."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The organization's strategic priorities for the upcoming fiscal year include digital transformation and sustainability."
   - "Resource allocation should be aligned with institutional priorities."

3. **Noun**: preparation (C/U, pl: preparations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action or process of getting ready for something or making something ready
   - *(Sense 2)* Something that is done to get ready for an event or activity
   
   **Synonyms**: planning, arrangement, groundwork, readiness, organization
   
   **Antonyms**: improvisation, unpreparedness, spontaneity, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: All registers, when discussing actions taken in advance, planning contexts, both formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing actual execution rather than advance planning, when spontaneity is emphasized
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in preparation for (getting ready for)
   - make preparations
   - advance preparation
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + par (make ready) + -ation (noun suffix, action/process)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praeparatio" → Old French "preparation" → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I didn't have much time to prepare for the test."
   - "The preparations for the party are almost done."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Adequate preparation is essential for successful project implementation."
   - "Extensive preparations were undertaken in advance of the international summit."

4. **Noun**: deadline (C, pl: deadlines)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A date or time before which something must be done or completed; the latest time by which something must be finished
   
   **Synonyms**: due date, time limit, cutoff, final date, target date
   
   **Antonyms**: open-ended, flexible timeline, no time limit
   
   **When to Use**: All registers and contexts, project management, academic and professional settings, time-sensitive situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When flexibility or ongoing processes are emphasized, when exact time requirements don't exist
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - meet/miss a deadline
   - tight/strict deadline
   - deadline approaching
   
   **Root Analysis**: dead (absolute, fixed) + line (boundary, limit)
   
   **Etymology**: American Civil War term (line beyond which prisoners would be shot) → general use meaning "time limit" (early 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "When's the deadline for this assignment?"
   - "I barely made the deadline!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "All submissions must be received prior to the established deadline of March 31st."
   - "Failure to meet the deadline may result in disqualification from consideration."

5. **Noun**: schedule (C, pl: schedules)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A plan that lists all the work or activities you have to do and when you must do each one; a timetable
   - *(Sense 2)* The amount of time you plan to spend on an activity
   
   **Synonyms**: timetable, program, agenda, plan, timeline, calendar
   
   **Antonyms**: spontaneity, unplanned time, flexibility, no schedule
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and organizational contexts, time management, planning discussions, both formal and informal business settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When flexibility is emphasized, for spontaneous activities, when rigid planning is inappropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ahead of/behind schedule
   - on schedule (according to plan)
   - tight/busy schedule
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "schedula" (small strip of paper with writing)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "schedula" → Old French "cedule" → English "schedule" (14th century, meaning evolved from "document" to "timetable")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "What's your schedule like next week?"
   - "I have a really busy schedule today."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The project schedule indicates completion prior to the end of Q3."
   - "Please refer to the attached schedule for detailed timing of all conference sessions."

6. **Noun**: advance (U/C, pl: advances when countable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Movement forward or progress; forward movement in time, space, or development
   - *(Sense 2)* Something done or provided before the usual time (in advance)
   
   **Synonyms**: progress, forward movement, headway, development, progression
   
   **Antonyms**: retreat, regression, setback, delay, postponement
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts, when discussing progress or early action, planning and logistics, "in advance" is common phrase
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts where simpler terms suffice, when regression rather than progress is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in advance (beforehand)
   - advance notice/warning
   - make advances (progress)
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + vance (from Latin "abante," before)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "abante" (before, in front) → Old French "avancer" (move forward) → English "advance" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Let me know ahead of time if you need anything."
   - "We're making good progress on the project."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Participants should register in advance to ensure adequate accommodation arrangements."
   - "Advance planning is critical for successful implementation of complex organizational changes."

7. **Noun**: prerequisite (C, pl: prerequisites)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that is necessary before something else can happen or be done; a required prior condition
   
   **Synonyms**: requirement, precondition, necessity, essential, condition
   
   **Antonyms**: optional element, non-requirement, consequence, result
   
   **When to Use**: Academic and professional contexts, when discussing requirements or necessary conditions, course planning, project dependencies
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "requirement"), when conditions are optional rather than necessary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - meet the prerequisites
   - course prerequisites
   - prerequisite for [something]
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + requisite (required, necessary)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "requisitus" (required) + prefix "pre-" → English formation (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "You need to take the intro class first."
   - "What are the requirements for this course?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Successful completion of the foundational course is a prerequisite for enrollment in advanced seminars."
   - "Meeting all regulatory prerequisites is essential prior to commencing clinical trials."

8. **Noun**: anticipation (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of expecting or looking forward to something; the state of preparing for or predicting future events
   - *(Sense 2)* Action taken in advance to deal with an expected situation
   
   **Synonyms**: expectation, prediction, foresight, preparation, readiness
   
   **Antonyms**: surprise, unpreparedness, reaction, hindsight
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts, when discussing forward planning or expectations, psychological and business contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation, when describing actual reactions rather than advance expectations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in anticipation of (expecting and preparing for)
   - with anticipation (looking forward to)
   - sense of anticipation
   
   **Root Analysis**: anti- (before) + cip (take) + -ation (noun suffix, action/state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "anticipatio" (preconception, anticipation) → Old French → English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'm really looking forward to the concert!"
   - "Everyone's excited about the announcement."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "In anticipation of increased demand, additional resources have been allocated."
   - "The policy changes were implemented in anticipation of regulatory requirements."

9. **Noun**: commencement (U/C, pl: commencements)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The beginning or start of something, especially something formal or important
   - *(Sense 2)* A graduation ceremony (American English)
   
   **Synonyms**: beginning, start, initiation, inception, opening, launch
   
   **Antonyms**: conclusion, end, termination, completion, finale
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and official contexts, legal documents, academic ceremonies, when formality is required
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "start" or "beginning"), informal writing, when simplicity is preferred
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prior to commencement
   - at/upon commencement
   - commencement of operations/work
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (intensive) + mence (begin) + -ment (noun suffix, result/action)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "com-" + "initiare" (begin) → Old French "comencier" → English "commence" + "-ment" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "When do we start the project?"
   - "The meeting begins at 2 PM."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "All documentation must be completed prior to commencement of the contract period."
   - "The commencement of operations is scheduled for the first quarter of the fiscal year."

10. **Noun**: timeline (C, pl: timelines)
    **Meaning**: 
    - A schedule showing when each stage of a project or process should be completed; a plan showing sequence of events over time
    - *(Sense 2)* A graphic representation showing the passage of time
    
    **Synonyms**: schedule, timetable, chronology, timeframe, plan, itinerary
    
    **Antonyms**: timelessness, open-ended schedule, no deadline
    
    **When to Use**: Professional and project management contexts, planning discussions, both formal and semi-formal business settings
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Very formal legal documents (use "schedule"), when exact times rather than periods are needed, historical chronologies (use "chronology")
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - project timeline
    - realistic/aggressive timeline
    - timeline for completion
    
    **Root Analysis**: time + line (linear representation)
    
    **Etymology**: Modern compound (20th century): "time" (Old English "tima") + "line" → initially graphic representation, now also abstract schedule
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - "What's the timeline for getting this done?"
    - "We need a realistic timeline for the project."
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - "The attached timeline outlines all critical milestones and deliverables for the implementation phase."
    - "Prior to approving the proposal, the committee must review the proposed timeline for feasibility."
