# Extract Multi-Word Expressions for Vocabulary Learning

**Task**: Extract multi-word expressions (collocations, idioms, phrases, fixed expressions) that are essential for fluent, native-like communication from the source text.

**Definition**: Each entry focuses on one multi-word expression with comprehensive learning information including meaning, usage context, fixedness level, and examples across registers.

**Rules**:

**Coverage:**
- Read entire source thoroughly—identify all multi-word expressions that represent natural word partnerships or carry special meaning
- Prioritize: collocations, idiomatic expressions, fixed phrases, binomials, phrasal constructions, domain-specific multi-word terms
- Extract comprehensively to reflect source richness; apply 80/20 principle (focus on high-frequency and culturally significant expressions)
- Do not skip sections; cover both transparent collocations and opaque idioms

**Fidelity:**
- Ground all information in expressions actually present in source
- Never invent expressions not in source
- Derive meaning from context when source provides it; supplement with standard dictionary definitions

**Focus:**
- **Collocations (transparent meaning)**:
  - Verb + noun (make a decision, take a break, do homework)
  - Adjective + noun (strong opinion, heavy traffic, bright future)
  - Adverb + adjective (highly effective, deeply concerned, utterly exhausted)
  - Verb + adverb (argue strongly, apologize profusely, examine closely)
  - Noun + verb (prices rise, rain falls, heart beats)
  - Noun + noun (round of applause, sense of humor, word of mouth)
  - Verb + preposition + noun (rely on intuition, result in failure)
- **Idioms & Fixed Phrases (non-literal meaning)**:
  - Idiomatic expressions with figurative meaning
  - Fixed phrases and binomials (salt and pepper, pros and cons)
  - Conversational formulae and discourse markers
  - Culturally-bound expressions

**Format:**
- **Expression**: [complete multi-word expression] (type: collocation/idiom/fixed phrase)
- **Meaning**: Collins Dictionary style definition(s)
  - *Explain the combined meaning clearly*
  - *For collocations: note transparency and partnership strength*
  - *For idioms: explain both literal and figurative meaning*
  - *Include register information (formal/informal/neutral)*
- **Synonyms**: 3-5 alternative expressions or single-word equivalents
- **Antonyms**: 2-4 expressions with opposite meaning (if applicable)
- **When to Use**: Context, situations, or registers where this expression is appropriate
- **When NOT to Use**: Contexts where this expression would sound unnatural, be incorrect, or be misunderstood
- **Common Variations**: Alternative forms or related expressions
- **Fixedness Level**: weak/medium/strong (how fixed the word partnership is)
- **Why This Partnership**: Brief explanation of why these words combine naturally (for collocations) or origin story (for idioms)
- **Root Analysis**: Etymology of key components (if relevant)
- **Examples [Casual]**: 2 natural example sentences in informal register
- **Examples [Formal]**: 2 natural example sentences in formal/academic register (or note if restricted)

1. **Expression**: behind the scenes (type: idiom/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: In the area behind the stage or set where performers prepare
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: In private, away from public view; the hidden work that makes something happen
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: backstage, privately, in the background, out of sight, off the record
   
   **Antonyms**: publicly, openly, in the spotlight, front and center
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing hidden work, private operations, secret activities, or the unseen effort that produces visible results; appropriate in both professional and casual contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when literal backstage area is meant without metaphorical extension; can sound clichéd in overuse
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - behind-the-scenes (adjective form: "behind-the-scenes work")
   - go behind the scenes
   - look behind the scenes
   - what happens behind the scenes
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originates from theater terminology where "scenes" referred to stage decorations and the area behind them where actors prepared; metaphorically extended to any hidden preparatory work
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *behindan* + Old French *scène* (from Latin *scaena* "stage")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You wouldn't believe what goes on behind the scenes at that restaurant—total chaos!
   - She does a lot of behind-the-scenes work to make these events run smoothly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Expression**: behind schedule (type: fixed phrase/collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Later than planned; not progressing at the expected or required pace
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very commonly used together)
   
   **Synonyms**: delayed, running late, overdue, lagging, off track
   
   **Antonyms**: on schedule, on time, on track, ahead of schedule
   
   **When to Use**: Use in project management, planning contexts, discussions of timelines and deadlines; appropriate in both professional and everyday contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate for describing people being literally late (use "late" or "running late" instead)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fall behind schedule
   - running behind schedule
   - days/weeks/months behind schedule
   - get behind schedule
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Schedule" naturally pairs with "behind" to express temporal lag; the spatial metaphor of "behind" applies to temporal progress
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *behindan* + Old French *cedule* (from Latin *schedula* "slip of paper, note")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're way behind schedule on the renovation—it's taking forever.
   - Sorry I'm behind schedule today; traffic was a nightmare.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The construction project is currently three weeks behind schedule due to supply chain disruptions.
   - Management has implemented corrective measures to address tasks that have fallen behind schedule.

1. **Expression**: behind closed doors (type: idiom/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: In a room with the door shut
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: In private, secretly, without public observation or participation
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: privately, in private, in secret, confidentially, in camera (legal)
   
   **Antonyms**: publicly, openly, in the open, transparently
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing private meetings, secret negotiations, confidential discussions, or activities hidden from public view; can carry neutral or negative connotations depending on context
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when literal door closure is the only meaning; be careful as it can imply secrecy or lack of transparency
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - what happens behind closed doors
   - meet behind closed doors
   - negotiate behind closed doors
   - behind-closed-doors (adjective: "behind-closed-doors meeting")
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Closed doors physically prevent observation, naturally extending to metaphor for privacy and secrecy in decision-making
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *behindan* + Old English *clūse* (past participle of *clūsian* "to close") + Old English *duru* "door"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Who knows what they talk about behind closed doors?
   - The real decisions get made behind closed doors, not in those public meetings.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The negotiations took place behind closed doors over several days.
   - Critics argue that policy decisions made behind closed doors lack democratic accountability.

1. **Expression**: leave behind (type: phrasal verb/collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To go away from a place or person without taking something or someone with you; to abandon or forget
   - *(Sense 2)* To make progress beyond; to surpass or outpace
   - *Fixedness*: medium (phrasal verb with consistent meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: abandon, forget, desert, outpace, surpass, move beyond
   
   **Antonyms**: bring along, take with, keep up with, stay with
   
   **When to Use**: Use for describing forgotten items, abandoned things/people, or surpassing others in progress; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be sensitive when describing people being "left behind" as it can sound harsh or judgmental in some contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - leave something/someone behind
   - get left behind
   - be left behind
   - leaving behind (gerund form)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Phrasal verb combining movement ("leave") with spatial position ("behind") to express abandonment or surpassing
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *lǣfan* "to allow to remain" + Old English *behindan* "at the back"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I left my phone behind at the restaurant—had to go back for it.
   - Don't worry about the past; leave it behind and move on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The educational reforms aim to ensure no student is left behind.
   - Rapid technological advancement has left many traditional industries behind.

1. **Expression**: fall behind (type: phrasal verb/collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To fail to keep up with a schedule, standard, or group; to lag in progress or payment
   - *Fixedness*: medium (common phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: lag behind, drop back, lose ground, get behind, slip back
   
   **Antonyms**: keep up, catch up, stay ahead, maintain pace
   
   **When to Use**: Use in contexts of progress tracking, academic performance, debt/payments, competitive situations; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not typically used for physical spatial falling (use "fall back" or "fall down" instead)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fall behind on (payments, work, studies)
   - fall behind with (tasks, obligations)
   - falling further behind
   - fallen behind
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Combines motion verb "fall" with spatial/temporal position "behind" to express loss of competitive position or progress
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *feallan* "to drop, descend" + Old English *behindan* "at the rear"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I fell behind in class when I was sick for two weeks.
   - We're falling behind on the mortgage payments this year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students who fall behind in the first semester often struggle to recover academically.
   - The company has fallen behind its competitors in market share and innovation.

1. **Expression**: the reason behind (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The underlying cause or explanation for something; the motivation or rationale
   - *Fixedness*: medium (strong collocation preference)
   
   **Synonyms**: the cause of, the explanation for, the motivation for, the rationale behind, why
   
   **Antonyms**: the result of, the consequence of, the effect of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when explaining causation, motivation, or rationale; common in analytical, explanatory contexts; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Slightly redundant with simpler "the reason for" in some contexts; avoid overuse in favor of variety
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - the reason behind [noun]
   - the reasons behind
   - the real reason behind
   - the main reason behind
   - understand the reason behind
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Behind" metaphorically positions causes as hidden or underlying, naturally pairing with "reason" to express uncovering explanations
   
   **Root Analysis**: Anglo-French *reisun* (from Latin *rationem*) + Old English *behindan*
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the reason behind all these new rules?
   - I never understood the reason behind his decision to quit.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This study examines the reasons behind consumer behavior changes.
   - The report analyzes the economic factors that constitute the reason behind the market decline.

1. **Expression**: hide behind (type: phrasal verb/collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal: To conceal oneself by positioning behind something
   - Figurative: To use something as an excuse or protection from criticism; to avoid responsibility by citing something
   - *Fixedness*: medium (phrasal verb with literal and figurative uses)
   
   **Synonyms**: shelter behind, take refuge in, use as an excuse, conceal oneself with, deflect with
   
   **Antonyms**: face directly, take responsibility, confront openly, own up to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing evasion of responsibility, using excuses, or literal concealment; figurative use often carries negative connotation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when straightforward "conceal" or "evade" would be clearer; figurative use can sound accusatory
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - hiding behind [excuse/policy/someone]
   - can't hide behind
   - refuse to hide behind
   - stop hiding behind
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Spatial verb "hide" naturally combines with spatial preposition "behind" for concealment; metaphorically extended to psychological/social evasion
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *hȳdan* "to conceal" + Old English *behindan* "at the rear"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't hide behind excuses—just admit you forgot.
   - He always hides behind company policy when he doesn't want to help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The administration cannot hide behind procedural technicalities to avoid accountability.
   - Organizations that hide behind legal immunity undermine public trust.

   **Examples [Casual]**:
