# Extract Verbs for Vocabulary Learning

**Task**: Extract verbs (action words, phrasal verbs, verb phrases) that are essential for language mastery from the source text.

**Definition**: Each entry focuses on one verb or phrasal verb with comprehensive learning information including meaning, usage context, synonyms, antonyms, etymology, and examples across registers.

**Rules**:

**Coverage:**
- Read entire source thoroughly—identify all verbs that are decision-critical for understanding the text
- Prioritize: academic verbs, professional terminology, phrasal verbs, and verbs with nuanced meanings
- Extract comprehensively to reflect source richness; apply 80/20 principle (focus on high-frequency and high-impact verbs)
- Do not skip sections; cover both explicit verbs and verb forms that carry semantic weight

**Fidelity:**
- Ground all information in verbs actually present in source
- Never invent verbs not in source
- Derive meaning from context when source provides it; supplement with standard dictionary definitions

**Focus:**
- Action verbs with specific, precise meanings
- Phrasal verbs (verb + preposition/adverb combinations)
- Verbs with multiple meanings or registers
- Technical or domain-specific verbs
- Verbs that are challenging for language learners

**Format:**
- **Verb**: [infinitive form] (include verb + particle for phrasal verbs)
- **Meaning**: Collins Dictionary style definition(s)
  - *If verb has multiple senses, number them clearly*
  - *Use simple, clear language to explain meaning*
  - *Include grammatical patterns (transitive/intransitive)*
- **Synonyms**: 3-5 words/phrases with similar meaning
- **Antonyms**: 2-4 words/phrases with opposite meaning (if applicable)
- **When to Use**: Context, situations, or registers where this verb is appropriate
- **When NOT to Use**: Contexts where this verb would be inappropriate or incorrect
- **Common Phrases**: 3-5 collocations or fixed expressions using this verb
- **Root Analysis**: Break down word components (prefix + root + suffix)
- **Etymology**: Language origin and historical development (brief)
- **Examples [Casual]**: 2 natural example sentences in informal register
- **Examples [Formal]**: 2 natural example sentences in formal/academic register

1. **Verb**: fall behind (phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To fail to maintain the required pace or schedule; to lag in progress compared to others or expectations (intransitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To become overdue in payments or obligations (intransitive, often + on/with)
   
   **Synonyms**: lag behind, drop back, slip back, lose ground, get behind
   
   **Antonyms**: keep up, catch up, stay ahead, maintain pace, advance
   
   **When to Use**: Use in contexts of competition, progress tracking, schedule adherence, academic performance, debt/payments; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not used for literal physical falling; don't confuse with "fall back" (retreat)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fall behind schedule
   - fall behind on payments
   - fall behind in class
   - fall behind the competition
   - fall further behind
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *feallan* "to drop" + be- (by) + hind (back) → phrasal verb construction
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *feallan* + *behindan* → Middle English phrasal verb development → Modern English "fall behind"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've fallen behind on my emails—there are hundreds unread.
   - Don't fall behind or you'll have trouble catching up later.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project has fallen behind schedule due to unforeseen complications.

1. **Verb**: lag behind (phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move or progress more slowly than others; to fail to keep pace (intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: fall behind, trail behind, drop back, move slowly, dawdle
   
   **Antonyms**: lead, advance, keep pace, surge ahead, outpace
   
   **When to Use**: Use in competitive contexts, progress comparisons, speed descriptions; often implies persistent slow progress; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less commonly used for sudden drops in progress (use "fall behind" instead); not for literal physical dragging
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lag behind competitors
   - lag behind in development
   - lag behind the curve
   - continue to lag behind
   - lag behind expectations
   
   **Root Analysis**: Origin uncertain (possibly Scandinavian) + be- (by) + hind (back) → phrasal verb
   
   **Etymology**: Lag (1500s, possibly from Norwegian *lagga* "to go slowly") + *behind* → Modern English phrasal verb "lag behind"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The little kid was lagging behind the group, so we slowed down.
   - Our internet speed lags behind what they promised.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The region continues to lag behind national averages in economic development.
   - Research suggests that the curriculum lags behind current industry practices.

1. **Verb**: stand behind (phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To support, endorse, or defend someone or something; to take responsibility for (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To be positioned at the back of someone or something (transitive/intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: support, back, endorse, uphold, defend, vouch for
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, abandon, denounce, distance from, undermine
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing loyalty, support, endorsement, or taking responsibility; positive connotation; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Literal spatial meaning less common than figurative; can sound overly formal in very casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stand behind a decision
   - stand behind someone 100%
   - fully stand behind
   - continue to stand behind
   - proud to stand behind
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *standan* "to be upright" + be- (by) + hind (back) → phrasal verb
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *standan* + *behindan* → Middle English → Modern English phrasal verb "stand behind" (support sense)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I stand behind everything I said in that meeting.
   - The whole team stands behind you on this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The administration stands behind its commitment to transparency and accountability.
   - We stand behind our research findings and welcome peer review.

1. **Verb**: hide behind (phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To use something as an excuse or shield from criticism; to avoid responsibility by citing something (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To conceal oneself by positioning behind something (transitive/intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: shelter behind, take refuge in, use as an excuse, evade via, deflect with
   
   **Antonyms**: face directly, confront, take responsibility, own up to, stand accountable
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing evasion of responsibility, using excuses, or literal concealment; figurative use often carries negative connotation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Figurative use can sound accusatory; avoid when neutral description is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hide behind excuses
   - hide behind policy
   - hide behind anonymity
   - can't hide behind
   - refuse to hide behind
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *hȳdan* "to conceal" + be- (by) + hind (back) → phrasal verb
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *hȳdan* + *behindan* → Middle English → Modern English phrasal verb with literal and figurative meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't hide behind excuses—just admit you made a mistake.
   - He's hiding behind his lawyer and won't talk to anyone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations cannot hide behind legal technicalities to evade ethical responsibilities.
   - The administration's tendency to hide behind procedural rules has eroded public trust.

   **Meaning**: 
   - To remain in a place after others have left; to not go with others (intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: remain, linger, wait, hang back, not leave
   
   **Antonyms**: leave, depart, go along, accompany, follow
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing voluntary or required remaining in a location; appropriate across all registers; common in school/work contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not typically used for permanent residence; use "stay" or "remain" for longer-term presence
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stay behind after class
   - stay behind to help
   - ask someone to stay behind
   - decide to stay behind
   - volunteer to stay behind
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old French *estare* (from Latin *stare* "to stand") + be- (by) + hind (back) → phrasal verb
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English *stayen* (from Old French) + *behindan* → Modern English phrasal verb "stay behind"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm staying behind to finish up—you guys go ahead.
   - The teacher asked me to stay behind to discuss my project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several staff members volunteered to stay behind to complete the inventory.
   - Students who stay behind for additional instruction often show marked improvement.
