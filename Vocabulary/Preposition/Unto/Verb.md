# Unto - Verb + Preposition Combinations

1. **Verb**: say unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To speak words to someone; archaic or biblical form of "say to" used when quoting or reporting speech directed toward a person or group.
   - *(Sense 2)* In solemn or poetic style, to declare something in a formal or weighty way.
   
   **Synonyms**: say to, tell, declare to, speak to, address
   
   **Antonyms**: remain silent toward, withhold speech from, conceal from
   
   **When to Use**: In biblical quotations, liturgy, or stylised narrative ("he said unto them"), especially when you want to echo older English rhythms.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In everyday speech or modern writing, use "say to" ("he said to them") instead; "say unto" sounds archaic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - he said unto them
   - I say unto you
   - thus saith unto the people
   - say unto my soul
   
   **Root Analysis**: say (Old English *secgan* – to speak) + unto (to, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Formula widely used in early English Bible translations ("Verily I say unto you"); persisted in religious and ceremonial language.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He read the old line out loud: "Verily I say unto you, fear not." 
   - In the play, the prophet turns and says unto the crowd, "Repent." 
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The liturgy begins, "Hear what the Lord saith unto His people." 
   - The text repeatedly uses the formula "I say unto you" to introduce authoritative teaching.

1. **Verb**: speak unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To address someone with words; archaic or elevated variant of "speak to".
   - *(Sense 2)* In devotional language, to address God in prayer or to expect God to address the heart.
   
   **Synonyms**: speak to, address, talk to, converse with
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, fall silent toward, refuse to address
   
   **When to Use**: In religious or poetic contexts ("the Lord spake unto Moses", "speak peace unto your people"), or when deliberately imitating that style.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For ordinary interactions, use "speak to" or "talk to"; "speak unto" is strongly marked as archaic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - speak peace unto your people
   - the Lord spake unto him
   - speak comfort unto Jerusalem
   - speak plainly unto us
   
   **Root Analysis**: speak (Old English *specan*) + unto (to, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Biblical and liturgical English favoured "spake unto" as past form; modern translations mostly replace with "spoke to".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The old text says, "He will speak peace unto His people and to His saints." 
   - The poem imagines the mountains speaking unto the valleys.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The oracle is described as one who speaks wisdom unto all who will listen.
   - In the vision narrative, a voice speaks unto him from the midst of the fire.

1. **Verb**: cry unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To call out loudly to someone, especially in distress, prayer, or urgent need.
   
   **Synonyms**: call out to, cry out to, plead with, appeal to, implore
   
   **Antonyms**: keep silent, hold back from calling, ignore (instead of appealing)
   
   **When to Use**: Common in Psalms and prayers ("cry unto the Lord", "they cried unto Him in their trouble") when describing desperate appeals directed unto a higher power.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In modern neutral prose, "cry out to" or "call out to" is less archaic. Avoid "cry unto" in light or humorous contexts unless you mean to parody the style.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cry unto the Lord
   - cry unto Him day and night
   - cry unto heaven
   - cry unto those in authority
   
   **Root Analysis**: cry (Old French *crier* – to shout) + unto (toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Rooted in older prayer and lament language where distress is directed unto God or rulers.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The characters cry unto heaven when they see the injustice.
   - In the song they cry unto God for mercy when all strength is gone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The chronicler records that the people cried unto the Lord in their affliction.
   - The petitioners cried unto the council for relief from oppressive taxes.

1. **Verb**: call unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To summon, invite, or appeal to someone; to ask them for help or attention.
   - *(Sense 2)* In theological language, to invoke God or to be summoned by God ("called unto holiness").
   
   **Synonyms**: call to, summon, appeal to, invoke, invite
   
   **Antonyms**: dismiss, send away, ignore, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Well‑established in older religious style ("call unto Me", "called unto His purpose") where the direction of the call matters.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For routine phone calls or informal invitations, "call" or "invite" without "unto" is normal.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - call unto Me and I will answer thee
   - called unto His kingdom
   - call unto the people
   - call unto your God
   
   **Root Analysis**: call (Old Norse *kalla* – to cry out) + unto (to, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Heavily used in older Bible translations; still recognisable but largely replaced by "call to" in contemporary English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The writer urges readers to call unto wisdom instead of their fears.
   - In the story, the prophet calls unto the crowd to turn back.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The text presents a divine voice that calls unto all nations to do justice.
   - They understood themselves as called unto a life of service beyond personal gain.

1. **Verb**: give unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give or grant something to someone; archaic or solemn form of "give to" used in formal declarations and scripture.
   
   **Synonyms**: give to, grant to, bestow on, present to, confer on
   
   **Antonyms**: withhold from, take from, deprive of, rob of
   
   **When to Use**: In ceremonial or religious style ("give unto the poor", "give unto Caesar the things which are Caesar’s") when emphasising the proper recipient.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In normal writing, "give to" is standard. "Give unto" should be reserved for intentional stylistic effect.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - give unto the poor
   - give unto each his due
   - give unto the Lord the glory due unto His name
   - give unto them eternal life
   
   **Root Analysis**: give (Old English *giefan*) + unto (to, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Formulaic in early modern English, especially in religious exhortations and legal‑sounding instructions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The verse keeps repeating, "Give unto others as has been given unto you." 
   - They agreed to give unto the project a share of every bonus.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The charter commands them to give unto each citizen the rights therein described.
   - The hymn calls worshippers to give unto the Lord the honour due unto His name.

1. **Verb**: render unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give back, pay, or return something that is owed; famously in the phrase "render unto Caesar".
   - *(Sense 2)* To deliver a decision or judgment unto someone.
   
   **Synonyms**: return to, give back to, pay to, deliver to, hand over to
   
   **Antonyms**: withhold from, retain, keep back from, deny to
   
   **When to Use**: Mostly in legal, religious, or rhetorical contexts where obligations are being assigned ("render unto each according to his works").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For everyday payments, "pay" or "give" is simpler. "Render unto" sounds formal and alludes to biblical language.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - render unto Caesar the things which are Caesar’s
   - render unto God the things that are God’s
   - render judgment unto the parties
   - render thanks unto the Lord
   
   **Root Analysis**: render (Old French *rendre* – to give back) + unto (to, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *reddere* (to give back) → French → English; cemented by its appearance in well‑known gospel passages.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He joked that tax season is when we render unto the government what is theirs.
   - The story ends with everyone trying to render unto each other what they are owed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court shall render judgment unto the parties in accordance with the evidence.
   - The sermon exhorted listeners to render unto God their hearts, not merely their words.

1. **Verb**: commit unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To entrust someone or something to another’s care, protection, or judgment.
   
   **Synonyms**: entrust to, commend to, place in the care of, hand over to
   
   **Antonyms**: keep back from, withdraw from, abandon without care
   
   **When to Use**: Often appears in blessings or formal closings ("commit you unto God", "commit this work unto your hands") where responsibility is being placed on or entrusted unto someone.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In everyday project assignments, "assign" or "hand over" may sound more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - commit you unto God
   - commit this cause unto your judgment
   - commit their souls unto a faithful Creator
   - commit the work unto their hands
   
   **Root Analysis**: com‑ (together) + mit (send) → commit + unto (to, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *committere* (to join, entrust) → English "commit"; pairing with "unto" is typical of older benedictions and formal letters.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In the letter he writes, "I commit this matter unto your wisdom." 
   - She felt ready to commit the project unto a younger team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The presiding officer concluded, "We now commit this work unto your careful stewardship." 
   - The blessing reads, "We commit you unto God and to the word of His grace."

1. **Verb**: deliver unto
   **Meaning**: 
   - To hand over or transfer someone or something to another, sometimes into their power or judgment.
   - *(Sense 2)* In theological language, to hand over to consequences, punishment, or protection.
   
   **Synonyms**: hand over to, give up to, consign to, entrust to, transfer to
   
   **Antonyms**: rescue from, withhold from, retain, keep
   
   **When to Use**: Appears in solemn narrative or legal style ("delivered unto the authorities", "delivered unto death", "delivered unto safety") where the recipient’s role is critical.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For everyday parcel delivery, simply use "deliver to"; "unto" would sound theatrical.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deliver unto the judge
   - deliver unto death
   - deliver unto Satan (in certain passages)
   - deliver unto safety
   
   **Root Analysis**: de‑ (from) + liver (from Latin *liberare* – set free, or *līberāre* via Old French *delivrer*) + unto (to, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *delivrer* (to set free, hand over); in older English often used transitively with "unto" to mark the party receiving control.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The old story says he was delivered unto his enemies because he would not listen.
   - In the drama, the letter is finally delivered unto the rightful heir.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The accused was delivered unto the civil authorities for trial.
   - The text claims that those who turn back will be delivered unto the consequences of their own choices.
