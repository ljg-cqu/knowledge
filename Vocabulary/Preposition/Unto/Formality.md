# Unto - Formality & Register Variants

## Expression 1: "Peace be unto you."

- **Core meaning**: A formal blessing or greeting that wishes deep inner calm, safety, and well‑being to the person addressed.

### Casual Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Hope you’re at peace."  
  - "Wishing you peace today."  
  - "Hope things feel calmer for you soon."
- **Use when**: 
  - Speaking to friends or family.
  - Offering comfort in messages or chats without sounding archaic.
- **Tone**: Warm, gentle, supportive; explicitly avoids religious or ceremonial weight unless context already has it.

### Semi‑Formal Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "I wish you peace and rest."  
  - "May you find peace in the coming days."  
  - "Sending you calm and strength as you go through this."
- **Use when**: 
  - Writing condolence or support emails.  
  - Speaking in small groups, support meetings, or reflective settings where a respectful tone is needed.
- **Tone**: Respectful, sincere, slightly elevated, but still modern.

### Formal Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Peace be unto you and your household."  
  - "May peace be unto you in body, mind, and spirit."  
  - "Peace be unto all who hear these words."
- **Use when**: 
  - Leading prayers, ceremonies, or memorial services.  
  - Writing liturgical or devotional texts.
- **Tone**: Ceremonial, explicitly echoing scriptural language; suited to contexts where religious language is expected.

### Humorous Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Peace be unto you—and to your inbox."  
  - "Peace be unto you (and may your deadlines mysteriously disappear)."  
  - "Peace be unto whoever finally fixes this bug."
- **Use when**: 
  - Lightening the mood among friends or close colleagues.  
  - Playfully echoing religious style without mocking the core idea of peace.
- **Tone**: Playful, slightly dramatic; should only be used with people who will recognise the joke.

### Lively / Expressive Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "May calm just wash over you right now."  
  - "Here’s a big wave of peace coming your way."  
  - "Let peace crash into your day like cool water."
- **Use when**: 
  - Encouraging someone who feels overwhelmed.  
  - Speaking in informal talks, youth groups, or creative writing.
- **Tone**: Vivid, emotional, image‑rich; less formal but strongly supportive.

### Empathetic Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "I can hear how heavy this feels—may real peace be with you tonight."  
  - "I’m hoping you find some quiet and peace in the middle of all this."  
  - "You deserve a deep, steady peace after everything you’ve carried."
- **Use when**: 
  - Sitting with grief, anxiety, or burnout.  
  - Writing personal notes of care where you want to stay close to the person’s feelings.
- **Tone**: Soft, compassionate, focused on the listener’s experience.

### Register Selection Guide (Expression 1)
- **Everyday supportive text** → Casual or Empathetic.  
- **Condolence email** → Semi‑Formal or Empathetic, avoiding insider religious phrases unless invited.  
- **Religious service / liturgy** → Formal original form "Peace be unto you" fits well.  
- **Humorous riff in trusted group** → Humorous, but only where shared background allows playful use of sacred‑sounding language.

## Expression 2: "Woe be unto you."

- **Core meaning**: A strong prophetic‑style warning that serious trouble or moral consequences will fall on someone who acts wrongly.

### Casual Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "You’re going to be in big trouble if you do that."  
  - "That’s going to come back to bite you."  
  - "You’re asking for serious problems there."
- **Use when**: 
  - Warning friends about clear risks (breaking rules, ignoring health, etc.).  
  - Commenting on poor decisions in everyday conversation.
- **Tone**: Direct, concerned, but not theatrical.

### Semi‑Formal Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "There will be serious consequences if this continues."  
  - "This course of action is likely to bring significant trouble."  
  - "Persisting in this path will not end well."
- **Use when**: 
  - Giving professional or pastoral warnings.  
  - Writing reports or letters that must be clear but not melodramatic.
- **Tone**: Measured, warning, focused on consequences.

### Formal Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Woe be unto those who profit from the suffering of the poor."  
  - "Woe be unto the city that forgets justice."  
  - "Woe be unto him that trusts in violence rather than in peace."
- **Use when**: 
  - Preaching, prophetic writing, or rhetorical speeches that deliberately echo biblical style.  
  - Literary or dramatic contexts where a solemn denunciation is appropriate.
- **Tone**: Gravely rhetorical, morally charged.

### Humorous Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Woe be unto whoever touches my snacks without asking!"  
  - "Woe be unto you if you send that email before coffee."  
  - "Woe be unto the one who unplugs the router mid‑download."
- **Use when**: 
  - Joking with peers about small annoyances.  
  - Lightly warning friends, where exaggeration is clearly comic.
- **Tone**: Dramatic for fun; uses the archaic pattern as a joke.

### Lively / Expressive Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Disaster is basically waving at you from down that road."  
  - "You’re running straight toward a brick wall with that plan."  
  - "This path is lit up with warning signs—everywhere." 
- **Use when**: 
  - Trying to shake someone awake to risk in a vivid, image‑rich way.
- **Tone**: Energetic, metaphorical; strong but not necessarily religious.

### Empathetic Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "I’m really worried this decision will hurt you later."  
  - "I care about you, and I’m afraid this could lead to serious pain."  
  - "It’s hard to say, but I think this choice may bring consequences you don’t want."
- **Use when**: 
  - You want to warn someone clearly while preserving the relationship.  
  - Difficult conversations where tone must stay caring, not condemning.
- **Tone**: Gentle, person‑centred, mixing concern with honesty.

### Register Selection Guide (Expression 2)
- **Close friend making risky choice** → Empathetic or Casual.  
- **Formal report / policy** → Semi‑Formal warnings about consequences.  
- **Prophetic or literary speech** → Formal "Woe be unto…" can carry thematic weight.  
- **Playful exaggeration** → Humorous, in safe, informal spaces.

## Expression 3: "I say unto you…"

- **Core meaning**: A solemn way of introducing an important statement or teaching; signals personal authority and emphasis.

### Casual Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Let me be honest with you…"  
  - "I’m telling you, …"  
  - "Seriously, here’s what I think…"
- **Use when**: 
  - Emphasising a point to friends or peers.  
  - Framing a personal opinion or warning in conversation.
- **Tone**: Direct, familiar, informal.

### Semi‑Formal Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Allow me to say this clearly:"  
  - "I want to state this as plainly as I can:"  
  - "Let me emphasise the following point:" 
- **Use when**: 
  - Presenting in meetings, classes, or written reports where you need emphasis without sounding archaic.
- **Tone**: Clear, professional, slightly elevated.

### Formal Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Verily I say unto you…"  
  - "Truly I say unto you…"  
  - "I say unto you that this principle must not be ignored."
- **Use when**: 
  - Quoting or echoing biblical discourse.  
  - Writing pastiche, sermons, or literary monologues.
- **Tone**: Strongly scriptural, authoritative, ceremonial.

### Humorous Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "Verily I say unto you: this coffee is a miracle."  
  - "I say unto you, whoever finishes the snacks must buy the next round."  
  - "Lo, I say unto you: save your work early and often."
- **Use when**: 
  - Playing with old‑fashioned style to add humour to everyday comments.
- **Tone**: Light, ironic, affectionate parody of solemn speech.

### Lively / Expressive Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "I need you to really hear this…"  
  - "Please let this sink in:"  
  - "From my heart, I want to say this to you:" 
- **Use when**: 
  - Emphasising heartfelt advice or encouragement.
- **Tone**: Emotional, engaging, more about connection than authority.

### Empathetic Variants
- **Forms**: 
  - "As someone who cares about you, I need to say this:"  
  - "From a place of concern, I want to share something hard to hear:"  
  - "Gently, but honestly, I have to tell you:" 
- **Use when**: 
  - Offering difficult feedback or truths in a caring relationship.
- **Tone**: Soft but firm, mixing clarity with emotional safety.

### Register Selection Guide (Expression 3)
- **Serious advice to a friend** → Lively or Empathetic.  
- **Meetings / presentations** → Semi‑Formal framing like "Allow me to say this clearly:".  
- **Scriptural echo / literary effect** → Formal "I say unto you" or "Verily I say unto you".  
- **Joking among friends** → Humorous "Verily I say unto you…" style.
