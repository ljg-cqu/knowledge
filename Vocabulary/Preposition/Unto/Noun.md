# Unto - Related Nouns

1. **Noun**: glory (U/C, pl: glories)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Great honour, praise, or admiration given to someone, especially for achievements or character.
   - *(Sense 2)* The majesty, radiance, or perfection attributed to God or a divine being.
   
   **Synonyms**: honour/honor, praise, splendour/splendor, majesty, renown, exaltation
   
   **Antonyms**: shame, disgrace, dishonour/dishonor, humiliation, obscurity
   
   **When to Use**: Often used with "unto" in religious or ceremonial language ("glory be unto God", "to the glory unto His name") to ascribe praise or honour to a divine recipient. Also used more generally to describe the highest credit or praise directed unto a person, group, or cause.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In ordinary conversation about simple satisfaction or success, words like "pride", "credit", or "praise" are more natural. Overusing "glory unto" in everyday contexts sounds archaic or overly grand.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - give glory unto God
   - glory be unto the Father/Son/Spirit
   - sing glory and praise unto His name
   - ascribe glory unto the Lord
   - glory unto all who served
   
   **Root Analysis**: glor (fame, praise) + -y (noun suffix forming abstract nouns)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *gloria* (fame, renown, praise) → Old French *gloire* → Middle English *glorie* → modern "glory"; strongly associated with religious and ceremonial language.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In the old hymn they kept singing, "Glory be unto God in the highest!"
   - The documentary gave glory unto the unsung workers behind the scenes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The inscription dedicates the monument "to the glory unto all who laid down their lives in service".
   - In the text, every victory is portrayed as glory unto the king rather than to his generals.

1. **Noun**: mercy (U, pl: mercies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Kindness, compassion, or forgiveness shown toward someone who is in one's power or who may deserve punishment.
   - *(Sense 2)* A relief or blessing that feels like compassionate help.
   
   **Synonyms**: compassion, clemency, leniency, kindness, pity, forgiveness
   
   **Antonyms**: severity, cruelty, harshness, judgment, condemnation
   
   **When to Use**: Strongly associated with prayers or appeals "unto" a higher power ("cry for mercy unto the Lord", "show mercy unto them"). Use when describing compassionate restraint in the face of deserved punishment.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For ordinary favours or help, "help", "support", or "assistance" may be more accurate. "Mercy" implies a power imbalance and potential punishment or suffering.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - show mercy unto
   - cry for mercy unto God
   - according to His tender mercies
   - have mercy upon/unto us
   - extend mercy unto the poor
   
   **Root Analysis**: merc (reward, wages) + -y (abstract noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *merci* (reward, grace, pity) ← Latin *merces* (wages, reward); sense shifted toward compassion, especially in Christian texts ("mercy unto you, and peace, and love, be multiplied").
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In the story they begged for mercy unto the king, hoping he would spare them.
   - The charity tries to turn compassion into real mercy unto families in crisis.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The prayer liturgy repeatedly asks that mercy be shown unto those who suffer injustice.
   - Historical sermons often portray disasters as warnings calling people to seek mercy unto God.

1. **Noun**: obedience (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of doing what you are told to do or what a rule, law, or authority requires; willing submission to commands or guidance.
   
   **Synonyms**: compliance, submission, conformity, allegiance, dutifulness, faithfulness
   
   **Antonyms**: disobedience, rebellion, defiance, resistance, insubordination
   
   **When to Use**: Frequently occurs with "unto" in religious or ethical contexts ("obedience unto God", "obedience unto your calling") to emphasise directed loyalty or submission to a person or principle.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral work situations, "following instructions" or "complying with policy" may sound less loaded. "Obedience unto" in everyday business writing can sound moralistic or archaic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - obedience unto God/Christ
   - walk in obedience unto His commands
   - total obedience unto the call
   - a life of obedience unto the truth
   
   **Root Analysis**: ob- (towards) + audi (hear, listen) + -ence (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *oboedientia* (willingness to listen, obey) → Old French → Middle English; literal idea is "listening toward" someone, then acting accordingly.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The book is all about learning obedience unto your conscience instead of just following the crowd.
   - In that community they talk a lot about obedience unto God's leading.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The rule of life calls members to sustained obedience unto the teachings they profess.
   - In the covenant text, blessings are linked to obedience unto the revealed law.

1. **Noun**: praise (U/C, pl: praises)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressions of approval, admiration, or gratitude spoken or sung about someone or something.
   - *(Sense 2)* In religious contexts, the verbal or musical honour given to God.
   
   **Synonyms**: acclaim, commendation, worship, adoration, applause, blessing (religious)
   
   **Antonyms**: criticism, blame, reproach, condemnation, complaint
   
   **When to Use**: Very common with "unto" in hymns and liturgy ("sing praise unto His name", "praise unto the Lord"). Use when emphasising directed verbal honour.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In everyday talk about simple compliments, "praise" can sound formal; phrases like "say something nice" or "give credit" may fit better. "Praise unto" is almost entirely religious or poetic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sing praise unto the Lord
   - lift up praise unto His name
   - offer praise and thanksgiving unto God
   - praise unto our King
   
   **Root Analysis**: prais(e) (from Latin *pretiare* - to value, prize)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *preisier* (to prize, value) → Middle English *preisen* → "praise"; sense narrowed toward verbal admiration and worship.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The chorus kept repeating, "All praise be unto You forever".
   - They wrote a song of praise unto the people who had supported them.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The dedication offers praise unto all whose unseen labour made the work possible.
   - In the psalm, praise is continually directed unto the Lord for His steadfast love.

1. **Noun**: judgment (C/U, pl: judgments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of forming an opinion or making a decision after careful thought.
   - *(Sense 2)* A formal decision given by a court; a legal ruling.
   - *(Sense 3)* In religious language, God’s decision about people’s actions and their consequences.
   
   **Synonyms**: decision, verdict, ruling, assessment, evaluation, sentence
   
   **Antonyms**: mercy (in contrastive religious pairs), acquittal, forgiveness, leniency, indecision
   
   **When to Use**: With "unto" often in solemn, legal, or biblical style ("judgment unto condemnation", "judgment unto life"), marking the result or direction of a decision.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual speech about opinions, "view" or "take" may be easier; the pairing "unto judgment" sounds very formal and theological.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unto judgment and condemnation
   - bring judgment unto victory
   - reserved unto judgment
   - judgment unto life
   
   **Root Analysis**: judg(e) (from Latin *judicare* - to decide) + -ment (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *jugement* → Middle English; used heavily in legal and religious registers, especially in phrases like "day of judgment" and "unto judgment".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The story turns from mercy unto judgment when the character keeps ignoring every warning.
   - Some passages talk about deeds being kept unto the day of judgment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The text contrasts a sentence of judgment unto death with a promise of grace unto life.
   - Certain actions are described as storing up wrath unto the day of righteous judgment.

1. **Noun**: covenant (C, pl: covenants)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A formal and serious agreement or promise between two or more parties.
   - *(Sense 2)* In religious writing, a solemn agreement between God and humans, with stated promises and responsibilities.
   
   **Synonyms**: agreement, compact, pact, treaty, contract, bond
   
   **Antonyms**: breach, violation, breaking of agreement, annulment
   
   **When to Use**: Often appears with "unto" to mark direction or parties involved ("covenant unto their children", "promise unto you and your seed"). Use when a relationship of binding commitment is being described in elevated or theological language.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In ordinary business English, "contract" or "agreement" is more standard. "Covenant unto" feels specifically religious, historical, or legal‑ceremonial.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - covenant unto you and your children
   - establish My covenant unto them
   - covenant of peace unto the people
   - swear a covenant unto the Lord
   
   **Root Analysis**: co- (together) + ven (come) + -ant (noun/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *covenant* (agreement) ← Latin *convenire* (to come together, agree). Adopted into English especially through biblical and legal language.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The story describes a covenant unto future generations, promising protection if they stay faithful.
   - They spoke of marriage as a covenant unto one another, not just a legal paper.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The charter is framed as a covenant unto all citizens, outlining mutual obligations and protections.
   - In the passage, the covenant is said to extend unto their seed after them in every generation.

1. **Noun**: blessing (C/U, pl: blessings)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that brings happiness, help, or benefit; a favour received.
   - *(Sense 2)* A prayer asking for divine favour and protection.
   
   **Synonyms**: favour/favor, gift, benefit, grace, boon, benediction
   
   **Antonyms**: curse, burden, misfortune, calamity
   
   **When to Use**: Naturally paired with "unto" when describing prayers or benefits directed to someone ("speak blessing unto your children", "a blessing unto the nations"). Emphasises the direction of good toward a recipient.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For neutral advantages (discounts, small conveniences), "benefit" or "advantage" may fit better. "Blessing unto" belongs mostly in spiritual, emotional, or highly appreciative contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - be a blessing unto others
   - pronounce a blessing unto them
   - make you a blessing unto many
   - blessing unto all nations
   
   **Root Analysis**: bless (Old English *blētsian* - to consecrate) + -ing (noun of action/result)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *blētsian* (mark with blood, consecrate) → "bless"; "blessing" came to mean both the act of invoking favour and the favour itself.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She tried to be a blessing unto everyone who crossed her path.
   - That teacher was a real blessing unto me during a hard season of life.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The foundation aims to become a lasting blessing unto the communities it serves.
   - In the text, the promise is that they will be a blessing unto all families of the earth.
