# Unto - Related Adjectives

1. **Adjective**: faithful (comp: more faithful, sup: most faithful)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Firm in loyalty, trust, or belief; continuing to support or obey someone or something over time.
   - *(Sense 2)* In religious use, loyal in love and obedience unto God or a calling, even in hardship.
   
   **Synonyms**: loyal, devoted, steadfast, constant, trustworthy, reliable
   
   **Antonyms**: unfaithful, disloyal, fickle, inconsistent, unreliable
   
   **When to Use**: Common with "unto" in elevated style ("faithful unto death", "faithful unto the end") to emphasise persistent loyalty directed toward a person, vow, or God.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In everyday talk about small preferences, "loyal" or "reliable" may sound more natural. "Faithful unto" in casual contexts can sound overly dramatic or archaic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - faithful unto death
   - faithful unto the end
   - faithful unto His word
   - faithful unto their calling
   - remain faithful unto you
   
   **Root Analysis**: faith (trust, belief) + -ful (full of / characterised by)
   
   **Etymology**: From "faith" (via Old French *feid* ← Latin *fides*, trust) + English suffix "-ful"; long associated with covenant loyalty in religious texts ("be thou faithful unto death").
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The story is about a small group that stayed faithful unto their promise when everyone else gave up.
   - She tried to be faithful unto what she believed was right, even when it cost her.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The inscription honours those who remained faithful unto their duty in the face of grave danger.
   - The text commends a community that held faithful unto its confession through many generations.

1. **Adjective**: righteous (non‑gradable in moral sense; sometimes gradable in everyday use)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Morally right or just; acting in accordance with what is considered just and good.
   - *(Sense 2)* In theology, accepted as being in the right relationship with God.
   
   **Synonyms**: just, upright, virtuous, blameless, principled, ethical
   
   **Antonyms**: wicked, unjust, corrupt, immoral, guilty
   
   **When to Use**: With "unto" in religious or moral language ("righteous unto all generations", "counted righteous unto him") to emphasise the direction or scope of right standing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral business or technical contexts, "fair" or "just" may be more appropriate. "Righteous unto" is strongly coloured by biblical style.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - counted righteous unto him
   - righteous unto all generations
   - righteous acts unto the poor
   - righteous judgment unto the nations
   
   **Root Analysis**: right (morally correct) + ‑eous (adjectival ending, from Old French)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *rihtwis* (right‑wise) → Middle English *rightuous* → "righteous"; heavily used in older English Bibles, often appearing with "unto".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The tale paints him as righteous unto the end, even when nobody believed him.
   - They wanted laws that would be righteous unto both rich and poor alike.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The passage speaks of a judgment that is righteous unto all peoples without partiality.
   - Their reform was seen as an attempt to make the system more righteous unto future generations.

1. **Adjective**: steadfast (non‑gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Firm, fixed, and unchanging in purpose, loyalty, or belief; not shaken by difficulty or time.
   
   **Synonyms**: unwavering, resolute, constant, firm, unshakable, loyal
   
   **Antonyms**: wavering, fickle, inconstant, unstable, changeable
   
   **When to Use**: Typically used with "unto" in high style ("steadfast unto the end", "steadfast unto the hope") to stress endurance directed toward a goal or person.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For casual praise like "reliable coworker", simpler adjectives may suffice. "Steadfast unto" sounds formal and often spiritual.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - steadfast unto the end
   - steadfast unto the hope set before us
   - steadfast unto their promise
   - remain steadfast unto the truth
   
   **Root Analysis**: stead (place, position) + fast (fixed, firm)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *stedefæst* (firm in its place) → "steadfast"; metaphorically extended to loyalty and commitment.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The novel shows a friendship that stays steadfast unto old age.
   - He tried to be steadfast unto what he had once promised.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The covenant called them to be steadfast unto their vows, regardless of shifting circumstances.
   - Her conduct remained steadfast unto the principles she had publicly affirmed.

1. **Adjective**: worthy (comp: worthier, sup: worthiest)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Deserving respect, honour, or some particular treatment.
   - *(Sense 2)* In religious writing, deserving praise or acceptance before God ("worthy unto all acceptation").
   
   **Synonyms**: deserving, meritorious, honourable, commendable, fit
   
   **Antonyms**: unworthy, undeserving, shameful, base, dishonourable
   
   **When to Use**: Appears with "unto" in elevated style ("worthy unto all acceptation", "worthy unto the calling") to describe suitability or honour directed toward a standard or role.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In normal performance reviews or feedback, "good", "strong", or "excellent" are more natural. "Worthy unto" will sound biblical or archaic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - worthy unto all acceptation
   - walk worthy unto the Lord
   - counted worthy unto this calling
   - worthy unto the task given
   
   **Root Analysis**: worth (value) + ‑y (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *weorþe* (valuable, honourable) → "worthy"; long used in formulas like "worthy of honour" and, in older translations, "worthy unto".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The story says they tried to live lives worthy unto the trust people placed in them.
   - She wanted her work to be worthy unto the sacrifices her family had made.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The charge is to walk worthy unto the vocation with which one has been called.
   - The award is given only to those whose service is judged worthy unto this distinction.

1. **Adjective**: humble (comp: humbler, sup: humblest)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having or showing a modest opinion of one’s importance; not proud or arrogant.
   - *(Sense 2)* Willingly lowly or submissive in attitude, especially before God or a higher authority.
   
   **Synonyms**: modest, meek, lowly, unassuming, submissive
   
   **Antonyms**: proud, arrogant, haughty, self‑important, conceited
   
   **When to Use**: With "unto" in devotional language ("humble unto God", "humble and obedient unto His will") to describe an attitude directed toward a higher authority.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral performance language, "modest" or "down‑to‑earth" may be better. "Humble unto" is strongly religious or poetic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - humble unto God
   - humble and obedient unto His commands
   - remain humble unto those you serve
   - humble unto the truth
   
   **Root Analysis**: hum- (earth, ground) + ‑ble (adjectival suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *humilis* (low, on the ground) ← *humus* (earth) → Old French → English; symbolism of bending low "unto" someone of higher rank is built into the image.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The mentor told them to stay humble unto the work, no matter how much praise came.
   - In the story he learns to be humble unto the people he once ignored.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The rule of life calls members to be humble unto God and gentle toward one another.
   - Her speech remained humble unto the contributions of earlier scholars.

1. **Adjective**: obedient (comp: more obedient, sup: most obedient)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Willing to obey; doing what one is told or what rules and instructions require.
   - *(Sense 2)* In theological language, fully submitted unto God’s will or a sacred calling.
   
   **Synonyms**: compliant, submissive, dutiful, responsive, docile
   
   **Antonyms**: disobedient, rebellious, defiant, insubordinate, resistant
   
   **When to Use**: Often paired with "unto" when the direction of obedience matters ("obedient unto death", "obedient unto the vision"). Emphasises how far the obedience extends.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In modern leadership or coaching, softer terms like "co‑operative" may be preferred. "Obedient unto" can sound harsh or old‑fashioned outside religious or historical texts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - obedient unto death
   - obedient unto the heavenly vision
   - obedient unto His commands
   - remain obedient unto the call
   
   **Root Analysis**: ob‑ (toward) + audi (hear) + ‑ent (present‑participle adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *oboedire* (to listen, obey) → *oboediens* (obedient) → Old French → English; linked historically to listening attentively unto someone.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The legend tells of a servant obedient unto every word of his master, even when it hurt.
   - She tried to be obedient unto the quiet sense of conscience inside.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The narrative portrays him as obedient unto death, refusing to renounce what he believed.
   - The order expects its members to be obedient unto the rule they freely accept.

1. **Adjective**: merciful (comp: more merciful, sup: most merciful)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Showing mercy; willing to forgive or to be kind to people in difficulty or who have done wrong.
   
   **Synonyms**: compassionate, forgiving, lenient, kindly, gracious
   
   **Antonyms**: merciless, cruel, harsh, unforgiving, severe
   
   **When to Use**: Frequently modified by "unto" in religious language ("merciful unto all who call upon Him", "merciful unto the poor") to highlight the direction of compassion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In neutral contexts, "kind" or "understanding" may sound less theological. "Merciful unto" belongs mostly to devotional or literary registers.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - merciful unto all who seek Him
   - merciful unto the weak
   - merciful unto sinners
   - be merciful unto me
   
   **Root Analysis**: mercy (compassion) + ‑ful (full of)
   
   **Etymology**: From "mercy" (Old French *merci*) + "-ful"; spread widely through prayers and psalms ("be merciful unto me, O God").
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The song asks, "Be merciful unto us when we fail." 
   - He tried to be merciful unto people who had made the same mistakes he once made.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The liturgy describes a judge who is both just and merciful unto those who turn back from wrongdoing.
   - Historical petitions often appeal to rulers to be merciful unto their subjects in times of famine.
