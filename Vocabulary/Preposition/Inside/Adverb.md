1. **Adverb**: inside (place)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In or into the interior of a place or container; within the bounds or limits of something
   - *(Sense 2)* Indoors rather than outside
   - *(Sense 3)* (Informal) In prison
   
   **Synonyms**: indoors, within, internally, inward, in
   
   **Antonyms**: outside, outdoors, externally, outward, out
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe location or movement toward the interior; appropriate in all registers; can appear at end or beginning of sentences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish from "inside of" (preposition); as adverb, doesn't require "of"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - come inside, go inside, stay inside
   - look inside, deep inside, locked inside
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (within) + side (location)
   
   **Etymology**: in + side → inside → compound adverb indicating interior location or direction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's cold out here—let's go inside.
   - The cat wants to come inside.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were directed to wait inside until the session commenced.
   - The structural damage is more extensive inside than initially assessed.

1. **Adverb**: inward (manner/direction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Toward the inside or center; in the direction of the interior
   - *(Sense 2)* Into or toward one's own thoughts, feelings, or mind; internally focused
   
   **Synonyms**: inwardly, internally, inside, toward the center, introspectively
   
   **Antonyms**: outward, outwardly, externally, away from center
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe direction toward interior or mental focus toward self; can modify verbs or appear as sentence adverb; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Often interchangeable with "inwards" in British English, but "inward" is standard in American English
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - turn inward, face inward, curl inward
   - look inward, reflect inward, focus inward
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (within) + -ward (direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English inweard → inward → directional adverb toward inside or self
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The door swings inward.
   - Sometimes you need to look inward for answers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis requires scholars to turn inward, examining their own interpretive assumptions.
   - Pressure differentials cause the membrane to deflect inward.

1. **Adverb**: inwardly (manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - On the inside; in one's mind or heart rather than outwardly or publicly
   - *(Sense 2)* Toward the inside or interior (less common than metaphorical usage)
   
   **Synonyms**: internally, privately, secretly, mentally, to oneself
   
   **Antonyms**: outwardly, externally, publicly, openly, visibly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe mental or emotional states not expressed externally; emphasizes contrast between internal feelings and external appearance; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common for physical direction; use "inward" for directional meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - smile inwardly, groan inwardly, rejoice inwardly
   - inwardly focused, inwardly pleased, curse inwardly
   
   **Root Analysis**: inward + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: inward + -ly → inwardly → manner adverb emphasizing internal mental/emotional states
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was annoyed but smiled inwardly.
   - She groaned inwardly at the thought.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The diplomat maintained composure despite inwardly questioning the decision's wisdom.
   - Participants reported experiencing anxiety inwardly while projecting confidence externally.

1. **Adverb**: internally (manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - On or from the inside; in a way that relates to the internal workings or structure
   - *(Sense 2)* Within an organization, country, or system rather than externally
   - *(Sense 3)* Within one's mind or body rather than outwardly
   
   **Synonyms**: inside, inwardly, within, domestically, privately
   
   **Antonyms**: externally, outside, outwardly, publicly, internationally
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or technical contexts for internal processes, organizational matters, or mental states; more formal than "inside"; common in business, medical, and technical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "inside" or "inwardly" for everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - internally consistent, managed internally, resolved internally
   - bleed internally, process internally, review internally
   
   **Root Analysis**: internal + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: internal + -ly → internally → formal adverb for internal processes and structures
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They decided to handle the problem internally.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization resolved disputes internally through established mediation procedures.
   - The injury caused internal bleeding that was not immediately visible externally.

1. **Adverb**: deeply (degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Far down or in; at or to a great depth below a surface or far inside something
   - *(Sense 2)* Intensely or profoundly; to a great degree emotionally or intellectually
   - *(Sense 3)* With intensity of color, sound, or quality
   
   **Synonyms**: profoundly, far inside, intensely, thoroughly, greatly
   
   **Antonyms**: shallowly, superficially, slightly, minimally, barely
   
   **When to Use**: Use to intensify verbs or adjectives, indicating great depth (physical or metaphorical); appropriate in all registers; highly versatile
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish between physical depth and metaphorical intensity meanings
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deeply embedded, deeply rooted, deeply concerned
   - breathe deeply, think deeply, care deeply
   
   **Root Analysis**: deep + -ly (manner/degree suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English deop + -ly → deeply → maintained dual meaning of physical depth and metaphorical intensity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I care deeply about this issue.
   - The nail was driven deeply into the wood.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Cultural assumptions are deeply embedded in institutional practices.
   - The analysis examines deeply rooted structural inequalities.

1. **Adverb**: thoroughly (degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely and with great attention to detail; in every part or aspect; through and through
   - *(Sense 2)* To the fullest extent; absolutely or entirely
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, entirely, fully, comprehensively, exhaustively
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, incompletely, superficially, hastily, carelessly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize completeness or intensity of an action; implies attention to all internal aspects; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can emphasize negative qualities (thoroughly annoying); be mindful of context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - thoroughly examine, thoroughly clean, thoroughly understand
   - thoroughly enjoyed, thoroughly convinced, thoroughly mixed
   
   **Root Analysis**: thorough + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: thorough (through + variant spelling) + -ly → thoroughly → completeness through all parts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I thoroughly enjoyed the movie.
   - Make sure you clean it thoroughly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee thoroughly examined all submitted proposals before rendering decisions.
   - Specimens must be thoroughly rinsed to eliminate contaminating residues.

1. **Adverb**: intrinsically (manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an essential or natural way; as an inherent or inseparable part of something's nature
   - *(Sense 2)* From within rather than from external factors
   
   **Synonyms**: inherently, essentially, fundamentally, naturally, by nature
   
   **Antonyms**: extrinsically, externally, artificially, superficially
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or academic contexts to emphasize inherent, essential qualities; emphasizes internal nature; common in philosophy, science, and analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "naturally" or "essentially" for everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - intrinsically motivated, intrinsically valuable, intrinsically linked
   - intrinsically good, intrinsically interesting, intrinsically flawed
   
   **Root Analysis**: intrinsic + -ally (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: intrinsic + -ally → intrinsically → adverb emphasizing inherent, internal nature
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The work is intrinsically rewarding.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology is intrinsically limited by its reliance on self-reported data.
   - Human dignity is considered intrinsically valuable rather than instrumentally useful.

1. **Adverb**: intimately (manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that involves close personal knowledge or deep familiarity; from close inside knowledge
   - *(Sense 2)* In a private, personal, or sexual way
   - *(Sense 3)* In a close, detailed way; thoroughly
   
   **Synonyms**: closely, deeply, personally, thoroughly, privately
   
   **Antonyms**: distantly, superficially, formally, remotely, casually
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize deep personal knowledge or close connection from inside experience; appropriate in all registers but be mindful of sexual connotations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be aware of potentially sexual connotations in some contexts; use carefully
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - intimately familiar, intimately connected, intimately involved
   - know intimately, intimately related, intimately linked
   
   **Root Analysis**: intimate + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin intimus (innermost) + -ly → intimately → from innermost knowledge or closest connection
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She knows the city intimately.
   - The two issues are intimately connected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The researcher was intimately familiar with the community through years of ethnographic fieldwork.
   - Economic and environmental sustainability are intimately linked in contemporary development discourse.

1. **Adverb**: secretly (manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a secret way; without others knowing; kept hidden inside from public knowledge
   - *(Sense 2)* Privately within one's own mind or heart
   
   **Synonyms**: privately, covertly, clandestinely, surreptitiously, inwardly
   
   **Antonyms**: openly, publicly, overtly, obviously, transparently
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe actions or feelings kept hidden from others; implies deliberate concealment; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply wrongdoing or deception; use "privately" for more neutral contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - secretly hoping, secretly pleased, secretly worried
   - meet secretly, plan secretly, operate secretly
   
   **Root Analysis**: secret + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: secret (Latin secretus "separated, hidden") + -ly → secretly → in a hidden manner
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was secretly hoping she'd say yes.
   - They met secretly to plan the surprise.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization operated secretly, concealing activities from regulatory oversight.
   - Participants privately harbored doubts while publicly expressing confidence.

1. **Adverb**: within (place/time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Inside the limits or bounds of something; not beyond a particular area, period, or set of limits
   - *(Sense 2)* Inside one's own mind, heart, or inner being
   
   **Synonyms**: inside, in, internally, in less than (time)
   
   **Antonyms**: beyond, outside, externally, exceeding
   
   **When to Use**: Use for spatial or temporal limits, or metaphorically for internal states; more formal than "inside"; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can function as preposition or adverb; distinguish based on whether it modifies verb or takes object
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - from within, deep within, lie within
   - within reach, within limits, within oneself
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (alongside) + in (inside)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English withinnan (with + innan "inside") → within → inside the bounds or limits
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The answer lies within.
   - The store is just within walking distance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizational change must emerge from within rather than being externally imposed.
   - Results must be delivered within the specified timeframe.

1. **Adverb**: underneath (place)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Below or beneath something; in or to a position directly under something
   - *(Sense 2)* Hidden by a surface appearance; at a deeper level
   
   **Synonyms**: below, beneath, under, underneath, under the surface
   
   **Antonyms**: above, on top, over, on the surface
   
   **When to Use**: Use for position below or hidden beneath surfaces; can be literal or metaphorical; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can function as preposition or adverb; distinguish based on context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look underneath, hidden underneath, lie underneath
   - trapped underneath, buried underneath, underneath it all
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (below) + neath (beneath)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English underneothan (under + neothan "below") → underneath → position below or beneath
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Look underneath—it might be there.
   - Underneath, he's actually quite shy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Structural supports are positioned underneath to distribute load evenly.
   - Underneath the superficial disagreement lies fundamental philosophical divergence.
