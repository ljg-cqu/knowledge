1. **Adjective**: internal (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Located, occurring, or existing inside something; relating to the inside of something
   - *(Sense 2)* Relating to affairs within a country or organization rather than external matters
   - *(Sense 3)* Existing or occurring within the mind; experienced mentally rather than expressed outwardly
   
   **Synonyms**: inner, interior, inside, inward, domestic
   
   **Antonyms**: external, outer, outside, foreign, outward
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or technical contexts for inside matters, processes, or structures; more formal than "inside"; common in medical, organizational, and political contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "inside" in everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - internal organs, internal affairs, internal conflict
   - internal memo, internal bleeding, internal audit
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (within) + tern (inner) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin internus (inward, internal) → maintained formal register for inside matters
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's dealing with some internal conflicts about the decision.
   - The company sent out an internal email.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation revealed systemic internal control deficiencies.
   - Internal policies must align with external regulatory requirements.

1. **Adjective**: inner (attributive, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Located further inside; situated toward the center or interior
   - *(Sense 2)* Private, personal, or mental rather than expressed or apparent; relating to the mind or spirit
   - *(Sense 3)* Restricted to a small, privileged group
   
   **Synonyms**: interior, inside, central, inward, private
   
   **Antonyms**: outer, external, peripheral, public, surface
   
   **When to Use**: Use for describing things closer to the center or private/personal aspects; can be used in physical or metaphorical contexts; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Only used attributively (before nouns), not predicatively; cannot say "this room is inner"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inner circle, inner peace, inner city
   - inner feelings, inner voice, inner workings
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + -ner (comparative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English innera (comparative of "in") → maintained meaning of further inside or more private
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to find my inner peace.
   - Only the inner circle knew about the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manuscript provides insight into the author's inner psychological landscape.
   - Decision-making remains concentrated within an inner circle of executives.

   - He's on an inward journey of self-discovery.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis examines intrinsic properties of the material independent of environmental factors.

1. **Adjective**: confined (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Kept within limits; restricted to a particular location or space
   - *(Sense 2)* Unable to leave a place because of illness, injury, or restriction
   
   **Synonyms**: restricted, limited, enclosed, imprisoned, contained
   
   **Antonyms**: free, unrestricted, open, liberated, expansive
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing restriction to specific spaces; can carry connotations of imprisonment or medical restriction; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be aware of potentially negative connotations; use "limited to" for more neutral contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - confined to bed, confined to quarters, confined space
   - confined area, closely confined, confined environment
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + fine (boundary) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin confinis (con- "together" + finis "boundary") → confined → kept within boundaries or restricted
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's been confined to bed with the flu.
   - The discussion was confined to practical matters.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Movement within the facility is confined to designated secure zones.
   - Analytical scope is deliberately confined to documented historical evidence.
