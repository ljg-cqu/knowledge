1. **Verb**: contain
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To have or hold something within; to have something as a part or component inside
   - *(Sense 2)* To keep something under control; to prevent from spreading or escaping
   
   **Synonyms**: hold, include, comprise, encompass, enclose
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, release, emit, expel, lack
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing something that holds or includes something else; appropriate in all registers; common in descriptions, technical writing, and everyday contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid confusion between "contain" (hold inside) and "restrain" (control) meanings
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contain information, contain ingredients, contain oneself
   - contain the spread, contain the damage, container ship
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + tain (hold)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin continere (con- "together" + tenere "to hold") → Old French contenir → English contain → maintained meaning of holding within
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This box contains all my old photos.
   - I couldn't contain my excitement when I heard the news.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The document contains confidential information requiring restricted access protocols.
   - Authorities implemented measures to contain the epidemic within affected regions.

1. **Verb**: enclose
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To surround or close off on all sides; to put something inside an envelope or package
   - *(Sense 2)* To place within boundaries or barriers
   
   **Synonyms**: surround, encircle, fence in, contain, include
   
   **Antonyms**: expose, open, release, exclude
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing surrounding or putting something inside; more formal than "put inside"; common in correspondence and formal descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal in casual contexts; use "put inside" or "include" for everyday situations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - enclose a check, enclose a document, enclosed space
   - enclose within walls, please find enclosed, enclosed garden
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (in, into) + close (shut)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French enclos (enclosed) → en- "in" + close "shut" → English enclose → maintained dual meaning of surrounding and including
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I enclosed a photo with the letter.
   - The garden is enclosed by a high fence.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please find enclosed the requested documentation for your review.
   - The medieval city walls enclose approximately four square kilometers.

1. **Verb**: internalize
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To make attitudes, beliefs, or patterns of behavior part of one's own nature by learning or unconscious assimilation
   - *(Sense 2)* To keep thoughts or feelings inside rather than expressing them
   
   **Synonyms**: absorb, assimilate, incorporate, adopt, integrate
   
   **Antonyms**: externalize, express, project, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Use in psychological, educational, or behavioral contexts; formal register; common in academic and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too technical for casual conversation; use "absorb" or "take in" for everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - internalize values, internalize beliefs, internalize criticism
   - internalize norms, internalize emotions, internalization process
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (within) + tern (inner) + -al (relating to) + -ize (make, cause)
   
   **Etymology**: internal + -ize → internalize → psychological term for making external ideas internal
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Kids internalize what they see their parents do.
   - He tends to internalize his problems instead of talking about them.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Socialization processes enable individuals to internalize cultural norms and values.
   - The therapeutic approach addresses maladaptive patterns that clients have internalized.

1. **Verb**: penetrate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive/intransitive) To pass into or through something; to succeed in entering or gaining access to an interior space
   - *(Sense 2)* To understand or gain insight into something complex or mysterious
   
   **Synonyms**: enter, pierce, permeate, infiltrate, access
   
   **Antonyms**: exit, withdraw, surface, repel
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical entry through barriers or metaphorical understanding of complex matters; appropriate in all registers; can have technical or scientific connotations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be aware of potentially sexual connotations in some contexts; use carefully
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - penetrate the market, penetrate defenses, penetrate mysteries
   - penetrate deeply, penetrating insight, market penetration
   
   **Root Analysis**: pene- (almost, through) + -trate (thrust)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin penetrare (to put or get into, enter) → maintained meaning of entering interior spaces
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The rain penetrated through my jacket.
   - It took a while to penetrate the meaning of his words.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Electromagnetic radiation penetrates biological tissue at varying depths depending on wavelength.
   - The analysis penetrates superficial explanations to reveal underlying systemic causes.

1. **Verb**: inhabit
   **Meaning**:

   - *(Sense 2)* To exist as a spirit or force within something
   
   **Synonyms**: occupy, reside in, dwell in, populate, live in
   
   **Antonyms**: vacate, abandon, leave, evacuate
   
   **When to Use**: Use for describing living beings occupying spaces; more formal than "live in"; common in biology, geography, and formal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation about where you live; use "live in" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inhabit the earth, inhabit the forest, inhabit spaces
   - inhabit the body, inhabited by spirits, uninhabited island
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + habit (dwell)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inhabitare (in- "in" + habitare "to dwell") → maintained meaning of dwelling within
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Strange creatures inhabit that old house.
   - Bats inhabit the cave during winter.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Numerous endemic species inhabit the island's unique ecosystem.
   - The philosophical question concerns what consciousness inhabits the physical brain.

1. **Verb**: nestle
   **Meaning**:

   **Synonyms**: snuggle, cuddle, settle, tuck, position cozily
   
   **Antonyms**: emerge, exit, expose, displace
   
   **When to Use**: Use for cozy, comfortable positioning inside or against something; carries warm, comfortable connotations; appropriate in descriptive writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly literary or sentimental in technical or business contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nestle in bed, nestle in the valley, nestle against
   - nestle in one's arms, nestled among trees, cozy nestling
   
   **Root Analysis**: nest + -le (diminutive/frequentative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English nest + -le → nestle → maintained meaning of settling into cozy, protected positions
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The kitten nestled in my lap.
   - The village nestles in the valley between two mountains.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The historic settlement nestles within a naturally defensive geographic position.
   - The architectural design allows the structure to nestle harmoniously into the landscape.

1. **Verb**: embed
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To fix something firmly and deeply into a surrounding mass; to implant or insert
   - *(Sense 2)* To incorporate as an integral part of something; to make an element part of a system or structure
   
   **Synonyms**: implant, insert, fix, plant, incorporate
   
   **Antonyms**: extract, remove, dislodge, separate, detach
   
   **When to Use**: Use for fixing things permanently within something else; common in technical, scientific, and digital contexts; appropriate in formal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be too technical for casual conversation; use "stick into" or "put inside" for everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - embed in concrete, embed a chip, embed a journalist
   - deeply embedded, embed code, embedded system
   
   **Root Analysis**: em- (in) + bed (ground, base)
   
   **Etymology**: em- (variant of en-) + bed → embed → fix firmly in a surrounding mass
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The stone was embedded in the wall.
   - I can't get this splinter out—it's embedded too deep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Microchips are embedded within the identification cards for security purposes.
   - Cultural values become embedded in institutional practices through repeated enactment.

1. **Verb**: confine
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To keep or restrict someone or something within certain limits or boundaries; to keep inside
   - *(Sense 2)* To keep someone in a restricted space, especially as a prisoner
   
   **Synonyms**: restrict, limit, enclose, imprison, contain
   
   **Antonyms**: free, release, liberate, expand, open
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing restriction to specific spaces or limits; can carry negative connotations of imprisonment; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be aware of potentially negative or restrictive connotations; use "keep inside" for neutral contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - confine to bed, confine to quarters, confined space
   - confine oneself to facts, confine within limits, confinement period
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, completely) + fine (end, boundary)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin confinis (con- "together" + finis "boundary") → Old French confiner → English confine → kept meaning of restricting within boundaries
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The doctor said I should confine myself to bed rest.
   - Let's confine our discussion to the main topic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Quarantine protocols require individuals to confine themselves to designated isolation facilities.
   - The analysis confines itself to documented historical evidence.

1. **Verb**: harbor
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To keep or hold something in one's mind, especially a negative feeling; to give shelter or refuge to someone or something inside
   - *(Sense 2)* To provide a place of safety or concealment
   
   **Synonyms**: shelter, conceal, hold, nurture (feelings), hide
   
   **Antonyms**: expose, reveal, expel, release, disclose
   
   **When to Use**: Use for keeping feelings inside or providing shelter/refuge; appropriate in all registers; common for both physical shelter and emotional states (American spelling: harbor; British: harbour)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply secrecy or concealment; ensure appropriate context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - harbor resentment, harbor doubts, harbor criminals
   - harbor feelings, harbor suspicions, safe harbor
   
   **Root Analysis**: harbor (shelter, refuge)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English herebeorg (here "army" + beorg "shelter") → harbor → extended from physical shelter to concealing feelings or people
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't harbor bad feelings—talk it out.
   - She's been harboring that secret for years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Evidence suggests the organization harbored fugitives from prosecution.
   - Patients may harbor residual anxieties following traumatic medical procedures.

1. **Verb**: ingest
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To take food, drink, or another substance into the body through the mouth; to absorb or take in
   
   **Synonyms**: consume, swallow, eat, drink, take in
   
   **Antonyms**: expel, regurgitate, eject, excrete
   
   **When to Use**: Use in medical, scientific, or formal contexts for taking substances into the body; more technical than "eat" or "drink"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too technical for casual conversation about eating; use "eat," "drink," or "swallow" in everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ingest food, ingest medication, ingest toxins
   - accidentally ingest, safely ingest, ingestion rate
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + gest (carry)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin ingerere (in- "into" + gerere "to carry") → ingest → technical term for carrying substances into the body
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Be careful not to ingest any of that chemical.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients should not ingest food or beverages eight hours prior to the procedure.
   - Toxicological analysis determines the quantity of substance ingested.

1. **Verb**: secrete
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To hide or store something in a secret or hidden place; to conceal inside
   - *(Sense 2)* To produce and discharge a substance (biological process)
   
   **Synonyms**: hide, conceal, stash, cache, (biological) produce, discharge
   
   **Antonyms**: reveal, expose, display, discover
   
   **When to Use**: Use for hiding things in concealed places or for biological production processes; formal register; ensure context clarifies which meaning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Dual meanings can create confusion; distinguish clearly between hiding and biological secretion
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - secrete valuables, secrete documents, secrete oneself
   - glands secrete hormones, secrete insulin, secretion process
   
   **Root Analysis**: secrete (from Latin secretus, "separated, hidden")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin secretus (separated, hidden) → secrete → developed two distinct meanings: hide and biological production
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He secreted the money inside a book.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conspirators secreted classified documents within diplomatic pouches.
   - Pancreatic beta cells secrete insulin in response to elevated blood glucose levels.
