1. **Expression**: once upon a time (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to begin stories, especially fairy tales; indicates an indefinite time in the past
   - *Literal meaning*: At one point in time, on a particular time
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Traditional narrative opening; creates distance from present and signals storytelling mode
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed formula)
   
   **Synonyms**: long ago, in the past, in former times, at one time, in days of old
   
   **Antonyms**: nowadays, currently, these days, at present, in modern times
   
   **When to Use**: Beginning fairy tales, children's stories, nostalgic recollections; can be used ironically in adult contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Factual historical accounts with specific dates; formal academic or technical writing; when precision about time is required
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Once upon a time, there was/were...
   - Once upon a time, in a land far away...
   - Once upon a time, long ago...
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrase*: Formulaic opening for oral narratives dating to medieval storytelling traditions; "upon" provides formal, distancing effect; conventional marker signaling transition from reality to narrative world
   
   **Root Analysis**: once (one time) + upon (on/at) + a (article) + time (temporal period)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Once upon a time, we didn't have the internet.
   - Once upon a time, there lived a brave princess.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The narrative employs the traditional formula "Once upon a time" to establish temporal distance.
   - Historical consciousness often begins with "once upon a time" constructions that mythologize the past.
