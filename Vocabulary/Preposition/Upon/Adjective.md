1. **Adjective**: immediate (comp: more immediate, sup: most immediate)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Happening or done without delay; occurring at once
   - Nearest in time, space, or relationship; next in line
   - (Gradable) Can be used with modifiers like "more" or "most"
   
   **Synonyms**: instant, prompt, instantaneous, direct, swift
   
   **Antonyms**: delayed, gradual, eventual, remote, distant
   
   **When to Use**: When describing actions or reactions that happen upon certain triggers; formal contexts requiring precision about timing; emphasizing urgency
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing processes that naturally take time; when "quick" would sound more natural in casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immediate action
   - immediate response
   - immediate effect upon
   - take immediate steps
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (not) + medi (middle) + -ate (adjective suffix) → "not having anything in the middle"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin immediatus → Medieval Latin → "nothing coming between" → direct or instant
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to take immediate action upon hearing the news.
   - The medicine had an immediate effect.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Upon receipt of the complaint, immediate investigation procedures were initiated.
   - The regulation requires immediate compliance upon notification.

1. **Adjective**: subsequent (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Coming after something in time; following in order or succession
   - (Non-gradable) Either subsequent or not; doesn't typically take "very" or "more"
   
   **Synonyms**: following, ensuing, succeeding, later, next
   
   **Antonyms**: previous, prior, preceding, earlier, former
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, academic contexts, legal documents; when describing events that follow others in sequence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "next" or "later" are more natural; avoid overuse in simple narratives
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - subsequent to
   - subsequent events
   - subsequent actions upon
   - in subsequent years
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under, after) + sequ (follow) + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin subsequens (following after) → French → English; maintaining temporal sequence meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Subsequent to our meeting, things got better.
   - The subsequent chapters build upon the first one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Subsequent analysis revealed significant correlations with the initial findings.
   - Upon approval, subsequent phases of implementation shall commence immediately.
