1. **Preposition**: upon (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* On top of; in contact with the surface of something; physically positioned on
   - *(Temporal)* Immediately after; at the time of; when something happens
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; about; in relation to a particular subject or matter
   - *(Formal variant)* More formal equivalent of "on" in many contexts
   
   **Synonyms**: on (general), immediately after (temporal), concerning (abstract), atop
   
   **Antonyms**: beneath, below, under, before (temporal)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "upon" in formal writing, literary contexts, fixed expressions, or when emphasizing immediacy of temporal relationships; particularly common in legal, academic, and ceremonial language
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "on" is more natural; overuse can sound archaic or pretentious; don't use when "on" specifically refers to days of the week (e.g., "on Monday" not "upon Monday")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: upon arrival, upon request, upon reflection, upon investigation, upon closer examination
   - **Verb + prep**: depend upon, rely upon, build upon, act upon, look upon, call upon
   - **Adjective + prep**: based upon, contingent upon, dependent upon
   - **Noun + prep**: impact upon, effect upon, influence upon, claim upon
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (adverb: upward) + on (preposition: position/contact)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → combination of "up" + "on" → created compound preposition with formal register; originally emphasized vertical positioning, evolved to formal alternative to "on"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Once upon a time, there was a brave knight.
   - You can call upon me if you need help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee shall convene upon receipt of the final report.
   - This regulation is contingent upon approval by the board of directors.

1. **Preposition**: based upon (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Founded on; having as a foundation or source; derived from; using as evidence or reasoning
   
   **Synonyms**: based on, founded on, grounded in, derived from, predicated on
   
   **Antonyms**: regardless of, independent of, unrelated to
   
   **When to Use**: Use in academic writing, analysis, decision-making contexts; indicates logical or evidential foundation; slightly more formal than "based on"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual speech; "based on" is more common in everyday language
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: based upon evidence, based upon experience, based upon research, based upon assumptions
   - **Verb + prep**: is based upon, was based upon
   - **Adjective + prep**: solely based upon, primarily based upon
   - **Noun + prep**: decision based upon, conclusion based upon, judgment based upon
   
   **Root Analysis**: base (foundation) + -ed (past participle) + upon (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin basis (foundation) → French base → English base + upon; originally architectural/physical, extended to abstract foundations
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My guess is based upon what I saw yesterday.
   - The movie is based upon a true story.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court's decision was based upon substantial evidence presented during the trial.
   - This analysis is based upon data collected over a five-year period.

1. **Preposition**: upon arrival (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Immediately when someone or something arrives; at the moment of reaching a destination
   
   **Synonyms**: when arriving, immediately after arriving, on arrival, as soon as one arrives
   
   **Antonyms**: before departure, prior to leaving
   
   **When to Use**: Common in formal instructions, travel contexts, procedural language; indicates immediate action following arrival
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly formal in casual contexts; "when you arrive" or "as soon as you get there" are more natural conversationally
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: upon arrival at, immediately upon arrival, shortly upon arrival
   - **Verb + prep**: check in upon arrival, report upon arrival, register upon arrival
   - **Adjective + prep**: available upon arrival
   - **Noun + prep**: instructions upon arrival, procedures upon arrival
   
   **Root Analysis**: upon (preposition) + arrival (noun: act of arriving)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound phrase combining "upon" (immediate temporal) + "arrival" (French arriver: to reach shore)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Upon arrival, we grabbed our bags and headed to the hotel.
   - Call me upon arrival, so I know you're safe.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Guests are requested to proceed to the registration desk upon arrival.
   - Upon arrival at the facility, all visitors must present valid identification.

1. **Preposition**: upon request (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Conditional)* When asked for; available if someone asks; provided when requested
   
   **Synonyms**: if requested, when asked, on demand, if desired, as needed
   
   **Antonyms**: automatically, without asking, unprompted
   
   **When to Use**: Standard in business, service industries, institutional contexts; indicates conditional availability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate when something is given automatically; avoid when emphasizing proactive rather than reactive service
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: available upon request, provided upon request, furnished upon request
   - **Verb + prep**: send upon request, supply upon request, disclose upon request
   - **Adjective + prep**: available upon request, accessible upon request
   - **Noun + prep**: information upon request, documents upon request, assistance upon request
   
   **Root Analysis**: upon (preposition: conditional) + request (noun: act of asking)
   
   **Etymology**: upon (immediately following) + request (Latin re-: again + quaerere: to seek)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We can send you more details upon request.
   - Extra blankets are available upon request.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Additional documentation will be provided upon request by authorized personnel.
   - The company's financial statements are available upon request to shareholders.

1. **Preposition**: upon reflection (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal/Cognitive)* After thinking carefully about something; after considering something more deeply; when reconsidering
   
   **Synonyms**: after thinking about it, on second thought, after consideration, having thought it over
   
   **Antonyms**: immediately, without thinking, at first glance, initially
   
   **When to Use**: Used when revising an opinion or decision after careful thought; common in reflective writing, decision-making contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing immediate reactions or first impressions
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: upon further reflection, upon careful reflection, upon deeper reflection
   - **Verb + prep**: realize upon reflection, understand upon reflection, recognize upon reflection
   - **Adjective + prep**: clear upon reflection, obvious upon reflection
   - **Noun + prep**: thoughts upon reflection, insights upon reflection
   
   **Root Analysis**: upon (temporal: after) + reflection (noun: re-: back + flectere: to bend)
   
   **Etymology**: upon (temporal) + reflection (Latin reflectere: to bend back, consider again) → thinking back on something
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Upon reflection, I think I was too harsh with my criticism.
   - She changed her mind upon reflection.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Upon reflection, the committee determined that additional evidence was warranted.
   - Upon further reflection, the proposed methodology appears insufficiently rigorous.

1. **Preposition**: depend upon (type: phrasal - verb + preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* To rely on; to be determined by; to be contingent on; to need or trust someone/something
   
   **Synonyms**: depend on, rely on, count on, rest on, hinge on
   
   **Antonyms**: be independent of, be unaffected by
   
   **When to Use**: Slightly more formal than "depend on"; common in written English and formal speech; emphasizes conditionality or reliance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual conversation, "depend on" is more common and natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: depend upon circumstances, depend upon conditions, depend upon factors
   - **Verb + prep**: will depend upon, can depend upon, should depend upon
   - **Adjective + prep**: dependent upon
   - **Noun + prep**: dependence upon
   
   **Root Analysis**: depend (de-: down + pendere: to hang) + upon (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin dependere (to hang from) + upon → literally "to hang upon" → metaphorically "to rely on"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Whether we go depends upon the weather.
   - You can depend upon Sarah to help out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The success of this initiative depends upon the cooperation of all stakeholders.
   - The outcome will depend upon several interrelated variables.

1. **Preposition**: once upon a time (type: phrasal - fixed expression)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal/Formulaic)* At some time in the past; used to begin fairy tales and stories; indicates indefinite past time with narrative function
   
   **Synonyms**: long ago, in the past, in former times, at one time
   
   **Antonyms**: nowadays, currently, these days, in the present
   
   **When to Use**: Standard opening for fairy tales, children's stories, and narrative beginnings; can be used ironically or nostalgically in adult contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate for factual historical accounts requiring specific dates; avoid in formal academic or technical writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: Once upon a time, there was/were..., Once upon a time in...
   - **Verb + prep**: begins with "once upon a time"
   - **Fixed formula**: "Once upon a time, [subject] [verb]..."
   
   **Root Analysis**: once (one time, formerly) + upon (on) + a (article) + time (temporal noun)
   
   **Etymology**: Traditional storytelling formula; "upon" as formal variant of "on"; creates distance from present time; conventional opening for oral narratives since Middle English period
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Once upon a time, we didn't have smartphones everywhere.
   - Once upon a time, there lived a wise old woman.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The narrative commences with the traditional formula: "Once upon a time, in a kingdom far away..."
   - Historical consciousness often begins with "once upon a time" constructions that mythologize the past.

1. **Preposition**: call upon (type: phrasal - verb + preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Action)* To ask someone formally to do something; to request someone's help or service; to visit someone
   - *(Formal request)* To demand or require someone to take action
   
   **Synonyms**: call on, ask, request, summon, appeal to, visit
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, avoid, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: Formal requests, official contexts, ceremonial language; when emphasizing the seriousness or formality of the request
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual requests among friends; "ask" or "call on" are more natural in everyday contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: call upon someone to do something, call upon expertise, call upon resources
   - **Verb + prep**: may call upon, can call upon, should call upon
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready to be called upon
   - **Noun + prep**: ability to call upon
   
   **Root Analysis**: call (summon/request) + upon (preposition indicating direction toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse kalla (to call) + upon → originally "to call toward someone," evolved to formal request or invocation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Feel free to call upon me anytime you need assistance.
   - The teacher called upon three students to answer questions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The president called upon Congress to pass immediate legislation.
   - We call upon all member states to honor their commitments under the treaty.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

   - **Noun + prep**: act of stumbling upon
