1. **Linking Word**: upon + [noun phrase] (type: connector - temporal/conditional)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Indicates temporal connection: immediately when or after something happens
   - Indicates conditional trigger: when a certain condition is met
   - *Function*: Creates temporal or conditional relationship between clauses; more formal than "when"
   
   **Synonyms**: when, as soon as, immediately after, once, the moment that
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, until, unless (in some contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, legal documents, procedural language; when emphasizing immediacy of connection between events or conditions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "when" is more natural; repetitive use can sound overly formal or archaic
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Action 1] + upon + [noun phrase], + [Action 2]
   - **Position**: typically introduces prepositional phrase that acts as temporal/conditional modifier
   - **Punctuation**: usually followed by comma when phrase comes first; no comma when phrase comes at end
   - **Common errors to avoid**: overuse in casual writing; using with specific days/dates where "on" is standard
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (upward) + on (position/contact) → compound preposition
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English combination of "up" + "on" → created formal register for temporal/spatial relationships
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Upon hearing the news, we left immediately.
   - Call me upon arrival.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Upon receipt of the application, processing shall commence within five business days.
   - Upon determination that standards have been met, certification will be granted.

1. **Linking Word**: immediately upon (type: connector - temporal emphasis)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At the exact moment when something happens; with no delay between two events
   - *Function*: Emphasizes instantaneous temporal connection; stronger than "upon" alone
   
   **Synonyms**: as soon as, the moment, right when, instantly when, the instant
   
   **Antonyms**: long after, eventually following, some time after
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing zero delay between trigger and response; urgent or time-critical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When there is actual delay or when immediacy isn't crucial
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Action] + immediately upon + [trigger event]
   - **Position**: mid-sentence or sentence-initial with following comma
   - **Punctuation**: comma after introductory phrase
   - **Common errors to avoid**: using when actions aren't actually immediate; redundancy with other time markers
   
   **Root Analysis**: immediate (without interval) + upon (temporal trigger)
   
   **Etymology**: Combination emphasizing zero temporal gap between events
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Leave immediately upon getting the signal.
   - Immediately upon waking, I remembered the dream.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Evacuation procedures must be initiated immediately upon detection of hazardous conditions.
   - Notification shall occur immediately upon discovery of any discrepancies.

1. **Linking Word**: upon [verb]-ing (type: connector - temporal/conditional)
   **Meaning**: 
   - When the action of the verb occurs; at the time of doing something
   - *Function*: Creates temporal link using gerund form; more formal than "when" + present tense
   
   **Synonyms**: when [verb]-ing, while [verb]-ing, as one [verb]s, in [verb]-ing
   
   **Antonyms**: before [verb]-ing, without [verb]-ing, instead of [verb]-ing
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, technical documentation, academic prose; creates concise temporal connections
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual speech where "when you..." is more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: Upon [verb]-ing + [noun phrase], [main clause]
   - **Position**: typically sentence-initial, followed by comma
   - **Punctuation**: comma separates introductory phrase from main clause
   - **Common errors to avoid**: mixing subjects (ensure clear referent); using inappropriately complex constructions
   
   **Root Analysis**: upon (temporal marker) + gerund (noun form of verb indicating action)
   
   **Etymology**: Formal construction using gerund to create temporal clause
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Upon opening the box, I found a surprise inside.
   - Upon realizing my mistake, I apologized.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Upon completing the required coursework, students may apply for graduation.
   - Upon examining the evidence, investigators reached a determination.

1. **Linking Word**: thereupon (type: discourse marker - consequential/temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Immediately after that; as a consequence of that; then
   - *Function*: Connects previous statement to immediate consequence or next event; archaic/literary flavor
   
   **Synonyms**: immediately after that, then, consequently, as a result, at that point
   
   **Antonyms**: before that, previously, earlier, prior to that
   
   **When to Use**: Literary writing, legal language, formal historical narratives; creates formal causal or temporal link
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Modern casual writing; contemporary business writing (sounds archaic)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Event/statement] + thereupon + [consequence/next event]
   - **Position**: sentence-initial or mid-sentence after comma/semicolon
   - **Punctuation**: preceded by period, semicolon, or comma; followed by comma when mid-sentence
   - **Common errors to avoid**: overuse (very formal/archaic); using in casual contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: there (at that point) + upon (immediately following)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound adverb combining location/time reference with immediacy marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *[Rarely used in casual contexts; would sound pretentious]*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The motion was approved; thereupon, the committee adjourned.
   - Upon receiving notification, the board convened; thereupon, a decision was reached.

1. **Linking Word**: whereupon (type: subordinating conjunction - consequential)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Immediately after which; as a consequence of which; and then
   - *Function*: Connects cause to immediate effect; formal literary conjunction
   
   **Synonyms**: after which, upon which, at which point, and then, consequently
   
   **Antonyms**: before which, prior to which, preceding which
   
   **When to Use**: Formal narrative writing, legal documents, literary prose; connecting sequential events with causal undertones
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; modern business communication (archaic tone)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Clause 1], whereupon [Clause 2]
   - **Position**: connects two independent clauses; always mid-sentence after comma
   - **Punctuation**: preceded by comma; introduces second clause
   - **Common errors to avoid**: confusing with "where upon" (two words); overuse in modern writing
   
   **Root Analysis**: where (which place/point) + upon (immediately following)
   
   **Etymology**: Relative adverb + preposition creating formal consequential connector
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *[Rarely used in casual contexts]*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant failed to appear, whereupon the court issued a warrant.
   - Evidence was presented to the committee, whereupon deliberations commenced.

1. **Linking Word**: contingent upon (type: connector - conditional)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Depending on; conditional on; subject to a particular condition
   - *Function*: Creates conditional relationship; indicates dependency
   
   **Synonyms**: depending on, conditional on, subject to, provided that, if
   
   **Antonyms**: regardless of, independent of, unconditionally, irrespective of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contracts, business negotiations, conditional agreements, legal documents
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple casual conditions where "if" suffices
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Statement/offer] + is contingent upon + [condition]
   - **Position**: typically mid-sentence linking statement to condition
   - **Punctuation**: usually no comma before "contingent upon" when it's essential information
   - **Common errors to avoid**: using for simple conditions; overcomplication
   
   **Root Analysis**: contingent (conditional, touching) + upon (on the basis of)
   
   **Etymology**: Legal/business language combining conditionality with formal preposition
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The deal is contingent upon inspection results.
   - My acceptance is contingent upon the salary offer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employment is contingent upon successful completion of background verification procedures.
   - Funding allocation is contingent upon demonstrated achievement of performance metrics.

1. **Linking Word**: dependent upon (type: connector - conditional/causal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Determined by; relying on; conditional on
   - *Function*: Indicates dependency relationship; cause-effect or conditional connection
   
   **Synonyms**: depending on, determined by, reliant on, conditional on, based on
   
   **Antonyms**: independent of, unrelated to, regardless of, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, analysis, explaining relationships between variables or factors
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing independence or autonomy
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Outcome/effect] + is dependent upon + [cause/condition]
   - **Position**: connects outcome to determining factor
   - **Punctuation**: usually no comma before "dependent upon"
   - **Common errors to avoid**: confusion with "dependant" (noun); unnecessary formality
   
   **Root Analysis**: dependent (hanging from, relying) + upon (on, from)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphor of hanging from something extended to logical/causal dependency
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My decision is dependent upon your answer.
   - Success is dependent upon teamwork.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The outcome is dependent upon multiple interrelated variables requiring careful control.
   - Economic growth is dependent upon sustained investment in infrastructure development.

1. **Linking Word**: based upon (type: connector - evidential/causal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Founded on; derived from; using as evidence or foundation
   - *Function*: Indicates evidential or logical basis; links conclusion to premises
   
   **Synonyms**: based on, grounded in, founded on, derived from, stemming from
   
   **Antonyms**: regardless of, contrary to, in spite of, independent of
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, analytical contexts, decision explanations; showing logical foundations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no logical foundation exists; arbitrary decisions
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Conclusion/decision] + is based upon + [evidence/foundation]
   - **Position**: connects claim to supporting evidence or reasoning
   - **Punctuation**: usually no comma before "based upon"
   - **Common errors to avoid**: claiming baseless things are "based upon" evidence
   
   **Root Analysis**: based (having foundation) + upon (on, supported by)
   
   **Etymology**: Construction metaphor extended to logical and evidential foundations
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My opinion is based upon personal experience.
   - The story is based upon real events.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology is based upon established theoretical frameworks and empirical precedents.
   - Policy recommendations are based upon comprehensive data analysis and stakeholder consultation.

1. **Linking Word**: subsequent to / subsequent upon (type: connector - temporal sequence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Following in time; after; coming later than
   - *Function*: Formal temporal connector indicating sequence; "subsequent to" more common than "subsequent upon"
   
   **Synonyms**: after, following, later than, in the wake of, succeeding
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, preceding, earlier than, in advance of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, legal documents, technical procedures; establishing temporal sequences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual writing where "after" is clearer and more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: Subsequent to [event], [outcome/action occurred]
   - **Position**: sentence-initial or mid-sentence
   - **Punctuation**: comma after introductory phrase
   - **Common errors to avoid**: overuse of overly formal expressions; "subsequent upon" is rare
   
   **Root Analysis**: subsequent (following after) + to/upon (temporal marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin subsequens (following) + formal temporal preposition
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Subsequent to our meeting, things improved.
   - Subsequent to graduating, she traveled abroad.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Subsequent to regulatory approval, implementation may commence.
   - Subsequent to data collection, analysis was conducted using established protocols.

1. **Linking Word**: in consequence of / as a consequence upon (type: connector - causal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - As a result of; because of; due to
   - *Function*: Formal causal connector; "in consequence of" is standard; "upon" variant is archaic
   
   **Synonyms**: as a result of, because of, due to, owing to, on account of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, notwithstanding, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing showing clear cause-effect relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual writing; "because" or "due to" are simpler
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Clause structure**: [Effect] + in consequence of + [cause]
   - **Position**: can be sentence-initial or mid-sentence
   - **Punctuation**: comma after introductory phrase
   - **Common errors to avoid**: overly formal tone in casual contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: consequence (following result) + of/upon (indicating cause)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin consequentia (that which follows) → formal causal connection
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In consequence of the rain, the game was postponed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In consequence of budget constraints, the project timeline was extended.
   - In consequence of regulatory changes, procedural modifications were implemented.
