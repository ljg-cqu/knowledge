1. **Preposition**: underneath (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* directly below or beneath something, often with the sense of being covered or hidden by it
   - *(Abstract)* at a deeper level; beyond the surface appearance; fundamentally or essentially
   
   **Synonyms**: below (in general contexts), beneath (more formal), under (more common), at the bottom of (spatial emphasis)
   
   **Antonyms**: above, on top of, over, atop
   
   **When to Use**: Use "underneath" for physical position below something with coverage or concealment; for abstract contexts indicating hidden aspects or fundamental nature; in both casual and formal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in mathematical/technical contexts where "below" is standard (e.g., "below zero" not "underneath zero"); avoid when simple vertical position without coverage is meant (prefer "under" or "below")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: underneath the table, underneath the surface, underneath it all, underneath the bed
   - **Verb + prep**: hide underneath, look underneath, placed underneath, buried underneath
   - **Adjective + prep**: visible underneath, hidden underneath, found underneath
   - **Noun + prep**: layer underneath, area underneath, space underneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (Old English "under") + neath (Old English "niþan" = below)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → Middle English "undernethe" (literally "under-below") → Modern English "underneath" (reinforcing/intensifying the meaning of "under")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I found my keys underneath the couch cushions.
   - There's a whole storage area underneath the house.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The geological survey revealed substantial mineral deposits underneath the mountain range.
   - Underneath the political rhetoric lies a fundamental disagreement about resource allocation.

1. **Preposition**: under (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* in or to a position below or beneath something
   - *(Temporal)* less than a specified amount, age, or level
   - *(Abstract)* subject to the authority, control, or influence of; in the process of; according to
   
   **Synonyms**: below, beneath, underneath (more emphatic), underneath (with coverage sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, on top of
   
   **When to Use**: Most common and versatile preposition for "below" relationships; use for physical position, numerical comparisons, authority relationships, and ongoing processes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasis on complete coverage or concealment is needed (prefer "underneath"); when formal tone requires more elegant phrasing (consider "beneath")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: under the table, under pressure, under control, under investigation, under the age of
   - **Verb + prep**: come under, fall under, go under, put under
   - **Adjective + prep**: (less common as adjective + prep pattern)
   - **Noun + prep**: area under, region under, authority under
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "under" (same form in modern usage)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "under" → Proto-Germanic "*under" → Indo-European root "*nter-" (between, among)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The cat's hiding under the bed again.
   - Kids under 12 get in free.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research was conducted under the supervision of Dr. Martinez.
   - Properties valued under £500,000 are subject to standard taxation rates.

1. **Preposition**: beneath (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* in or to a lower position than something; under or underneath
   - *(Abstract)* not worthy of; below the dignity or level of
   
   **Synonyms**: under (more common), underneath (more emphatic), below (general)
   
   **Antonyms**: above, over, atop
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or literary contexts for spatial relationships; use for expressing unworthiness or status ("beneath contempt," "beneath her station")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "under" or "underneath" would sound more natural; avoid when simple numerical comparison is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: beneath the surface, beneath contempt, beneath notice, beneath one's dignity
   - **Verb + prep**: lie beneath, buried beneath, hidden beneath
   - **Adjective + prep**: (less common)
   - **Noun + prep**: layer beneath, ground beneath
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "bi" (by) + "neoþan" (below)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "beneoþan" → Middle English "benethe" → Modern English "beneath" (more formal register than "under")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can see the fish swimming beneath the dock.
   - That joke is beneath you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Archaeological excavations revealed ancient settlements beneath the contemporary city structure.
   - Such petty accusations are beneath the dignity of this institution.

1. **Preposition**: below (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* at a lower level or layer than; underneath
   - *(Temporal/Numerical)* less than a particular amount, rate, or level
   - *(Abstract)* lower in rank, quality, or importance
   
   **Synonyms**: under (with contact), underneath (with coverage), beneath (formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: above, over
   
   **When to Use**: Use for vertical position without necessarily implying contact or coverage; use for numerical/temperature comparisons; use in formal writing and technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When physical contact or coverage is emphasized (prefer "under" or "underneath"); when expressing unworthiness (prefer "beneath")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: below the surface, below zero, below average, below sea level, below expectations
   - **Verb + prep**: fall below, sink below, drop below
   - **Adjective + prep**: (less common)
   - **Noun + prep**: temperature below, level below, area below
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "be" (by) + "low" (low position)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "bilooghe" → Modern English "below" (opposite of "above")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The treasure is buried somewhere below the old oak tree.
   - Temperatures will drop below freezing tonight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Performance metrics fell below the established benchmarks for the third consecutive quarter.
   - The submarine descended to depths below 500 meters.

1. **Preposition**: at the bottom of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* in the lowest part or position of something
   - *(Abstract)* as the fundamental cause or reason for something
   
   **Synonyms**: underneath (with coverage), under (general), beneath (formal), at the base of (structural)
   
   **Antonyms**: at the top of, on top of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing the lowest point in a container, structure, or hierarchy; use when explaining root causes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple "under" would be more natural; when the position is not specifically the lowest point
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at the bottom of the page, at the bottom of the list, at the bottom of the problem, at the bottom of the sea
   - **Verb + prep**: sit at the bottom of, lie at the bottom of, be at the bottom of
   - **Adjective + prep**: visible at the bottom of
   - **Noun + prep**: drawer at the bottom of, cause at the bottom of
   
   **Root Analysis**: at (position marker) + the bottom (lowest part) + of (relationship marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound phrasal preposition combining Germanic elements: "at" (position) + "bottom" (Old English "botm") + "of" (relationship)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I found my phone at the bottom of my bag.
   - At the bottom of all this drama is just a misunderstanding.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The signature appears at the bottom of the contract document.
   - At the bottom of the economic crisis lies a fundamental failure of regulatory oversight.
