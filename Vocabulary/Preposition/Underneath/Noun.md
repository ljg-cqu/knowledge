1. **Noun**: underside (C, pl: undersides)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The lower or bottom surface of something
   - *(Sense 2)* The hidden or less visible aspect of something
   
   **Synonyms**: bottom, lower surface, underneath, belly (informal), base
   
   **Antonyms**: top, upper surface, topside
   
   **When to Use**: Use when referring to the bottom surface of an object; in technical descriptions of physical structures; when discussing hidden aspects metaphorically
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "bottom" would be clearer and more direct; avoid in very formal academic writing unless specifically describing physical surfaces
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the underside of the table
   - examine the underside
   - the underside of society
   - the underside of leaves
   - dark underside
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (below) + side (surface/face)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "under" + "side" → Middle English compound → Modern English "underside" (the side that is under/below)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check the underside of the chair for the serial number.
   - The underside of the mushroom cap is where the spores grow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The inspection revealed significant corrosion on the underside of the bridge structure.
   - This documentary explores the underside of the financial industry.

1. **Noun**: bottom (C/U, pl: bottoms)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The lowest part or point of something
   - *(Sense 2)* The ground under a body of water
   - *(Sense 3)* The underside or lower surface
   
   **Synonyms**: base, floor, foot, lowest part, underside
   
   **Antonyms**: top, peak, summit, apex
   
   **When to Use**: Most common and versatile term for the lowest part; use in all registers; appropriate for physical objects, containers, rankings, and abstract hierarchies
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "base" or "foundation" would better convey structural or conceptual importance
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at the bottom
   - from top to bottom
   - bottom line
   - rock bottom
   - get to the bottom of something
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "botm" (ground, lowest part)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "botm" → Proto-Germanic "*butmaz" → Modern English "bottom" (fundamental lowest position)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The remote's probably at the bottom of the couch.
   - I hit rock bottom before I decided to make changes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sediment samples were collected from the bottom of the lake.
   - The bottom quintile of earners experienced disproportionate economic hardship.

1. **Noun**: foundation (C, pl: foundations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The lowest load-bearing part of a building, typically below ground level
   - *(Sense 2)* An underlying basis or principle for something; a fundamental concept
   - *(Sense 3)* An institution established with an endowment for charitable purposes
   
   **Synonyms**: base, groundwork, basis, underpinning, footing
   
   **Antonyms**: superstructure, top, surface
   
   **When to Use**: Use in architectural/construction contexts; use metaphorically for fundamental principles or starting points; use in formal and technical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple "bottom" or "base" would be clearer; avoid overuse in non-technical contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lay the foundation
   - foundation stone
   - solid foundation
   - shake the foundations
   - foundation of knowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "fundare" (to found/establish) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "fundatio" (a founding) → Old French "fondation" → Middle English "foundacioun" → Modern English "foundation"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need a solid foundation before you build anything else.
   - That class gave me the foundation I needed for advanced studies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Structural engineers discovered cracks in the building's foundation during the inspection.
   - Kant's Critique of Pure Reason laid the foundation for modern epistemology.

1. **Noun**: base (C, pl: bases)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The lowest or supporting part of something; the part on which it stands or rests
   - *(Sense 2)* A main place where someone works or stays; headquarters
   - *(Sense 3)* The fundamental principle or starting point
   
   **Synonyms**: foundation, bottom, foot, pedestal, groundwork
   
   **Antonyms**: top, apex, summit, peak
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical support structures; use in military/organizational contexts; use for fundamental concepts in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "bottom" would be more natural in casual contexts; when "foundation" better conveys permanence
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - base of operations
   - touch base
   - cover all the bases
   - off base
   - base level
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "basis" → Greek "basis" (foundation, pedestal)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "basis" → Old French "base" → Middle English "base" → Modern English (both architectural and conceptual meanings)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The lamp sits on a heavy marble base.
   - Let's touch base next week about the project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The military established a strategic base at the mountain's foot.
   - The research methodology provides a rigorous base for subsequent analysis.

1. **Noun**: substrate (C, pl: substrates)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An underlying substance or layer
   - *(Sense 2)* (Biology) The surface or material on or from which an organism lives, grows, or obtains nutrients
   - *(Sense 3)* (Chemistry) A substance or molecule that is acted upon by an enzyme
   
   **Synonyms**: base layer, underlying material, foundation layer, substratum
   
   **Antonyms**: overlay, surface layer, coating
   
   **When to Use**: Use in technical, scientific, and specialized contexts; appropriate in biology, chemistry, materials science, and geology
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in general conversation; avoid when simpler terms like "base" or "layer" would suffice for non-technical audiences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - substrate material
   - enzyme-substrate complex
   - growth substrate
   - substrate preparation
   - substrate specificity
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "sub" (under) + "stratum" (layer/spread)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "substratum" (foundation layer) → Modern English "substrate" (specialized technical term in scientific fields)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The moss grows on almost any substrate.
   - You need to prepare the substrate before applying the coating.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The enzyme exhibits high specificity for its substrate molecule.
   - Researchers analyzed the chemical composition of the geological substrate underlying the aquifer.

1. **Noun**: layer (C, pl: layers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A sheet, quantity, or thickness of material covering a surface or forming one level in a structure
   - *(Sense 2)* A level of meaning, complexity, or hierarchy
   
   **Synonyms**: stratum, level, tier, coat, thickness
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for describing stacked or overlapping materials; use metaphorically for complexity levels; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "coat" or "coating" would be more specific for applied surfaces
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - layer upon layer
   - add another layer
   - peel back the layers
   - layer of meaning
   - protective layer
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English "leggen" (to lay) + -er (agent/thing)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "leyer" (one who lays) → extended to mean "something laid down" → Modern English "layer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a layer of dust on everything.
   - The story has so many layers to it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The archaeological excavation revealed multiple occupational layers spanning three millennia.
   - The atmosphere consists of distinct layers with varying temperature and pressure profiles.

1. **Noun**: depth (C/U, pl: depths)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The distance from the top or surface to the bottom of something
   - *(Sense 2)* Complexity, intensity, or profundity
   - *(Sense 3)* The lowest, most intense, or most extreme part (usually plural: depths)
   
   **Synonyms**: deepness, profundity, extent, intensity
   
   **Antonyms**: shallowness, superficiality, surface
   
   **When to Use**: Use for measuring vertical distance downward; use metaphorically for intellectual or emotional complexity; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "height" is the appropriate measurement; when simple "deep" (adjective) would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in depth
   - depth of field
   - plumb the depths
   - depth of knowledge
   - out of one's depth
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "deop" (deep) + -th (abstract noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "dīepþ" → Middle English "depthe" → Modern English "depth" (measurement of downward extent)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the depth of this pool?
   - He's got real depth as a person.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The submersible reached a depth of 3,000 meters before equipment malfunction necessitated ascent.
   - The novel explores the psychological depths of trauma and recovery.
