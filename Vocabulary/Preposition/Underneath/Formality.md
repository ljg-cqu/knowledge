1. **Original Expression**: There's a deeper issue underneath all this.  
   **Context**: Someone explaining that surface problems reflect more fundamental causes
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that visible problems stem from hidden, more fundamental issues
   
   **Casual**: There's something bigger going on under all this.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary, conversational "going on," informal preposition "under"  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, close colleagues, informal problem-solving discussions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: There's an underlying issue beneath these symptoms.  
   *Characteristics*: Professional vocabulary ("underlying," "symptoms"), neutral tone, standard grammar  
   *Use when*: Workplace discussions, team meetings, professional emails, talking with colleagues
   
   **Formal**: A fundamental problem underlies these superficial manifestations.  
   *Characteristics*: Academic/technical vocabulary, verb form "underlies," abstract noun "manifestations"  
   *Use when*: Academic writing, formal reports, executive presentations, research papers
   
   **Humorous**: There's a whole can of worms hiding under the surface here!  
   *Characteristics*: Idiomatic expression ("can of worms"), playful exaggeration  
   *Use when*: Lighthearted discussions, building rapport, informal team meetings  
   *Avoid when*: Serious problems, formal contexts, when humor would be inappropriate
   
   **Lively**: There's SO much more going on beneath all this drama!  
   *Characteristics*: Emphasis words ("SO much"), energetic tone, engagement markers  
   *Use when*: Animated discussions, storytelling, expressing enthusiasm or surprise
   
   **Empathetic**: I can see there's something deeper troubling you underneath all this.  
   *Characteristics*: "I can see," personal pronouns, emotional attunement ("troubling you")  
   *Use when*: Supportive conversations, counseling contexts, showing understanding and care
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status/formal contexts require "formal"; equals allow "casual"
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/professional contexts need "semi-formal" or "formal"; friends allow "casual"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious organizational problems require "formal" or "semi-formal"; minor issues allow "casual"
   - *Cultural Context*: Academic/professional cultures expect formal registers; creative/startup cultures may prefer casual
   - *Medium*: Written reports require formal; spoken conversations allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "there's something bigger going on" in formal report—sounds unprofessional and imprecise
   - Using "fundamental problem underlies" in casual conversation—sounds pretentious and distancing
   - Mixing registers: "There's a fundamental issue going on underneath"—inconsistent formality
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Anglo-American cultures often prefer directness but value hedging for sensitive topics; Asian cultures may favor more indirect formal variants even in closer relationships
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Team meeting about recurring production errors → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("There's an underlying issue")—professional context with colleagues
   - **Situation 2**: Conversation with friend about relationship → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual or empathetic—close relationship, emotional topic
   - **Situation 3**: Research paper analyzing social phenomena → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("underlies these manifestations")—academic context requiring precision
   - **Situation 4**: Lighthearted discussion about minor tech glitch → **Appropriate Variant**: Humorous—low stakes, rapport-building appropriate
