1. **Adjective**: underlying (non-gradable, attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Lying or situated beneath something; fundamental or basic
   - Existing as a basis or foundation, though not immediately apparent
   
   **Synonyms**: fundamental, basic, foundational, root, core
   
   **Antonyms**: surface, superficial, apparent, overlying
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or analytical contexts to describe fundamental causes, principles, or physical layers; appropriate in academic, medical, technical, and business writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "basic" or "real" would be clearer; avoid redundancy with words like "fundamental"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - underlying cause
   - underlying principle
   - underlying assumption
   - underlying condition
   - underlying issue
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (beneath) + lying (present participle of "lie")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "underlicgan" → Middle English "underlien" → Modern English "underlying" (participial adjective meaning "being beneath")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's an underlying reason why she's upset.
   - The underlying problem is trust.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research identified the underlying mechanisms responsible for cellular differentiation.
   - Economic analysis revealed underlying structural weaknesses in the financial system.

1. **Adjective**: hidden (non-gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Concealed from view; kept out of sight
   - Not easily noticed or recognized; obscure
   
   **Synonyms**: concealed, obscured, secret, unseen, covert
   
   **Antonyms**: visible, obvious, apparent, exposed, revealed
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe things deliberately concealed or naturally obscured; appropriate in all registers; use for both physical concealment and abstract meanings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "secret" better conveys intentional confidentiality; when "obscure" better conveys difficulty in understanding
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hidden agenda
   - hidden cost
   - hidden meaning
   - hidden talent
   - hidden treasure
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "hȳdan" (to hide) + -en (past participle suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gehȳded" (past participle) → Middle English "hid/hidden" → Modern English "hidden" (participial adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a hidden door behind the bookshelf.
   - He has so many hidden talents.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract contained several hidden clauses that significantly altered the terms.
   - Hidden biases in algorithmic systems can perpetuate systemic discrimination.

1. **Adjective**: lower (comp: lower, sup: lowest - non-standard comparative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Situated below something else; less high in position
   - *(Sense 2)* Less important or of reduced status
   - *(Sense 3)* Smaller in amount or degree
   
   **Synonyms**: inferior (in position), bottom, underneath, subordinate, lesser
   
   **Antonyms**: upper, higher, superior, elevated
   
   **When to Use**: Use for comparative positioning (already comparative form); use in all registers; appropriate for physical position, hierarchy, and quantity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundant constructions like "more lower" (already comparative); when "bottom" would be more specific
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lower level
   - lower class
   - lower back
   - lower priority
   - lower expectations
   
   **Root Analysis**: Comparative form of "low" (Old English "lāh")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "lǣgra" (comparative of "lāh") → Middle English "lower" → Modern English (comparative adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check the lower shelf.
   - My lower back has been hurting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined organisms inhabiting the lower stratosphere.
   - Lower income brackets experienced disproportionate tax burden increases.

1. **Adjective**: subterranean (non-gradable, attributive/predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Existing, occurring, or situated below the earth's surface; underground
   - *(Sense 2)* Secret or hidden, especially in a sinister way
   
   **Synonyms**: underground, below-ground, subsurface, buried
   
   **Antonyms**: surface, above-ground, aerial, elevated
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, technical, or literary contexts; appropriate in geology, architecture, and metaphorical descriptions of secret activities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "underground" is more natural; avoid when simpler terms would be clearer for general audiences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - subterranean passage
   - subterranean water
   - subterranean chamber
   - subterranean network
   - subterranean cavity
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "sub-" (under) + "terra" (earth) + "-anean" (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "subterraneus" (underground) → Late Latin → Modern English "subterranean" (formal term for underground)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They discovered a subterranean cave system.
   - The building has subterranean parking.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Archaeological surveys revealed an extensive subterranean architectural complex.
   - Subterranean aquifers provide critical water resources for arid regions.

1. **Adjective**: concealed (non-gradable, attributive/predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Hidden from view; kept secret or private
   - Deliberately prevented from being seen or known
   
   **Synonyms**: hidden, covered, obscured, veiled, secret
   
   **Antonyms**: revealed, exposed, visible, open, obvious
   
   **When to Use**: Use when deliberate hiding is involved; appropriate in legal, security, and formal contexts; implies intentionality more than "hidden"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when hiding is unintentional (use "hidden" or "obscured"); avoid in casual contexts where "hidden" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - concealed weapon
   - concealed entrance
   - concealed identity
   - concealed camera
   - concealed carry
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "con-" (completely) + "celare" (to hide) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "concelare" → Old French "conceler" → Middle English → Modern English "concealed" (participial adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a concealed door in the wall.
   - He kept his feelings concealed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant was arrested for carrying a concealed firearm without proper licensing.
   - Concealed surveillance equipment was deployed throughout the facility.

1. **Adjective**: buried (non-gradable, attributive/predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Placed or covered with earth or another substance
   - Hidden from view or difficult to access
   
   **Synonyms**: interred, entombed, covered, hidden, submerged
   
   **Antonyms**: excavated, unearthed, exposed, surfaced
   
   **When to Use**: Use for things covered with earth or other materials; use metaphorically for deeply hidden information or emotions; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "hidden" would be clearer without the sense of depth; when "stored" is more accurate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - buried treasure
   - buried alive
   - buried emotion
   - buried secret
   - buried evidence
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "byrgan" (to bury) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "byrgan" → Middle English "burien" → Modern English "buried" (participial adjective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's supposed to be buried treasure here.
   - He kept his buried emotions bottled up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Archaeological artifacts remained buried for millennia before discovery.
   - The investigation uncovered buried evidence of systematic fraud.

1. **Adjective**: deep (comp: deeper, sup: deepest - gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Extending far down from the top or surface
   - *(Sense 2)* Profound, intense, or far-reaching
   - *(Sense 3)* Difficult to understand; complex
   
   **Synonyms**: profound, extensive, thorough, intense, far-reaching
   
   **Antonyms**: shallow, superficial, surface-level, cursory
   
   **When to Use**: Use for vertical extent downward; use metaphorically for intensity, complexity, or profundity; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "profound" would better convey intellectual depth; when "intense" would be clearer for emotions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deep water
   - deep thoughts
   - deep understanding
   - deep sleep
   - deep trouble
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "dēop" (extending far down)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "dēop" → Proto-Germanic "*deupaz" → Modern English "deep"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The pool is really deep at that end.
   - That's pretty deep stuff to think about.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The trench extends to depths exceeding 10,000 meters, representing one of Earth's deepest oceanic features.
   - The philosopher developed a deep critique of contemporary epistemological assumptions.
