1. **Preposition**: against (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In opposition to; contrary to; in resistance to someone or something
   - *(Spatial)* In physical contact with and supported by a surface
   - *(Temporal)* In preparation for or anticipation of a future event
   
   **Synonyms**: opposed to (abstract), versus (abstract), counter to (abstract), touching (spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: for, in favor of, with, supporting
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing opposition, resistance, or contrast; physical contact; formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common learner error: using "against" for general opposition when "opposed to" would be clearer
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: against the law, against the rules, against one's will
   - **Verb + prep**: fight against, argue against, vote against, rebel against
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not common with adjectives in this meaning)
   - **Noun + prep**: opposition against, argument against, case against
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (intensive) + gain (direct, straight) + -st (superlative)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → ongegn "opposite to, against" → Middle English againes → against (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally against the new parking policy—it's way too expensive.
   - Everyone voted against the proposal except for two people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legislation represents a decisive action against systematic discrimination in employment.
   - Philosophical arguments against utilitarianism emphasize its potential disregard for individual rights.

1. **Preposition**: opposite (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In a position facing or across from something; on the other side of something from
   - *(Abstract)* Completely different from; contrary to
   
   **Synonyms**: across from (spatial), facing (spatial), contrary to (abstract), inverse to (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: next to, beside, adjacent to, similar to
   
   **When to Use**: For spatial positioning or to indicate contrasting nature; both formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common as pure preposition; often used with "to" or as adjective/noun
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: opposite the bank, opposite each other, opposite to this view
   - **Verb + prep**: sit opposite, stand opposite, position opposite
   - **Adjective + prep**: diametrically opposite to, completely opposite from
   - **Noun + prep**: the opposite of
   
   **Root Analysis**: op- (against) + posit (placed)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → oppositus "placed against" → Old French → English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The coffee shop is right opposite the train station.
   - My apartment is opposite yours, just across the hall.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The witness testified that the defendant was seated directly opposite the victim.
   - This theoretical approach stands opposite to conventional interpretations of the phenomenon.

1. **Preposition**: versus (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Against; in competition, opposition, or contrast with
   - *(Legal)* Used to indicate the two sides in a legal case
   
   **Synonyms**: against, as opposed to, contrasted with, compared to
   
   **Antonyms**: with, alongside, together with, allied with
   
   **When to Use**: In competitive contexts, legal cases, or when contrasting options; formal and semi-formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Often abbreviated as "vs." or "v." in writing; less common in casual speech
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: A versus B, nature versus nurture, quality versus quantity
   - **Verb + prep**: compete versus, play versus, argue A versus B
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not commonly used)
   - **Noun + prep**: debate between A versus B, choice of A versus B
   
   **Root Analysis**: vers (turn) + -us (Latin participle ending)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → versus "turned toward, against" → English (15th century, legal usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Tonight's game is the Lakers versus the Celtics.
   - It's really a question of convenience versus cost.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The landmark case Brown versus Board of Education transformed American educational policy.
   - The analysis examines centralized versus decentralized organizational structures.

1. **Preposition**: contrary to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In opposition to; in conflict with; opposite to what is stated or expected
   - Different from or opposed to something
   
   **Synonyms**: opposite to, against, counter to, in opposition to, despite
   
   **Antonyms**: in accordance with, consistent with, in line with, agreeing with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts to introduce information that contradicts expectations or previous statements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal in casual speech; "against" or "opposite to" may be simpler
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: contrary to expectations, contrary to popular belief, contrary to advice
   - **Verb + prep**: act contrary to, run contrary to, go contrary to
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not commonly used)
   - **Noun + prep**: (noun usually follows: advice contrary to, evidence contrary to)
   
   **Root Analysis**: contra- (against) + -ary (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → contrarius "opposite" → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Contrary to what you think, I actually did finish my homework!
   - The results were contrary to everything we expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Contrary to prevailing economic theory, the intervention produced unexpected market stability.
   - The findings, contrary to the initial hypothesis, demonstrated inverse correlation patterns.

1. **Preposition**: as opposed to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In contrast with; rather than; used to compare or contrast two things
   - Indicating a preference or distinction between alternatives
   
   **Synonyms**: rather than, instead of, in contrast to, versus, compared to
   
   **Antonyms**: together with, along with, in addition to, as well as
   
   **When to Use**: When making contrasts or distinctions between options; neutral to formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be wordy; "rather than" or "instead of" often more concise
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: A as opposed to B, quality as opposed to quantity
   - **Verb + prep**: choose A as opposed to B, prefer A as opposed to B
   - **Adjective + prep**: different as opposed to, theoretical as opposed to practical
   - **Noun + prep**: emphasis on A as opposed to B
   
   **Root Analysis**: oppose (from op- "against" + posit "place")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → opponere "set against" → English phrase (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I prefer coffee as opposed to tea in the morning.
   - We're talking about behavior, as opposed to actual ability.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines qualitative methodologies as opposed to quantitative approaches.
   - Policy focuses on preventive measures as opposed to reactive interventions.

1. **Preposition**: in contrast to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* When compared with something that is different; showing noticeable difference from
   - Used to introduce something that differs strikingly from what was just mentioned
   
   **Synonyms**: unlike, as opposed to, contrary to, different from, compared to
   
   **Antonyms**: similar to, like, in line with, consistent with, corresponding to
   
   **When to Use**: In analytical or descriptive contexts to emphasize differences; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Relatively formal; "unlike" or "different from" often simpler in casual contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in contrast to previous studies, in contrast to expectations
   - **Verb + prep**: stand in contrast to, appear in contrast to
   - **Adjective + prep**: different in contrast to, stark in contrast to
   - **Noun + prep**: (usually precedes the phrase)
   
   **Root Analysis**: contra- (against) + stand
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → contra "against" + stare "to stand" → contrast → English phrase (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In contrast to my brother, I'm actually pretty good at sports.
   - This year's results, in contrast to last year's, show real improvement.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In contrast to traditional pedagogical approaches, constructivist methods emphasize active learning.
   - These findings, in contrast to previous research, suggest alternative causal mechanisms.

1. **Preposition**: counter to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In opposition to; contrary to; against the direction or tendency of
   - Going in an opposite direction from what is usual or expected
   
   **Synonyms**: contrary to, against, opposite to, opposed to, in conflict with
   
   **Antonyms**: in accordance with, consistent with, aligned with, in line with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts to indicate opposition or contrary movement; emphasizes going against something
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Quite formal; "against" or "contrary to" often more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: counter to expectations, counter to advice, counter to trends
   - **Verb + prep**: run counter to, go counter to, act counter to
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not commonly used)
   - **Noun + prep**: actions counter to, behavior counter to
   
   **Root Analysis**: contra (against) → counter
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → contra "against" → Old French contre → English counter (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His decision was totally counter to all the advice he'd received.
   - That goes counter to everything we've been taught.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy implementation ran counter to stated organizational objectives.
   - Such interventions operate counter to principles of patient autonomy.

1. **Preposition**: unlike (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Different from; not similar to; in contrast to
   - Not characteristic of; atypical for
   
   **Synonyms**: different from, in contrast to, as opposed to, dissimilar to
   
   **Antonyms**: like, similar to, the same as, resembling
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing difference or contrast; both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "dislike" (verb); "unlike" only for comparisons
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: unlike anything else, unlike before, unlike most people
   - **Verb + prep**: seem unlike, appear unlike, sound unlike
   - **Adjective + prep**: totally unlike, completely unlike, quite unlike
   - **Noun + prep**: (precedes unlike phrase)
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + like (similar)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → un- + lic "similar" → Middle English (12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Unlike you, I actually enjoy doing math homework.
   - This coffee tastes unlike anything I've ever had before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Unlike previous interventions, this approach addresses systemic rather than symptomatic issues.
   - The results, unlike those of comparable studies, demonstrate statistical significance.

1. **Preposition**: in opposition to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Disagreeing with or resisting something; acting against
   - Standing in contrast or contrary position to something
   
   **Synonyms**: against, opposed to, counter to, contrary to, in conflict with
   
   **Antonyms**: in support of, in favor of, in agreement with, aligned with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts for expressing disagreement, resistance, or contrary positions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Quite formal; "against" or "opposed to" often more concise
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in opposition to the proposal, in opposition to established norms

   - **Adjective + prep**: staunchly in opposition to, firmly in opposition to
   - **Noun + prep**: position in opposition to, stance in opposition to
   
   **Root Analysis**: op- (against) + posit (place) + -ion
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → oppositio "a placing against" → English phrase (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They organized the protest in opposition to the new regulations.

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The coalition formed in opposition to the proposed constitutional amendments.

1. **Preposition**: vis-à-vis (type: simple/phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In relation to; with regard to; as compared with
   - *(Less common)* Face to face with; opposite to
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, compared to, in relation to
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonym; depends on context)
   
   **When to Use**: Formal, academic, or business contexts for comparisons or relationships; sophisticated register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal; can sound pretentious; avoid in casual conversation
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: vis-à-vis competitors, vis-à-vis previous years, vis-à-vis other options
   - **Verb + prep**: position oneself vis-à-vis, stand vis-à-vis
   - **Adjective + prep**: different vis-à-vis, superior vis-à-vis
   - **Noun + prep**: relationship vis-à-vis, position vis-à-vis
   
   **Root Analysis**: French phrase "face to face"
   
   **Etymology**: French → vis-à-vis "face to face" (literally "face to face") → English (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How does our company stack up vis-à-vis the competition?
   - What's your position vis-à-vis the merger?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis examines European regulatory frameworks vis-à-vis American counterparts.
   - National sovereignty must be reconsidered vis-à-vis emerging supranational governance structures.

1. **Preposition**: at variance with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In disagreement or conflict with; inconsistent with; differing from
   - Not in accordance with; contradicting
   
   **Synonyms**: in conflict with, inconsistent with, contrary to, at odds with, disagreeing with
   
   **Antonyms**: in agreement with, consistent with, in harmony with, aligned with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts for expressing inconsistency, conflict, or disagreement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal phrase; "inconsistent with" or "contradicting" often clearer
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at variance with expectations, at variance with policy
   - **Verb + prep**: be at variance with, stand at variance with, find oneself at variance with
   - **Adjective + prep**: completely at variance with, directly at variance with
   - **Noun + prep**: findings at variance with, conclusions at variance with
   
   **Root Analysis**: variance (from vary: to differ)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → variare "to change, vary" → French variance → English phrase (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your story is completely at variance with what he told me.
   - Those results are at variance with everything we expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant's testimony stands at variance with corroborated witness accounts.
   - Implementation practices were at variance with established organizational protocols.

1. **Preposition**: at odds with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In disagreement or conflict with; not agreeing with
   - In a state of difference or opposition to something or someone
   
   **Synonyms**: in conflict with, in disagreement with, at variance with, opposed to, contrary to
   
   **Antonyms**: in agreement with, in harmony with, consistent with, aligned with
   
   **When to Use**: For expressing disagreement, conflict, or inconsistency; less formal than "at variance with"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Emphasizes conflict or incompatibility; not for neutral differences
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at odds with each other, at odds with expectations, at odds with policy
   - **Verb + prep**: be at odds with, find oneself at odds with, remain at odds with
   - **Adjective + prep**: completely at odds with, fundamentally at odds with
   - **Noun + prep**: position at odds with, views at odds with
   
   **Root Analysis**: odd (strange, unmatched) → odds (inequality, difference)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse → oddi "point, triangle" → odd → odds → English phrase (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're totally at odds with each other on this issue—can't agree at all.
   - His behavior is at odds with what he claims to believe.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy decision appears at odds with evidence-based recommendations.
   - Organizational practices remain at odds with stated diversity objectives.
