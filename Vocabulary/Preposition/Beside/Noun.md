1. **Noun**: side (C, pl: sides)
   **Meaning**: 
   - a position to the left or right of an object, place, or central point
   - *(Sense 2)* a surface of something that is not the top or bottom, front or back
   - *(Sense 3)* either of the two halves of something divided by an imaginary line
   - *(Sense 4)* one of the groups, teams, or positions in a dispute, contest, or argument
   
   **Synonyms**: flank, edge, face, surface, aspect, position, team
   
   **Antonyms**: center, middle, core, top, bottom
   
   **When to Use**: Use "side" for describing lateral positions; when discussing perspectives or positions in disputes; in spatial descriptions and competitive contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid ambiguity when multiple meanings possible; specify "left side," "right side," etc., when needed for clarity
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - by your side, at your side, by my side
   - side by side, side to side
   - on the side (additionally), take sides (choose position)
   
   **Root Analysis**: sīde (flank, lateral surface) from Old English
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "sīde" (flank, lateral surface) → indicating lateral position or surface
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Come stand by my side and help me with this.
   - There's a coffee shop on the other side of the street.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team examined both sides of the theoretical debate.
   - Additional facilities will be constructed on the eastern side of the campus.

2. **Noun**: proximity (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - nearness in space, time, or relationship; the state of being close to someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: nearness, closeness, adjacency, propinquity, vicinity
   
   **Antonyms**: distance, remoteness, separation, farness
   
   **When to Use**: Use "proximity" in formal, academic, or technical contexts; when discussing spatial or relational closeness; common in urban planning, real estate, and social sciences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; avoid when simple "closeness" or "nearness" is clearer; can sound pretentious in everyday speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in proximity to, in close proximity to
   - proximity effect, geographic proximity
   - proximity to amenities/services/resources
   
   **Root Analysis**: proxim- (nearest, from prope = near) + -ity (noun suffix indicating state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "proximitas" (nearness) → French "proximité" → English "proximity" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare) The proximity of the airport is really convenient.
   - (More natural) It's really convenient being so close to the airport.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The property's proximity to major transportation infrastructure enhances its value.
   - Research indicates that geographic proximity facilitates knowledge transfer between institutions.

3. **Noun**: adjacency (U/C, pl: adjacencies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - the state of being adjacent; the quality of being next to or adjoining something
   - *(Technical)* in network theory, a connection or relationship between nodes
   
   **Synonyms**: contiguity, proximity, nearness, juxtaposition, closeness
   
   **Antonyms**: separation, distance, remoteness, disconnection
   
   **When to Use**: Use "adjacency" in technical, architectural, or formal analytical contexts; in network analysis and graph theory; when discussing spatial relationships systematically
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too technical for everyday conversation; often clearer to use "being next to" or "proximity"; primarily used in specialized or academic writing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - spatial adjacency, physical adjacency
   - adjacency matrix (mathematics/computer science)
   - adjacency relationship, zone of adjacency
   
   **Root Analysis**: adjacent (ad- = to + jacent = lying) + -cy (noun suffix indicating state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "adjacēre" → "adjacent" → "adjacency" (state of being adjacent)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare) The adjacency of the two properties creates some issues.
   - (More natural) The fact that the properties are right next to each other creates issues.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis examines spatial adjacency patterns in urban development.
   - The adjacency matrix represents connections between network nodes.

4. **Noun**: neighbor (C, pl: neighbors)
   **Meaning**: 
   - a person living next door to or very near to another person
   - *(Sense 2)* a person, place, or thing situated next to or very near another
   - *(Sense 3)* any person in need of one's help or kindness (biblical/ethical usage)
   
   **Synonyms**: next-door resident, adjoining resident, nearby person, fellow resident
   
   **Antonyms**: stranger, distant resident, non-resident
   
   **When to Use**: Use "neighbor" for people or things in close proximity; in social and community contexts; when discussing residential relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when precise spatial relationships need technical terms; cultural variations exist (British "neighbour")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - next-door neighbor, immediate neighbor
   - good neighbor, friendly neighbor
   - love thy neighbor (biblical reference)
   
   **Root Analysis**: neigh (near) + bor (dweller, from gebūr) → "near-dweller"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "nēahgebūr" (near-dweller) → Middle English "neighebor" → Modern English "neighbor"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My neighbor's dog keeps barking all night.
   - We're having the neighbors over for dinner tomorrow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy encourages cooperation among neighboring municipalities.
   - Relations with neighboring states have improved significantly.

5. **Noun**: vicinity (C/U, pl: vicinities)
   **Meaning**: 
   - the area near or surrounding a particular place; the neighborhood around something
   - the state of being near in space or relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: area, neighborhood, locality, proximity, surroundings, environs
   
   **Antonyms**: distance, remoteness, faraway place
   
   **When to Use**: Use "vicinity" for general areas around a location; in formal descriptions of proximity; common in official announcements and formal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal in casual conversation; often clearer to say "around here" or "nearby"; avoid when precise location is required
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the vicinity of, in the immediate vicinity
   - in close vicinity, within the vicinity
   - general vicinity, immediate vicinity
   
   **Root Analysis**: vicin- (from vicinus = near, neighboring) + -ity (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "vicinitas" (neighborhood, nearness) → French "vicinité" → English "vicinity" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are there any good restaurants in the vicinity?
   - The suspect was last seen in the vicinity of the train station.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All residents in the vicinity of the construction site were notified in advance.
   - The study examined housing prices in the vicinity of the proposed development.

6. **Noun**: boundary (C, pl: boundaries)
   **Meaning**: 
   - a line that marks the limits of an area; a dividing line between two areas
   - *(Sense 2)* a limit of something abstract, such as a subject or activity
   
   **Synonyms**: border, edge, limit, frontier, perimeter, demarcation
   
   **Antonyms**: center, interior, middle, core
   
   **When to Use**: Use "boundary" for defining edges or limits; in legal, geographic, and abstract contexts; when discussing divisions between areas or concepts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple "edge" or "border" is clearer; be specific about whether physical or abstract boundary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - property boundary, natural boundary
   - set boundaries, cross boundaries
   - boundary line, boundary dispute
   
   **Root Analysis**: bound (limit, from Latin "bodina" = boundary) + -ary (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Anglo-Latin "bodina" or "bundina" → Old French "bodne" → English "bound" → "boundary" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our property boundary is marked by that old fence.
   - You need to set better boundaries with your coworkers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The survey establishes precise property boundaries for legal purposes.
   - The research explores boundaries between traditional disciplinary domains.

7. **Noun**: border (C, pl: borders)
   **Meaning**: 
   - the edge or boundary of something; the line separating two areas or countries
   - *(Sense 2)* a decorative strip around the edge of something
   
   **Synonyms**: boundary, edge, frontier, perimeter, margin, rim
   
   **Antonyms**: center, interior, middle, core
   
   **When to Use**: Use "border" for edges and boundaries; especially in geographic and political contexts; when describing decorative edges
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid confusion between political borders (countries) and general edges; specify context when ambiguous
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cross the border, border crossing
   - border area, border region
   - along the border, at the border
   
   **Root Analysis**: bord (edge, ship's side) + -er (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "bordure" (edge, margin) → Middle English "border" → indicating edge or boundary
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We live right on the border between the two towns.
   - The tablecloth has a pretty flower border around it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Enhanced security measures have been implemented at the international border.
   - The manuscript features illuminated borders in the medieval style.

8. **Noun**: edge (C, pl: edges)
   **Meaning**: 
   - the outside limit of an object, area, or surface; the line where something begins or ends
   - *(Sense 2)* the sharpened side of a blade
   - *(Sense 3)* an advantage or superiority (informal: "have an edge")
   
   **Synonyms**: border, margin, rim, brink, verge, periphery
   
   **Antonyms**: center, middle, interior, core, heart
   
   **When to Use**: Use "edge" for physical boundaries and limits; in both literal and metaphorical contexts; common in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Specify which meaning when context is ambiguous; avoid confusion between physical edge and competitive advantage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - on the edge, at the edge, over the edge
   - cutting edge, competitive edge
   - edge of town/cliff/table
   
   **Root Analysis**: ecg (sharp side, cutting edge) from Old English
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "ecg" (blade, sharp edge) → indicating boundary or limit
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Be careful—don't sit too close to the edge!
   - Her house is right on the edge of town.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is located at the edge of the designated development zone.
   - This innovation provides a significant competitive edge in the marketplace.
