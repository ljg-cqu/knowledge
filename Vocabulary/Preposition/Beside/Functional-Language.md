1. **Functional Expression**: Come/Sit beside me (function: inviting/requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Inviting someone to position themselves in physical proximity; requesting companionship or presence
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Come sit next to me, Sit here with me, Join me here, Take a seat beside me
   
   **Antonyms**: Stay over there, Keep your distance, Sit somewhere else
   
   **When to Use**: When inviting someone for conversation, comfort, or shared activity; with friends, family, or familiar colleagues; when physical proximity is desired for intimacy or collaboration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When cultural norms discourage close physical proximity; with strangers in formal settings without context; when personal space boundaries may be uncomfortable
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Come sit next to me (more casual)
   - Please sit beside me (more polite)
   - Why don't you sit here beside me? (softer suggestion)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Direct invitation creates intimacy; physical proximity has social meaning—friendship, support, collaboration; some cultures have stricter proximity norms
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, thanks / Of course (accepting)
   - I'm okay here, thanks / Maybe in a moment (declining politely)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Come sit beside me and tell me what happened."
   - "Hey, sit beside me so we can work on this together."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Please take a seat beside me so we may review the documents together."
   - "Would you be seated beside me for the duration of the ceremony?"

2. **Functional Expression**: Beside the point (function: dismissing/refocusing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Indicating that something is irrelevant or not directly related to the main issue being discussed
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (dismissive)
   
   **Synonyms**: That's irrelevant, That doesn't matter, That's not the issue, That's off-topic
   
   **Antonyms**: That's exactly the point, That's relevant, That's what matters, That's the core issue
   
   **When to Use**: When redirecting conversation to main topic; when dismissing tangential arguments; in debates or discussions when focus is lost
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound dismissive or rude if overused; avoid when others' contributions should be validated; not appropriate when building rapport is priority
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - That's beside the point (standard form)
   - That's completely beside the point (emphatic)
   - While interesting, that's beside the point (softened)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness strategy—can threaten face by dismissing contribution; soften with hedges ("perhaps," "somewhat") or acknowledgment ("I see your point, but...")
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're right, let me refocus (accepting)
   - Actually, it is relevant because... (defending relevance)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Whether he's nice or not is beside the point—can he do the job?"
   - "That's beside the point right now; we need to decide today."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "While that observation has merit, it remains beside the point of our immediate discussion."
   - "The personal circumstances, though unfortunate, are beside the point legally."

3. **Functional Expression**: Stand/Be beside someone (function: expressing support/solidarity)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offering support, loyalty, or solidarity; committing to support someone through difficulties
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct (commitment statement)
   
   **Synonyms**: Stand with someone, Support someone, Have someone's back, Be there for someone, Stand by someone
   
   **Antonyms**: Abandon someone, Turn your back on someone, Leave someone alone, Withdraw support
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing loyalty or solidarity; offering support during challenges; in emotional or difficult situations; building trust and commitment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When unable to follow through with actual support (empty promises damage trust); in contexts requiring neutral stance; when support could be seen as taking sides inappropriately
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll stand beside you (personal commitment)
   - We stand beside you (collective support)
   - Standing beside you through this (emphasizing duration)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness strategy—building solidarity and in-group membership; strong commitment marker; metaphorical use of physical proximity to show emotional/practical support
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you, that means a lot (grateful acceptance)
   - I appreciate your support (formal acceptance)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'll be standing right beside you when you tell them."
   - "We're beside you all the way—whatever you need."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The organization stands beside its members during this challenging period."
   - "We remain beside you in your pursuit of justice."

4. **Functional Expression**: Place/Position X beside Y (function: giving instructions/directions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Instructing or directing someone to locate one item adjacent to another
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (instruction)
   
   **Synonyms**: Put X next to Y, Set X beside Y, Place X adjacent to Y, Position X alongside Y
   
   **Antonyms**: Separate X from Y, Move X away from Y, Keep X apart from Y
   
   **When to Use**: When giving assembly instructions; directing spatial arrangements; in teaching or training contexts; when precise positioning matters
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "near" or approximate positioning is acceptable (overspecification); avoid overly technical "adjacent" in casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Please place X beside Y (polite request)
   - Put X right beside Y (emphasizing proximity)
   - Position X beside Y (more formal/technical)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Imperative form requires attention to politeness markers (please, could you); clarity important in instructions; "beside" more specific than "near"
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, done / Got it (compliance)
   - Like this? (seeking confirmation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Just put the chair beside the table, anywhere there is fine."
   - "Can you place this photo beside that one so I can compare?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Position the sensor beside the primary measurement device."
   - "Please ensure that Document A is filed beside Document B in sequence."

5. **Functional Expression**: Beside myself (with emotion) (function: expressing intense emotion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing overwhelming emotion (typically anger, grief, joy) that causes loss of composure
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (emotional expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: Overwhelmed with, Overcome with, Extremely upset/happy, Out of control with emotion
   
   **Antonyms**: Composed, Calm, Collected, Under control
   
   **When to Use**: When describing or expressing intense emotional states; in storytelling; when explaining loss of composure; typically followed by "with" + emotion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In contexts requiring professional composure; avoid in formal reports (use "extremely upset/concerned"); can sound dramatic if emotion isn't actually that intense
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Beside myself with anger/grief/worry (negative emotions)
   - Beside myself with joy/excitement (positive emotions)
   - Absolutely beside myself (emphatic)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Idiomatic expression; ancient origin (emotion takes you "outside yourself"); justifies emotional behavior or explains intensity; face-threatening if used to excuse inappropriate behavior
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I understand, that's really difficult (empathetic)
   - Try to calm down (directive)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I was beside myself when I heard the news—I couldn't believe it!"
   - "She's beside herself with worry about the test results."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The family was beside themselves with grief following the tragedy."
   - "Upon receiving the award, he was beside himself with gratitude and joy."

6. **Functional Expression**: Besides (that/which) (function: adding information/reasons)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introducing additional information, reasons, or considerations; functioning as discourse marker for addition
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Moreover, Furthermore, In addition, Also, What's more, Plus
   
   **Antonyms**: However, On the other hand, Nevertheless, Conversely
   
   **When to Use**: When adding supporting points or reasons; building arguments; listing considerations; in persuasion or explanation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "beside" (next to); avoid overuse which creates list-like writing; not appropriate for contrasting information
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Besides, ... (sentence-initial)
   - And besides, ... (emphatic addition)
   - Besides that/which, ... (referring back)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Additive discourse marker; signals speaker has more to say; can indicate that additional reason is even more compelling; strengthens argument by accumulation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's true / Fair point (accepting addition)
   - But even so... (acknowledging but contrasting)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I don't want to go. Besides, I'm too tired."
   - "The car's too expensive, and besides, we don't really need it."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The proposal lacks sufficient funding. Besides which, the timeline is unrealistic."
   - "This approach offers cost savings; besides, it enhances operational efficiency."

7. **Functional Expression**: Sit beside the point/issue (function: avoiding main topic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Discussing matters tangential to the main issue; avoiding the central point
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (avoidance)
   
   **Synonyms**: Avoid the main issue, Skirt the issue, Talk around the point, Evade the question
   
   **Antonyms**: Address the point directly, Get to the heart of the matter, Focus on the issue
   
   **When to Use**: When observing or pointing out that discussion is unfocused; in meta-discussion about conversation flow; when redirecting to main topic
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common than "beside the point"; can be confused with that phrase; better to use more direct descriptions of avoidance
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We're sitting beside the main issue (avoiding focus)
   - Let's not sit beside the point (directive to focus)
   - That argument sits beside the central question (describing irrelevance)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Less idiomatic than "beside the point"; spatial metaphor for relevance—being "beside" rather than "on" the point/issue; can sound more formal or literary
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're right, let's focus (accepting redirect)
   - I am addressing it by... (defending relevance)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We keep sitting beside the real issue—nobody wants to talk about money."
   - "Stop dancing around it; we're just sitting beside the point here."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The discussion continues to sit beside the central policy question."
   - "We must not allow these concerns to sit beside the core issue at hand."
