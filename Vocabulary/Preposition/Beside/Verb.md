1. **Verb**: border
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to form an edge along or beside; to be next to; to adjoin
   - (intransitive with "on") to be almost the same as; to come close to
   
   **Synonyms**: adjoin, abut, edge, bound, neighbor, flank
   
   **Antonyms**: separate from, distance from, detach from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "border" when describing geographic adjacency; when something forms an edge or boundary; in formal descriptions of spatial relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "next to" is clearer; less appropriate for temporary positioning; "border on" suggests approximation rather than exact adjacency
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - border on something (come close to a quality/state)
   - countries that border each other
   - properties that border the park
   
   **Root Analysis**: bord (edge, margin) + -er (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "bordure" (edge, rim) → Middle English "border" → indicating boundary or edge
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our backyard borders the forest—it's pretty cool.
   - Her behavior borders on rude sometimes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed development borders protected wetland areas.
   - This approach borders on negligence in its disregard for established protocols.

2. **Verb**: adjoin
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive/intransitive) to be next to and joined with; to be contiguous to; to share a boundary
   
   **Synonyms**: border, abut, neighbor, connect with, be adjacent to
   
   **Antonyms**: separate from, stand apart from, be distant from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "adjoin" in formal, technical, or real estate contexts; when emphasizing shared boundaries; in architectural and planning descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; avoid when simple "next to" or "beside" is more natural; implies fixed, stable adjacency
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - adjoining rooms/properties/buildings
   - adjoin the main structure
   - properties that adjoin each other
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + join (from jungere = to join)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "adjoindre" → Middle English "adjoinen" → indicating joining to or placing beside
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare) Can we get adjoining hotel rooms?
   - (More natural) Can we get rooms next to each other?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conference room adjoins the executive suite on the third floor.
   - Parcels adjoining the conservation area require special permits for development.

3. **Verb**: flank
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to be situated on each side of or on one side of; to border
   - (transitive, military) to guard or strengthen the side of; to attack the side of
   
   **Synonyms**: border, edge, bound, frame, side
   
   **Antonyms**: center on, be distant from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "flank" when describing positioning on sides; common in military, architectural, and formal descriptive contexts; implies protective or framing positioning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in casual speech; can sound dramatic or formal; not appropriate for simple adjacency without side positioning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - flanked by guards/trees/buildings
   - flank the entrance/building/structure
   - flank on both sides
   
   **Root Analysis**: flank (from Old French "flanc" = side, hip)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "flanc" (side) → verb usage "to flank" (be at the side of)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The house is flanked by two huge oak trees.
   - He walked in flanked by bodyguards on either side.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The central pavilion is flanked by symmetrical wings in classical style.
   - The delegation entered the chamber flanked by security personnel.

4. **Verb**: neighbor
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive) to live or be located near to; to adjoin
   - (transitive, rare) to be adjacent to; to border on
   
   **Synonyms**: adjoin, border, be adjacent to, be next to, abut
   
   **Antonyms**: be distant from, be separated from, be remote from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "neighbor" as verb in formal or literary contexts; more common as noun in everyday usage; regional variations in usage frequency
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Uncommon as verb in modern casual speech; often clearer to use "live next to" or "be neighbors with"; can sound archaic
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - neighbor on/upon (be adjacent to)
   - neighboring properties/countries
   - (more common as participial adjective than verb)
   
   **Root Analysis**: neigh (near) + bor (dweller) → neighbor
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "nēahgebūr" (near-dweller) → verb form "to neighbor" (be adjacent to)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare as verb) Our properties neighbor each other.
   - (More natural) We're neighbors / We live next door to each other.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The estates neighbor upon protected conservation land.
   - Nations that neighbor one another often share cultural characteristics.

5. **Verb**: abut
   **Meaning**: 
   - (intransitive, often with "on" or "against") to touch at the end or boundary line; to border on; to be contiguous
   
   **Synonyms**: border on, adjoin, touch, meet, be adjacent to
   
   **Antonyms**: separate from, be detached from, stand apart from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "abut" in technical, legal, or real estate contexts; when emphasizing direct contact at boundaries; in surveying and property descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too technical for casual conversation; avoid when general proximity rather than direct contact is meant; primarily used in formal writing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - abut on/upon/against something
   - properties that abut the highway
   - walls that abut each other
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (to) + but (end, boundary) from Old French "abouter" (to end at, border)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "abouter" (to border on, end at) → Anglo-French → English "abut" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare) Our fence abuts their property line.
   - (More natural) Our fence runs right along their property line.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The eastern boundary of the parcel abuts upon state conservation land.
   - As specified in the survey, the structure abuts the public right-of-way.

6. **Verb**: juxtapose
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to place close together or side by side, especially for comparison or contrast
   
   **Synonyms**: place beside, position alongside, contrast, compare, set side by side
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, isolate, scatter, disperse
   
   **When to Use**: Use "juxtapose" when emphasizing deliberate placement for analytical purposes; common in art, literature, and academic analysis; implies intentional arrangement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; implies purposeful arrangement rather than coincidental proximity; not for simple physical adjacency
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - juxtapose A with/against/and B
   - juxtapose images/ideas/concepts
   - deliberately/carefully juxtaposed
   
   **Root Analysis**: juxta- (beside, near) + pose (from French "poser" = to place)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "juxta" (beside) + French "poser" (to place) → "juxtapose" (coined in 19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The editor juxtaposed the before and after photos really effectively.
   - I like how they juxtaposed the old footage with new interviews.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author juxtaposes traditional narrative structures with experimental techniques.
   - The exhibit juxtaposes works from different periods to illuminate thematic continuities.

7. **Verb**: parallel
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to be or run side by side with; to correspond to; to match
   - (transitive) to be similar or analogous to; to compare with
   
   **Synonyms**: run alongside, correspond to, match, mirror, align with
   
   **Antonyms**: diverge from, contrast with, differ from, oppose
   
   **When to Use**: Use "parallel" when describing alignment or correspondence; appropriate in formal analysis; when discussing analogous developments or matching patterns
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal in casual contexts; be clear whether geometric or metaphorical meaning is intended
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - parallel the main road
   - parallel developments/trends
   - closely parallel, roughly parallel
   
   **Root Analysis**: para- (beside, alongside) + allel (from allelon = one another)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "parallēlos" → Latin → French → English "parallel" as verb (to be alongside, to match)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This road parallels the highway for about ten miles.
   - His career path kind of paralleled mine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed route parallels existing transportation infrastructure.
   - These findings parallel those reported in earlier studies on the subject.

8. **Verb**: position
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) to put or arrange in a particular place or position; to locate
   
   **Synonyms**: place, situate, locate, arrange, set, put
   
   **Antonyms**: remove, displace, relocate, move
   
   **When to Use**: Use "position" when emphasizing deliberate or strategic placement; common in technical, professional, and instructional contexts; implies careful consideration of location
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be unnecessarily formal when simple "put" or "place" suffices; less common for casual, temporary placement
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - position something beside/next to/near
   - strategically positioned, carefully positioned
   - position oneself (metaphorically: take a stance)
   
   **Root Analysis**: posit (from Latin "ponere" = to place) + -ion (noun suffix) → back-formation to verb "position"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ponere" (to place) → "positio" (position) → English noun → verb form "to position"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you position your chair beside mine?
   - She positioned herself right next to the celebrity.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Position the sensor adjacent to the primary measurement device.
   - The company has strategically positioned itself alongside industry leaders.
