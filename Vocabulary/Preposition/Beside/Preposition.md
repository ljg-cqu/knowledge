1. **Preposition**: beside (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* at the side of; next to someone or something
   - *(Comparison)* in comparison with; when compared to
   - *(Abstract)* in addition to; apart from
   
   **Synonyms**: next to (spatial), alongside (spatial), compared to (comparison), apart from (abstract), in addition to (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, distant from, far from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "beside" when indicating physical proximity or adjacency; when making comparisons between two entities; when adding supplementary information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse with "besides" (adverb meaning "in addition" or "moreover"); avoid when indicating precise location requiring "at," "on," or "in"; incorrect for indicating emotional support (use "by your side" instead)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: beside the point, beside the road, beside the lake, beside me/you/him
   - **Verb + prep**: sit beside, stand beside, walk beside, place beside, set beside
   - **Adjective + prep**: (less common with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: seat beside, position beside, house beside
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by, near) + side (flank, edge) → "by the side"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "be sīdan" (by the side of) → Middle English "biside" → Modern English "beside"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Come sit beside me and tell me what happened.
   - Your problems seem small beside what they're going through.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research findings appear modest beside the groundbreaking discoveries of previous decades.
   - The new facility will be constructed beside the existing administrative building.

2. **Preposition**: next to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* immediately adjacent to; at the side of
   - *(Sequence)* following in order or position
   
   **Synonyms**: beside, alongside, adjacent to, by
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, far from, opposite to
   
   **When to Use**: Use "next to" for indicating immediate adjacency; common in everyday speech to show physical closeness; appropriate for describing spatial arrangements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in highly formal academic writing where "adjacent to" or "beside" may be preferred; not suitable for abstract or metaphorical relationships
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: next to nothing, next to impossible, next to each other
   - **Verb + prep**: sit next to, stand next to, park next to, live next to
   - **Adjective + prep**: (typically not used)
   - **Noun + prep**: seat next to, house next to, office next to
   
   **Root Analysis**: next (nearest, immediately following) + to (preposition indicating direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "nēhst" (nearest) + "to" → Middle English "next to" → indicating immediate proximity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I sit next to you on the bus?
   - There's a coffee shop next to the library.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The laboratory is located next to the research center on the university campus.
   - Please ensure the files are stored next to the corresponding documentation.

3. **Preposition**: alongside (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* close to the side of; parallel to
   - *(Abstract)* together with; in cooperation with
   - *(Comparison)* at the same time as; in addition to
   
   **Synonyms**: beside, next to (spatial), together with (abstract), in tandem with (cooperation)
   
   **Antonyms**: opposite to, away from, separately from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "alongside" for parallel positioning; when describing collaborative work or joint efforts; in formal and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple "beside" would be clearer; not appropriate for indicating mere presence without parallel arrangement
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: alongside the road, alongside colleagues, work alongside
   - **Verb + prep**: work alongside, run alongside, walk alongside, develop alongside
   - **Adjective + prep**: positioned alongside, placed alongside
   - **Noun + prep**: collaboration alongside, development alongside
   
   **Root Analysis**: along (in a line, lengthwise) + side → "along the side of"
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "along" + "side" → indicating parallel positioning and collaborative action
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I parked my car alongside yours.
   - She's been working alongside me for five years now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new curriculum will be implemented alongside existing educational frameworks.
   - This research was conducted alongside international collaborators from three institutions.

4. **Preposition**: by (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* next to; at the side of; near
   - *(Agency)* through the action or means of
   - *(Temporal)* not later than; before
   - *(Measurement)* according to; in units of
   
   **Synonyms**: beside, next to (spatial), through (agency), before (temporal), according to (measurement)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, far from (spatial), after (temporal)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "by" for proximity in all contexts; indicating the agent of passive constructions; specifying deadlines; showing means or method
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "beside" provides clearer spatial meaning; be careful with "by the side" versus "beside" distinction
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by the way, by the side, by the window, by myself
   - **Verb + prep**: pass by, stand by, go by, live by, abide by
   - **Adjective + prep**: (less common)
   - **Noun + prep**: location by, position by
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (near, about) → by
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bī" (near, in, by) → indicating proximity and instrumentality
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Come stand by me so I can show you.
   - The book you want is by the lamp.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal must be submitted by the deadline specified in the guidelines.
   - The policy was developed by the committee after extensive consultation.

5. **Preposition**: adjacent to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* next to and joining with; bordering on; very close to
   
   **Synonyms**: next to, beside, neighboring, adjoining, contiguous to
   
   **Antonyms**: distant from, remote from, separated from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "adjacent to" in formal, technical, or academic contexts; when emphasizing immediate proximity or shared boundaries; in real estate, architecture, and geography
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Overly formal for casual conversation; avoid when simple "next to" or "beside" would be more natural; not used for abstract relationships
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: adjacent to the building, adjacent to the property, adjacent to each other
   - **Verb + prep**: located adjacent to, positioned adjacent to, situated adjacent to
   - **Adjective + prep**: immediately adjacent to, directly adjacent to
   - **Noun + prep**: property adjacent to, room adjacent to, facility adjacent to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + jacent (lying) → "lying near to"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "adjacēre" (to lie near) → Old French → English "adjacent" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Less common in casual speech) The park is adjacent to our neighborhood.
   - (More natural) The park is right next to our neighborhood.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed development will be constructed on land adjacent to the existing infrastructure.
   - The laboratory is located in the facility adjacent to the main research building.

6. **Preposition**: near (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* at or to a short distance from; close to
   - *(Temporal)* close to (a particular time)
   - *(Abstract)* close to (a particular state or condition)
   
   **Synonyms**: close to, beside, by, next to, in proximity to
   
   **Antonyms**: far from, distant from, remote from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "near" for general proximity without requiring immediate adjacency; indicating approximate time; showing closeness to a state or condition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when precise adjacency is meant (use "beside" or "next to"); not appropriate when "at" or "in" indicates specific location
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: near the end, near the beginning, near me, near here
   - **Verb + prep**: come near, draw near, live near, stay near
   - **Adjective + prep**: (preposition "near" can also function as adjective)
   - **Noun + prep**: area near, location near, position near
   
   **Root Analysis**: nēah (close, nigh) → near
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "nēar" (comparative of "nēah" = nigh) → indicating closer proximity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is there a good restaurant near here?
   - Come sit near me so we can talk.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research facility is located near the city center.
   - We are near completion of the project's initial phase.

7. **Preposition**: in comparison with/to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* when measured or judged against; relative to
   
   **Synonyms**: compared to, compared with, beside, against, relative to
   
   **Antonyms**: irrespective of, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "in comparison with/to" for formal comparisons; in academic writing; when explicitly highlighting relative differences or similarities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; avoid when simple "compared to" suffices; redundant in some contexts where comparison is implicit
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in comparison with last year, in comparison to other methods
   - **Verb + prep**: pale in comparison with, suffer in comparison to
   - **Adjective + prep**: small in comparison with, significant in comparison to
   - **Noun + prep**: results in comparison with, performance in comparison to
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + comparison (com- = with, together + par = equal) + with/to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "comparare" (to couple, match) → "comparison" → phrasal preposition "in comparison with/to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (More natural) My car is tiny compared to yours.
   - (Less common) How does it look in comparison with the original?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The current results appear modest in comparison with those obtained in previous studies.
   - In comparison to traditional methodologies, this approach demonstrates superior efficiency.

8. **Preposition**: apart from (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* except for; other than; excluding
   - *(Abstract)* in addition to; besides
   
   **Synonyms**: besides, except for, other than, aside from, in addition to
   
   **Antonyms**: including, together with
   
   **When to Use**: Use "apart from" for exceptions or exclusions; when adding supplementary information; in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous between "except" and "in addition" meanings; clarify context to avoid confusion
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: apart from that, apart from this, apart from the fact
   - **Verb + prep**: (less common direct pattern)
   - **Adjective + prep**: (typically sentence-initial modifier)
   - **Noun + prep**: (typically sentence-initial)
   
   **Root Analysis**: apart (separated, at a distance) + from → "separated from" or "in addition to"
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "apart" (separately) + "from" → dual meaning development
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Apart from John, everyone's coming to the party.
   - The movie was great, apart from the ending.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Apart from the methodological concerns raised, the research demonstrates significant merit.
   - The proposal, apart from offering immediate benefits, provides a framework for future development.
