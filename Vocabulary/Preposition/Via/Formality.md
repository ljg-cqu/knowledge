1. **Original Expression**: You can send the document via email  
   **Context**: Instructions for submitting documents in a business or academic setting
   
   **Core Intent**: Informing someone that email is an acceptable method for document submission
   
   **Casual**: Just email me the document  
   *Characteristics*: Simple verb "email," omits "the," direct command  
   *Use when*: Speaking to colleagues, peers, friends; informal work environments; quick verbal instructions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Please send the document via email  
   *Characteristics*: Includes "please," uses "via" for professional tone, complete noun phrase  
   *Use when*: Professional emails, general business communication, mixed-hierarchy settings, written instructions
   
   **Formal**: The document should be submitted via electronic mail  
   *Characteristics*: Full form "electronic mail," passive voice "should be submitted," impersonal construction  
   *Use when*: Official documentation, formal procedures, legal contexts, institutional guidelines
   
   **Humorous**: Fire it over via the digital postal service  
   *Characteristics*: Playful phrase "fire it over," ironic "digital postal service"  
   *Use when*: Casual work environments with established rapport, team communication with friendly dynamics  
   *Avoid when*: Formal settings, first communications, with superiors or unfamiliar parties
   
   **Lively**: Shoot me that document via email and let's get this moving!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic "shoot me," enthusiastic "let's get this moving," exclamation mark  
   *Use when*: Collaborative projects requiring urgency, motivating team members, expressing enthusiasm for progress
   
   **Empathetic**: I know you're busy, but when you get a chance, could you send the document via email?  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges workload "I know you're busy," softens request "when you get a chance," tentative "could you"  
   *Use when*: Requesting from overwhelmed colleagues, showing consideration for others' time, sensitive professional relationships
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher authority (formal), peers (semi-formal to casual), subordinates (semi-formal with politeness)
   - *Social Distance*: First contact (formal/semi-formal), established relationship (casual), strangers (semi-formal)
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Official submission (formal), routine work (semi-formal), quick exchange (casual)
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect higher formality in professional settings regardless of relationship
   - *Medium*: Written formal documentation (formal), email (semi-formal), text/chat (casual)
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "email" as verb in very formal contexts: "Please email the document" sounds too casual for official procedures
   - Overusing formality in quick exchanges: "The document should be transmitted via electronic mail" sounds stilted in routine work
   - Mixing registers: "You can submit the doc via electronic mail" combines casual "doc" with formal "electronic mail"
   
   **Cultural Notes**: British English tends to use "via" more frequently than American English, which often prefers "by email" in casual contexts. Asian professional cultures may expect higher formality levels even among peers.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: First email to potential employer → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("should be submitted via electronic mail")
   - **Situation 2**: Daily communication with team member → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("just email me the doc")
   - **Situation 3**: Written procedure manual → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("documents must be submitted via email")
