1. **Adjective**: prompt (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Done or given without delay; immediate or quick (non-gradable in some contexts)
   - Arriving or acting at the expected or arranged time; punctual
   
   **Synonyms**: quick, immediate, swift, timely, speedy
   
   **Antonyms**: delayed, late, slow, tardy
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts emphasizing timeliness; customer service; business correspondence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing thoughtful, deliberate processes where speed is not valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prompt response
   - prompt reply
   - prompt action
   - prompt service
   - prompt payment
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "promptus" = ready, quick
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "promptus" (brought forth, ready) → Old French "prompt" → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for the prompt reply! I really appreciate it.
   - The customer service was super prompt this time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We anticipate a prompt response to our inquiry regarding the contract terms.
   - Prompt payment of invoices is required to maintain account standing.

1. **Adjective**: immediate (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Happening or done without delay; instant
   - Occurring at once without any intervening time or space
   
   **Synonyms**: instant, instantaneous, direct, prompt, swift
   
   **Antonyms**: delayed, gradual, eventual, postponed
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing zero delay or direct connection; urgent situations; cause-and-effect relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Situations where some time lag is expected or acceptable
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immediate response
   - immediate reaction
   - immediate effect
   - immediate action
   - immediate attention
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (not) + mediate (Latin "mediatus" = in the middle)
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin "immediatus" (without anything between) → Middle French → English (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need an immediate answer - we don't have much time!
   - Her immediate reaction was to call the police.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy change had an immediate impact on operational procedures.
   - Immediate action is required to address the identified security vulnerabilities.

1. **Adjective**: delayed (non-gradable, past participle used as adjective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Happening or done later than expected or intended
   - Postponed or held back from the original time
   
   **Synonyms**: postponed, deferred, late, overdue, belated
   
   **Antonyms**: prompt, immediate, timely, punctual
   
   **When to Use**: Describing responses, actions, or events that occur later than planned or expected
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Situations where timing met expectations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - delayed response
   - delayed reaction
   - delayed reply
   - delayed effect
   - delayed delivery
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, from) + lay (Old French "laier")
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "delaier" (to put off) → Middle English (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry for the delayed response - I've been swamped.
   - His delayed reaction suggested he didn't fully understand what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delayed response from regulatory authorities created uncertainty in the market.
   - Delayed implementation of the recommendations may compromise program effectiveness.

1. **Adjective**: appropriate (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suitable or proper for a particular person, situation, or purpose
   - Fitting or acceptable according to circumstances or standards
   
   **Synonyms**: suitable, fitting, proper, apt, relevant
   
   **Antonyms**: inappropriate, unsuitable, improper, irrelevant
   
   **When to Use**: Evaluating suitability of responses, actions, or choices in various contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When absolute correctness rather than suitability is required
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - appropriate response
   - appropriate action
   - appropriate measures
   - appropriate behavior
   - appropriate to the situation
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + proprius (Latin = one's own) + -ate
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin "appropriatus" → English (15th century) - originally meant "belonging to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't think that was an appropriate response to her question.
   - What's the appropriate thing to say in this situation?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee determined that the agency's response was appropriate given the circumstances.
   - Researchers must select appropriate methodologies based on their research questions.

1. **Adjective**: timely (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Done or happening at the right time; neither too late nor too early
   - Occurring at a favorable or useful time; opportune
   
   **Synonyms**: opportune, well-timed, prompt, punctual, seasonable
   
   **Antonyms**: untimely, late, premature, belated
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and formal contexts emphasizing appropriateness of timing; business and legal settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When speed alone (not appropriateness of timing) is the focus
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - timely response
   - timely manner
   - timely fashion
   - timely intervention
   - timely information
   
   **Root Analysis**: time + -ly (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "tīma" + "-ly" → Middle English "timely" (opportune, seasonal)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for your timely help - it came right when I needed it!
   - Your timely reminder saved me from missing the deadline.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Timely submission of applications is required to ensure consideration.
   - The intervention proved timely, preventing further deterioration of the situation.

1. **Adjective**: responsive (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Quick to react or respond to suggestions, requests, or changes
   - Reacting readily and positively to input or influence
   
   **Synonyms**: reactive, receptive, sensitive, attentive, amenable
   
   **Antonyms**: unresponsive, indifferent, inert, uncooperative
   
   **When to Use**: Describing individuals, organizations, or systems that react well to input; customer service contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When independence or resistance to influence is valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - responsive to feedback
   - responsive to needs
   - highly responsive
   - responsive service
   - responsive government
   
   **Root Analysis**: respond + -ive (adjective suffix indicating tendency)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "responsus" + "-ive" → English (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The customer service team has been really responsive to all my questions.
   - She's very responsive to suggestions and criticism.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization demonstrated a responsive approach to stakeholder concerns.
   - Effective governance requires institutions that are responsive to citizen needs.

1. **Adjective**: direct (non-gradable in this sense)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Straightforward and explicit, without intermediate stages or intermediaries
   - Going straight to the point without evasion or ambiguity
   
   **Synonyms**: straightforward, frank, explicit, candid, unambiguous
   
   **Antonyms**: indirect, evasive, oblique, circuitous
   
   **When to Use**: Contexts valuing clarity and straightforwardness; business communication; feedback
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Situations requiring diplomacy or tactful communication (may be perceived as blunt)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - direct response
   - direct answer
   - direct approach
   - direct communication
   - direct reply
   
   **Root Analysis**: di- (variant of dis- = apart) + rect (Latin "rectus" = straight)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "directus" (straight, direct) → Old French → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I appreciate your direct response - no beating around the bush.
   - Can you give me a direct answer? Yes or no?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report provides a direct response to each of the committee's recommendations.
   - Direct communication with stakeholders is essential for project success.

1. **Adjective**: comprehensive (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Complete and including everything that is necessary; thorough
   - Covering or dealing with all or nearly all aspects of something
   
   **Synonyms**: thorough, complete, exhaustive, extensive, all-inclusive
   
   **Antonyms**: partial, incomplete, limited, superficial
   
   **When to Use**: Academic, professional contexts emphasizing thoroughness; formal reports and analyses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Quick, brief exchanges where brevity is valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - comprehensive response
   - comprehensive answer
   - comprehensive approach
   - comprehensive coverage
   - comprehensive analysis
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together, with) + prehend (Latin "prehendere" = to grasp) + -ive
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "comprehensivus" (including much) → French → English (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Wow, that's a really comprehensive answer to my question!
   - I need a comprehensive response that covers all the issues.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agency provided a comprehensive response addressing all concerns raised by the audit.
   - A comprehensive analysis of the data reveals several significant trends.

1. **Adjective**: satisfactory (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Good enough for a particular purpose; meeting requirements or expectations
   - Adequate but not exceptional; acceptable
   
   **Synonyms**: acceptable, adequate, sufficient, reasonable, passable
   
   **Antonyms**: unsatisfactory, inadequate, unacceptable, insufficient
   
   **When to Use**: Formal evaluations, assessments, and situations requiring minimum standards to be met
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When excellence or exceptional quality is the standard
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - satisfactory response
   - satisfactory answer
   - satisfactory outcome
   - satisfactory level
   - satisfactory performance
   
   **Root Analysis**: satisfy (Latin "satisfacere" = to do enough) + -ory (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "satisfacere" (to do enough) → French "satisfactoire" → English (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is that response satisfactory, or do you need more information?
   - The explanation was satisfactory, though not particularly detailed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee deemed the institution's response to be satisfactory.
   - Performance levels must remain satisfactory to maintain accreditation status.

1. **Adjective**: measured (non-gradable, past participle used as adjective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Careful and controlled; not rushed or extreme
   - Showing careful thought and restraint
   
   **Synonyms**: careful, deliberate, calculated, restrained, considered
   
   **Antonyms**: hasty, impulsive, rash, extreme
   
   **When to Use**: Describing responses that are thoughtful and controlled; diplomatic or sensitive situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When spontaneity or quick reactions are valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - measured response
   - measured tone
   - measured approach
   - measured words
   - measured reaction
   
   **Root Analysis**: measure (Latin "mensura") + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: From "measure" (to assess carefully) → figurative use for "restrained, careful" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I appreciate your measured response instead of just blowing up.
   - He gave a measured reply, carefully choosing his words.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The government issued a measured response to the international crisis.
   - The chairman's measured tone helped defuse the contentious situation.
