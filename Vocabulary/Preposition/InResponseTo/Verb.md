1. **Verb**: respond
   **Meaning**: 
   - To say or write something as a reaction to a question, statement, or situation (transitive/intransitive)
   - To react to something by taking action or showing a physical change
   
   **Synonyms**: reply, answer, react, retort, counter
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, disregard, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: Professional, academic, and everyday situations when acknowledging and addressing questions, requests, or situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts requiring immediate physical action without communication (use "react" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - respond to criticism
   - respond appropriately
   - respond in writing
   - respond promptly
   - fail to respond
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + spond (Latin "spondere" = to pledge, promise)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → Old French "respondre" → Middle English (14th century) - originally meant "to answer" or "make a return"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll respond to your email when I get home tonight.
   - She didn't respond to my text, so I guess she's busy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee responded to the allegations with a detailed written statement.
   - Patients responded positively to the new treatment protocol.

1. **Verb**: reply
   **Meaning**: 
   - To answer someone by saying or writing something (transitive/intransitive)
   - To respond to a letter, email, or message in writing
   
   **Synonyms**: answer, respond, write back, get back to, acknowledge
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, disregard
   
   **When to Use**: Everyday situations, especially for written or spoken communication; neutral formality
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in contexts requiring physical reactions or behavioral changes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reply to a message
   - reply in the affirmative
   - reply promptly
   - await a reply
   - reply by email
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + ply (Latin "plicare" = to fold)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "replicare" (to fold back) → Old French "replier" → Middle English (14th century) - originally meant "to repeat" or "say again"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you reply to my message when you have a chance?
   - He replied with a simple "yes" and walked away.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please reply to this invitation by March 15th.
   - The minister replied to questions during the parliamentary session.

1. **Verb**: react
   **Meaning**: 
   - To behave or change in a particular way as a result of or in response to something (intransitive)
   - To show immediate emotion or physical change when something happens
   
   **Synonyms**: respond, behave, act, reciprocate, counter
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, remain passive, stay neutral
   
   **When to Use**: Situations emphasizing immediate, instinctive, or emotional responses; physical or chemical changes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing thoughtful, deliberate written responses (use "respond" or "reply" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - react quickly
   - react negatively
   - react to criticism
   - react instinctively
   - react with anger
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + act (Latin "agere" = to do, drive)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin "reactus" → French "réagir" (17th century) → English "react" - originally used in scientific contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How did she react when you told her the news?
   - I reacted without thinking and said something stupid.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Markets reacted negatively to the announcement of increased interest rates.
   - The chemical compound reacts with oxygen to produce heat.

1. **Verb**: address
   **Meaning**: 
   - To deal with or respond to a problem, question, or issue (transitive)
   - To speak or write to someone directly about a particular subject
   
   **Synonyms**: tackle, handle, deal with, attend to, confront
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, avoid, evade, sidestep
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and academic contexts when dealing with issues, concerns, or questions systematically
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Informal casual conversation about simple questions (use "answer" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - address concerns
   - address the issue
   - address criticism
   - address a question
   - fail to address
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + dress (Latin "directus" = straight, direct)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ad" + "directiare" → Old French "adresser" → Middle English (14th century) - originally meant "to set straight"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to address this problem before it gets worse.
   - He addressed my concerns pretty quickly in the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The administration has addressed the stakeholders' concerns in a comprehensive report.
   - This study addresses a significant gap in the existing research literature.

1. **Verb**: acknowledge
   **Meaning**: 
   - To accept or admit the existence, truth, or importance of something (transitive)
   - To indicate that you have received or noticed something, especially a message or letter
   
   **Synonyms**: recognize, accept, admit, confirm, concede
   
   **Antonyms**: deny, reject, ignore, disavow
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts for confirming receipt or recognizing contributions; formal acknowledgment of facts or situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When detailed responses are needed (acknowledge alone indicates receipt but not full engagement)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - acknowledge receipt
   - acknowledge a contribution
   - acknowledge the problem
   - acknowledge an error
   - fail to acknowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + knowledge (Old English "cnāwan" = to know)
   
   **Etymology**: Prefix "a-" + "knowledge" (15th century) - formed in Middle English to mean "to own knowledge of"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - At least acknowledge that I was right about this!
   - She acknowledged my email but hasn't given me a real answer yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company acknowledged the allegations and promised a thorough investigation.
   - We acknowledge the limitations of this methodology in the discussion section.

1. **Verb**: counter
   **Meaning**: 
   - To respond to an argument, criticism, or attack by arguing back or taking opposite action (transitive/intransitive)
   - To oppose or contradict something
   
   **Synonyms**: refute, rebut, contradict, oppose, dispute
   
   **Antonyms**: agree, accept, concur, support
   
   **When to Use**: Debates, arguments, or situations requiring opposition or contradictory responses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple acknowledgments or neutral responses; overly aggressive for polite disagreements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - counter an argument
   - counter with evidence
   - counter criticism
   - counter a claim
   - counter effectively
   
   **Root Analysis**: counter- (against, opposite) from Latin "contra"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "contra" (against) → Old French "contre" → Middle English "counter" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He countered every point I made with his own examples.
   - She countered that argument pretty easily.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defense attorney countered the prosecution's claims with compelling evidence.
   - Critics countered that the methodology was fundamentally flawed.

1. **Verb**: rebut
   **Meaning**: 
   - To say or prove that a statement, argument, or criticism is false (transitive)
   - To argue against evidence or claims in a formal manner
   
   **Synonyms**: refute, counter, contradict, disprove, challenge
   
   **Antonyms**: confirm, support, validate, corroborate
   
   **When to Use**: Formal debates, legal contexts, academic arguments where strong opposition is necessary
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations; can sound overly formal or aggressive in everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rebut an argument
   - rebut the evidence
   - rebut accusations
   - rebut claims
   - attempt to rebut
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + but (Old French "boter" = to push, thrust)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "reboter" (to repel) → Anglo-French → Middle English (13th century) - legal term meaning "to contradict"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tried to rebut what he said, but nobody was listening.
   - She rebutted his points one by one during the discussion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The researchers rebutted the criticism by providing additional data and analysis.
   - Counsel for the defense rebutted the prosecution's characterization of the evidence.

1. **Verb**: retort
   **Meaning**: 
   - To reply quickly in an angry or humorous way (transitive/intransitive)
   - To make a sharp or witty reply to a comment or accusation
   
   **Synonyms**: snap back, counter, riposte, rejoin, answer back
   
   **Antonyms**: remain silent, accept quietly
   
   **When to Use**: Quick, sharp, or witty responses; often implies emotion or cleverness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal professional correspondence; situations requiring measured, thoughtful responses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - retort angrily
   - retort with wit
   - sharp retort
   - quick retort
   - retort sarcastically
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + tort (Latin "torquere" = to twist)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "retortus" (twisted back) → used metaphorically for "sharp reply" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "That's none of your business!" she retorted angrily.
   - He retorted with a joke that made everyone laugh.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The senator retorted that such accusations were baseless and politically motivated.
   - When questioned about the decision, the CEO retorted that the board had full confidence in the strategy.

1. **Verb**: answer
   **Meaning**: 
   - To say, write, or do something as a reaction to a question, letter, or situation (transitive/intransitive)
   - To provide information requested or respond to a need
   
   **Synonyms**: reply, respond, react, acknowledge, return
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, question, ask
   
   **When to Use**: Universal contexts - questions, requests, needs, calls; most neutral and widely applicable term
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing emotional or physical reactions (use "react" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - answer a question
   - answer the phone
   - answer to charges
   - answer in writing
   - answer honestly
   
   **Root Analysis**: and- (against) + swear (Old English "swerian")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "andswarian" (to swear against, reply) → Middle English "answeren" - one of the oldest response words in English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you answer my question about the homework?
   - She answered the door still wearing her pajamas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applicants must answer all questions on the form completely and accurately.
   - The defendant answered the charges with a plea of not guilty.

1. **Verb**: reciprocate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To respond to an action or gesture by making a similar one (transitive/intransitive)
   - To give or do something in return for something received
   
   **Synonyms**: return, requite, respond in kind, match, exchange
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, reject, refuse
   
   **When to Use**: Situations involving mutual exchange, especially of feelings, gestures, or actions; often implies balance or fairness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple question-answer exchanges; situations without expectation of equal exchange
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reciprocate feelings
   - reciprocate a gesture
   - reciprocate kindness
   - fail to reciprocate
   - reciprocate in kind
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + cipro (Latin "reciprocus" = returning, alternating)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "reciprocare" (to move back and forth) → English (16th century) - mathematical and mechanical term before social use
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I invited him to my party, but he never reciprocates when he has events.
   - She smiled at me, and I reciprocated warmly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The gesture was intended to reciprocate the hospitality shown during the previous visit.
   - Nations are expected to reciprocate trade concessions granted by their partners.
