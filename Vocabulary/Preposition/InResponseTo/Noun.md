1. **Noun**: response (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A spoken or written answer to a question, request, or situation
   - A reaction to something that has happened or been said
   
   **Synonyms**: reply, answer, reaction, feedback, acknowledgment
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, indifference, inaction
   
   **When to Use**: Universal contexts - professional, academic, casual; most common and neutral term for any type of answer or reaction
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing spontaneous emotion (use "reaction" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in response to
   - prompt response
   - positive response
   - give a response
   - await a response
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + spons (Latin "sponsus" = pledged)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "responsum" → Old French "response" → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I sent her a message but got no response yet.
   - What was his response when you told him the news?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization issued a formal response to the committee's recommendations.
   - Patient response to the treatment varied significantly across demographic groups.

1. **Noun**: reply (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that is said, written, or done as an answer or response
   - A written answer to a letter, email, or message
   
   **Synonyms**: response, answer, rejoinder, retort, acknowledgment
   
   **Antonyms**: question, inquiry, silence
   
   **When to Use**: Written and spoken communication contexts; slightly more formal than "answer" but less formal than "response"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Physical or behavioral reactions without verbal/written component
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in reply to
   - await a reply
   - send a reply
   - receive a reply
   - prompt reply
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + ply (Latin "plicare" = to fold)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "replicare" → Old French "replie" → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I didn't get a reply to my text from last night.
   - Her reply was short but friendly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please submit your reply to the invitation by the specified deadline.
   - The minister's reply to the parliamentary question was published in the official record.

1. **Noun**: reaction (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something you do, say, or feel because of something that has happened
   - An immediate and often instinctive response to a stimulus or situation
   
   **Synonyms**: response, reflex, answer, feedback, backlash
   
   **Antonyms**: inaction, passivity, indifference
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing emotional, physical, or immediate responses; scientific contexts (chemical/physical reactions)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Deliberate, well-considered written responses (use "response" or "reply" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immediate reaction
   - negative reaction
   - chemical reaction
   - gut reaction
   - knee-jerk reaction
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + action (Latin "actio" = doing, performance)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin "reactio" → French "réaction" (17th century) - originally scientific term
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What was everyone's reaction to the announcement?
   - My first reaction was to laugh, but then I realized he was serious.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Market reactions to the policy change were swift and predominantly negative.
   - The study examined patient reactions to various dosage levels of the medication.

1. **Noun**: feedback (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Information or comments about how well or badly someone has done something, which is given to help them improve
   - Response or reactions to a particular process, product, or service
   
   **Synonyms**: response, input, comments, evaluation, critique
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, disregard
   
   **When to Use**: Professional, educational, and technical contexts; emphasizes constructive or evaluative nature of response
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple yes/no answers or brief acknowledgments
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - provide feedback
   - receive feedback
   - constructive feedback
   - feedback loop
   - positive feedback
   
   **Root Analysis**: feed (provide input) + back (in return)
   
   **Etymology**: Originally electronics/engineering term (1920s) → general usage (1940s-1950s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you give me some feedback on my presentation?
   - I really appreciate your feedback on the draft.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participant feedback was collected through anonymous surveys administered post-intervention.
   - The system incorporates user feedback to continuously improve performance.

1. **Noun**: acknowledgment (C/U, also: acknowledgement)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A statement or action that recognizes something has been received, recognized, or is true
   - Official recognition of the existence or validity of something
   
   **Synonyms**: recognition, confirmation, acceptance, admission, receipt
   
   **Antonyms**: denial, rejection, disavowal
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts where official recognition or confirmation is required; professional correspondence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Detailed substantive responses (acknowledgment confirms receipt but doesn't address content)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - send an acknowledgment
   - in acknowledgment of
   - acknowledgment of receipt
   - acknowledgment letter
   - written acknowledgment
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + knowledge + -ment (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: "Acknowledge" (15th century) + "-ment" (noun suffix)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need at least an acknowledgment that you got my message.
   - She nodded in acknowledgment when I waved.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company issued a formal acknowledgment of the data breach within 24 hours.
   - Please confirm your acknowledgment of these terms by signing below.

1. **Noun**: rejoinder (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A quick and often clever or critical reply to something that has been said
   - A response to a reply, especially in legal or formal debate contexts
   
   **Synonyms**: retort, comeback, riposte, counter, rebuttal
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, acceptance
   
   **When to Use**: Formal debates, legal contexts, or literary descriptions of sharp verbal exchanges
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Everyday casual conversations (sounds archaic or overly formal); simple responses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sharp rejoinder
   - witty rejoinder
   - offer a rejoinder
   - rejoinder to criticism
   - file a rejoinder
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + join + -der (agent suffix from French)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "rejoindre" (to answer to a legal charge) → Middle English (15th century) - legal term
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He had a clever rejoinder ready for every criticism.
   - Her rejoinder was so perfect that everyone laughed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The plaintiff's attorney filed a rejoinder to the defendant's counterclaim.
   - The minister delivered a sharp rejoinder to opposition questions during the debate.

1. **Noun**: retort (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A quick, angry, or witty reply to something someone has said
   - A sharp or clever response to criticism or accusation
   
   **Synonyms**: rejoinder, comeback, riposte, counter, rebuttal
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, acceptance, agreement
   
   **When to Use**: Describing sharp, clever, or emotional verbal responses; literary or narrative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Professional correspondence or formal written responses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sharp retort
   - witty retort
   - angry retort
   - fire back a retort
   - deliver a retort
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + tort (Latin "torquere" = to twist)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "retortus" (twisted back) → metaphorical use for "sharp reply" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His retort was so sharp that it left everyone speechless.
   - She delivered a witty retort that made everyone laugh.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The senator's retort to the accusation was both swift and forceful.
   - The author's retort to critics appeared in the subsequent edition's preface.

1. **Noun**: rebuttal (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A statement or argument that refutes or contradicts another statement
   - Evidence or reasoning offered in opposition to an argument or claim
   
   **Synonyms**: refutation, counter-argument, contradiction, denial, disproof
   
   **Antonyms**: confirmation, agreement, support, validation
   
   **When to Use**: Formal debates, academic arguments, legal proceedings where systematic opposition is required
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple responses or acknowledgments; casual conversations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - offer a rebuttal
   - strong rebuttal
   - rebuttal argument
   - rebuttal evidence
   - in rebuttal
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + but (Old French "boter" = to push) + -al (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: "Rebut" (13th century) + "-al" (noun suffix) - legal and debate terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He prepared a detailed rebuttal to all the accusations.
   - Her rebuttal was convincing enough to change some minds.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defense presented a comprehensive rebuttal to the prosecution's forensic evidence.
   - The research team published a rebuttal to the methodological criticisms in a subsequent article.

1. **Noun**: counterargument (C, also: counter-argument)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An argument or set of reasons put forward to oppose an idea or theory
   - A response that presents an opposing viewpoint or contradictory evidence
   
   **Synonyms**: rebuttal, refutation, objection, opposition, contrary argument
   
   **Antonyms**: support, agreement, confirmation, corroboration
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, formal debates, critical analysis where opposing views are systematically presented
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple disagreements; contexts not requiring structured argumentation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - present a counterargument
   - strong counterargument
   - address counterarguments
   - anticipate counterarguments
   - refute counterarguments
   
   **Root Analysis**: counter- (against) + argument (Latin "argumentum")
   
   **Etymology**: "Counter" + "argument" - compound formed in English (17th century) for logical discourse
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you have any counterarguments to what I just said?
   - She raised several counterarguments that I hadn't considered.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author anticipates potential counterarguments in the discussion section.
   - Researchers must address counterarguments to establish the validity of their conclusions.

1. **Noun**: correspondence (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The letters, emails, or messages that someone sends and receives
   - The activity of writing and receiving letters or emails
   
   **Synonyms**: communication, letters, messages, mail, exchanges
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, disconnection
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts referring to written communication exchanges, especially professional or official
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Verbal conversations or informal text messages
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - business correspondence
   - enter into correspondence
   - maintain correspondence
   - official correspondence
   - correspondence with
   
   **Root Analysis**: correspond (co- + respond) + -ence (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin "correspondentia" → French "correspondance" → English (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been in correspondence with her for months about this project.
   - Keep all your correspondence in one folder so you can find it easily.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All official correspondence must be conducted through the designated channels.
   - The archive contains extensive correspondence between the two scholars spanning three decades.
