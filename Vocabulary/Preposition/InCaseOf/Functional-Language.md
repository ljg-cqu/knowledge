1. **Functional Expression**: In case of ..., ... (function: giving safety instructions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A fixed pattern used to introduce what people must do when a specific emergency, risk, or problem happens.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit instructions)
   
   **Synonyms**: If there is ..., In the event of ..., Should ... occur, In case of emergency, ...
   
   **Antonyms**: Regardless of ..., Even if ..., Despite ... (expressions that remove conditional limits)
   
   **When to Use**: Safety notices, manuals, building instructions, policies, and public signs that tell people what to do in case of fire, accident, outage, or similar events.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation ("If there's a fire" is more natural); when you need a clause rather than a noun phrase (*"in case it rains"*, not *"in case of it rains"*).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - In case of emergency, call ...
   - In case of fire, use the stairs.
   - In case of doubt, contact your supervisor.
   - In case of system failure, follow the backup procedure.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals official, rule‑like instructions that only apply in special situations. Often appears in all caps on signs. Focuses on clear behaviour, not emotions.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, so if that happens, we should...
   - Got it—so in that case, I need to...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The sign says, "In case of fire, use the stairs, not the elevator."
   - Put my number down as the one to call in case of emergency.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In case of emergency, occupants must follow the posted evacuation routes.
   - In case of system failure, operations shall be transferred to the designated backup environment.

1. **Functional Expression**: If anything goes wrong, ... (function: offering contingency support/reassurance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to promise help or describe backup actions if problems appear.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct about support, indirect about the possibility of problems
   
   **Synonyms**: If there are any problems..., If something happens..., Should anything go wrong..., In case of any issues, ...
   
   **Antonyms**: If everything goes as planned..., Assuming nothing goes wrong..., Once everything is fine, ...
   
   **When to Use**: Reassuring someone before a trip, task, or event; explaining backup options; setting expectations for support in case of difficulties.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal contracts and policies (prefer "Should any issues arise..."); situations where mentioning problems may create unnecessary anxiety.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - If anything goes wrong, call me.
   - If anything goes wrong, we'll switch to the backup plan.
   - If anything goes wrong, follow the emergency procedure.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Reduces anxiety by showing that the speaker has thought about in‑case‑of scenarios. Also softens the possibility of failure by wrapping it in reassurance.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks, that's reassuring.
   - Okay, so if something does go wrong, I'll...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If anything goes wrong on the trip, call me right away.
   - If anything goes wrong with the event, we’ll just move it indoors.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If anything goes wrong during deployment, the team will immediately roll back to the previous stable version.
   - If anything goes wrong with the service, operations will be restored from the backup site.

1. **Functional Expression**: In the event of ..., ... (function: specifying formal procedures)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A very formal way to say "if" when you describe what must happen in case of specific events.
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct (obligations and procedures)
   
   **Synonyms**: In case of ..., Should ... occur, If ..., In the event that ...
   
   **Antonyms**: Regardless of whether ..., Notwithstanding ..., Even if ... (expressions that disconnect the condition from the result)
   
   **When to Use**: Contracts, policies, SLAs, safety procedures, and legal documents outlining obligations in emergencies or exceptional situations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Everyday speech or casual writing; speaking to learners with very low proficiency where plain "if" is more transparent.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - In the event of fire, do not use the elevators.
   - In the event of a data breach, the regulator must be notified.
   - In the event that evacuation is required, staff must assist visitors.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Frames in‑case‑of scenarios as part of a rule system. Often appears together with modal verbs like "shall" or "must" that signal strong obligation.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Understood; if that happens, we...
   - So our obligations in that event are...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare; speakers would normally say "If there's a fire...")
   - In the event of bad weather, we'll move things indoors. (Sounds semi‑formal.)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the event of a major outage, services shall be restored from the designated backup site.
   - In the event of a medical emergency, staff must follow the approved escalation protocol.

1. **Functional Expression**: Should there be any ..., ... (function: formal conditional + instruction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Uses inversion with "should" to introduce a formal conditional clause meaning "if there is any ...".
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect form, but very clear obligation
   
   **Synonyms**: If there is any..., In case of any..., In the event of any..., Should any ... arise, ...
   
   **Antonyms**: Unless there is any..., Even if there is no..., Provided that there are no ...
   
   **When to Use**: Written notices, instructions, and policies that explain what to do in case of questions, incidents, or emergencies.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Spontaneous conversation, text messages, or simple instructions for beginners.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Should there be any questions, please contact ...
   - Should there be any doubt in case of emergency, follow this procedure.
   - Should there be any incident, inform security immediately.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Polite, slightly distant tone that suits formal relations and documents. Keeps the focus on procedure rather than on personal feelings.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you, we will.
   - Understood; we'll contact you if anything arises.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Uncommon in speech; would usually be "If you have any questions, just ask.")
   - Should there be any noise tonight, just text me. (Sounds jokingly formal.)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Should there be any uncertainty in case of emergency, staff must follow the posted instructions.
   - Should there be any incident, the duty manager shall complete an incident report form.

1. **Functional Expression**: As a precaution, ... (function: introducing preventive action)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces actions taken before problems appear, just in case something goes wrong.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct about prevention
   
   **Synonyms**: Just in case..., As a preventive measure..., To be on the safe side..., As a safety measure...
   
   **Antonyms**: Recklessly..., Without taking precautions..., Despite the risk..., Even though it's unsafe, ...
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining why you do extra checks, backups, or preparations in case of possible emergencies or failures.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the action is mandatory, not optional (then use direct imperatives instead of the softer "as a precaution").
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As a precaution, keep copies of key documents in case of loss.
   - As a precaution, run regular backups in case of system failure.
   - As a precaution, close all windows in case of strong winds.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Softens instructions by framing them as wise preparation rather than strict rules. Useful when you want cooperation without sounding authoritarian.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That makes sense.
   - Good idea, I'll do that.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As a precaution, I always carry some cash in case of card problems.
   - As a precaution, we packed extra medicine in case of delays.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As a precaution, daily off‑site backups are maintained in case of primary‑site loss.
   - As a precaution, additional staff are scheduled in case of surge demand.

1. **Functional Expression**: Just in case, ... (function: signalling informal precaution)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Informal way to introduce an action done not because a problem is certain, but because it might happen.
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (focuses on the precaution, not the risk itself)
   
   **Synonyms**: Just to be safe..., You never know, so..., Better safe than sorry, so..., On the off chance that...
   
   **Antonyms**: No need to..., Don't bother..., There's no risk, so...
   
   **When to Use**: Everyday situations where you want to explain an extra step you are taking in case of possible problems—packing extra clothes, saving a backup file, arriving early, etc.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal documents, safety policies, or legal texts where clearer, less colloquial phrasing is required.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just in case, take an umbrella.
   - Take some cash, just in case.
   - I'll save another copy, just in case something crashes.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Creates a light, friendly tone around risk and preparation. It recognises uncertainty without sounding anxious or dramatic; often used to normalise reasonable caution.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yeah, good call.
   - True, better to be prepared.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just in case, keep my number with you in case of an emergency.
   - I'll print a hard copy of the tickets, just in case my phone dies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Typically avoided; rephrased as "As a precaution" or "As a preventive measure" in formal writing.)
   - As a precaution, a printed copy of all critical documents is retained in case of digital access issues.
