1. **Verb**: deal with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (phrasal verb, transitive) To handle, manage, or take action concerning a situation, problem, or person; to address or cope with something
   - *(Sense 2)* To do business with; to have commercial relations with
   
   **Synonyms**: handle, manage, address, tackle, cope with
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid, ignore, neglect, evade
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing problem-solving, handling difficult situations, managing responsibilities, or conducting business. Common in professional, academic, and everyday contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when more specific verbs would be clearer (use "solve" not "deal with" for mathematical problems in technical contexts). Don't use in contexts requiring emotional nuance where "process" or "work through" is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deal with pressure
   - deal with customers
   - deal with the consequences
   - deal with stress
   - learn to deal with
   
   **Root Analysis**: deal (Old English "dǣlan" = distribute, divide) + with (accompaniment preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "dǣlan" (to divide, share) → Middle English "delen" (to distribute, do business) → phrasal verb "deal with" emerged in 16th century meaning "have to do with, manage"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't deal with this drama right now.
   - How do you deal with annoying coworkers?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee was established to deal with emerging cybersecurity threats.
   - Organizations must develop strategies to deal with rapid technological change.

1. **Verb**: agree with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (phrasal verb, transitive) To have the same opinion as someone; to share someone's viewpoint or perspective
   - *(Sense 2)* (of food, climate, or conditions) To be suitable for someone; to not cause problems or discomfort
   
   **Synonyms**: concur with, share the view of, see eye to eye with, align with, support
   
   **Antonyms**: disagree with, differ from, oppose, contradict
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing concordance of opinion, harmony of views, or physical compatibility. Common in discussions, debates, and when discussing personal tolerance.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "agree to" (consent to a proposal) or "agree on" (reach consensus about something). Avoid when "support" or "endorse" would be more precise for formal positions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - agree with someone's opinion
   - agree with the decision
   - food doesn't agree with me
   - climate agrees with someone
   - agree with the assessment
   
   **Root Analysis**: agree (Latin "ad-" = to + "gratus" = pleasing) + with (accompaniment preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ad" + "gratus" → Old French "agreer" (to please, accept) → Middle English "agreen" → phrasal verb "agree with" developed to show alignment with person or thing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I totally agree with you on that.
   - Spicy food doesn't agree with me anymore.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research findings agree with previously published data on this phenomenon.
   - We agree with the committee's recommendations regarding policy reform.

1. **Verb**: provide with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive verb + preposition) To supply or furnish someone with something needed or wanted; to give or make available necessary resources, tools, or support
   
   **Synonyms**: supply with, furnish with, equip with, present with, endow with
   
   **Antonyms**: deprive of, withhold from, deny, strip of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing the act of supplying necessary items, resources, information, or support to someone. Common in formal, professional, and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "give" would be simpler and more natural in casual contexts. Don't use when the object is obvious from context (prefer "provide information" to "provide him with information" when "him" is already established).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - provide with information
   - provide with resources
   - provide with support
   - provide with equipment
   - provide with access
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward, for) + videre (to see, foresee) + with (instrumental preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "providere" (to foresee, provide) → Old French "provider" → Middle English "providen" → Modern English developed transitive pattern "provide [someone] with [something]"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They provided us with free snacks at the meeting.
   - Can you provide me with your email address?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution provides students with comprehensive academic and career counseling services.
   - Participants were provided with detailed instructions and informed consent documentation.

1. **Verb**: associate with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (phrasal verb, transitive) To connect or link mentally; to make a mental connection between ideas, concepts, or things
   - *(Sense 2)* To spend time with or be in the company of someone socially; to have social connections
   
   **Synonyms**: connect with, link with, relate to, correlate with, socialize with (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: dissociate from, separate from, disconnect from, distance oneself from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing mental connections between concepts (academic/analytical contexts) or social relationships (general contexts). Common in psychology, sociology, and everyday social discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "connect" or "link" alone would be clearer. Don't use in formal contexts when "affiliate with" or "collaborate with" is more precise for professional relationships.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - associate with negative outcomes
   - associate with a particular group
   - associate one thing with another
   - closely associated with
   - commonly associated with
   
   **Root Analysis**: as- (to, toward) + sociare (to join, unite) + with (accompaniment preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "associare" (to unite, join) → Middle French "associer" → English "associate" (16th century) → phrasal verb pattern "associate with" developed for both mental and social connections
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I always associate that song with summer vacations.
   - You shouldn't associate with people who bring you down.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research has consistently associated regular exercise with improved cognitive function.
   - The archaeological findings are associated with the Bronze Age civilization.

1. **Verb**: comply with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (phrasal verb, transitive) To act in accordance with rules, regulations, requests, or standards; to conform to requirements or commands
   
   **Synonyms**: adhere to, conform to, follow, obey, observe, abide by
   
   **Antonyms**: violate, breach, contravene, defy, disobey
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, legal, regulatory, or professional contexts when discussing adherence to rules, standards, regulations, or formal requests. Essential in business and legal communication.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "follow" or "do what someone asks" is more natural. Don't use for informal suggestions where it sounds overly rigid.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - comply with regulations
   - comply with requirements
   - comply with standards
   - fail to comply with
   - comply with the law
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together, with) + plere (to fill) + with (instrumental preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "complere" (to fill up, complete) → Italian "complire" (to be courteous, fulfill) → English "comply" (17th century, to yield, agree) → phrasal pattern emphasizing accordance with external requirements
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just do what they're asking, okay?
   - Follow the instructions they gave you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All organizations must comply with data protection regulations by the specified deadline.
   - Failure to comply with safety protocols may result in immediate suspension of operations.

1. **Verb**: interfere with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (phrasal verb, transitive) To prevent something from happening or developing normally; to disrupt, hinder, or obstruct a process, activity, or situation
   
   **Synonyms**: disrupt, hinder, obstruct, impede, hamper, disturb
   
   **Antonyms**: facilitate, assist, support, enhance, promote
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing actions or factors that negatively impact processes, plans, or activities. Common in professional, academic, and everyday contexts when discussing obstacles or disruptions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "affect" would be more neutral (interfere implies negative impact). Don't confuse with "intervene in" which can have positive connotations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - interfere with work
   - interfere with plans
   - interfere with sleep
   - interfere with normal functioning
   - don't interfere with
   
   **Root Analysis**: inter- (between, among) + ferire (to strike) + with (object preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "entreferer" (to strike each other) → Latin "inter" + "ferire" → Middle English "interferen" → Modern phrasal verb "interfere with" emphasizing disruption or obstruction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't let stress interfere with your sleep.
   - The noise is interfering with my concentration.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - External factors may interfere with the accuracy of experimental measurements.
   - Certain medications can interfere with the effectiveness of other treatments.

1. **Verb**: equipped with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (past participle adjective/passive verb) Having been provided with necessary tools, supplies, knowledge, or abilities; furnished with equipment or capabilities
   
   **Synonyms**: furnished with, supplied with, fitted with, armed with, provided with
   
   **Antonyms**: deprived of, lacking, without, stripped of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing possession of tools, resources, skills, or features. Common in technical descriptions, product specifications, and when discussing preparedness or capabilities.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "has" or "includes" would be simpler for basic descriptions. Don't use for abstract qualities where "characterized by" is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - equipped with tools
   - equipped with knowledge
   - equipped with modern technology
   - fully equipped with
   - well-equipped with
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (out) + quip (Old Norse "skipa" = to arrange) + -ed (past participle) + with (instrumental preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "skipa" (to arrange, order) → Old French "esquiper" (to fit out a ship) → English "equip" (16th century) → passive construction "equipped with" emphasizing provision or furnishing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The apartment comes equipped with all new appliances.
   - I'm not equipped with the skills to fix that.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The laboratory is equipped with state-of-the-art analytical instrumentation.
   - Participants were equipped with theoretical frameworks necessary for critical analysis.

1. **Verb**: correspond with
   **Meaning**: 
   - (phrasal verb, intransitive) To communicate by exchanging letters or emails; to maintain written communication
   - *(Sense 2)* To be in agreement with; to match or align with something
   
   **Synonyms**: communicate with (sense 1), write to (sense 1), match with (sense 2), align with (sense 2), accord with (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: differ from, contradict, conflict with
   
   **When to Use**: Use for written communication (increasingly archaic for sense 1) or when describing alignment/matching relationships. Sense 2 is more common in modern formal and academic writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Correspond with" for letter-writing is dated; prefer "email," "message," or "communicate with" in contemporary contexts. For sense 2, "match" or "align with" may be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - correspond with colleagues (dated)
   - correspond with theoretical predictions
   - correspond with established patterns
   - closely correspond with
   - findings correspond with
   
   **Root Analysis**: cor- (together) + respond (to answer, reply) + with (accompaniment preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "correspondere" (to answer, be in harmony with) → Medieval Latin (mutual written communication) → English "correspond" (16th century) → phrasal verb for both communication and alignment meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The colors match perfectly.
   - I've been emailing back and forth with her.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The empirical data correspond with predictions derived from the theoretical model.
   - The architectural features correspond with stylistic conventions of the Georgian period.
