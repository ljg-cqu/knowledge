1. **Adverb**: along with (type: manner/addition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In addition to; together with; as well as; used to indicate that something or someone is included or accompanies something else
   - *Position*: typically sentence-initial or mid-sentence before noun phrase
   
   **Synonyms**: together with, in addition to, as well as, alongside, besides
   
   **Antonyms**: without, excluding, apart from, aside from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when adding information about what accompanies or is included with something. Common in formal and informal contexts to indicate accompaniment or addition.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simpler "and" would suffice. Don't overuse in lists where commas and "and" are clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - along with other things
   - along with several colleagues
   - along with the benefits
   - came along with
   - bring along with
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + long (beside, alongside) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "andlang" (alongside, during) → Middle English "along" → phrasal combination "along with" (accompanying, in addition to)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to the store, along with Sarah and Tom.
   - The apartment comes with parking, along with access to the gym.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report, along with supporting documentation, has been submitted for review.
   - Participants received financial compensation along with detailed feedback on their performance.

1. **Adverb**: together with (type: manner/combination)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In combination or conjunction with; in association with; used to indicate joint action, combination, or accompaniment
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence or sentence-initial, connecting elements
   
   **Synonyms**: along with, in combination with, combined with, in conjunction with, alongside
   
   **Antonyms**: separately from, apart from, independently of
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize combination, joint action, or collective consideration. Common in formal contexts, particularly academic and professional writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple "and" is clearer and more concise. Don't use redundantly with verbs that already imply combination.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - together with colleagues
   - together with other factors
   - taken together with
   - work together with
   - considered together with
   
   **Root Analysis**: to- (at, toward) + gather (assemble) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "togædere" (in a group, collectively) → Middle English "together" → phrasal combination "together with" emphasizing joint presence or action
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm working on this together with a couple of friends.
   - The main course, together with dessert, costs $30.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These findings, together with previous research, suggest a significant correlation.
   - The CEO, together with the board of directors, announced the strategic restructuring plan.

1. **Adverb**: simultaneously with (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At the same time as; occurring or existing at exactly the same time; in temporal coincidence with something else
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence or sentence-initial, indicating temporal relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: concurrently with, at the same time as, in parallel with, alongside, contemporaneously with
   
   **Antonyms**: before, after, subsequently to, prior to
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or technical contexts when emphasizing exact temporal coincidence or parallel occurrence. Common in academic, scientific, and professional writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "at the same time" is more natural. Don't use when "while" or "during" would be simpler.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - occur simultaneously with
   - happen simultaneously with
   - launch simultaneously with
   - develop simultaneously with
   - simultaneously with other events
   
   **Root Analysis**: simul (at the same time) + -taneous (adjective) + -ly (adverb suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "simul" (at the same time) → "simultaneous" (occurring together in time) → adverb "simultaneously" + prepositional phrase pattern
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The movie came out at the same time as the book.
   - Can you not talk while I'm trying to concentrate?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The product was launched simultaneously with a comprehensive marketing campaign.
   - Neural activation occurred simultaneously with behavioral responses in experimental trials.

1. **Adverb**: jointly with (type: manner/agency)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In cooperation or collaboration with; in a shared or combined manner with others; used to indicate shared responsibility or action
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence, indicating collaborative relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: in collaboration with, in cooperation with, in partnership with, together with, in conjunction with
   
   **Antonyms**: independently of, separately from, alone, unilaterally
   
   **When to Use**: Use in professional or formal contexts when emphasizing shared responsibility, collaborative effort, or joint ownership. Common in business, legal, and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "with" alone suffices. Don't use when individual contributions need emphasis over collaboration.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - work jointly with
   - developed jointly with
   - owned jointly with
   - undertaken jointly with
   - managed jointly with
   
   **Root Analysis**: join (Latin "jungere" = to join) + -t + -ly (adverb suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "jungere" (to join, unite) → Old French "joindre" → English "joint" (shared, combined) → adverb "jointly" + prepositional pattern
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're working on this together.
   - They bought the house together.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research was conducted jointly with international collaborators from three institutions.
   - The property is owned jointly with her business partner under a legal partnership agreement.

1. **Adverb**: directly with (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an immediate or unmediated manner with someone or something; without intermediaries or indirect channels
   - *Position*: typically modifying verbs, positioned before or after the verb
   
   **Synonyms**: immediately with, straight with, in person with, one-on-one with
   
   **Antonyms**: indirectly with, through intermediaries, via others
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing personal contact, unmediated communication, or immediate connection. Common in business contexts for highlighting direct relationships.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundancy with verbs like "contact" that already imply directness. Don't use when "personally" would be more appropriate for emphasis on individual involvement.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - communicate directly with
   - work directly with
   - speak directly with
   - interact directly with
   - deal directly with
   
   **Root Analysis**: direct (di- = apart + regere = to guide) + -ly (adverb suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "dirigere" (to set straight, guide) → "direct" (straight, undeviating) → adverb "directly" + prepositional construction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just talk to him directly instead of going through his assistant.
   - I prefer dealing directly with the owner.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Clients work directly with senior consultants throughout the engagement.
   - Researchers collaborated directly with community stakeholders to ensure cultural appropriateness.

1. **Adverb**: closely with (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a near or intimate manner; with careful attention or tight cooperation; in close proximity or relationship
   - *Position*: typically modifying verbs, emphasizing degree of proximity or intensity of relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: intimately with, tightly with, in close cooperation with, hand-in-hand with
   
   **Antonyms**: distantly from, loosely with, independently of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing tight cooperation, careful attention, or strong relationships. Common in professional contexts to highlight collaboration intensity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when physical proximity is literal (use "near" instead). Don't use when "carefully" would be more precise for attention to detail.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - work closely with
   - monitor closely with
   - collaborate closely with
   - associated closely with
   - aligned closely with
   
   **Root Analysis**: close (Latin "clausus" = shut, enclosed) + -ly (adverb suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "claudere" (to close, shut) → Old French "clos" → English "close" (near, tight) → adverb "closely" + prepositional pattern
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been working closely with the design team on this.
   - He's closely tied to that political group.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The department collaborates closely with industry partners to ensure curriculum relevance.
   - Findings closely correlate with theoretical predictions established in prior literature.

1. **Adverb**: actively with (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an engaged, participatory, or energetic manner; with active involvement or effort; not passively
   - *Position*: typically modifying verbs of engagement or participation
   
   **Synonyms**: energetically with, vigorously with, dynamically with, engagedly with
   
   **Antonyms**: passively with, inactively with, without engagement
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing energetic participation, deliberate engagement, or proactive involvement. Common in professional and educational contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "actively" alone (without "with") is more natural. Don't overuse when simple present tense implies ongoing action.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - engage actively with
   - work actively with
   - collaborate actively with
   - participate actively with
   - interact actively with
   
   **Root Analysis**: act (Latin "agere" = to do, drive) + -ive + -ly (adverb suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "activus" (active, practical) → Middle English "actif" → Modern "active" + adverb suffix + prepositional pattern
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's actively involved with the community center.
   - They're actively working with the developers on this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students are encouraged to engage actively with course materials through discussion and reflection.
   - The organization collaborates actively with governmental agencies to implement policy reforms.

1. **Adverb**: exclusively with (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Solely or only with; to the exclusion of others; in a manner that excludes all other options or partners
   - *Position*: typically modifying verbs, emphasizing singularity or exclusivity of relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: solely with, only with, uniquely with, purely with
   
   **Antonyms**: broadly with, inclusively with, in addition to others
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing singular relationships, exclusive arrangements, or restricted scope. Common in business, legal, and contractual contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when arrangements aren't truly exclusive. Don't use when "only" would be simpler and clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - work exclusively with
   - deal exclusively with
   - available exclusively with
   - partner exclusively with
   - focus exclusively with
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out, from) + claudere (to close) + -ive + -ly (adverb suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "excludere" (to shut out) → "exclusive" (excluding others) → adverb "exclusively" + prepositional pattern
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This designer only works with high-end clients.
   - I deal exclusively with one supplier for all my parts.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The firm partners exclusively with certified vendors meeting stringent quality standards.
   - This technology is available exclusively with premium subscription packages.
