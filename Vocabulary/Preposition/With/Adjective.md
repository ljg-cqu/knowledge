1. **Adjective**: satisfied with (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Pleased or content with something; having one's expectations or needs met by something or someone; experiencing contentment regarding a situation or outcome
   
   **Synonyms**: pleased with, content with, happy with, gratified by, fulfilled by
   
   **Antonyms**: dissatisfied with, unhappy with, displeased with, disappointed with
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express contentment or approval regarding results, situations, products, or services. Common in customer service, performance reviews, and personal satisfaction contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when enthusiastic terms like "thrilled" or "delighted" would better convey strong positive emotion. Don't use for minimal acceptance where "acceptable" is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - highly satisfied with
   - fully satisfied with
   - not satisfied with
   - satisfied with the results
   - satisfied with performance
   
   **Root Analysis**: satis (enough) + fac (make, do) + -ed (adjective suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "satisfacere" (to do enough, fulfill) → Old French "satisfier" → Middle English "satisfien" → adjective form "satisfied" + dependent preposition "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm pretty satisfied with how the project turned out.
   - Are you satisfied with your new phone?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Respondents indicated they were highly satisfied with the quality of service provided.
   - The committee is satisfied with the progress demonstrated in the quarterly report.

1. **Adjective**: familiar with (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having knowledge of or experience with something; well-acquainted or conversant with something through previous exposure or study
   
   **Synonyms**: acquainted with, conversant with, knowledgeable about, versed in, experienced in
   
   **Antonyms**: unfamiliar with, unacquainted with, ignorant of, inexperienced in
   
   **When to Use**: Use when indicating prior knowledge, experience, or comfort level with subjects, procedures, tools, or people. Essential in job qualifications and skill discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "expert in" would be more accurate for deep knowledge. Don't overstate familiarity when you have only superficial awareness.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very familiar with
   - quite familiar with
   - not familiar with
   - become familiar with
   - already familiar with
   
   **Root Analysis**: familia (household, family) + -ar (relating to) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "familiaris" (of a household, intimate) → Old French "familier" → English "familiar" (well-known, common) → adjective + dependent preposition "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not too familiar with that band.
   - Are you familiar with how this coffee maker works?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applicants should be familiar with current regulatory frameworks and compliance requirements.
   - Researchers familiar with qualitative methodologies were selected for the study.

1. **Adjective**: consistent with (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In agreement or harmony with something; not contradicting or conflicting with established facts, principles, or patterns; compatible or coherent with something
   
   **Synonyms**: compatible with, in accordance with, in line with, congruent with, coherent with
   
   **Antonyms**: inconsistent with, contrary to, incompatible with, at odds with
   
   **When to Use**: Use in academic, professional, or analytical contexts when discussing alignment between data, theories, findings, or behaviors. Important for logical arguments and scientific discourse.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where simpler terms like "matches" or "agrees with" are more natural. Don't use redundantly with verbs that already imply consistency.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - entirely consistent with
   - broadly consistent with
   - consistent with expectations
   - consistent with previous findings
   - not consistent with
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + sist (stand) + -ent (adjective suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "consistere" (to stand together, be firm) → Middle French "consistant" → English "consistent" (standing firm, compatible) + dependent preposition "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That story isn't consistent with what you told me before.
   - His behavior is consistent with someone who's stressed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The experimental results are consistent with theoretical predictions.
   - These findings are entirely consistent with previously published research on the topic.

1. **Adjective**: compatible with (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Able to exist or work together without conflict; able to be used together or to function together successfully
   - *(Sense 2)* (of people) Able to have a harmonious relationship; having similar personalities or interests
   
   **Synonyms**: consistent with, harmonious with, congruent with, suited to, well-matched with
   
   **Antonyms**: incompatible with, inconsistent with, conflicting with, unsuited to
   
   **When to Use**: Use in technical contexts for systems/software interoperability, in relationship contexts for interpersonal harmony, or in general contexts for things that work well together.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "works with" is simpler for basic functionality. Don't use for philosophical agreement where "consistent with" is more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fully compatible with
   - highly compatible with
   - not compatible with
   - compatible with most devices
   - compatible with each other
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + pati (suffer, endure) + -ible (able to) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin "compatibilis" (sympathetic) → French "compatible" → English (able to coexist harmoniously) + dependent preposition "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This charger isn't compatible with my phone.
   - I don't think we're compatible with each other.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new software is fully compatible with existing enterprise systems.
   - These policy objectives are compatible with broader organizational strategic goals.

1. **Adjective**: associated with (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Connected or linked with something; related to or involved with something; accompanied by or occurring in conjunction with something
   
   **Synonyms**: connected with, linked to, related to, correlated with, tied to
   
   **Antonyms**: dissociated from, unrelated to, disconnected from, independent of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing connections, relationships, or correlations between concepts, factors, or entities. Common in academic, medical, and analytical contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "caused by" would be more accurate for causal relationships. Don't use when simple "related to" is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - closely associated with
   - commonly associated with
   - strongly associated with
   - associated with increased risk
   - associated with better outcomes
   
   **Root Analysis**: as- (to, toward) + soci (companion) + -ate (verb) + -ed (adjective) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "associare" (to join, unite) → "associate" (connect, join) → past participle adjective "associated" + dependent preposition "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stress is associated with all kinds of health problems.
   - I don't want to be associated with that group.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Higher education levels are strongly associated with improved economic outcomes.
   - The syndrome is associated with a range of developmental abnormalities.

1. **Adjective**: pleased with (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling happy, satisfied, or gratified about something; experiencing positive emotions regarding an outcome or situation
   
   **Synonyms**: happy with, satisfied with, delighted with, content with, gratified by
   
   **Antonyms**: displeased with, unhappy with, disappointed with, dissatisfied with
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express positive emotions about results, actions, or situations. Common in feedback, reviews, and expressing approval.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when stronger emotions like "thrilled" or "ecstatic" are more accurate. Don't use sarcastically without clear contextual markers.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very pleased with
   - quite pleased with
   - pleased with the outcome
   - pleased with oneself
   - not pleased with
   
   **Root Analysis**: please (Latin "placere" = to please) + -ed (adjective suffix) + with

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really pleased with how the party went.
   - She seems pleased with her new job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board of directors is pleased with the company's performance this quarter.
   - We are pleased with the positive reception to our research findings.

1. **Adjective**: angry with (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling strong displeasure, hostility, or rage toward someone; experiencing irritation or annoyance directed at a person
   
   **Synonyms**: mad at, furious with, annoyed with, irritated with, upset with
   
   **Antonyms**: pleased with, happy with, satisfied with, content with
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing feelings of displeasure or hostility toward people. Note: "angry with" (people) vs. "angry about" (situations). British English often prefers "angry with"; American English also uses "angry at."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "frustrated by" would better describe disappointment with circumstances rather than people. Use "angry about" for situations, not "angry with."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very angry with
   - extremely angry with
   - angry with oneself
   - get angry with
   - shouldn't be angry with
   
   **Root Analysis**: anger (Old Norse "angr" = grief, sorrow) + -y (adjective suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "angr" (grief, affliction) → Middle English "angry" (troubled, hostile) → Modern English adjective + dependent preposition "with" (for persons)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't be angry with me, it wasn't my fault!
   - I'm so angry with myself for forgetting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Stakeholders were understandably angry with the delayed response to the crisis.
   - Patients may feel angry with healthcare providers when communication is inadequate.

1. **Adjective**: patient with (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Able to remain calm and tolerant when dealing with someone or something difficult; showing forbearance and understanding toward people or situations
   
   **Synonyms**: tolerant of, forbearing with, understanding with, calm with, lenient with
   
   **Antonyms**: impatient with, intolerant of, frustrated with, irritated by
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing tolerance, calmness, or willingness to wait when dealing with people, situations, or processes. Important in teaching, caregiving, and leadership contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing medical patients (different sense). Don't confuse "patient with people" vs. "patient about situations."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - very patient with
   - extremely patient with
   - be patient with
   - patient with children
   - patient with beginners
   
   **Root Analysis**: pati (suffer, endure) + -ent (adjective suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "patiens" (bearing, enduring) → Old French "pacient" → Middle English (enduring without complaint) → Modern adjective + dependent preposition "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for being so patient with me while I learn.
   - You need to be more patient with your little brother.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Educators must be patient with students who require additional support.
   - Effective managers demonstrate patience with team members during transitional periods.
