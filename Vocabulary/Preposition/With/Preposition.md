1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* in the company of; alongside or accompanying someone or something
   - *(Instrumental)* using something as a tool, instrument, or means
   - *(Abstract)* having or possessing a particular quality, feature, or characteristic; in a state of having something
   - *(Manner)* in a way that shows or involves a particular quality or emotion
   
   **Synonyms**: alongside (in spatial sense), by means of (in instrumental sense), having (in possessive sense), through (in instrumental sense), via (in instrumental sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: without, lacking, absent of, devoid of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "with" to express accompaniment (people or things together), instrumentality (tools/means), possession (having characteristics), or manner (how something is done). Common in all registers from casual conversation to academic writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts where "by" is required for passive voice agents ("The book was written by/*with John"). Don't confuse with "to" in direction contexts ("go to/*with the store"). Avoid redundancy with verbs that already imply accompaniment ("accompany with" is redundant).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with care, with difficulty, with pleasure, with pride, with enthusiasm
   - **Verb + prep**: deal with, agree with, disagree with, help with, provide with, supply with, associate with, connect with, communicate with
   - **Adjective + prep**: pleased with, satisfied with, familiar with, consistent with, compatible with, angry with, patient with, happy with
   - **Noun + prep**: relationship with, connection with, problem with, issue with, experience with, difficulty with
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "wiþ" meaning "against, opposite, toward"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wiþ" (against, opposite) → Middle English "with" (by, near, against, in opposition to) → Modern English "with" (meanings expanded to include accompaniment, instrument, manner)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to the movies with Sarah tonight.
   - Can you cut this with scissors?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research was conducted with rigorous methodological standards.
   - Organizations must comply with regulatory requirements established by governing bodies.

1. **Preposition**: with regard to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* concerning, about, or in relation to a particular subject or matter; used to introduce a topic
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding (formal), concerning (formal), with respect to (very formal), in relation to (formal), as regards (formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or professional contexts when introducing a specific topic or referring to a particular matter. Common in business correspondence, academic writing, and formal reports.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where it sounds overly formal ("about" is more natural). Don't use in contexts where a simpler preposition suffices ("write about" not "write with regard to" in everyday speech).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with regard to your question, with regard to the matter, with regard to recent developments
   - **Sentence position**: typically sentence-initial or after comma as transition
   - **Verb + phrasal prep**: speak with regard to, write with regard to, inquire with regard to
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (accompaniment) + regard (attention, consideration) + to (direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "regardare" (to look at, consider) → French "regarder" → English "regard" (consideration, attention) + phrasal construction developed in formal English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - So, about that thing you mentioned earlier...
   - What did you want to say about the project?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regard to your inquiry dated March 15th, we are pleased to provide the following information.
   - The committee will convene to discuss policy changes with regard to environmental sustainability.

1. **Preposition**: with the exception of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* excluding; not including; apart from; used to indicate that something or someone is excluded from a general statement
   
   **Synonyms**: except for (neutral), apart from (neutral), aside from (neutral), excluding (formal), barring (formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: including, together with, in addition to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making a general statement but needing to exclude one or more items from that statement. Common in both formal and informal contexts, though slightly more formal than "except."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "except" alone would be clearer and more concise, especially in casual speech. Don't use redundantly with other exclusionary words.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with the exception of one, with the exception of a few cases, with the exception of certain circumstances
   - **Sentence position**: mid-sentence or end-sentence modification
   - **Scope markers**: typically followed by noun phrase or pronoun
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (accompaniment) + the + exception (exclusion) + of (possession/relation)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "exceptio" (taking out, exclusion) → French "exception" → English phrasal construction combining "with" + "exception of" to create formal exclusionary marker
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone's coming except Tom.
   - I like all vegetables except Brussels sprouts.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All participants completed the survey with the exception of three individuals who withdrew consent.
   - The policy applies to all employees with the exception of executive management.

1. **Preposition**: with respect to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* concerning, regarding, or in relation to; used to specify the particular aspect or perspective being discussed
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding (formal), concerning (formal), with regard to (formal), in terms of (neutral), as to (formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Use in academic, technical, or professional contexts to introduce specific topics or frame discussions within particular parameters. Particularly common in mathematics, science, and formal analysis.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in everyday conversation where simpler alternatives ("about," "for") are more natural. Don't overuse in writing as it can create unnecessarily formal or bureaucratic tone.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with respect to time, with respect to previous findings, with respect to X axis
   - **Technical/Academic contexts**: "differentiate with respect to," "analyze with respect to"
   - **Sentence framing**: used to establish analytical perspective or framework
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (accompaniment) + respect (relation, reference) + to (direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "respectus" (regard, consideration, looking back) → English "respect" (relation, reference) + phrasal construction common in academic and technical English since 17th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What do you think about the new policy?
   - Let me know about your plans for the weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The derivative is calculated with respect to the independent variable.
   - With respect to your proposal, the board requires additional financial projections before proceeding.

1. **Preposition**: with reference to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* concerning, about, or in connection with; used to cite or refer back to something previously mentioned or documented
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding (formal), concerning (formal), in reference to (formal), referring to (neutral), about (casual)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal correspondence, especially business letters and official documents, when referring to previous communication, documents, or specific matters. Often used with dates or reference numbers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where it sounds excessively formal. Don't use when a simpler reference method would suffice.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with reference to your letter, with reference to the attached document, with reference to our conversation
   - **Business correspondence**: often followed by dates, reference numbers, or document titles
   - **Citation contexts**: formal acknowledgment of prior communication
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (accompaniment) + reference (citation, mention) + to (direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "referre" (to carry back, refer) → English "reference" (act of referring, citation) + phrasal construction standard in business English since 19th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - About that email you sent yesterday...
   - So, regarding what we talked about last week...
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With reference to your inquiry dated November 15, 2024, please find the requested documentation enclosed.
   - This report is submitted with reference to the annual compliance requirements outlined in Section 3.2.
