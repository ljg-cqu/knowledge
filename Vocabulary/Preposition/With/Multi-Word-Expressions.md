1. **Expression**: deal with (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To handle, manage, or take action concerning a problem, situation, or person; to address difficulties or responsibilities
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very commonly paired; "deal" rarely used without preposition in this sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: handle, manage, address, tackle, cope with, take care of
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid, ignore, neglect, evade
   
   **When to Use**: Use for problem-solving, managing responsibilities, handling difficult situations, or conducting business. Universal across registers from casual to formal.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when more specific verbs are available ("solve" for math problems, "treat" for medical issues). Don't use as euphemism when direct language is needed ("reject" not "deal with rejection").
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - have to deal with
   - learn to deal with
   - deal effectively with
   - deal directly with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Deal" evolved from "divide/distribute" to "handle/manage." The preposition "with" naturally followed to indicate the object being handled. This pairing became conventionalized in business and problem-solving contexts.
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "d«£lan" (divide, distribute) + "with" (instrumental/accompaniment preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't deal with this drama right now.
   - How do you deal with difficult customers?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations must develop comprehensive strategies to deal with cybersecurity threats.
   - The committee was established to deal with emerging regulatory compliance issues.

1. **Expression**: in accordance with (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In agreement with; following or complying with rules, standards, or expectations; conforming to established guidelines
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly formulaic expression, particularly in legal/formal contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: in compliance with, in conformity with, according to, pursuant to, following
   
   **Antonyms**: in violation of, contrary to, in defiance of
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, legal, professional, or regulatory contexts when emphasizing adherence to rules, standards, or procedures. Essential in compliance and legal writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "following" or "according to" is more natural. Don't overuse in general writing where simpler alternatives exist.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - strictly in accordance with
   - fully in accordance with
   - in accordance with regulations
   - in accordance with established procedures
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Accordance" (harmony, agreement) naturally pairs with "with" to indicate what one is in harmony with. This fixed phrase became standard in legal and formal English for expressing compliance.
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + cord (heart, agreement) + -ance (noun suffix) + with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just follow what the manual says.
   - Do it the way they told you to.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All procedures must be conducted in accordance with institutional review board guidelines.
   - The implementation was completed in accordance with the project timeline and specifications.

1. **Expression**: come up with (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: to move upward alongside something
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: to produce, create, or think of an idea, plan, or solution; to devise or invent something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (idiomatic meaning dominates; highly conventionalized expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: devise, think of, create, produce, generate, conceive, develop, formulate
   
   **Antonyms**: fail to think of, be stumped, draw a blank
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing idea generation, problem-solving, creative thinking, or producing solutions. Common in brainstorming, planning, and innovation contexts across all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when credit or attribution matters (use "develop" or "create" for more formal recognition). Don't use for discovered (rather than invented) solutions.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - come up with an idea
   - come up with a solution
   - come up with a plan
   - manage to come up with
   - couldn't come up with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - Originally metaphorical: ideas "come up" (emerge) "with" (accompanying) the thinker. The spatial metaphor of ideas rising became conventionalized into today's idiom meaning "produce/devise."
   
   **Root Analysis**: come (Old English "cuman" = arrive, move) + up (direction) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you come up with a better excuse than that?
   - I'm trying to come up with ideas for the party.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers came up with an innovative methodology to address data collection challenges.
   - The task force came up with comprehensive recommendations for policy reform.

1. **Expression**: in line with (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In agreement, harmony, or alignment with something; consistent with expectations, standards, or established patterns
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly conventionalized in formal/professional contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: consistent with, in accordance with, in agreement with, aligned with, conforming to
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, inconsistent with, out of line with, at odds with
   
   **When to Use**: Use in professional, business, or formal contexts when discussing alignment with strategies, policies, expectations, or standards. Common in corporate and organizational communication.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "matches" or "agrees with" is simpler. Don't use for literal physical lines or queues.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in line with expectations
   - in line with company policy
   - in line with industry standards
   - broadly in line with
   - directly in line with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Line" metaphorically represents alignment or arrangement. "In line with" extends this spatial metaphor to mean "aligned/consistent with," becoming standard business and formal English.
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (position) + line (row, alignment) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That matches what we discussed before.
   - Is this what you were expecting?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed budget is in line with strategic priorities established by the board.
   - Performance metrics are in line with industry benchmarks for organizations of comparable size.

1. **Expression**: put up with (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: to place upward alongside
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: to tolerate, endure, or accept something unpleasant or annoying; to bear with patience despite discomfort
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fully idiomatic; components don't retain literal meanings)
   
   **Synonyms**: tolerate, endure, bear, stand, accept, suffer, abide
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, refuse to tolerate, not stand for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing tolerance of unpleasant situations, annoying behaviors, or difficult circumstances. Common across all registers, though somewhat informal.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal writing where "tolerate" or "endure" is more appropriate. Don't use when active acceptance (rather than reluctant tolerance) is meant.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - put up with someone's behavior
   - won't put up with
   - have to put up with
   - refuse to put up with
   - tired of putting up with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - Etymology uncertain, but likely from nautical/lodging origins ("put up" = house/lodge + "with" = alongside). Metaphorical extension to "tolerate the presence of" became conventionalized.
   
   **Root Analysis**: put (place) + up (direction) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not putting up with this nonsense anymore.
   - How do you put up with that noise every day?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees should not be required to put up with harassment or discrimination.
   - Residents have put up with construction disruptions for over six months.

1. **Expression**: keep up with (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To maintain the same pace, level, or standard as someone or something else; to stay informed about or current with developments
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly conventionalized phrasal verb + preposition combination)
   
   **Synonyms**: stay current with, maintain pace with, match, stay abreast of, stay informed about
   
   **Antonyms**: fall behind, lag behind, lose track of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing maintaining pace (physical or metaphorical), staying informed, or matching standards. Common in discussions of progress, trends, and competition.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "follow" or "understand" would be clearer. Don't use for one-time catching up (use "catch up with" instead).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - keep up with the times
   - keep up with changes
   - keep up with demand
   - struggle to keep up with
   - hard to keep up with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Keep up" (maintain position/level) + "with" (alongside) creates transparent meaning of maintaining pace alongside someone/something. Strong collocation through frequent co-occurrence.
   
   **Root Analysis**: keep (maintain, hold) + up (maintained level) + with (alongside)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't keep up with all these new apps.
   - Are you keeping up with the show?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations must keep up with rapidly evolving technological innovations.
   - Students are expected to keep up with assigned readings throughout the semester.

1. **Expression**: in conjunction with (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Together with; in combination or cooperation with; working alongside or in coordination with something else
   - *Fixedness*: strong (formal fixed expression, particularly common in academic/professional writing)
   
   **Synonyms**: in combination with, together with, along with, in coordination with, alongside
   
   **Antonyms**: separately from, independently of, in isolation from
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, academic, or professional contexts when describing combined efforts, coordinated actions, or complementary elements. Common in research and business writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "with" or "together with" is simpler. Don't overuse when straightforward "and" would suffice.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - used in conjunction with
   - work in conjunction with
   - in conjunction with other methods
   - developed in conjunction with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Conjunction" (joining together) + "with" emphasizes combined action or coordination. This Latin-derived formal phrase became standard in academic and professional registers.
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + junct (join) + -ion (noun suffix) + with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Use this together with the other treatment.
   - We're working on this with the marketing team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention was implemented in conjunction with comprehensive staff training programs.
   - Qualitative analysis was conducted in conjunction with quantitative statistical methods.

1. **Expression**: get away with (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: to move away while bringing something
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: to escape punishment, detection, or consequences for wrongdoing; to do something wrong or inappropriate without being caught or penalized
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fully idiomatic expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: escape punishment for, avoid consequences for, go unpunished for, not be caught for
   
   **Antonyms**: be punished for, face consequences for, be caught for, answer for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing avoiding punishment, escaping detection, or succeeding with something questionable. Common across all registers when discussing accountability.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing legitimate success (use "accomplish" or "succeed"). Don't use in contexts where wrongdoing shouldn't be implied.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get away with murder (idiom within idiom = get away with anything)
   - let someone get away with
   - won't get away with
   - think you can get away with
   - get away with it
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - "Get away" (escape) + "with" (bringing along) originally meant physically escaping while retaining something. Metaphorical extension to escaping consequences while retaining benefit became idiomatic.
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (obtain, achieve) + away (from a place) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You're not going to get away with lying to me.
   - He always gets away with being late.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations cannot be permitted to get away with violating environmental regulations.
   - The investigation revealed how individuals had gotten away with fraudulent practices for years.
