1. **Noun**: relationship with (C, pl: relationships)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The way in which two or more people, groups, or things are connected, interact, or relate to each other; the state of being connected or associated
   - *(Sense 2)* A romantic or sexual connection between two people
   
   **Synonyms**: connection with, association with, rapport with, bond with, tie with
   
   **Antonyms**: disconnection from, separation from, estrangement from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing personal connections, professional associations, interpersonal dynamics, or connections between concepts or entities. Common in psychology, sociology, business, and everyday contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "connection" or simpler terms would suffice. In romantic contexts, be aware of cultural/contextual appropriateness.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - develop a relationship with
   - maintain a relationship with
   - healthy relationship with
   - professional relationship with
   - close relationship with
   
   **Root Analysis**: relate (re- = back + latus = carried) + -ion (noun suffix) + -ship (state, condition) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "relatus" (carried back, referred) → "relate" (to tell, connect) → "relationship" (19th century, state of being related) + prepositional phrase pattern with "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She has a great relationship with her boss.
   - I'm trying to build a better relationship with my parents.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization seeks to establish mutually beneficial relationships with strategic partners.
   - Research examines the relationship with environmental factors and cognitive development.

1. **Noun**: connection with (C, pl: connections)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A link, association, or relationship between people, things, or ideas; something that joins or relates different elements
   
   **Synonyms**: link with, association with, tie with, bond with, relationship with
   
   **Antonyms**: disconnection from, separation from, isolation from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing links between concepts, personal or professional networks, causal relationships, or logical associations. Versatile across formal and informal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "relationship" would be more appropriate for human interactions. Don't overuse in technical contexts where "interface" or "coupling" is more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - establish a connection with
   - make a connection with
   - strong connection with
   - emotional connection with
   - lose connection with
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + nect (bind) + -ion (noun suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "connectere" (to bind together) → Middle English "connecten" → "connection" (14th century, act of connecting) + prepositional pattern with "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I feel a real connection with this place.
   - There's no connection with what you're saying and the actual problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers identified a significant connection with socioeconomic status and educational outcomes.
   - The organization maintains connections with leading institutions worldwide.

1. **Noun**: problem with (C, pl: problems)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A difficulty, issue, or unwanted situation involving something or someone; a matter requiring resolution or causing concern
   
   **Synonyms**: issue with, difficulty with, trouble with, concern with, challenge with
   
   **Antonyms**: solution to, resolution of, advantage of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when identifying difficulties, malfunctions, conflicts, or challenges associated with specific things, people, or situations. Universal across all registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "challenge" would be more positive/constructive. Don't use unnecessarily in contexts where direct description is clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have a problem with
   - encounter a problem with
   - serious problem with
   - no problem with
   - address a problem with
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward, forth) + blem (throw, cast) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "problema" (something thrown forward, question) → Latin "problema" → Middle English "probleme" (question for discussion) → Modern "problem" + prepositional use with "with"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm having a problem with my computer again.
   - What's your problem with asking for help?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee identified several problems with the proposed implementation timeline.
   - Research addresses fundamental problems with current methodological approaches.

1. **Noun**: experience with (C/U, pl: experiences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Knowledge, skill, or practical involvement with something gained through direct participation, observation, or practice
   
   **Synonyms**: familiarity with, knowledge of, expertise in, background in, exposure to
   
   **Antonyms**: inexperience in, unfamiliarity with, ignorance of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing practical knowledge, past involvement, or hands-on familiarity. Essential in job contexts, skill assessments, and personal histories.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "expertise" or "qualification" would be more specific for professional contexts. Don't confuse countable (specific event) with uncountable (general background) uses.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have experience with
   - gain experience with
   - previous experience with
   - extensive experience with
   - lack of experience with
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + perire (to try, test) + -ence (noun suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "experientia" (trial, experiment, knowledge gained by testing) → Old French "experience" → Middle English → Modern prepositional pattern "experience with [something]"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you have any experience with graphic design?
   - My experience with online dating has been pretty terrible.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Candidates must demonstrate substantial experience with enterprise-level software systems.
   - The research team's experience with longitudinal studies proved invaluable.

1. **Noun**: difficulty with (C/U, pl: difficulties)
   **Meaning**:

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have difficulty with
   - experience difficulty with
   - great difficulty with
   - overcome difficulty with
   - without difficulty
   
   **Root Analysis**: dif- (not, apart) + facil (easy) + -ty (noun suffix) + with

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm having difficulty with this math homework.
   - She has difficulty with authority figures.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students experiencing difficulty with course material should consult with advisors promptly.
   - Participants reported significant difficulty with comprehending technical terminology.

1. **Noun**: issue with (C, pl: issues)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A problem, concern, or point of disagreement involving something; a matter requiring attention or resolution
   
   **Synonyms**: problem with, concern with, matter with, dispute with, disagreement with
   
   **Antonyms**: agreement with, accord with, solution for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing problems, concerns, disagreements, or matters requiring attention. Slightly more formal/professional than "problem" in many contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse as euphemism for "problem" when directness is needed. Don't confuse with "issue" meaning publication or distribution.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have an issue with
   - raise an issue with
   - address an issue with
   - technical issue with
   - no issue with
   
   **Root Analysis**: is- (out) + sue (go) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "exitus" (exit, outcome) → Old French "issue" (exit, outcome) → Middle English (result, outcome) → Modern "issue" (problem, matter for discussion) + prepositional pattern
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't have any issue with that plan.
   - There's some issue with the Wi-Fi today.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board identified several critical issues with the proposed merger.
   - Researchers noted methodological issues with previous studies in this domain.

1. **Noun**: interaction with (C, pl: interactions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of communicating with or doing things with other people, systems, or things; mutual or reciprocal action or influence
   
   **Synonyms**: engagement with, exchange with, communication with, dealings with, contact with
   
   **Antonyms**: isolation from, separation from, avoidance of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing communication, engagement, or reciprocal influence between entities. Common in social sciences, technology, and professional contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simpler terms like "contact" or "communication" suffice. Don't use for one-way processes where "response to" is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - social interaction with
   - interaction with users
   - meaningful interaction with
   - direct interaction with
   - increase interaction with
   
   **Root Analysis**: inter- (between, among) + act (do, perform) + -ion (noun suffix) + with
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "inter" + "agere" (to do, act) → Medieval Latin "interactio" → Modern English "interaction" (mutual action, 1830s) + prepositional construction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't have much interaction with my neighbors.
   - The app's interaction with social media is seamless.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines children's interaction with digital learning environments.
   - Effective interaction with stakeholders requires clear communication protocols.
