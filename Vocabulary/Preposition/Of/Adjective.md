1. **Adjective**: aware (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having knowledge or perception of a situation or fact; conscious of something
   
   **Synonyms**: conscious, mindful, cognizant, informed, knowledgeable
   
   **Antonyms**: unaware, ignorant, oblivious, unconscious
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate knowledge or consciousness of something; common in formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "aware of" (knowledge) with "aware that" (clause)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - aware of the situation
   - aware of the risks
   - fully aware of
   - well aware of
   - become aware of
   
   **Root Analysis**: a (on) + ware (watchful/cautious)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English gewær (watchful) → Middle English aware → consciousness meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm aware of the problem.
   - Are you aware of what's happening?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were made aware of the study's objectives.
   - The administration is fully aware of the challenges.

1. **Adjective**: proud (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling deep pleasure or satisfaction from achievements, qualities, or possessions
   - Having an excessively high opinion of oneself; arrogant
   
   **Synonyms**: pleased, satisfied, honored, delighted, gratified
   
   **Antonyms**: ashamed, embarrassed, humble, modest
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate the source of pride; versatile across registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can also use "proud to" (infinitive) or "proud that" (clause)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proud of someone
   - proud of achievements
   - very proud of
   - justifiably proud of
   - proud of yourself
   
   **Root Analysis**: proud (having pride)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French prud (valiant) → Middle English proud → satisfaction sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm so proud of you!
   - She's proud of her garden.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution is proud of its distinguished history.
   - We are proud of these significant achievements.

1. **Adjective**: capable (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the ability, skill, or qualities to do something; competent
   
   **Synonyms**: able, competent, qualified, skilled, proficient
   
   **Antonyms**: incapable, unable, incompetent, inept
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify what ability or action is possible; common in evaluative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can also use "capable of" + noun/gerund
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - capable of doing
   - capable of handling
   - highly capable of
   - fully capable of
   - perfectly capable of
   
   **Root Analysis**: cap (take/hold) + able (able to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin capabilis (able to hold) → Middle French capable → English capable
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's capable of anything.
   - I'm capable of doing this myself.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system is capable of processing complex data.
   - Participants were capable of achieving significant improvement.

1. **Adjective**: afraid (predicative only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling fear or anxiety; frightened of something
   - Reluctant or unwilling to do something for fear of consequences
   
   **Synonyms**: fearful, frightened, scared, terrified, apprehensive
   
   **Antonyms**: fearless, brave, courageous, unafraid
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify the source of fear; versatile across registers; only used predicatively
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used attributively (*"an afraid person" is incorrect); use "frightened" or "fearful" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - afraid of the dark
   - afraid of failure
   - afraid of heights
   - terribly afraid of
   - not afraid of
   
   **Root Analysis**: a (in) + fraid (frighten)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French affrayer (to frighten) → Middle English affraied → afraid
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm afraid of spiders.
   - Don't be afraid of making mistakes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many individuals are afraid of public speaking.
   - Participants expressed being afraid of negative consequences.

1. **Adjective**: fond (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having an affection or liking for someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: affectionate toward, partial to, keen on, attached to
   
   **Antonyms**: averse to, disliking, opposed to
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate affection or preference; slightly formal or literary tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very informal speech where "like" would be more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fond of someone
   - fond of reading
   - very fond of
   - particularly fond of
   - grow fond of
   
   **Root Analysis**: fond (foolish, then affectionate)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English fond (foolish) → affectionate meaning by 16th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm quite fond of her.
   - He's fond of classical music.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author was fond of classical allusions.
   - She grew increasingly fond of the rural lifestyle.

1. **Adjective**: ashamed (predicative only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling embarrassed or guilty because of one's actions or characteristics
   
   **Synonyms**: embarrassed, guilty, remorseful, mortified, humiliated
   
   **Antonyms**: proud, unashamed, pleased
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify the source of shame; common in emotional and moral contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Only used predicatively; cannot modify nouns directly
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ashamed of yourself
   - ashamed of behavior
   - deeply ashamed of
   - feel ashamed of
   - ashamed of what
   
   **Root Analysis**: a (intensive) + shame + ed
   
   **Etymology**: Old English asceamian (to feel shame) → Middle English ashamed
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You should be ashamed of yourself!
   - I'm ashamed of what I said.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The official expressed being ashamed of the institution's past policies.
   - Participants reported feeling ashamed of their previous actions.

1. **Adjective**: guilty (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Responsible for a wrongdoing or crime; culpable
   - Feeling responsibility or remorse for an offense
   
   **Synonyms**: culpable, responsible, blameworthy, at fault
   
   **Antonyms**: innocent, blameless, guiltless
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" in legal contexts or to express responsibility; both attributive and predicative
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish legal "guilty of a crime" from emotional "feel guilty about"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - guilty of murder
   - guilty of fraud
   - found guilty of
   - guilty of wrongdoing
   - plead guilty of
   
   **Root Analysis**: guilt + y (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English gylt (sin, fault) → Middle English guilty
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I feel guilty of eating all the cookies.
   - He's guilty of being lazy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant was found guilty of embezzlement.
   - The organization was guilty of regulatory violations.

1. **Adjective**: jealous (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling resentment toward someone because of their possessions, qualities, or advantages
   - Fiercely protective or vigilant of one's rights or possessions
   
   **Synonyms**: envious, covetous, resentful, possessive
   
   **Antonyms**: content, satisfied, generous
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate what causes jealousy; common in emotional and relational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "jealous of" (envy) with "jealous about/over" (possessive)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - jealous of someone
   - jealous of success
   - very jealous of
   - become jealous of
   - jealous of attention
   
   **Root Analysis**: jealous (zealous/fervent)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French jalous → Middle English jelous → envy/possessive meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's jealous of her sister's success.
   - Don't be jealous of what others have.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Competitors were jealous of the company's market position.
   - Historical records suggest he was jealous of his rival's influence.

1. **Adjective**: tired (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In need of sleep or rest; weary
   - Bored with or no longer interested in something due to overexposure
   
   **Synonyms**: weary, exhausted, fatigued, fed up with, bored with
   
   **Antonyms**: energetic, refreshed, enthusiastic, interested
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate what causes weariness or boredom; versatile across registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish physical tiredness (no "of") from boredom ("tired of")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tired of waiting
   - tired of trying
   - sick and tired of
   - get tired of
   - never tired of
   
   **Root Analysis**: tire (become weary) + d
   
   **Etymology**: Old English teorian (to fail, become weary) → Middle English tired
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm tired of this weather.
   - She's tired of his excuses.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The public grew tired of political rhetoric.
   - Employees reported being tired of excessive bureaucracy.

1. **Adjective**: independent (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Free from outside control; not subject to another's authority
   - Not depending on others for support or aid; self-sufficient
   
   **Synonyms**: autonomous, self-sufficient, self-reliant, free, sovereign
   
   **Antonyms**: dependent, reliant, subordinate
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify freedom from something; common in political, economic, and personal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can also use "independent from"; "of" is slightly more formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - independent of others
   - independent of influence
   - completely independent of
   - independent of government
   - independent of control
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (not) + dependent (hanging from)
   
   **Etymology**: French indépendant → English independent → autonomy sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's very independent of her parents.
   - I want to be independent of anyone's help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agency operates independent of governmental oversight.
   - Research findings were independent of external influences.
