1. **Verb**: consist
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be composed or made up of; to have as components or elements
   - *(with "in")* To have as an essential feature
   
   **Synonyms**: comprise, be composed of, be made up of, contain
   
   **Antonyms**: None directly applicable
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to describe composition or components; common in academic and descriptive writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "consist of" (composition) with "comprise" (which doesn't take "of")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - consist of parts
   - consist of elements
   - consist entirely of
   - consist mainly of
   - consist primarily of
   
   **Root Analysis**: con (together) + sist (stand)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin consistere (to stand together) → Middle French consister → English consist
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The team consists of five people.
   - Breakfast consists of toast and coffee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology consists of three distinct phases.
   - The sample consisted of 300 participants from diverse backgrounds.

1. **Verb**: think
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have a particular opinion or idea about something
   - To direct one's mind toward someone or something; to consider
   - To recall or remember
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, believe, regard, contemplate, reflect on
   
   **Antonyms**: forget, dismiss, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate consideration, opinion, or mental focus; versatile in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish "think of" (consider) from "think about" (contemplate)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - think of someone
   - think of an idea
   - think highly of
   - think poorly of
   - think of it
   
   **Root Analysis**: think (cogitate)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English thencan (to conceive in the mind) → Middle English thinken → unchanged
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't think of her name right now.
   - What do you think of this idea?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Scholars think highly of her contributions to the field.
   - The committee thought of several alternative approaches.

1. **Verb**: approve
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have a favorable opinion of something; to regard as good or acceptable
   - To officially accept or sanction something
   
   **Synonyms**: favor, support, endorse, accept, sanction
   
   **Antonyms**: disapprove, reject, oppose, condemn
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to express positive judgment or acceptance; common in formal and evaluative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "approve of" (have positive opinion) with "approve" (officially sanction, no "of")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approve of the decision
   - approve of someone
   - approve of the plan
   - highly approve of
   - not approve of
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap (to) + prove (test/demonstrate)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin approbare (to assent to, prove) → Old French aprover → Middle English approve
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My parents don't approve of my career choice.
   - I don't really approve of that behavior.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board approved of the strategic direction proposed.
   - Stakeholders generally approve of the reform measures.

1. **Verb**: remind
   **Meaning**: 
   - To cause someone to remember something; to bring something to someone's attention
   - To be similar to someone or something, causing recollection
   
   **Synonyms**: recall to mind, bring to mind, cause to remember, prompt
   
   **Antonyms**: forget, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate what someone is reminded about; common in everyday and professional communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish "remind of" (similarity) from "remind about" (notification) and "remind to" (prompt action)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - remind me of
   - remind someone of something
   - constantly remind of
   - gently remind of
   - remind us of
   
   **Root Analysis**: re (again) + mind (memory/attention)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin re (again) + mens (mind) → Old French remembrer → Middle English reminden
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You remind me of my sister.
   - This song reminds me of summer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The incident reminds us of the need for vigilance.
   - This case reminds scholars of similar historical precedents.

1. **Verb**: die
   **Meaning**: 
   - To stop living; to cease to exist
   - *(with "of")* To die as a result of a particular cause
   
   **Synonyms**: perish, expire, pass away, succumb
   
   **Antonyms**: live, survive, exist
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify the cause of death; also used figuratively ("dying of embarrassment")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Prefer "from" for external causes ("died from injuries"); "of" for diseases or conditions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - die of cancer
   - die of old age
   - die of hunger
   - die of thirst
   - dying of boredom (figurative)
   
   **Root Analysis**: die (cease living)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse deyja → Middle English dien → unchanged basic meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm dying of hunger; let's eat!
   - He nearly died of embarrassment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The patient died of complications related to pneumonia.
   - Many individuals died of preventable diseases.

1. **Verb**: accuse
   **Meaning**: 
   - To charge someone with wrongdoing or a crime; to blame someone for something
   
   **Synonyms**: charge, blame, indict, incriminate, allege against
   
   **Antonyms**: exonerate, absolve, clear, defend
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify what someone is accused of; formal and legal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "accuse for" (incorrect); always use "accuse of"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accuse someone of
   - falsely accuse of
   - accuse of wrongdoing
   - accuse of fraud
   - accuse of lying
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac (to) + cause (legal charge)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin accusare (to call to account) → Old French acuser → Middle English accuse
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't accuse me of not trying!
   - She accused him of stealing her idea.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant was accused of embezzlement.
   - Critics accused the administration of mismanagement.

1. **Verb**: suspect
   **Meaning**: 
   - To believe someone is guilty of something without certain proof
   - To have doubts about something; to distrust
   
   **Synonyms**: distrust, doubt, mistrust, question
   
   **Antonyms**: trust, believe, accept
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify what someone is suspected of; common in investigative and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can also be used without "of" ("I suspect he's lying")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - suspect someone of
   - suspect of wrongdoing
   - suspect of involvement
   - strongly suspect of
   - suspect of being
   
   **Root Analysis**: sus (under) + spect (look)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin suspectus (mistrusted) → Old French suspecter → Middle English suspect
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I suspect him of taking my lunch.
   - They suspected her of cheating.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Authorities suspect the individual of involvement in the scheme.
   - The organization is suspected of violating regulations.

1. **Verb**: dispose
   **Meaning**: 
   - To get rid of something; to throw away or remove
   - To deal with or settle a matter
   
   **Synonyms**: discard, eliminate, remove, get rid of, throw away
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, retain, preserve, acquire
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate removal or elimination; also means to arrange or incline
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "dispose of" (discard) with "be disposed to" (inclined to)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - dispose of waste
   - dispose of evidence
   - dispose of the matter
   - properly dispose of
   - dispose of assets
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis (apart) + pose (place)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin disponere (to arrange) → Old French disposer → Middle English dispose
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How should I dispose of these old batteries?
   - Let's dispose of this issue quickly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company must dispose of hazardous materials responsibly.
   - The court disposed of the case summarily.

1. **Verb**: dream
   **Meaning**: 
   - To experience images and sensations during sleep
   - To imagine or fantasize about something desired
   - To contemplate the possibility of something
   
   **Synonyms**: fantasize, imagine, envision, aspire to
   
   **Antonyms**: None directly applicable
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate what is dreamed about or desired; both literal and figurative uses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can also use "dream about"; "of" is slightly more formal or poetic
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - dream of someone
   - dream of success
   - dream of becoming
   - dream of the future
   - never dream of
   
   **Root Analysis**: dream (vision during sleep)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English drēam (joy, music) → Middle English dreme → vision during sleep
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I dreamed of you last night.
   - She dreams of becoming a doctor.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The founders dreamed of creating an equitable society.
   - Many dream of achieving such recognition.

1. **Verb**: deprive
   **Meaning**: 
   - To prevent someone from having or using something; to take something away
   
   **Synonyms**: deny, strip of, rob of, dispossess, withhold from
   
   **Antonyms**: provide, give, grant, endow
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to specify what is taken away or withheld; often describes unfair or harmful removal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "deny" or "withhold" would be clearer and less formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deprive someone of
   - deprive of rights
   - deprive of opportunities
   - deprive of freedom
   - deprive of sleep
   
   **Root Analysis**: de (away) + prive (private/separate)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deprivare (to deprive) → Old French depriver → Middle English deprive
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't deprive yourself of rest.
   - They were deprived of sleep for days.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy deprived citizens of fundamental rights.
   - Children were deprived of educational opportunities.
