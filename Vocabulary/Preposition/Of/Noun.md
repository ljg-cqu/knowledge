1. **Noun**: sense (C/U, pl: senses)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A feeling that something is the case; an awareness or perception of something
   - Any of the five faculties of sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch
   - A reasonable or comprehensible rationale; good judgment
   
   **Synonyms**: feeling, awareness, perception, consciousness, understanding
   
   **Antonyms**: nonsense, insensibility, unawareness
   
   **When to Use**: Used in "sense of" phrases to describe feelings, awareness, or qualities; common in both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when concrete perception organs are meant (use specific terms)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sense of humor
   - sense of direction
   - sense of purpose
   - sense of belonging
   - sense of responsibility
   
   **Root Analysis**: sens (feel/perceive) + e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin sensus (perception, feeling) → Old French sens → Middle English sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She has a great sense of humor.
   - I get the sense of being watched.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy aims to foster a sense of community among residents.
   - Participants reported an enhanced sense of well-being.

1. **Noun**: kind (C, pl: kinds)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A category of things distinguished by some common characteristic; a type or sort
   
   **Synonyms**: type, sort, variety, category, class
   
   **Antonyms**: None directly applicable
   
   **When to Use**: Used in "kind of" phrases to categorize or classify; very common in everyday speech; also used informally as a hedge
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid "these kind of" (use "these kinds of" or "this kind of")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - kind of person
   - kind of thing
   - all kinds of
   - what kind of
   - same kind of
   
   **Root Analysis**: kind (natural type)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English gecynd (nature, race) → Middle English kind → classification sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What kind of music do you like?
   - She's not that kind of person.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This kind of analysis requires specialized training.
   - Multiple kinds of evidence support the hypothesis.

1. **Noun**: lot (C, pl: lots)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A large number or amount; a great quantity
   - A particular group or set of people or things
   - *(Informal)* Used in "a lot of" to mean many or much
   
   **Synonyms**: quantity, amount, bunch, heap, collection
   
   **Antonyms**: little, few, scarcity
   
   **When to Use**: Used in "a lot of" or "lots of" to express large quantities; informal to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid "alot" (two words: "a lot"); too informal for very formal academic writing (prefer "many," "much," or "numerous")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - a lot of people
   - a lot of time
   - lots of fun
   - a lot of work
   - a whole lot of
   
   **Root Analysis**: lot (portion, destiny)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English hlot (object used in decision-making) → Middle English lot → quantity sense by 18th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We have a lot of homework tonight.
   - There are lots of reasons to visit.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A significant amount of research has been conducted on this topic.
   - The project requires substantial resources and time.

1. **Noun**: piece (C, pl: pieces)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A portion or part of something; a fragment or bit
   - A single item or example of something
   - An artistic or creative work
   
   **Synonyms**: part, portion, segment, fragment, bit
   
   **Antonyms**: whole, entirety, totality
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate a portion of uncountable nouns or to specify individual items
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse with countable nouns that have better partitives
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - piece of cake
   - piece of paper
   - piece of advice
   - piece of information
   - piece of work
   
   **Root Analysis**: piece (fragment)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French piece → Middle English pece → portion/fragment
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I have a piece of that pizza?
   - Let me give you a piece of advice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Each piece of evidence was carefully examined.
   - This represents a significant piece of the overall strategy.

1. **Noun**: part (C, pl: parts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A portion, division, or component of something larger
   - A role or function in a situation or activity
   - Some but not all of something
   
   **Synonyms**: portion, section, segment, component, element
   
   **Antonyms**: whole, entirety, totality
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate membership, composition, or portion; extremely common in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundancy with "part and parcel of" unless emphasizing inseparability
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - part of the problem
   - part of the solution
   - part of the team
   - part of life
   - for the most part
   
   **Root Analysis**: part (portion)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin pars (part, piece, share) → Old French part → Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's part of growing up.
   - I'm part of the organizing committee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This constitutes a fundamental part of the theoretical framework.
   - The intervention is part of a comprehensive strategy.

1. **Noun**: number (C, pl: numbers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A quantity or amount; a numerical value
   - Several; some; a collection of things
   
   **Synonyms**: quantity, amount, total, figure, count
   
   **Antonyms**: None directly applicable
   
   **When to Use**: Used in "a number of" to mean several or some; also literal numerical meanings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish "a number of" (several, plural verb) from "the number of" (total count, singular verb)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - a number of people
   - a number of reasons
   - a large number of
   - a small number of
   - the number of
   
   **Root Analysis**: numer (count) + al ending
   
   **Etymology**: Latin numerus (number, quantity) → Old French nombre → Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A number of people asked about it.
   - We've been here a number of times.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A significant number of respondents indicated agreement.
   - The number of participants exceeded expectations.

1. **Noun**: member (C, pl: members)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person belonging to a group, organization, or community
   - A constituent part of a complex structure
   
   **Synonyms**: participant, constituent, associate, affiliate
   
   **Antonyms**: outsider, non-member, stranger
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "of" to indicate belonging to a group; common in organizational and institutional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "participant" or "part" would be more accurate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - member of the team
   - member of parliament
   - member of the family
   - member of society
   - founding member of
   
   **Root Analysis**: membr (limb/part) + er
   
   **Etymology**: Latin membrum (limb, body part) → Old French membre → Middle English → group membership
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's a member of our running club.
   - Are you a member of the gym?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Each member of the committee has voting rights.
   - The institution is a member of several international consortia.

1. **Noun**: matter (U/C, pl: matters when countable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A subject or situation under consideration; an affair or concern
   - Physical substance; material
   - A problem or difficulty
   
   **Synonyms**: issue, subject, affair, topic, concern
   
   **Antonyms**: irrelevance, triviality
   
   **When to Use**: Used in "a matter of" to indicate the nature or essence of something; also "matters of" for topics or concerns
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid confusion between physical matter and abstract matters
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - a matter of time
   - a matter of fact
   - a matter of opinion
   - matters of importance
   - as a matter of
   
   **Root Analysis**: mater (substance)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin materia (substance, timber) → Old French matiere → Middle English matter
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's just a matter of time before they find out.
   - This is a matter of life and death.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The resolution of this matter requires immediate attention.
   - Matters of public policy demand careful consideration.

1. **Noun**: variety (C/U, pl: varieties)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A number or range of things of different types; diversity
   - A different form or version of something
   
   **Synonyms**: diversity, assortment, range, mixture, collection
   
   **Antonyms**: uniformity, monotony, sameness
   
   **When to Use**: Used in "a variety of" to indicate diversity or multiple types; emphasizes difference and range
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when a specific number is more appropriate than a general range
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - a variety of options
   - a wide variety of
   - a variety of reasons
   - variety of species
   - for a variety of
   
   **Root Analysis**: vari (different) + ety
   
   **Etymology**: Latin varietas (diversity) → Middle French varieté → Middle English variety
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We tried a variety of restaurants.
   - They sell a variety of products.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined a variety of methodological approaches.
   - Participants represented a variety of demographic backgrounds.
