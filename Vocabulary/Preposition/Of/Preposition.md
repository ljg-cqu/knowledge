1. **Preposition**: of (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Possession/Association)* Indicating belonging or relationship between parts and wholes
   - *(Material/Composition)* Indicating what something is made from or consists of
   - *(Origin/Source)* Indicating where something comes from or derives from
   - *(Partition/Quantity)* Indicating a portion or amount of a larger group
   - *(Quality/Characteristic)* Describing an attribute or quality of something
   - *(Time/Date)* Indicating a specific point in time (the morning of June 5th)
   - *(Cause/About)* Indicating the subject matter or reason for something
   
   **Synonyms**: from (origin), belonging to (possession), made from (material), concerning (about)
   
   **Antonyms**: to (direction), without (absence)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "of" to express relationships of possession, composition, origin, quantity, quality, and association; essential for expressing complex noun phrases and abstract relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "from" for direction/movement; avoid overuse in noun strings (prefer compound nouns or hyphens for clarity)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: of course, of interest, of importance, of value, of concern
   - **Verb + prep**: consist of, think of, dream of, approve of, die of
   - **Adjective + prep**: aware of, proud of, fond of, capable of, afraid of
   - **Noun + prep**: part of, lot of, sense of, piece of, kind of, type of
   
   **Root Analysis**: of (simple preposition, no decomposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English of → unchanged basic meaning expressing relationship and origin; cognate with German ab
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's a friend of mine from college.
   - Give me a piece of that cake!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The development of renewable energy technologies requires substantial investment.
   - The committee expressed approval of the proposed amendments.

1. **Preposition**: out of (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving from inside to outside a space or container
   - *(Source)* From within a larger group or quantity
   - *(Material)* Made or constructed from a particular substance
   - *(Motivation)* Because of a particular feeling or reason
   - *(State)* No longer having something; depleted or exhausted
   
   **Synonyms**: from within, from among, lacking (for depletion), beyond (exceeding)
   
   **Antonyms**: into, inside of
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates movement from inside to outside, selection from a group, or depletion of resources; also expresses motivation or cause
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for surface movement (use "off of" or "from"); avoid when simple "from" would be clearer
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: out of bounds, out of order, out of reach, out of sight, out of control
   - **Verb + prep**: come out of, get out of, run out of, make out of, go out of
   - **Adjective + prep**: made out of
   - **Noun + prep**: way out of, exit out of
   
   **Root Analysis**: out (external) + of (relationship)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English combination of out (external position) + of (relation/source)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm out of milk; need to go shopping.
   - She made a sculpture out of recycled materials.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The participants were selected out of a pool of 500 applicants.
   - The decision emerged out of extensive deliberation and consultation.

1. **Preposition**: because of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Causal)* For the reason that; due to; as a result of something
   
   **Synonyms**: due to, owing to, on account of, as a result of, thanks to (positive)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, in spite of, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Introduces the cause or reason for something; can be used in both formal and informal contexts; typically followed by a noun phrase
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use before a clause (use "because" instead); avoid in very formal academic writing (prefer "due to" or "owing to")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: because of this, because of that, because of you, because of the weather
   - **Verb + prep**: happen because of, occur because of, fail because of, succeed because of
   - **Adjective + prep**: delayed because of, cancelled because of
   - **Noun + prep**: reason because of (avoid; use "reason for")
   
   **Root Analysis**: because (causal conjunction) + of (relationship)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English bi cause (by cause) + of → phrasal preposition by 14th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The game was postponed because of rain.
   - I was late because of traffic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project was delayed because of unforeseen technical complications.
   - Enrollment declined because of demographic shifts in the region.

1. **Preposition**: instead of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Substitution)* In place of; as an alternative to; rather than
   
   **Synonyms**: rather than, in place of, in lieu of, as an alternative to
   
   **Antonyms**: as well as, in addition to, along with
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates a choice or substitution where one option is selected over another; useful for expressing preferences or alternatives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when both options are being chosen (use "as well as"); avoid redundancy with "rather"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: instead of this, instead of that, instead of doing
   - **Verb + prep**: use instead of, choose instead of, opt instead of
   - **Adjective + prep**: preferred instead of
   - **Noun + prep**: alternative instead of, substitute instead of
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + stead (place) + of (relationship)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English in stede of (in the place of) → modern phrasal preposition
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's have pizza instead of burgers tonight.
   - She took the bus instead of driving.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee recommended mediation instead of litigation.
   - Participants received certificates instead of monetary compensation.

1. **Preposition**: in front of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In a position ahead of or before something; facing something
   - *(Presence)* In the presence of someone; before an audience
   
   **Synonyms**: before, ahead of, facing
   
   **Antonyms**: behind, in back of, at the rear of
   
   **When to Use**: Describes position ahead or facing direction; also indicates presence before an audience or witnesses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "before" in temporal sense; avoid when "facing" would be clearer
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in front of the house, in front of everyone, in front of me
   - **Verb + prep**: stand in front of, park in front of, appear in front of, perform in front of
   - **Adjective + prep**: positioned in front of
   - **Noun + prep**: spot in front of, area in front of
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + front (forward part) + of (relationship)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English combination → phrasal preposition indicating forward position
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Park the car in front of my house.
   - I hate speaking in front of large groups.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The witness testified in front of the grand jury.
   - The monument stands in front of the municipal building.

1. **Preposition**: in spite of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Concession)* Despite; notwithstanding; regardless of; without being affected by
   
   **Synonyms**: despite, notwithstanding, regardless of
   
   **Antonyms**: because of, due to, owing to
   
   **When to Use**: Introduces a contrasting circumstance that doesn't prevent something from happening; shows that something occurs despite obstacles
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use before a clause (use "although" or "even though"); avoid in very informal speech (prefer "despite")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in spite of this, in spite of everything, in spite of the fact
   - **Verb + prep**: succeed in spite of, continue in spite of, thrive in spite of
   - **Adjective + prep**: happy in spite of, successful in spite of
   - **Noun + prep**: achievement in spite of, progress in spite of
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + spite (malice/opposition) + of (relationship)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English despit → in spite of (opposing force) → concessive meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We went hiking in spite of the cold weather.
   - She's happy in spite of all her problems.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The economy grew in spite of significant external pressures.
   - The research continued in spite of funding constraints.

1. **Preposition**: on behalf of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Representation)* As the representative of someone; in the interest of someone; for the benefit of
   - *(Substitution)* Acting in place of someone; speaking for someone
   
   **Synonyms**: for, representing, in the name of, as a representative of
   
   **Antonyms**: against, in opposition to
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates acting as someone's representative or in their interest; formal contexts such as business, legal, or official communications
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "in behalf of" (less common British variant); avoid in casual speech where "for" would suffice
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on behalf of the team, on behalf of the company, on behalf of all
   - **Verb + prep**: speak on behalf of, act on behalf of, accept on behalf of, thank on behalf of
   - **Adjective + prep**: grateful on behalf of
   - **Noun + prep**: statement on behalf of, action on behalf of
   
   **Root Analysis**: on + be + half (side/part) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English on bihalve → representing one's side or interest
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for coming, on behalf of everyone here.
   - I'm calling on behalf of my sister.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The attorney spoke on behalf of the defendant.
   - We are pleased to accept this award on behalf of our organization.

1. **Preposition**: by means of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Instrument)* Through the use of; using as a method or tool; via
   
   **Synonyms**: through, via, using, by way of, with the help of
   
   **Antonyms**: without
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts to describe the method, instrument, or means of achieving something; emphasizes the mechanism or process
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech (use "by" or "using"); avoid when simple "with" is clearer
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by means of communication, by means of technology, by means of force
   - **Verb + prep**: achieve by means of, accomplish by means of, resolve by means of
   - **Adjective + prep**: accessible by means of, obtainable by means of
   - **Noun + prep**: solution by means of, method by means of
   
   **Root Analysis**: by (via) + means (method/resource) + of (relationship)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English combination emphasizing instrumental function
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He got there by means of a rental car.
   - You can contact us by means of email.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data was transmitted by means of encrypted channels.
   - Conflict resolution was achieved by means of diplomatic negotiation.
