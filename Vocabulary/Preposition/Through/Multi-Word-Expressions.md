1. **Expression**: go through the roof (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To increase to a very high level suddenly and dramatically (for prices, costs, numbers)
   - To become extremely angry; to lose one's temper completely (informal)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly idiomatic, non-literal)
   
   **Synonyms**: skyrocket, soar, spike dramatically (first sense); lose one's temper, explode, go ballistic (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: plummet, drop sharply (first sense); stay calm, keep cool (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use informally to describe dramatic increases or intense anger. First sense common in business/economics. Second sense common in casual conversation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for formal financial reports (use "increased dramatically" instead). Can be ambiguous which sense is meant.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - go through the ceiling (similar idiom)
   - prices are going through the roof (continuous aspect)
   - went through the roof (past tense)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: Metaphor of explosive upward movement so strong it breaks through physical barrier (roof); vivid image of exceeding normal bounds
   
   **Root Analysis**: Figurative extension of physically breaking through a roof (upper limit) due to explosive force
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When he saw the damage to his car, he went through the roof!
   - Gas prices are going through the roof again—it's crazy expensive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consumer prices increased dramatically during the third quarter. (*Avoid idiom; use literal description*)
   - Healthcare costs have risen sharply in recent years. (*Formal equivalent*)

1. **Expression**: through thick and thin (type: idiom - fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In both good times and bad times; through all difficulties and challenges; remaining loyal and committed regardless of circumstances
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed binomial pair)
   
   **Synonyms**: through good times and bad, in all circumstances, no matter what, come what may
   
   **Antonyms**: only when convenient, fair-weather (as in "fair-weather friend")
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize loyalty, commitment, or persistence through varying circumstances. Common in relationship contexts and commitment statements.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Somewhat clichéd; can sound trite if overused. Consider whether fresher phrasing would be more impactful.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - stick together through thick and thin
   - been through thick and thin together
   - support through thick and thin
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: "Thick" (dense forest, difficult terrain) and "thin" (easy open ground) = metaphor for difficult and easy circumstances; medieval hunting terminology
   
   **Root Analysis**: From 14th century: "thick" (dense undergrowth, obstacles) and "thin" (clear terrain); hunting/travel terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We've been friends through thick and thin since elementary school.
   - My dog has been with me through thick and thin—best companion ever.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The partnership has endured through thick and thin, demonstrating resilience across multiple economic cycles.
   - The organization supports veterans through thick and thin, regardless of changing political priorities.

1. **Expression**: put someone through the wringer (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To subject someone to a difficult, stressful, or exhausting experience; to test or examine someone severely
   - *Fixedness*: strong (idiomatic with specific historical reference)
   
   **Synonyms**: put through a difficult ordeal, give someone a hard time, subject to harsh treatment
   
   **Antonyms**: treat gently, handle with care, go easy on
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe exhausting, demanding, or stressful experiences—interviews, examinations, difficult situations. Implies thoroughness and intensity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can trivialize genuinely traumatic experiences. Be sensitive to context and severity of situation described.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - been put through the wringer
   - really put me through the wringer
   - going through the wringer
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: "Wringer" = old-fashioned laundry device with rollers that squeeze water from clothes; metaphor for being squeezed/pressed/stressed thoroughly
   
   **Root Analysis**: From clothes wringer (mangle): mechanical device with rollers that squeeze fabric between them
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That job interview really put me through the wringer—six rounds of interviews!
   - The divorce put him through the wringer emotionally and financially.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The rigorous selection process puts candidates through the wringer to ensure only the most qualified are hired.
   - Regulatory compliance audits put organizations through the wringer with extensive documentation requirements.

1. **Expression**: slip through the cracks (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be overlooked, missed, or neglected in a system or process; to fail to receive needed attention or assistance
   - *Fixedness*: strong (common idiomatic expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: be overlooked, be missed, fall through the gaps, go unnoticed
   
   **Antonyms**: be caught, be noticed, receive attention
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe failures in systems, processes, or organizations to catch or address something/someone. Often used for vulnerable populations or quality control failures.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply negligence or system failure; be cautious in defensive contexts. Don't use when deliberate ignoring occurred.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fall through the cracks (very similar)
   - slipped through the cracks
   - things slip through the cracks
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: Metaphor of small items falling through gaps in floorboards or structures; image of inadequate coverage or protection
   
   **Root Analysis**: Physical image of objects small enough to fall through gaps in protection or coverage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Some emails must have slipped through the cracks—I never saw them!
   - Kids in foster care sometimes slip through the cracks and don't get the support they need.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audit revealed that several critical safety checks had slipped through the cracks.
   - Vulnerable populations often slip through the cracks of fragmented social service systems.

   **Antonyms**: have an easy path, face no obstacles
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe bureaucratic obstacles, excessive requirements, or demanding processes. Often implies frustration with unnecessary complexity.

   **Common Variations**: 
   - make someone jump through hoops

   **Why This Partnership**:

   **Root Analysis**: From circus performances where animals jump through suspended hoops as tricks
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I had to jump through so many hoops just to get a refund—ridiculous!
   - They make you jump through hoops for even the simplest requests around here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applicants must jump through numerous hoops to demonstrate eligibility for the program.
   - The regulatory framework requires organizations to jump through multiple compliance hoops.

1. **Expression**: see the light at the end of the tunnel (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To perceive signs that a difficult or unpleasant situation is finally coming to an end; to have hope that problems will soon be resolved
   - *Fixedness*: strong (well-established metaphorical expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: see hope ahead, perceive an end to difficulties, see an end in sight
   
   **Antonyms**: see no end in sight, face ongoing difficulties
   
   **When to Use**: Use when difficult situation shows signs of improvement or nearing completion. Provides hope and perspective during challenges.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use prematurely if end isn't genuinely near—creates false hope. Be careful not to trivialize ongoing suffering.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - there's light at the end of the tunnel
   - finally seeing the light at the end of the tunnel
   - can see a light at the end of the tunnel
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: Metaphor of traveling through dark tunnel (difficulty) and seeing light ahead indicating exit (resolution); railway/mining imagery
   
   **Root Analysis**: Physical experience of emerging from tunnel into light; symbolic journey from darkness to light
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After months of studying, I can finally see the light at the end of the tunnel—exams are almost over!
   - We're starting to see the light at the end of the tunnel with this project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Economic indicators suggest the recession may be ending; stakeholders are seeing light at the end of the tunnel.
   - With the final phase underway, project managers can see the light at the end of the tunnel.

1. **Expression**: through and through (type: fixed phrase - emphatic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely; in every way; thoroughly; to the core of one's being or throughout every part
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed repetitive construction)
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, totally, entirely, to the core, in every way
   
   **Antonyms**: superficially, partially, on the surface
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize that someone completely embodies a quality or that something is thorough in every aspect. Common for character descriptions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound informal or colloquial in very formal academic writing. May be overly emphatic for subtle situations.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - [quality] through and through (e.g., "honest through and through")
   - through-and-through [noun] (hyphenated as attributive)
   - is [X] through and through
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocation*: Repetition of "through" emphasizes completeness from every direction and in every aspect; rhetorical device of repetition for emphasis
   
   **Root Analysis**: Emphatic repetition construction for emphasis (through from every angle/direction)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's a New Yorker through and through—you can tell from how he talks!
   - She's an artist through and through; everything she does is creative.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The document reveals him to be a pragmatist through and through.
   - The organization's culture is collaborative through and through, from leadership to entry-level positions.

1. **Expression**: break through the noise (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To get attention or be noticed despite heavy competition or information overload; to stand out from the crowd
   - *Fixedness*: medium (modern business/marketing collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: stand out, get noticed, rise above the clutter, cut through the noise
   
   **Antonyms**: get lost in the crowd, be drowned out, go unnoticed
   
   **When to Use**: Use in marketing, communication, or competitive contexts. Describes successfully gaining attention in crowded spaces (literal or figurative).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Modern business jargon; may sound clichéd in some contexts. Less common outside professional/marketing contexts.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - cut through the noise (very similar)
   - breaking through all the noise
   - hard to break through the noise
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocation*: "Noise" = metaphor for competing messages/information; "break through" = penetrate barrier; reflects information-saturated modern communication environment
   
   **Root Analysis**: "Noise" as metaphor for information overload + "break through" (penetrate barrier)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's hard to break through the noise on social media these days—everyone's posting content.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Effective marketing strategies break through the noise to reach target audiences.
   - In a crowded marketplace, innovative products break through the noise to capture consumer attention.

   - *Fixedness*: strong (established medical and crisis context collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: recover, survive, make it through, come through
   
   **Antonyms**: succumb, not survive, fail to recover
   
   **When to Use**: Use primarily for medical recovery or surviving serious crises. Emphasizes overcoming significant danger or difficulty.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Generally reserved for serious situations; trivializing reduces impact. Don't overuse for minor inconveniences.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - pulled through successfully
   - help someone pull through
   - manage to pull through
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocation*: "Pull" suggests active effort to draw someone/something from danger "through" to safety; metaphor of rescue or extraction
   
   **Root Analysis**: Physical image of pulling someone through dangerous passage to safety
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He was really sick, but he pulled through—he's home from the hospital now.
   - I don't know how I pulled through finals week, but I did it!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The patient pulled through the critical phase and achieved stable condition.
   - With strong leadership, the organization pulled through the financial crisis.

1. **Expression**: work through issues/problems (type: strong collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To address and resolve problems, conflicts, or challenges through effort and process; to actively engage with difficulties until resolved
   - *Fixedness*: medium-strong (very common professional collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: resolve problems, address issues, sort out difficulties, tackle challenges
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid problems, ignore issues, leave unresolved
   
   **When to Use**: Use in professional, therapeutic, or relationship contexts when emphasizing process of resolution. Implies commitment and effort.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound like jargon if overused. Consider whether simpler "solve" or "resolve" would be clearer.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - working through our differences
   - work through the challenges
   - need to work through some things
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocation*: "Work" emphasizes active effort; "through" emphasizes process from beginning to resolution; common in therapy and conflict resolution contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: "Work" (active effort) + "through" (complete process from start to resolution)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to work through some stuff before we can move forward with this friendship.
   - Let's work through these issues one at a time, okay?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is working through budgetary constraints to identify viable solutions.
   - Organizations must work through implementation challenges systematically to ensure success.

1. **Expression**: read through the lines (type: idiom - note: correctly "read between the lines")
   **Meaning**: 
   - To understand hidden meaning or implications not explicitly stated; to infer what is meant but not said directly

   **Antonyms**: take at face value, interpret literally
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing implied meanings, subtext, or hidden messages. Common in communication analysis and interpretation contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Correct form is "read between the lines" NOT "read through the lines." "Through" version is non-standard.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - reading between the lines (standard form)
   - if you read between the lines
   - easy to read between the lines
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: "Between the lines" refers to unwritten implications found in spaces between written text; "through" is not the standard form
   
   **Root Analysis**: Metaphor of finding meaning in spaces between written lines of text
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If you read between the lines, it's clear she's not happy with the decision. (correct form)

   - Careful analysis requires reading between the lines to understand implicit assumptions. (correct form)

1. **Expression**: run through the gauntlet (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**:

   **Synonyms**: face severe criticism, endure harsh treatment, undergo an ordeal
   
   **Antonyms**: be treated gently, receive praise
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe experiences of facing multiple critics, difficult interrogation, or intense scrutiny from multiple directions.

   - run the gauntlet (shorter, equally common)
   - running through the gauntlet

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Expression**: put through the paces (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To test someone or something thoroughly by requiring demonstration of all abilities; to examine capabilities comprehensively
   - *Fixedness*: strong (horse training terminology)
   
   **Synonyms**: test thoroughly, examine comprehensively, try out completely
   
   **Antonyms**: give minimal testing, not fully evaluate
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing thorough testing, examination, or demonstration of capabilities. Common for job candidates, products, skills.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Origins in horse training; some may find animal training metaphor inappropriate for human contexts.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - put someone through their paces
   - being put through the paces
   - really put it through its paces
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: Horse training term: "paces" = different gaits (walk, trot, canter, gallop); training horses by making them demonstrate all paces/abilities
   
   **Root Analysis**: From equestrian training where horses demonstrate all gaits (paces) to show complete training
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The trainer really put us through our paces today—I'm exhausted!
   - Let me put this new phone through its paces and see if it's worth the money.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The interview process puts candidates through their paces to assess both technical and interpersonal skills.
   - Quality assurance teams put new software through its paces before public release.
