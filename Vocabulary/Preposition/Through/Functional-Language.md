1. **Functional Expression**: Let me walk you through [X] (function: offering instruction/guidance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offer to provide step-by-step instruction or explanation of a process, procedure, or concept
   - *Formality*: neutral/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite offer)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let me show you [X], Let me explain [X], I'll guide you through [X], Allow me to demonstrate [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: Figure it out yourself, You're on your own
   
   **When to Use**: Use when offering to teach, train, or explain something complex. Establishes helpful, collaborative tone. Appropriate in workplace training, customer service, technical support.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May sound condescending if offering unsolicited help. Don't use with experts or when assistance wasn't requested unless relationship allows.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll walk you through it (less formal)
   - Allow me to walk you through this (more formal)
   - Let me quickly walk you through [X] (emphasizes brevity)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedges potential face threat of assuming recipient needs help. "Let me" softens directiveness. "Walk through" metaphor suggests guided journey.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That would be great, thank you" / "Yes, please"
   - Declining: "That's okay, I think I've got it" / "I'll figure it out, but thanks"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Let me walk you through how to use this app—it's pretty simple once you know the basics."
   - "Want me to walk you through the setup? It can be tricky the first time."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Allow me to walk you through the implementation procedure step by step."
   - "I would be happy to walk you through the requirements in our next meeting."

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm going through [difficult situation] (function: disclosing difficulty/seeking understanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Disclose that one is experiencing a challenging period or situation; often implies request for understanding, flexibility, or support
   - *Formality*: neutral/informal
   - *Directness*: direct (personal disclosure)
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm dealing with [X], I'm experiencing [X], I'm facing [X], I'm enduring [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: Everything's fine, I'm managing well
   
   **When to Use**: Use when explaining personal circumstances that may affect work, relationships, or commitments. Creates understanding and may request accommodation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Oversharing in professional contexts can be inappropriate. Assess relationship and boundaries. Very formal contexts may require more euphemistic language.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm going through some stuff right now (vague, informal)
   - I'm going through a challenging period (more formal)
   - I've been going through [X] lately (temporal emphasis)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Vulnerability marker that invites empathy. "Going through" implies temporary state with eventual end. Less dramatic than "suffering from" or "struggling with."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Supportive: "I'm so sorry to hear that. Is there anything I can do to help?" / "That must be really difficult"
   - Acknowledging: "Thank you for letting me know. Take whatever time you need" / "I understand"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'm going through a rough breakup right now, so I might not be super social for a bit."
   - "Just so you know, I'm going through some health issues and might need to cancel plans sometimes."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I wanted to inform you that I am currently going through some personal difficulties that may require occasional schedule flexibility."
   - "I am going through a family situation that necessitates my attention during this period."

1. **Functional Expression**: Could you run me through [X]? (function: requesting explanation/instruction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request step-by-step explanation or review of a process, procedure, or set of information
   - *Formality*: neutral/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite request using modal "could")
   
   **Synonyms**: Could you explain [X]?, Can you walk me through [X]?, Would you mind showing me [X]?, Could you go over [X]?
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't need help, I understand already
   
   **When to Use**: Use when requesting instruction or clarification. "Could" makes it polite and optional. Appropriate in workplace learning, training contexts, seeking clarification.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Repeated requests may suggest inattention. Don't use if information was recently explained unless acknowledging that.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Can you run me through it? (slightly less formal)
   - Would you mind running me through [X]? (more formal, more tentative)
   - Could you quickly run me through [X]? (emphasizes brevity request)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Modal "could" creates negative politeness (minimizes imposition). "Run through" suggests quicker review than "walk through." Face-saving for requester by framing as favor.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Of course, I'd be happy to" / "Sure, let me show you"
   - Hedging: "I'm a bit busy right now, but I can later" / "Let me find the documentation for you"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Could you run me through how to submit expenses? I totally forgot the process."
   - "Hey, could you quickly run me through what happened in the meeting? I had to leave early."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Could you please run me through the compliance requirements for this project?"
   - "Would it be possible for you to run me through the approval workflow at your earliest convenience?"

1. **Functional Expression**: We need to work through [issue/problem] (function: proposing collaborative problem-solving)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Propose addressing and resolving an issue, conflict, or challenge together through discussion and effort
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (clear statement of need)
   
   **Synonyms**: We need to resolve [X], We should address [X], We must tackle [X], We need to sort out [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: We should ignore this, Let's avoid dealing with this
   
   **When to Use**: Use when identifying need for collaborative problem-solving. Establishes shared responsibility and commitment to resolution. Appropriate in conflicts, project challenges, relationship issues.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May sound demanding if power imbalance exists. Consider softening with "might need to" or "should probably" in hierarchical contexts.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We should work through this (softer, more suggestive)
   - We'll need to work through some issues (acknowledges multiple problems)
   - Let's work through this together (emphasizes collaboration)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Work through" implies process requiring effort and time. "Need to" creates urgency and importance. "We" distributes responsibility and creates team framing.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: "You're right, let's set aside time to discuss it" / "I agree, when should we talk?"
   - Reluctant: "Do we have to deal with this now?" / "Can this wait?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "We need to work through this awkwardness between us—it's getting weird."
   - "Look, we need to work through the budget issues before we can move forward."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "We need to work through several implementation challenges before the launch date."
   - "It is essential that we work through these regulatory concerns systematically."

1. **Functional Expression**: I'll see [X] through (to completion/to the end) (function: committing/promising completion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Commit to continuing with something until it is successfully completed, despite difficulties
   - *Formality*: neutral/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit commitment)
   
   **Synonyms**: I'll complete [X], I'll finish [X], I'll stick with [X], I won't give up on [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm giving up, I can't continue, I'm abandoning this
   
   **When to Use**: Use when making commitments or promises to complete difficult tasks. Demonstrates determination and reliability. Common in project commitments, personal promises.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overcommit if completion is uncertain. Empty promises damage credibility.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll see it through (omits object when clear from context)
   - I'm committed to seeing this through (more formal)
   - I'll see this through no matter what (emphasizes determination)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "See through" implies sustained vision and commitment from start to finish. Positive politeness strategy emphasizing reliability. Often used when difficulty is acknowledged.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Appreciative: "Thank you, I really appreciate your commitment" / "That means a lot"
   - Skeptical: "Are you sure? It's going to be tough" / "I hope you mean that"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Don't worry, I'll see this project through even if it kills me!"
   - "I promised I'd help, and I'll see it through—you can count on me."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I am fully committed to seeing this initiative through to successful completion."
   - "The team will see the implementation through regardless of the challenges we encounter."

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's talk this through (function: proposing discussion/negotiation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggest having a thorough discussion to understand all aspects of an issue and reach resolution or clarity
   - *Formality*: neutral/informal
   - *Directness*: direct but collaborative ("let's" inclusive)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's discuss this thoroughly, Let's hash this out, Let's work this out, Let's think this through
   
   **Antonyms**: There's nothing to discuss, I don't want to talk about it
   
   **When to Use**: Use when conflicts arise, decisions need to be made, or misunderstandings need clarification. Creates collaborative, problem-solving atmosphere.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May seem dismissive of strong emotions if used too quickly in heated conflicts. Timing matters—allow cooling period first if needed.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's sit down and talk this through (more formal, scheduled)
   - We should talk this through (less immediate, more suggestive)
   - Can we talk this through? (request form, more tentative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Talk through" suggests comprehensive discussion reaching clarity. "Let's" creates collaborative framing. Implies mutual willingness to understand.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Yes, I think we should" / "Good idea, when works for you?"
   - Resisting: "I need some space first" / "I'm not ready to talk yet"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Hey, I think there's been a misunderstanding. Let's talk this through over coffee?"
   - "Before anyone gets upset, let's just talk this through calmly, okay?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I suggest we schedule a meeting to talk this through and ensure all perspectives are considered."
   - "Let us talk this through systematically to identify the optimal solution."

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm not through with [X] yet (function: expressing incompletion/requesting more time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Indicate that one has not finished with something and needs additional time or intends to continue
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (clear statement of status)
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm not finished with [X] yet, I still need [X], I haven't completed [X] yet
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm done with [X], I'm finished, You can have it back
   
   **When to Use**: Use when someone requests something you're still using, or to indicate ongoing work. Also used metaphorically to indicate unfinished business or unresolved issues.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound possessive or defensive depending on tone. In formal contexts, "I have not completed" is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm not through yet (omits object when clear)
   - I'm still not through with [X] (emphasizes duration)
   - I'm nowhere near through with [X] (emphasizes much work remains)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Not through" implies expected completion point not yet reached. Can convey determination (positive) or frustration (negative) depending on context and tone.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Understanding: "No problem, take your time" / "Just let me know when you're done"
   - Impatient: "How much longer do you need?" / "I really need it soon"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'm not through with the report yet—give me another hour?"
   - "Don't put that book away, I'm not through with it!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I have not completed the analysis yet and will require additional time."
   - "The review process is not through yet; we anticipate completion by Friday."

1. **Functional Expression**: Did my [message/call/email] get through? (function: checking successful communication)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Check whether communication was successfully received or understood by the intended recipient
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit checking)
   
   **Synonyms**: Did you receive my [X]?, Did you get my [X]?, Was my message received?
   
   **Antonyms**: I assume you received it
   
   **When to Use**: Use when uncertain about successful communication delivery, or when expecting response but none received. Appropriate for technical issues or confirmation needs.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Asking too soon after sending can seem impatient. Don't use repeatedly—may irritate recipient.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Did you get my message? (simpler, more direct)
   - I'm checking if my email got through (softer, explanatory)
   - Just making sure my call got through (casual confirmation)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Get through" acknowledges potential barriers to communication (technical or attention-based). Face-saving by attributing non-response to delivery failure rather than ignoring.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Confirming: "Yes, I got it" / "I received it, sorry for not responding yet"
   - Denying: "No, I didn't receive anything" / "Nothing came through"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Hey, did my text get through? I know the signal was bad."
   - "I sent you a voicemail yesterday—did it get through?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I am following up to confirm whether my previous email communication got through to your office."
   - "Could you please confirm that the documents I sent got through successfully?"

1. **Functional Expression**: Think it through before [action] (function: advising/warning)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Advise someone to consider something carefully and thoroughly before taking action; warn against hasty decisions
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (imperative form) but softened by implication it's for recipient's benefit
   
   **Synonyms**: Consider it carefully, Think about it thoroughly, Don't rush into it, Weigh your options
   
   **Antonyms**: Just do it, Don't overthink it, Act now
   
   **When to Use**: Use when offering advice about important decisions, warning against impulsivity, or encouraging reflection. Shows concern for recipient's wellbeing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound condescending if overused or if recipient is already thoughtful. May frustrate action-oriented people.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Make sure you think it through (emphasizes importance)
   - You might want to think this through (softer, more tentative)
   - Have you thought this through? (question form, checking)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies current thinking is insufficient. Face-threatening but usually well-intentioned. "Through" emphasizes thoroughness and considering consequences.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "You're right, I should think about it more" / "Good point"
   - Defensive: "I have thought it through" / "I know what I'm doing"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Think it through before you quit your job—do you have a backup plan?"
   - "Maybe think it through before sending that angry email, okay?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I would advise you to think this through carefully before committing resources."
   - "Please think through the implications before making your final decision."

1. **Functional Expression**: I can see it through your eyes/from your perspective (function: expressing empathy/understanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Express ability to understand or imagine another person's viewpoint, feelings, or situation
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit empathy statement)
   
   **Synonyms**: I understand your perspective, I can see your point of view, I get where you're coming from
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't understand your position, I can't relate
   
   **When to Use**: Use when demonstrating empathy, validating another's perspective, or showing effort to understand. Builds rapport and trust in conflicts or emotional conversations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can ring false if not genuine or if you actually don't understand. Don't use to dismiss concerns ("I see your point, but...")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm trying to see it from your perspective (acknowledges effort)
   - I can see where you're coming from (more casual)
   - Help me see it through your eyes (request for understanding)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "See through" emphasizes adopting another's lens/viewpoint. Positive politeness showing attention to recipient's wants. Validates feelings and perspective.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Appreciative: "Thank you for understanding" / "I appreciate that"
   - Skeptical: "Do you really?" / "I'm not sure you do"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I can totally see it from your perspective—that would upset me too."
   - "Help me see it through your eyes. What am I missing here?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I appreciate your position and can see the situation from your perspective."
   - "Allow me to consider this matter from your organization's perspective to better understand your concerns."
