1. **Adjective**: through (attributive use)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Going directly from one point to another without stopping or changing; direct and continuous (used before nouns like "train," "ticket," "route")
   - Extending from one end or side to the other; passing all the way through
   
   **Synonyms**: direct, nonstop, continuous, uninterrupted
   
   **Antonyms**: indirect, stopping, connecting
   
   **When to Use**: Use before nouns to describe transportation, routes, or connections that are direct and uninterrupted. Common in travel and logistics contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use predicatively (*"the train is through" is incorrect for this sense). Not used for general directness outside transportation contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - through train
   - through ticket
   - through route
   
   **Root Analysis**: From preposition "through" extended to attributive adjective use
   
   **Etymology**: Extended from prepositional use to adjectival function in 19th century, particularly in transportation terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is this a through train to Manchester, or do I need to change?
   - I got a through ticket, so I don't have to buy another one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company offers through freight services between major industrial centers.
   - Passengers with through tickets may proceed directly to the departure gate.

1. **Adjective**: thoroughgoing
   **Meaning**: 
   - Complete and absolute; thorough in every respect; comprehensive and extreme (often used for changes, reforms, or characteristics)
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, absolute, thorough, comprehensive, total
   
   **Antonyms**: partial, incomplete, superficial, halfhearted
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to emphasize completeness or extremity. Common in descriptions of reforms, changes, analyses, or personal characteristics.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation; sounds formal or literary. Not appropriate for minor or partial actions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - thoroughgoing reform
   - thoroughgoing analysis
   - thoroughgoing change
   
   **Root Analysis**: thorough (complete) + going (proceeding through)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound formed in late 16th century combining "thorough" (variant of "through") with present participle "going" to emphasize completeness
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's a thoroughgoing skeptic—doesn't believe anything without proof.
   - The place needs a thoroughgoing cleanup, not just tidying up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee recommended thoroughgoing reform of the existing regulatory framework.
   - A thoroughgoing examination of the evidence reveals significant inconsistencies.

1. **Adjective**: see-through
   **Meaning**: 
   - Transparent or translucent; able to be seen through; allowing light and images to pass through so that objects behind can be distinctly seen
   
   **Synonyms**: transparent, translucent, sheer, diaphanous, clear
   
   **Antonyms**: opaque, solid, non-transparent
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe materials or fabrics that allow visibility through them. Common in fashion, architecture, and material descriptions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with the phrasal verb "see through" (detect deception). Hyphenation is required for the adjective form.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - see-through fabric
   - see-through material
   - see-through packaging
   
   **Root Analysis**: see (perceive visually) + through (to the other side) - hyphenated compound
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century compound adjective formed from phrasal verb "see through," particularly common in fashion and design terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That blouse is way too see-through; you need to wear something underneath.
   - I love these see-through storage boxes—you can see what's inside!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The architect specified see-through panels to maximize natural light transmission.
   - See-through packaging allows consumers to inspect product quality before purchase.

1. **Adjective**: through-and-through (attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Complete in every way; thoroughly and entirely; having a specified quality in every aspect (typically follows the noun it modifies)
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, absolute, total, thorough, out-and-out
   
   **Antonyms**: partial, superficial, halfhearted
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize that someone or something completely embodies a particular characteristic. Often used for personal qualities or essential characteristics.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound informal or colloquial; less common in formal academic writing. Typically used predicatively or following the noun.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - gentleman through-and-through
   - professional through-and-through
   - honest through-and-through
   
   **Root Analysis**: through + and + through (repetition for emphasis)
   
   **Etymology**: Idiomatic compound formed by repeating "through" to emphasize completeness, developed in 19th century American and British English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's a New Yorker through-and-through—born and raised in Brooklyn.
   - She's a teacher through-and-through; she can't help but explain everything!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The document reveals him to be a pragmatist through-and-through, focused on achievable outcomes.
   - The organization's culture is collaborative through-and-through, from leadership to entry-level positions.

1. **Adjective**: pass-through (attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Allowing movement or passage through without obstruction; designed for transit rather than stopping
   - Relating to costs or charges that are transferred from one party to another without markup (business/finance)
   
   **Synonyms**: transit, transitional, transferred (depending on context)
   
   **Antonyms**: terminating, final-destination, retained
   
   **When to Use**: Use in architectural contexts for spaces, or business contexts for costs/entities. Technical term in finance and real estate.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Requires hyphenation when used attributively. Context determines whether spatial or financial meaning applies.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pass-through room
   - pass-through costs
   - pass-through entity
   
   **Root Analysis**: pass (move by/through) + through (from one side to another) - compound
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century compound, originally architectural term, later extended to business/financial terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The kitchen is just a pass-through room to get to the backyard.
   - They're adding a pass-through window between the kitchen and dining room.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Pass-through entities avoid double taxation by transferring income directly to stakeholders.
   - The contract stipulates that pass-through costs must be documented with original receipts.

1. **Adjective**: straight-through
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proceeding directly without deviation, interruption, or stopping; continuous from start to finish
   
   **Synonyms**: direct, uninterrupted, continuous, nonstop
   
   **Antonyms**: indirect, circuitous, interrupted
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize directness and lack of interruption in processes, routes, or procedures. Common in technical and transportation contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Somewhat technical or specialized; simpler terms like "direct" may be preferable in casual contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - straight-through processing
   - straight-through connection
   - straight-through cable
   
   **Root Analysis**: straight (direct, without curves) + through (from beginning to end)
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century technical compound emphasizing both directness and completeness of passage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We took the straight-through highway route—no scenic detours.
   - I prefer the straight-through flight even if it's a bit more expensive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Straight-through processing reduces transaction time and eliminates manual intervention.
   - The system employs straight-through cable connections to minimize signal interference.

1. **Adjective**: drive-through (attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Designed to allow customers to receive service while remaining in their vehicles; accessible without leaving one's car
   
   **Synonyms**: drive-in (partially synonymous), drive-up
   
   **Antonyms**: walk-in, dine-in, sit-down
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe service facilities accessed from vehicles. Primarily North American usage, common in fast food and banking contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Regional term more common in American English. British English often uses "drive-through" but less frequently than American usage.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - drive-through restaurant
   - drive-through window
   - drive-through service
   
   **Root Analysis**: drive (operate a vehicle) + through (passage without stopping)
   
   **Etymology**: American English innovation from mid-20th century, reflecting car culture and convenience services
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's just grab something from the drive-through—I don't want to go inside.
   - The drive-through line is always crazy during lunch hour.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The bank installed a new drive-through facility to enhance customer convenience.
   - Drive-through service options increased significantly during the pandemic period.

1. **Adjective**: walk-through (attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Designed to be passed through on foot; allowing pedestrian passage
   - Designating a demonstration or practice session where procedures are explained step by step
   
   **Synonyms**: pedestrian (for first sense), guided, practice (for second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: dead-end, blocked (for first sense), unguided (for second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical spaces allowing pedestrian passage, or for instructional/practice sessions. Common in real estate, security, and entertainment contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Requires hyphenation in attributive position. Context determines whether physical or procedural meaning applies.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - walk-through closet
   - walk-through metal detector
   - walk-through rehearsal
   
   **Root Analysis**: walk (pedestrian movement) + through (passage from one side to another)
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century compound with architectural sense preceding the rehearsal/demonstration sense (theater terminology)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The master bedroom has a walk-through closet that connects to the bathroom.
   - Can we do a quick walk-through before opening night?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Security personnel conducted a walk-through inspection of the facility perimeter.
   - The director scheduled a technical walk-through to resolve staging issues.

1. **Adjective**: run-through (attributive when hyphenated)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to a practice or rehearsal session; involving a complete execution from beginning to end for practice purposes
   
   **Synonyms**: practice, rehearsal, trial
   
   **Antonyms**: final, performance, actual
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe practice sessions, particularly in performance arts, presentations, or procedural contexts. Requires hyphenation when used as adjective.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More commonly used as a noun ("a run-through"). As adjective, less common than noun form.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - run-through session
   - run-through rehearsal
   - run-through practice
   
   **Root Analysis**: run (execute continuously) + through (from beginning to end)
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century theatrical terminology, extended to other contexts requiring complete practice executions
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need one more run-through session before the presentation tomorrow.
   - That was just a run-through version—the real thing will be better!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee scheduled a run-through session to identify potential procedural issues.
   - Run-through rehearsals are mandatory for all performers prior to the opening performance.

1. **Adjective**: soaked-through / wet-through
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely soaked or wet throughout; saturated with liquid in every part
   
   **Synonyms**: soaked, saturated, drenched, waterlogged, sodden
   
   **Antonyms**: dry, arid, moisture-free
   
   **When to Use**: Use predicatively to emphasize complete saturation. Common in informal British English especially "wet-through."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Somewhat informal or colloquial. In formal writing, "completely saturated" or "thoroughly soaked" may be preferable.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - clothes soaked-through
   - wet-through from rain
   - soaked-through to the skin
   
   **Root Analysis**: soaked/wet (saturated with liquid) + through (completely, throughout)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound participle construction using "through" as intensifier, particularly common in British English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I got caught in the rain and now I'm wet-through—need to change!
   - The tent leaked and our sleeping bags are soaked-through.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The archaeological specimens were soaked-through with moisture, requiring immediate conservation treatment.
   - Garments that are wet-through should be removed promptly to prevent hypothermia.
