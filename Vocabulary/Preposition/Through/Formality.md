1. **Original Expression**: I'm going through a tough time right now  
   **Context**: Personal disclosure of difficulty

   **Core Intent**: Communicate that one is experiencing a difficult period

   **Casual**: I'm having a rough time right now  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary, direct, emotionally open  
   *Use when*: Close friends, family, informal support settings

   **Semi-Formal**: I'm dealing with some challenges at the moment  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral vocabulary, professional distance, measured tone  
   *Use when*: Colleagues, acquaintances, professional contexts requiring some openness

   **Formal**: I am currently navigating a particularly challenging period  
   *Characteristics*: Elevated vocabulary ("navigating," "particularly"), complete sentences, professional distance  
   *Use when*: Professional correspondence, formal workplace, authority figures

   **Humorous**: I'm in the middle of my own personal disaster movie right now  
   *Characteristics*: Exaggeration, pop culture reference, self-deprecating humor  
   *Use when*: Close friends, lighthearted contexts, coping through humor  
   *Avoid when*: Serious situations, professional settings, people who don't know you well

   **Lively**: Things are crazy intense for me right now—total rollercoaster!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic vocabulary, metaphor, exclamatory tone  
   *Use when*: Casual conversation, sharing updates with friends, storytelling

   **Empathetic**: I'm going through something really difficult right now, and it's been hard  
   *Characteristics*: Emotional openness, vulnerability markers, invitation for support  
   *Use when*: Seeking emotional support, therapy, close relationships

   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status contexts require formal; equal status allows casual
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/colleagues need semi-formal; close relationships allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious professional matters require formal; personal sharing allows more casualness
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures value stoicism in professional settings
   - *Medium*: Written formal communication requires more distance than face-to-face

   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual "tough time" in formal professional contexts—sounds too colloquial
   - Being too formal with close friends seeking emotional connection—creates distance
   - Using humorous variant when situation is genuinely serious—seems insensitive

   **Cultural Notes**: Anglo cultures generally value emotional restraint in professional contexts, while personal relationships allow openness. Mediterranean and Latin American cultures may accept more emotional expression across contexts.

   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Email to boss explaining need for flexibility → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal or Formal (professional boundary while explaining need)
   - **Situation 2**: Text to best friend explaining absence → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual (closeness allows directness)
   - **Situation 3**: Meeting with HR about accommodations → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (official, documented conversation)

   *Use when*: Engaging presentations, teaching contexts, building excitement

   **Empathetic**: I know this seems complicated, so I'll guide you through it carefully, and we'll go at your pace  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges difficulty, reassurance, pacing sensitivity  
   *Use when*: Anxious learners, complex material, supportive teaching contexts

   - *Power Distance*: Training subordinates allows semi-formal; presenting to executives requires formal
   - *Social Distance*: Training strangers requires semi-formal minimum; friends allow casual

   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect formal instructor-student hierarchy

   *Use when*: Animated conversation, expressing strong reaction, storytelling

   **Empathetic**: I notice some things don't add up, and it makes it hard for me to trust what you're saying  
   *Characteristics*: "I" statements, non-accusatory, focus on personal response  
   *Use when*: Direct conversation with deceiver, maintaining relationship, therapeutic contexts

   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Challenging authority requires formal, careful framing; discussing peers allows casual
   - *Social Distance*: Direct confrontation with stranger needs semi-formal; venting to friends allows casual

   - Using accusatory casual language in workplace confrontation—escalates conflict
   - Being too formal when situation requires direct honesty—sounds evasive
   - Using humorous variant when genuinely hurt by deception—dismisses own feelings

   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Discussing colleague's false claims with supervisor → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal to Formal (professional, evidence-based)
