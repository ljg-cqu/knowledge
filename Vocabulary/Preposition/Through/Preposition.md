1. **Preposition**: through (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving in one side or end and out of the other side or end of something; from one side to the other
   - *(Temporal)* From the beginning to the end of a period of time; during the entire time
   - *(Abstract)* By means of; by the agency or instrumentality of someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: via (in transit contexts), throughout (for duration), by means of (for agency), across (for movement)
   
   **Antonyms**: around (avoiding), outside (not entering)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express movement from one end to another (spatial), continuous time periods (temporal), or means/agency (abstract). Essential for describing passage, completion, and instrumental relationships.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use for surface contact (use "on" instead), or for enclosed location without passage (use "in"). Avoid confusing "through" (completed action) with "throughout" (distributed across entire area/time).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: through the door, through the night, through experience
   - **Verb + prep**: go through, pass through, break through, see through, live through
   - **Adjective + prep**: halfway through, partway through
   - **Noun + prep**: passage through, journey through, path through
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "thurh" meaning "from end to end, from side to side"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "thurh, þurh" → Middle English "thurgh, thorow" → Modern English "through" (Germanic origin, related to Dutch "door" and German "durch")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I walked through the park to get home faster.
   - We stayed up talking through the whole night.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research was conducted through systematic analysis of historical documents.
   - Progress was achieved through collaborative efforts across multiple departments.

1. **Preposition**: through with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Finished with; having completed something; no longer involved in or dealing with something
   
   **Synonyms**: finished with, done with, over with, completed with
   
   **Antonyms**: engaged in, involved with, working on
   
   **When to Use**: Use in informal and semi-formal contexts to express completion or cessation of involvement. Common in spoken English and casual writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in formal academic writing; use "completed," "finished," or "concluded" instead. Not appropriate for ongoing situations.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: through with the project, through with him/her
   - **Verb + prep**: be through with, get through with
   - **Adjective + prep**: nearly through with, finally through with
   
   **Root Analysis**: Combination of "through" (completion) + "with" (association)
   
   **Etymology**: Emerged in American English in the 19th century as an idiomatic expression combining the sense of "through" (completion) with "with" (instrumental case)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm through with trying to help him—he never listens anyway.
   - Are you through with the computer? I need to use it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee indicated it was through with preliminary discussions and ready to proceed.
   - Once the organization is through with the restructuring, operations will resume normally.

1. **Preposition**: all through (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* During the entire duration of a period; continuously from beginning to end
   - *(Spatial)* In every part of; throughout the entire area or extent
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, all throughout, during the entirety of, for the whole of
   
   **Antonyms**: intermittently during, partially through
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize continuous duration or comprehensive spatial coverage. Adds emphasis compared to simple "through" or "throughout."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundancy; often "throughout" alone is sufficient. Not needed when the continuous nature is already clear from context.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: all through the day, all through the house, all through winter
   - **Verb + prep**: work all through, sleep all through, stay all through
   - **Adverb + prep**: consistently all through, present all through
   
   **Root Analysis**: "all" (entirety) + "through" (from end to end)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic construction combining quantifier "all" with preposition "through" to stress completeness (developed in Middle English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The baby cried all through the flight—it was exhausting!
   - You can find her drawings all through the notebook.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theme of resilience appears all through the author's literary works.
   - Economic stability was maintained all through the fiscal year despite market volatility.

1. **Preposition**: right through (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Directly through; straight through without deviation
   - *(Abstract)* Completely through; with total penetration or completion
   
   **Synonyms**: straight through, directly through, clean through, completely through
   
   **Antonyms**: around, partially through
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize directness or completeness of passage. Common in both spatial descriptions and abstract contexts to stress thoroughness.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts where "through" alone is sufficiently clear. May sound overly emphatic in formal writing unless emphasis is specifically intended.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: right through the middle, right through winter
   - **Verb + prep**: go right through, pass right through, see right through (idiom)
   - **Adverb + prep**: straight right through, directly right through
   
   **Root Analysis**: "right" (directly, exactly) + "through" (from one end to the other)
   
   **Etymology**: Combination of intensifying adverb "right" (from Old English "riht") with "through" to emphasize directness or completeness (common in Modern English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The bullet went right through the wall—scary stuff!
   - I can see right through his excuses; he's clearly lying.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation proceeded right through standard protocols without deviation.
   - The reform measures cut right through bureaucratic inefficiencies to address core issues.

1. **Preposition**: halfway through (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal/Spatial)* At the middle point of a period, process, distance, or object; at the midpoint
   
   **Synonyms**: midway through, at the middle of, in the middle of
   
   **Antonyms**: at the beginning of, at the end of
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate the middle or approximate middle point of something. Useful for progress reports or spatial descriptions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when precise measurement is needed; "halfway" suggests approximately 50% but is not exact. Don't use for non-linear or non-continuous processes.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: halfway through the book, halfway through the project
   - **Verb + prep**: be halfway through, get halfway through, stop halfway through
   - **Adverb + prep**: only halfway through, already halfway through
   
   **Root Analysis**: "halfway" (middle point) + "through" (progressive passage)
   
   **Etymology**: "Halfway" (Old English "healfweg") combined with "through" to express midpoint position in a progressive action
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm only halfway through season two—no spoilers, please!
   - We were halfway through dinner when the power went out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team is currently halfway through data collection for the longitudinal study.
   - The company reached the halfway through point of its five-year strategic plan.

1. **Preposition**: through to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Continuing from one point in time until another; extending all the way to a specified end point
   - *(Abstract)* Successfully progressing from one stage to the next (especially in competitions)
   
   **Synonyms**: until, up to, all the way to, onwards to
   
   **Antonyms**: up to but not including
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize continuity from a starting point to a specific endpoint. Common in British English for time periods and progression contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More common in British than American English; American English often uses "through" alone or "until." Avoid redundancy when simple "to" or "until" is clearer.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: Monday through to Friday, through to the finals
   - **Verb + prep**: continue through to, extend through to, run through to
   - **Noun + prep**: progression through to, advancement through to
   
   **Root Analysis**: "through" (continuous passage) + "to" (directional endpoint)
   
   **Etymology**: British English construction emphasizing continuous progression with clear endpoint (20th century usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The café is open Monday through to Saturday, 7am to 6pm.
   - She made it through to the final round of auditions!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy remains in effect from January through to December of the current fiscal year.
   - Three candidates progressed through to the interview stage based on their qualifications.

1. **Preposition**: see through (type: phrasal combination)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* To perceive the true nature of someone or something, especially when deceptive; to not be deceived by
   - *(Abstract)* To continue with something until completion; to support someone until the end
   
   **Synonyms**: perceive the truth about, detect deception in (first sense); complete, finish, support through (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: be deceived by, fall for (first sense); abandon, quit (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use in contexts involving deception detection or commitment to completion. Common in both informal and formal contexts with slightly different connotations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with literal physical seeing through transparent objects. Context must make clear which sense is intended.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: see through lies, see through pretense
   - **Verb + prep**: can see through, easily see through, see right through
   - **Verb + object + prep**: see it through, see him through, see the project through
   
   **Root Analysis**: "see" (perceive) + "through" (penetrating to the other side)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical extension of physical "seeing through" to abstract perception, developed in Middle English; completion sense emerged later from "through" meaning "to the end"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can totally see through his fake apology—he doesn't mean it.
   - Don't worry, I'll see you through this tough time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Experienced investors can see through misleading financial reports.
   - The organization committed resources to see the initiative through to completion.

1. **Preposition**: go through (type: phrasal combination)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* To experience or endure something, especially something difficult or unpleasant
   - *(Abstract)* To examine or search through something carefully and systematically
   - *(Abstract)* To be approved or accepted; to pass successfully (for proposals, bills, etc.)
   
   **Synonyms**: experience, endure, undergo (first sense); examine, review, search (second sense); be approved, pass (third sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid, escape (first sense); overlook, skip (second sense); be rejected, fail (third sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Versatile phrasal verb appropriate in most contexts. First sense common for difficulties, second for examination, third for formal processes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context must clarify which sense is intended, as meanings vary significantly. Don't use the "experience" sense for trivial positive experiences.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: go through difficulties, go through the documents, go through parliament
   - **Verb + prep**: have to go through, will go through, going through
   - **Noun + prep**: go through the process, go through a phase, go through changes
   
   **Root Analysis**: "go" (move, proceed) + "through" (from beginning to end)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb developed from combining motion verb "go" with "through"; metaphorical senses of experiencing and examining developed in Modern English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't believe what she's going through right now—it's heartbreaking.
   - Can you go through these emails and delete the spam?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients going through chemotherapy require comprehensive support services.
   - The proposal must go through several committees before final approval.

1. **Preposition**: break through (type: phrasal combination)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* To force a passage through something by breaking or penetrating it
   - *(Abstract)* To make a sudden advance, especially after overcoming obstacles; to achieve success after a period of difficulty
   
   **Synonyms**: penetrate, pierce (first sense); succeed, achieve a breakthrough, overcome (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: be blocked by, be stopped by (first sense); be stymied, remain unsuccessful (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical penetration or metaphorical success after struggle. Common in scientific, military, and achievement contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for gradual progress; "break through" implies sudden or forceful advancement. Avoid for routine successes without prior difficulty.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: break through barriers, break through the surface
   - **Verb + prep**: manage to break through, finally break through, break through into
   - **Noun + prep**: breakthrough moment, breakthrough discovery (related noun form)
   
   **Root Analysis**: "break" (force apart) + "through" (to the other side)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "brecan" combined with "through"; metaphorical sense of overcoming obstacles developed in Modern English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The sun finally broke through the clouds around noon.
   - She's been struggling for years, but I think she's about to break through as an artist.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers broke through significant technical barriers to achieve these results.
   - The military forces broke through enemy defenses at the northern perimeter.

1. **Preposition**: fall through (type: phrasal combination)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* To fail to be completed or to happen; to collapse or come to nothing (for plans, arrangements, deals)
   
   **Synonyms**: collapse, fail, come to nothing, not materialize, break down
   
   **Antonyms**: succeed, materialize, be completed, go ahead
   
   **When to Use**: Use when plans, negotiations, or arrangements fail to come to fruition. Common in business, legal, and everyday contexts for disappointed expectations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for deliberate cancellations; "fall through" implies unintended failure. Not appropriate for successful outcomes or ongoing situations.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: deal fell through, plans fell through
   - **Verb + prep**: might fall through, could fall through, fell through at the last minute
   - **Noun + prep**: transaction fell through, arrangement fell through
   
   **Root Analysis**: "fall" (collapse, fail) + "through" (to completion—but negated by "fall")
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical use combining "fall" (descend, fail) with "through" to suggest something that should have been supported "falls through" gaps, like falling through a weak floor (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our vacation plans fell through when the airline went bankrupt.
   - I was supposed to buy his car, but the deal fell through.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The merger fell through due to regulatory complications and disagreements over valuation.
   - Multiple real estate transactions fell through during the economic downturn.
