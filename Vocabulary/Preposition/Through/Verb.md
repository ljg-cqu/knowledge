1. **Verb**: go through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To experience or endure something, especially something difficult, unpleasant, or challenging
   - (Transitive) To examine, search, or review something carefully and systematically
   - (Intransitive) To be approved, accepted, or successfully completed (for proposals, legislation, transactions)
   - (Transitive) To use up or consume something completely
   
   **Synonyms**: experience, endure, undergo (first sense); examine, review, search (second sense); be approved, pass (third sense); consume, use up (fourth sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid (first sense); overlook, ignore (second sense); be rejected, fail (third sense); conserve, save (fourth sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Extremely versatile phrasal verb appropriate in most contexts. Meaning depends heavily on context and object type.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ensure context makes intended meaning clear, as this verb has multiple distinct senses that can create ambiguity.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go through difficulties
   - go through the documents
   - go through parliament
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (move, proceed) + through (from beginning to end, completely)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gan" + "thurh"; metaphorical senses developed from physical passage to experiencing and examining
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't imagine what she's going through right now.
   - Can you go through these files and organize them?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients going through treatment require comprehensive support services.
   - The legislation must go through multiple committees before ratification.

1. **Verb**: get through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To succeed in making contact, especially by telephone or communication
   - (Transitive) To finish or complete something, especially something difficult or tedious
   - (Transitive) To survive or endure a difficult period
   - (Transitive) To make someone understand something; to communicate successfully
   
   **Synonyms**: make contact, reach (first sense); complete, finish (second sense); survive, endure (third sense); communicate, make understood (fourth sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: fail to reach (first sense); abandon, quit (second sense); succumb (third sense); fail to communicate (fourth sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use in contexts involving completion, communication, or survival through difficulty. Common in both formal and informal registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple meanings require clear context. Can be ambiguous without sufficient contextual clues.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get through to someone
   - get through the day
   - get through the work
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (achieve, accomplish) + through (to completion, to the other side)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb combining "get" with "through," developed multiple figurative senses in Modern English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been trying to call all morning but can't get through.
   - How did you get through all that work so fast?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The office was unable to get through to the remote facility due to network failures.
   - Organizations must implement strategies to help employees get through crisis periods.

1. **Verb**: see through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To perceive the true nature of something or someone, especially when deceptive; to not be deceived
   - (Transitive) To continue with something until it is completed; to support or help someone through difficulty
   
   **Synonyms**: detect, perceive, discern (first sense); complete, finish, support (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: be deceived by, believe (first sense); abandon, desert (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: First sense common for deception detection; second sense emphasizes commitment and completion. Context usually makes meaning clear.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with literal "see through" (physical transparency). Idiomatic senses are separate from literal meaning.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - see through lies
   - see through deception
   - see someone through
   
   **Root Analysis**: see (perceive) + through (to the truth/to completion)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical extension from physical seeing through to perceptual penetration and completion commitment
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can totally see through his excuses—he just doesn't want to come.
   - Don't worry, I'll see you through this tough time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Experienced analysts can see through misleading financial presentations.
   - The foundation committed resources to see the initiative through to completion.

1. **Verb**: break through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive/Transitive) To force a passage through something by breaking or penetrating it
   - (Intransitive) To achieve success or make important progress after a period of difficulty or stagnation
   
   **Synonyms**: penetrate, pierce, force through (first sense); succeed, achieve, make progress (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: be blocked, be stopped (first sense); stagnate, fail (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical penetration or metaphorical success after struggle. Implies force or sudden advancement.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies significant effort or breakthrough moment. Don't use for gradual progress or routine advancement.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - break through barriers
   - break through defenses
   - break through to success
   
   **Root Analysis**: break (force apart, penetrate) + through (to the other side)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "brecan" + "through"; military usage predates metaphorical success sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The sun finally broke through around lunchtime.
   - After years of struggle, she's finally breaking through as an artist.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers broke through significant technical obstacles to achieve these findings.
   - The diplomatic team broke through longstanding negotiation impasses.

1. **Verb**: fall through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To fail to happen or be completed; to collapse or come to nothing (for plans, arrangements, deals)
   
   **Synonyms**: collapse, fail, break down, come to nothing, not materialize
   
   **Antonyms**: succeed, materialize, be completed, work out
   
   **When to Use**: Use when plans or arrangements fail despite expectations they would succeed. Implies unintended failure rather than cancellation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for deliberate cancellations or decisions not to proceed. "Fall through" suggests plans collapsed unexpectedly.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - plans fell through
   - deal fell through
   - arrangement fell through
   
   **Root Analysis**: fall (collapse, fail) + through (implying falling through gaps in support)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical image of plans falling through weak floor or gaps, 19th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our weekend plans fell through when everyone got sick.
   - The apartment deal fell through at the last minute.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed merger fell through due to regulatory complications.
   - Multiple financing arrangements fell through during the economic downturn.

1. **Verb**: pull through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To survive a dangerous illness or difficult situation; to recover
   - (Transitive) To help someone survive or recover from difficulty
   
   **Synonyms**: survive, recover, make it (first sense); help survive, save (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: succumb, die, fail (first sense); abandon (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use particularly for medical recovery or survival through crisis. Emphasizes overcoming serious difficulty.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Generally reserved for serious situations involving danger or severe difficulty. Overuse diminishes impact.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pull through surgery
   - pull through a crisis
   - help someone pull through
   
   **Root Analysis**: pull (draw with effort) + through (to safety/recovery)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphor of pulling someone through danger to safety, 19th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The doctors weren't sure he'd make it, but he pulled through.
   - Your support really helped me pull through that rough patch.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The patient pulled through the critical phase and is now stable.
   - Organizational resilience enabled the company to pull through the economic crisis.

1. **Verb**: come through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To succeed in doing what is needed or expected, especially in difficult circumstances; to fulfill commitments
   - (Intransitive) To survive or successfully complete a difficult or dangerous situation
   - (Intransitive) To be transmitted or received successfully (for messages, calls, signals)
   
   **Synonyms**: deliver, succeed, fulfill promises (first sense); survive, endure (second sense); be received, arrive (third sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: fail, disappoint (first sense); succumb (second sense); be lost, fail to arrive (third sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize reliability or successful completion against expectations. Positive connotation of meeting or exceeding requirements.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies previous doubt or difficulty. Not appropriate when success was certain or easy.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - come through for someone
   - come through with results
   - message came through
   
   **Root Analysis**: come (arrive, succeed) + through (to completion, successfully)
   
   **Etymology**: Developed from literal "come through" (arrive via passage) to metaphorical success and reliability
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I knew you'd come through for me—you always do!
   - Did my text message come through? I wasn't sure it sent.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The team came through with outstanding results despite resource constraints.
   - The funding application came through, enabling project continuation.

1. **Verb**: follow through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To continue an action or task to its conclusion; to complete what one has started
   - (Intransitive) In sports: to continue a motion after hitting or throwing, completing the full swing or stroke
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, finish, carry through, see through (first sense); complete the motion (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: abandon, quit, give up, stop short
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize completion and commitment. Common in contexts requiring reliability and finishing what was started.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Verb form is two words ("follow through"); noun form is hyphenated ("follow-through").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - follow through on promises
   - follow through with plans
   - follow through on commitments
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow (continue after) + through (to completion)
   
   **Etymology**: From sports terminology (19th century) to general usage emphasizing completion of commitments
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He never follows through—always starts things but never finishes.
   - Make sure you follow through on your golf swing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Effective leadership requires the ability to follow through on strategic initiatives.
   - Athletes must follow through completely to achieve optimal performance results.

1. **Verb**: run through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To rehearse or practice something completely from beginning to end
   - (Transitive) To review or summarize something quickly; to go over main points
   - (Transitive) To use up or spend money completely and often wastefully
   - (Transitive) To pierce or stab through with a blade or weapon
   
   **Synonyms**: rehearse, practice (first sense); review, summarize (second sense); squander, deplete (third sense); pierce, stab (fourth sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: skip, omit (first/second sense); save, conserve (third sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Versatile phrasal verb with distinct meanings. Context determines which sense applies.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Violent fourth sense quite different from others; ensure context prevents misunderstanding.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - run through the presentation
   - run through the budget
   - run through one's inheritance
   
   **Root Analysis**: run (move through quickly/continuously) + through (from beginning to end)
   
   **Etymology**: Multiple senses developed from basic notion of moving continuously through something
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's run through the presentation one more time before tomorrow.
   - He ran through his entire paycheck in one weekend—terrible with money!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The director requested an opportunity to run through key procedural steps with staff.
   - The organization ran through its reserves during the economic downturn.

1. **Verb**: look through
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To examine or search through something; to browse or review contents
   - (Transitive) To deliberately ignore someone by pretending not to see them; to look past intentionally
   - (Transitive) To look at something transparent or to see through to the other side
   
   **Synonyms**: examine, search, browse (first sense); ignore, snub (second sense); see through (third sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: overlook, ignore (first sense); acknowledge, greet (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: First sense most common. Second sense implies intentional social snubbing. Third sense literal.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Second sense can sound confrontational; ensure it matches your intent.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look through documents
   - look through someone
   - look through the window
   
   **Root Analysis**: look (direct one's gaze) + through (throughout/past/to other side)
   
   **Etymology**: Literal and metaphorical senses developed from basic visual perception through space
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you look through these photos and pick your favorites?
   - She looked right through me like I wasn't even there—so rude!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Reviewers were asked to look through submitted proposals and provide assessments.
   - The committee will look through applications systematically before making decisions.
