1. **Noun**: breakthrough (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A sudden, dramatic, and important discovery or development, especially in science or technology that removes a barrier to progress
   - An instance of achieving success in a particular sphere or activity, especially after a long period of difficulty
   
   **Synonyms**: discovery, advance, development, innovation, leap forward
   
   **Antonyms**: setback, obstacle, impasse, dead end
   
   **When to Use**: Use for significant advances or discoveries that overcome previous limitations. Common in scientific, medical, technological, and personal achievement contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for minor improvements or routine progress. "Breakthrough" implies overcoming significant barriers or achieving something previously thought difficult.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - major breakthrough
   - scientific breakthrough
   - breakthrough discovery
   
   **Root Analysis**: break (force through barriers) + through (to the other side) - compound noun
   
   **Etymology**: Originally (1930s) military term for penetrating enemy lines; extended metaphorically to scientific discoveries and general achievements
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The team finally had a breakthrough in solving the bug that's been plaguing them for weeks.
   - This new treatment is a real breakthrough for diabetes patients.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research represents a significant breakthrough in our understanding of cellular mechanisms.
   - Recent breakthroughs in renewable energy technology have accelerated the transition from fossil fuels.

1. **Noun**: walkthrough (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A demonstration, tour, or practice session in which procedures or steps are explained in sequence
   - In gaming: a detailed guide showing how to complete a game or level
   - A passage that can be walked through; a space allowing pedestrian access
   
   **Synonyms**: demonstration, tutorial, guide, rehearsal, practice run (depending on context)
   
   **Antonyms**: final performance, actual execution
   
   **When to Use**: Use for guided demonstrations, practice sessions, or instructional contexts. Common in theater, gaming, software, and real estate.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Spelling varies: "walkthrough" (one word) is common in gaming/software; "walk-through" (hyphenated) more common as adjective or in formal contexts; "walk through" (two words) is the verb form.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - do a walkthrough
   - provide a walkthrough
   - video walkthrough
   
   **Root Analysis**: walk (pedestrian movement/guided tour) + through (from beginning to end)
   
   **Etymology**: Theater terminology (early 20th century) extended to other domains; gaming sense emerged in late 20th century with video games
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you give me a quick walkthrough of how to use this software?
   - I'm stuck on level 5—need to find a walkthrough online.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The architect provided a comprehensive walkthrough of the proposed building design.
   - A technical walkthrough was conducted to identify potential system vulnerabilities.

1. **Noun**: runthrough (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A rehearsal or practice session in which something is performed or executed completely from beginning to end
   - A brief review or summary of key points
   
   **Synonyms**: rehearsal, practice session, trial run, overview, summary
   
   **Antonyms**: final performance, actual execution, detailed analysis
   
   **When to Use**: Use primarily in performance, presentation, or procedural contexts. Can also mean a quick review or summary.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Spelling conventions vary: "runthrough" (one word), "run-through" (hyphenated), or "run through" (two words as verb). Hyphenated form most common as noun.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - quick runthrough
   - final runthrough
   - do a runthrough
   
   **Root Analysis**: run (execute continuously) + through (from beginning to end)
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century, primarily from theatrical and performance contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's do one more runthrough before we present to the client.
   - Can I get a quick runthrough of what happened at the meeting?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A final runthrough of operational procedures was conducted prior to system launch.
   - The director scheduled multiple runthroughs to ensure performance quality.

1. **Noun**: drive-through (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A facility, typically a restaurant or bank, designed to serve customers who remain in their vehicles
   - The lane or area where such service is provided
   
   **Synonyms**: drive-thru (informal spelling), drive-in window, drive-up window
   
   **Antonyms**: dine-in, walk-in, sit-down service
   
   **When to Use**: Use primarily in North American contexts for automotive-accessible service facilities. Common in fast food and banking industries.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Regional term, primarily American English. British English understands it but uses less frequently. Alternative spelling "drive-thru" is informal.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go through the drive-through
   - drive-through window
   - drive-through service
   
   **Root Analysis**: drive (operate vehicle) + through (passage without stopping)
   
   **Etymology**: American English, mid-20th century, reflecting automobile culture and convenience services
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's just hit the drive-through—I don't have time for a sit-down meal.
   - The drive-through line is always super long on weekends.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The bank expanded its drive-through facilities to accommodate increased customer demand.
   - Drive-through operations require efficient workflow management to minimize wait times.

1. **Noun**: throughput (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The amount of material, data, or items passing through a system or process in a given time period
   - The rate of production or processing capacity of a system
   
   **Synonyms**: output, capacity, productivity, flow rate, processing rate
   
   **Antonyms**: bottleneck, blockage, stagnation
   
   **When to Use**: Use in technical, manufacturing, computing, or business contexts to describe processing capacity or flow rates. Always uncountable.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Technical term inappropriate for casual conversation unless discussing systems, production, or technical processes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - high throughput
   - throughput capacity
   - increase throughput
   
   **Root Analysis**: through (from input to output) + put (place, process) - compound technical term
   
   **Etymology**: Industrial/technical term from mid-20th century, originally manufacturing, extended to computing and data processing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They need to increase throughput in the warehouse to meet holiday demand.
   - The new router has way better throughput than the old one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system demonstrates throughput rates significantly exceeding initial specifications.
   - Optimizing throughput requires careful analysis of bottlenecks and resource allocation.

1. **Noun**: pass-through (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A room or space that serves primarily as a passage to another area rather than as a destination itself
   - In business: a cost, tax, or charge that is transferred from one party to another without being absorbed
   - In finance: an entity that passes income directly to investors without being taxed at the corporate level
   
   **Synonyms**: transit room, corridor (first sense); transferred cost (second sense); flow-through entity (third sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: destination room (first sense); absorbed cost (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use in architectural contexts for transitional spaces, or in business/finance for transferred costs or specific tax structures.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent meaning requires clarity. Specify which sense you intend if ambiguity is possible.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pass-through room
   - pass-through costs
   - pass-through entity
   
   **Root Analysis**: pass (move through) + through (from one side to another)
   
   **Etymology**: Architectural term (early 20th century) extended to business and financial terminology (mid-20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The kitchen is really just a pass-through to get to the backyard.
   - They're charging us pass-through fees for shipping and handling.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The floor plan includes a pass-through connecting the living areas to the service wing.
   - Pass-through entities allow business income to be taxed at individual rather than corporate rates.

1. **Noun**: follow-through (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of continuing an action, activity, or process to its conclusion; completion of something started
   - In sports: the continuation of a motion after striking a ball or completing a swing
   
   **Synonyms**: completion, continuation, execution, implementation (first sense); follow-up motion (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: abandonment, incompletion, giving up
   
   **When to Use**: Use for completion of commitments or continuation of actions. Common in business, personal responsibility contexts, and sports.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Hyphenation required for noun form. Verb form is two words: "follow through."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lack of follow-through
   - good follow-through
   - follow-through on commitments
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow (continue after) + through (to completion)
   
   **Etymology**: Sports terminology (19th century) extended to general usage for completing commitments (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's got great ideas but terrible follow-through—never finishes anything.
   - Your golf swing needs better follow-through.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Effective leadership requires consistent follow-through on strategic initiatives.
   - The player's follow-through demonstrates proper biomechanical technique.

1. **Noun**: see-through (C) - informal/rare as noun
   **Meaning**: 
   - A garment or fabric that is transparent or translucent (informal, rare as standalone noun)
   
   **Synonyms**: transparent garment, sheer fabric
   
   **Antonyms**: opaque garment
   
   **When to Use**: Rarely used as standalone noun; more commonly used as adjective ("see-through blouse"). As noun, very informal.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Primarily adjectival; noun usage is uncommon and informal. Better to use "transparent garment" or "sheer fabric."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wearing a see-through (informal)
   - see-through material (adjectival use more common)
   
   **Root Analysis**: see (perceive) + through (to the other side)
   
   **Etymology**: 20th century fashion terminology, primarily adjectival; noun usage emerged informally
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She wore a see-through over her tank top—it looked really stylish.
   - I can't wear that; it's totally a see-through!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The designer's collection featured garments constructed from see-through materials. (adjectival use preferred)
   - Fashion trends included increased use of translucent and see-through fabrics. (adjectival)

1. **Noun**: cut-through (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A shortcut or passage that allows one to go through rather than around something
   - The act of reducing complexity or bureaucracy to achieve direct action (business jargon)
   
   **Synonyms**: shortcut, passage, direct route (first sense); simplification, streamlining (second sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: detour, roundabout route (first sense); complication, red tape (second sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical shortcuts or metaphorical simplification of processes. British English more common than American.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound like jargon in business contexts. Physical sense clearer and more widely understood.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take a cut-through
   - find a cut-through
   - cut-through strategy
   
   **Root Analysis**: cut (create passage) + through (to the other side)
   
   **Etymology**: Originally physical passage (18th-19th century), extended to business metaphor (late 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a cut-through behind the shops that'll save us ten minutes.
   - Let me show you the cut-through to the park.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The urban plan includes several pedestrian cut-throughs to improve neighborhood connectivity.
   - The strategy emphasizes cut-through approaches to reduce administrative overhead.

1. **Noun**: throughway / thruway (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A major road or highway designed for fast, through traffic; an expressway
   - Often used as proper noun for specific highways (particularly in New York: "New York State Thruway")
   
   **Synonyms**: expressway, freeway, highway, motorway (British), superhighway
   
   **Antonyms**: local road, side street, residential street
   
   **When to Use**: Use for major highways designed for through traffic. "Thruway" is simplified spelling often used in proper names.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Regional term, particularly American English. British English uses "motorway." Spelling varies: "throughway" or "thruway."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - state thruway
   - major throughway
   - interstate throughway
   
   **Root Analysis**: through (passage without stopping) + way (road, path)
   
   **Etymology**: American English, mid-20th century, coined for new limited-access highways; "thruway" is phonetic simplification
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Take the thruway; it's way faster than the local roads.
   - Traffic is backed up on the throughway for miles.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The state invested heavily in throughway infrastructure development during the post-war period.
   - Throughway systems facilitate efficient long-distance vehicular transportation.
