1. **Adverb**: through (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - From one end or side to the other; passing completely from one side or end to another
   - To completion; all the way to the end; completely finished
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, all the way, from end to end, thoroughly
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, incompletely, halfway
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate complete passage or completion. Can appear in multiple positions but often follows the verb or appears at the end of the clause.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with prepositional uses. As adverb, it doesn't take an object and modifies the verb directly.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - let someone through
   - go through (completely)
   - read through
   
   **Root Analysis**: From preposition "through" used without object
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "thurh" extended from prepositional to adverbial function, meaning "from one end to the other"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The water leaked through and damaged the ceiling below.
   - Are you through with your homework yet?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The application was reviewed and approved, allowing it to pass through successfully.
   - Once the analysis is complete, the recommendations will be implemented through.

1. **Adverb**: right through (type: manner/degree - intensified)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely through without stopping or deviation; straight through to the other side
   - Emphasizes thoroughness or completeness of passage or penetration
   
   **Synonyms**: straight through, completely through, all the way through
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, incompletely, around
   
   **When to Use**: Use for emphasis on directness or completeness. Common in both spatial and temporal contexts to stress uninterrupted passage.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundancy; use only when emphasis is genuinely needed. "Through" alone may be sufficient in many contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pass right through
   - go right through
   - sleep right through
   
   **Root Analysis**: right (intensifier meaning "directly, exactly") + through (from end to end)
   
   **Etymology**: Intensifying construction where "right" serves as adverbial intensifier (common since Middle English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The noise was so loud it went right through my headphones!
   - I slept right through my alarm—didn't hear a thing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The projectile passed right through the protective barrier, demonstrating its inadequacy.
   - The reform measures cut right through existing bureaucratic structures.

1. **Adverb**: all the way through (type: manner - emphasizing completeness)
   **Meaning**: 
   - From the very beginning to the very end; completely and entirely; throughout the entire duration or extent
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, entirely, throughout, from start to finish
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, halfway, incompletely
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize complete coverage or duration. Adds strong emphasis compared to "through" alone.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be redundant in some contexts. Consider whether "through" or "throughout" alone would suffice.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go all the way through
   - read all the way through
   - last all the way through
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (entirety) + the way (path/extent) + through (to completion)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic construction combining quantifier "all" with "the way" and "through" for maximum emphasis (Modern English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you actually read all the way through? It's like 800 pages!
   - The movie was boring—I couldn't sit all the way through.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants who completed the program all the way through showed significant improvement.
   - The investigation examined evidence all the way through from initial incident to final outcome.

1. **Adverb**: straight through (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Directly without stopping, turning, or deviating; in a direct line from start to finish
   - Without interruption or break; continuously to the end
   
   **Synonyms**: directly, nonstop, continuously, without stopping
   
   **Antonyms**: indirectly, with stops, intermittently
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize directness and lack of interruption. Common in travel contexts and process descriptions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Similar to "right through" but emphasizes directness more than thoroughness. Choose based on nuance needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - drive straight through
   - go straight through
   - work straight through
   
   **Root Analysis**: straight (direct, without deviation) + through (from beginning to end)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound construction emphasizing both direct path and complete passage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We drove straight through the night and arrived at dawn.
   - Let's work straight through lunch and finish this project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegation proceeded straight through immigration without the customary delays.
   - The transaction processed straight through without requiring manual intervention.

1. **Adverb**: halfway through (type: degree/temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At the midpoint; at approximately the middle of a period, process, or distance
   
   **Synonyms**: midway, at the middle, in the middle
   
   **Antonyms**: at the beginning, at the end
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate approximate midpoint position. Common in progress descriptions and spatial contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Suggests approximate rather than exact 50%. Don't use when precision is critical.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stop halfway through
   - give up halfway through
   - realize halfway through
   
   **Root Analysis**: halfway (at the middle point) + through (in the process of passing)
   
   **Etymology**: Combination of "halfway" (Old English "healf" + "weg") with "through" to express midpoint in progressive action
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I got bored halfway through and just stopped watching.
   - Halfway through dinner, the power went out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team is approximately halfway through data collection.
   - Participants who withdrew halfway through were excluded from final analysis.

1. **Adverb**: partway through (type: degree/temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At some point during; after a portion but before completion; at an intermediate stage
   
   **Synonyms**: partially through, midway, during, at some point in
   
   **Antonyms**: at the start, at the conclusion
   
   **When to Use**: Use when indicating an unspecified intermediate point. Less specific than "halfway through."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when specific progress measurement is needed. Use specific percentages or "halfway" for more precision.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stop partway through
   - change partway through
   - realize partway through
   
   **Root Analysis**: partway (partially along the way) + through (in progress)
   
   **Etymology**: American English construction combining "partway" with "through," particularly common in 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We changed plans partway through the trip and decided to stay longer.
   - Partway through the movie, I realized I'd seen it before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology was revised partway through the study based on preliminary findings.
   - Several participants withdrew partway through the longitudinal investigation.

1. **Adverb**: clear through (type: manner - regional/emphatic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely through; all the way through; entirely from one side to the other (emphatic, particularly American regional)
   
   **Synonyms**: completely through, all the way through, right through
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, incompletely
   
   **When to Use**: Use for emphasis in informal contexts. More common in American English, particularly regional dialects.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Quite informal and regional; avoid in formal or academic writing. May sound folksy or dialectal.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go clear through
   - cut clear through
   - see clear through
   
   **Root Analysis**: clear (completely, entirely - intensifier) + through (from one side to other)
   
   **Etymology**: American English intensifying construction using "clear" as degree adverb (19th-20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That branch fell and went clear through the roof!
   - The cold wind cut clear through my jacket.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The structural damage extended clear through the foundation. (informal tone even in formal context)
   - Analysis revealed the fissure penetrated clear through the load-bearing wall.

1. **Adverb**: through and through (type: degree - emphatic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely; in every way; thoroughly; to the core; in every aspect (emphasizes totality of a characteristic)
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, thoroughly, entirely, totally, in every way
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, superficially, incompletely
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize someone or something completely embodies a quality. Often appears predicatively after "be" verb.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound informal or colloquial. May be less appropriate in formal academic contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - honest through and through
   - professional through and through
   - American through and through
   
   **Root Analysis**: through (completely) + and + through (repetition for emphasis)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic construction repeating "through" with coordinating "and" for intensity (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's an artist through and through—always creating something.
   - He's honest through and through; he'd never lie to you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization's approach is collaborative through and through.
   - Analysis reveals the system to be flawed through and through.

1. **Adverb**: well through (type: degree - British informal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Thoroughly; considerably advanced; significantly into a period or process (British informal usage)
   
   **Synonyms**: well into, considerably into, significantly through
   
   **Antonyms**: just beginning, barely started
   
   **When to Use**: British informal usage to indicate significant progress. Less common in American English.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Regional and informal; not appropriate for formal writing. American English speakers may prefer "well into."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - well through the year
   - well through the process
   - well through the term
   
   **Root Analysis**: well (considerably, significantly) + through (into the progression)
   
   **Etymology**: British English construction using "well" as intensifying degree adverb with "through"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're well through winter now; spring is coming soon.
   - By the time we noticed, we were well through our budget.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution is now well through the transition period to the new administrative structure.
   - The research project is well through its second phase of data collection.
