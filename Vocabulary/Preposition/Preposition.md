# Prepositions for Preposition Vocabulary Learning

1. **Preposition**: in (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Within the confines or bounds of; surrounded by; inside
   - *(Temporal)* During (a period of time); within the time of
   - *(Abstract)* Within the state or condition of; involved in; part of
   
   **Synonyms**: within (in contexts), inside (for spatial), during (for temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: out, outside, beyond
   
   **When to Use**: Location within boundaries, time periods (months/years/seasons), states/conditions, inclusion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Surfaces (use "on"), specific times (use "at"), directions (use "into")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in the box, in time, in love
   - **Verb + prep**: believe in, result in, succeed in
   - **Adjective + prep**: interested in, involved in, rich in
   - **Noun + prep**: increase in, interest in, belief in
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English in (in, into) → Modern English in
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll meet you in the morning.
   - She's in a really good mood today.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data are presented in Table 3.
   - Recent developments in technology have transformed communication.

1. **Preposition**: on (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In contact with and supported by the surface of; located at or attached to
   - *(Temporal)* Occurring during or at the time of (specific days/dates)
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; about; in the state of; dependent upon
   
   **Synonyms**: upon (formal), atop (for spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: off, under, beneath
   
   **When to Use**: Surface contact, days/dates, topics, electronic devices, transportation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Enclosed spaces (use "in"), clock times (use "at"), inside containers (use "in")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on the table, on Monday, on purpose
   - **Verb + prep**: depend on, rely on, focus on
   - **Adjective + prep**: based on, dependent on, keen on
   - **Noun + prep**: impact on, effect on, influence on
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English on (on, in, into) → Modern English on
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your keys are on the counter.
   - Let's meet on Friday afternoon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study focuses on prepositional usage patterns.
   - On the basis of these findings, we conclude that...

1. **Preposition**: at (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In or to a particular position or location (point in space)
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating a particular point in time (clock time, specific moments)
   - *(Abstract)* Engaged in; in a state of; directed toward
   
   **Synonyms**: (context-dependent; no exact synonyms)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, from
   
   **When to Use**: Specific points (time/space), addresses, events, activities, rates/prices
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Larger areas (use "in"), surfaces (use "on"), periods of time (use "in")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at home, at 3 o'clock, at the moment
   - **Verb + prep**: look at, laugh at, arrive at
   - **Adjective + prep**: good at, bad at, surprised at
   - **Noun + prep**: glance at, look at, attempt at
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English æt (at, near, by) → Modern English at
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll see you at the coffee shop at noon.
   - She's really good at languages.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conference begins at 9:00 AM.
   - At this juncture, it is necessary to reconsider the hypothesis.

1. **Preposition**: to (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Expressing motion in the direction of; toward
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating the end point of a time period
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating relationship, attachment, or reference
   
   **Synonyms**: toward, into (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Destinations, recipients, ranges, comparisons, infinitives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Static location (use "at" or "in"), when movement into is emphasized (use "into")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: to school, to the left, from Monday to Friday
   - **Verb + prep**: listen to, belong to, lead to
   - **Adjective + prep**: similar to, close to, relevant to
   - **Noun + prep**: access to, approach to, solution to
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English to (to, toward, until) → Modern English to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to the store. Want anything?
   - According to the news, it'll rain tomorrow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings are relevant to current policy debates.
   - Participants were asked to complete the survey.

1. **Preposition**: from (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating the point in space at which a journey, motion, or action starts
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating the starting point of a period
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating source, origin, cause, or material
   
   **Synonyms**: out of (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: to, toward, into
   
   **When to Use**: Origins, sources, starting points, causes, materials, prevention
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing entry (use "into"), when indicating agent (use "by")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from home, from 9 to 5, from scratch
   - **Verb + prep**: come from, differ from, suffer from
   - **Adjective + prep**: different from, absent from, far from
   - **Noun + prep**: distance from, departure from, escape from
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English from, fram (from, by) → Modern English from
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Where are you from?
   - I learned a lot from that experience.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data were collected from three sources.
   - These results differ significantly from previous findings.

1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Accompanied by; in the company of
   - *(Abstract)* Having; possessing; characterized by; by means of; indicating manner or instrument
   
   **Synonyms**: alongside (for accompaniment), using (for instruments)
   
   **Antonyms**: without, against
   
   **When to Use**: Accompaniment, possession, instruments/tools, manner, agreement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When indicating location on someone (use "on"), when indicating agent in passives (use "by")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with friends, with care, with permission
   - **Verb + prep**: agree with, deal with, provide with
   - **Adjective + prep**: pleased with, satisfied with, familiar with
   - **Noun + prep**: relationship with, problem with, experience with
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wiþ (against, opposite, toward) → Modern English with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going with Sarah to the concert.
   - Cut it with scissors, not a knife.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis was conducted with appropriate statistical methods.
   - Participants with prior experience were excluded from the study.

1. **Preposition**: by (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Near; beside; past
   - *(Temporal)* Not later than; before
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating means, method, or agent; through the agency of
   
   **Synonyms**: beside (for location), via (for means), through (for agency)
   
   **Antonyms**: (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Agent in passives, means/method, proximity, deadlines, measurement units
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing instrument used by agent (use "with"), for time periods (use "in")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by the door, by tomorrow, by bus
   - **Verb + prep**: abide by, go by, judge by
   - **Adjective + prep**: impressed by, surprised by, confused by
   - **Noun + prep**: (passive agent marker, not typical noun + prep collocation)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English be, bi (by, near) → Modern English by
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Come sit by me.
   - I need this done by Friday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study was conducted by researchers at the university.
   - Results were analyzed by means of regression analysis.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the duration of; for the period of
   - *(Abstract)* In support of; intended to benefit; as a reason or purpose; in place of; in exchange for
   
   **Synonyms**: during (for time), in favor of (for support)
   
   **Antonyms**: against, anti
   
   **When to Use**: Duration, purpose, beneficiary, reasons, destination after verbs of motion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With present perfect + time period (use "since" for starting point), when emphasizing specific moment (use "at")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for three hours, for this reason, for example
   - **Verb + prep**: wait for, look for, pay for
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready for, responsible for, suitable for
   - **Noun + prep**: need for, reason for, demand for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English for (for, before, in place of) → Modern English for
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've lived here for five years.
   - This gift is for you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were monitored for a period of six months.
   - For this reason, the hypothesis must be reconsidered.

1. **Preposition**: about (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the vicinity of; around; here and there in
   - *(Abstract)* On the subject of; concerning; approximately
   
   **Synonyms**: concerning (for topic), around (for approximate), approximately (for numbers)
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms)
   
   **When to Use**: Topics/subjects, approximate quantities, locations around an area
   
   **When NOT to Use**: After certain adjectives requiring specific prepositions (e.g., "interested in" not "about"), for exact numbers
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: about time, about ten people, about the topic
   - **Verb + prep**: talk about, think about, worry about
   - **Adjective + prep**: concerned about, excited about, curious about
   - **Noun + prep**: information about, question about, concern about
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- [on] + bout [outside] (Middle English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English onbutan (around, on the outside) → Middle English about
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What are you talking about?
   - There were about fifty people there.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study provides information about prepositional usage.
   - Approximately 73% of participants (about three-quarters) reported improvement.

1. **Preposition**: through (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving in one side and out the other; from one end to the other
   - *(Temporal)* From the beginning to the end of (a period)
   - *(Abstract)* By means of; because of; via
   
   **Synonyms**: via (for means), throughout (for duration), across (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: around, over (in some contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: Movement from one side to another, means/medium, throughout duration, completion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For movement over surfaces (use "across"), for scattered occurrence (use "throughout")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: through the door, through the night, through experience
   - **Verb + prep**: go through, look through, get through
   - **Adjective + prep**: (limited collocations)
   - **Noun + prep**: way through, path through, journey through
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English þurh (through) → Middle English thurgh → Modern English through
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We drove through the tunnel.
   - I learned it through trial and error.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Knowledge was disseminated through professional networks.
   - The study examines changes through time.

1. **Preposition**: over (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Above; higher than; across the surface of; covering
   - *(Temporal)* During; throughout
   - *(Abstract)* More than; concerning; by means of; recovered from
   
   **Synonyms**: above (for position), across (for movement), more than (for quantity)
   
   **Antonyms**: under, below, beneath
   
   **When to Use**: Position above, movement across, quantities exceeding, duration, recovery, coverage
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Vertical position without coverage (prefer "above"), direct contact with surface (use "on")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: over the bridge, over time, over and over
   - **Verb + prep**: get over, take over, go over
   - **Adjective + prep**: (limited collocations: concerned over)
   - **Noun + prep**: control over, power over, influence over
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ofer (over, above, across) → Modern English over
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The bird flew over the house.
   - Let's talk about it over coffee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Over 80% of participants completed the survey.
   - Significant changes occurred over the course of the study.

1. **Preposition**: under (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Beneath; below; lower than
   - *(Abstract)* Subject to; governed by; in the process of; less than
   
   **Synonyms**: below (for position), beneath (formal), less than (for quantity)
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, on top of
   
   **When to Use**: Position below, subordination, conditions, processes, quantities less than specified
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For vertical position without coverage relation (prefer "below"), for topics (use "about")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: under the table, under pressure, under consideration
   - **Verb + prep**: come under, fall under, labor under
   - **Adjective + prep**: (limited collocations)
   - **Noun + prep**: (typically follows noun: pressure under)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple form (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English under (under, beneath, among) → Modern English under
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The cat's hiding under the bed.
   - I'm under a lot of stress right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project was conducted under the supervision of Dr. Smith.
   - Under these circumstances, alternative approaches must be considered.

1. **Preposition**: between (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the space separating two things; in the middle of
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating relationship, choice, or distribution involving two entities
   
   **Synonyms**: among (when more than two, though usage varies)
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; contrasts with: among)
   
   **When to Use**: Two distinct entities, relationships, choices, shared actions, intermediate position
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More than two entities (traditionally use "among," though "between" increasingly accepted)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: between us, between 9 and 5, between the lines
   - **Verb + prep**: choose between, distinguish between, differentiate between
   - **Adjective + prep**: (limited: caught between)
   - **Noun + prep**: difference between, relationship between, distinction between
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- [by] + tweonum [two] (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English betweonum (by two, in the middle) → between
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's keep this between you and me.
   - I can't decide between the red one and the blue one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The relationship between language and cognition has been extensively studied.
   - Significant differences were observed between the two groups.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Expressing movement or direction to a point within; entering
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating transformation, change of state, or division
   
   **Synonyms**: to (in some contexts, though "into" emphasizes entry)
   
   **Antonyms**: out of, from
   
   **When to Use**: Movement with entry, transformation, collision, investigation, division
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Static location (use "in"), movement toward without entry (use "to")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into the room, into pieces, into account
   - **Verb + prep**: turn into, break into, look into
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare collocations)
   - **Noun + prep**: insight into, inquiry into, entry into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (compound)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English into (into, in) → Modern English into
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She walked into the room.
   - The cup fell and broke into pieces.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The substance was divided into three equal portions.
   - Further research into this phenomenon is warranted.

1. **Preposition**: out of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* From the inside to the outside; away from within
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating origin, material, motivation, or lack/depletion
   
   **Synonyms**: from (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: into, in
   
   **When to Use**: Exit/emergence, source/origin, material composition, lack/absence, motivation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple source without emphasis on containment (prefer "from")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: out of the box, out of curiosity, out of control
   - **Verb + prep**: get out of, run out of, make out of
   - **Adjective + prep**: (follows adjective: made out of)
   - **Noun + prep**: way out of, lack of (simplified to "of")
   
   **Root Analysis**: out + of (phrasal combination)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ut (out) + of (of, from) → out of
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She came out of nowhere!
   - We're out of milk—can you grab some?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The structure was constructed out of recycled materials.
   - Out of 200 participants, 187 completed the full protocol.
