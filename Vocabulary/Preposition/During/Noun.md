1. **Noun**: duration (U/C, pl: durations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The length of time that something continues or exists; the period during which something lasts
   - (Countable when referring to specific periods; uncountable when referring to the concept)
   
   **Synonyms**: length, period, span, timeframe, extent
   
   **Antonyms**: moment, instant, brevity, shortness
   
   **When to Use**: Essential for discussing how long events, processes, or states last. Common in formal contexts, technical descriptions, and temporal specifications. Appropriate in all registers but particularly common in professional and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal in casual contexts where "how long" or "length" would suffice. Avoid overuse in informal conversation.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - duration of the meeting
   - for the duration
   - duration of stay
   - throughout the duration
   - during the duration of
   
   **Root Analysis**: dur- (last, endure) + -ation (noun suffix indicating action/state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *duratio* from *durare* (to last, endure, harden) → Old French *duration* → English "duration" (14th century). Root word is same as "during."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the duration of the movie? I need to know if I have time.
   - For the duration of the trip, we'll be staying in hotels.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The medication should be taken daily for the duration of the treatment period.
   - Participants were monitored throughout the duration of the clinical trial.

2. **Noun**: period (C, pl: periods)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A length or portion of time; a particular span during which something happens or exists
   - An interval of time characterized by particular events or circumstances
   - (Countable)
   
   **Synonyms**: time, span, interval, phase, era, duration, term
   
   **Antonyms**: moment, instant, point (in time)
   
   **When to Use**: Universal temporal noun used across all contexts and registers. Essential for marking time divisions and discussing when events occur. One of the most common temporal nouns in English.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be vague if specific measurements would be clearer. Sometimes overused where more specific temporal nouns would be better.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - during this period
   - period of time
   - time period
   - trial period
   - transitional period
   
   **Root Analysis**: peri- (around) + -od (way, path)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek *periodos* (cycle, circuit) from *peri-* (around) + *hodos* (way) → Latin *periodus* → English "period" (15th century). Originally meant "cycle" or "complete sentence," extended to mean "span of time."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - During that period, I was living in Boston.
   - There's a waiting period before you can apply again.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined social changes during the post-war period.
   - A probationary period of six months is standard during initial employment.

3. **Noun**: interval (C, pl: intervals)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A period of time between events or activities; a pause or break during a process
   - A space of time during which something different happens or nothing happens
   - (Countable)
   
   **Synonyms**: gap, pause, break, interlude, intermission, span
   
   **Antonyms**: continuation, constancy, uninterrupted time
   
   **When to Use**: Common when discussing time gaps, breaks, or spacing between events. Useful in medical, scientific, and formal contexts. Also used for spatial distances in some contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal or technical in casual contexts. May be too precise when general terms like "break" or "gap" would work.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at regular intervals
   - during the interval
   - interval between doses
   - time interval
   - interval period
   
   **Root Analysis**: inter- (between) + -val (from *vallum*, rampart)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *intervallum* (space between ramparts) from *inter-* (between) + *vallum* (wall, rampart) → English "interval" (14th century). Extended from spatial to temporal meaning.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a short interval during the play when you can grab a drink.
   - During the intervals between sets, the band takes a break.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Measurements were taken at regular intervals during the observation period.
   - A minimum interval of four hours is required during the dosing schedule.

4. **Noun**: timeframe (C, pl: timeframes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A specific period during which something happens, should happen, or is planned to happen
   - A defined span of time with set boundaries
   - (Countable)
   
   **Synonyms**: time period, time span, schedule, timeline, deadline, duration
   
   **Antonyms**: open-ended period, indefinite time, no deadline
   
   **When to Use**: Common in business, project management, and planning contexts. Useful for discussing schedules, deadlines, and temporal boundaries. More specific than "period."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound corporate or bureaucratic in casual contexts. Sometimes "deadline" or "schedule" is more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - within the timeframe
   - during the timeframe
   - specified timeframe
   - realistic timeframe
   - target timeframe
   
   **Root Analysis**: time + frame (compound noun)
   
   **Etymology**: Modern English compound (20th century) combining "time" + "frame" (structure, boundary). Became common in business and project management discourse.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the timeframe for getting this done?
   - During the timeframe we discussed, I'll be out of town.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project must be completed within the specified timeframe.
   - Data collection occurred during a six-month timeframe from January to June 2023.

5. **Noun**: interim (U/C, pl: interims when countable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The intervening time; the period between two events or states
   - A temporary or provisional period during a transition
   - (Usually uncountable when meaning "the meantime"; countable when referring to specific interim periods)
   
   **Synonyms**: meantime, meanwhile, interval, interlude, transition
   
   **Antonyms**: permanence, finality, completion
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts, particularly business and official communication. Useful for discussing transition periods. Often used in phrase "in the interim."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation (use "meantime" or "meanwhile"). Not commonly used as a standalone countable noun in modern English.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the interim
   - during the interim
   - interim period
   - for the interim
   - throughout the interim
   
   **Root Analysis**: inter- (between) + -im (Latin suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *interim* (meanwhile, in the meantime) from *inter* (between) → English (16th century). Originally adverb, later developed noun usage.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In the interim, just use the old system.
   - During the interim, we'll be working from home.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the interim between board meetings, the executive committee will oversee operations.
   - During the interim period, provisional measures will remain in effect.

6. **Noun**: span (C, pl: spans)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The full extent or length of time during which something exists or functions
   - A period or duration, particularly one that can be measured or bounded
   - (Countable)
   
   **Synonyms**: duration, length, period, extent, stretch, interval
   
   **Antonyms**: instant, moment, point
   
   **When to Use**: Common for discussing measured or complete durations. Useful when emphasizing the full extent of time. Works in all registers but particularly common when discussing life spans, attention spans, or project spans.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound slightly formal in very casual contexts. Sometimes "length" or "time" is simpler and equally clear.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - time span
   - life span
   - attention span
   - span of time
   - during the span of
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Germanic root meaning "stretch, extend"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *spann* (distance between thumb and little finger) → extended to temporal meaning "extent of time" (15th century). Originally spatial measurement that extended to temporal use.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - During the span of a few weeks, everything changed.
   - That's a pretty short time span to get so much done.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined mortality rates across the entire life span.
   - Significant progress was achieved during the span of the five-year initiative.
