1. **Adverb**: strongly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With great intensity or force; used to emphasise a firm pro or anti position in debates and evaluations.
   - Commonly appears before stance verbs and adjectives ("strongly support", "strongly pro-democracy").
   
   **Synonyms**: firmly, decisively, forcefully, emphatically, powerfully
   
   **Antonyms**: weakly, mildly, tentatively, half-heartedly, softly
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasising clear, committed support or opposition to an idea, policy, or person ("strongly pro", "strongly against"). Works in both spoken and written registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing if overused; alternatives like "firmly" or "unequivocally" may sound more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strongly pro [policy/cause]
   - strongly support / oppose
   - strongly in favour of
   - strongly against
   - strongly recommend
   
   **Root Analysis**: strong + -ly (in a strong way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English `strang` (strong) + adverb-forming suffix `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm strongly pro remote work—it suits our team.
   - She strongly supports the new policy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Respondents were strongly in favour of pro-environment measures.
   - Several professional bodies strongly oppose the proposed amendment.

1. **Adverb**: firmly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a fixed, confident, and determined way; expressing a stable pro or anti position.
   - Typically placed before verbs or at the end of clauses ("firmly support", "oppose it firmly").
   
   **Synonyms**: resolutely, decisively, unwaveringly, solidly, steadfastly
   
   **Antonyms**: hesitantly, tentatively, weakly, uncertainly
   
   **When to Use**: When highlighting that a stance is not easily changed ("firmly pro-democracy", "firmly against the proposal").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts where openness or flexibility is being emphasised; in that case, use adverbs like "provisionally" or "cautiously" instead.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - firmly pro / firmly against
   - firmly support / reject
   - firmly believe
   - firmly in favour of
   - firmly committed to
   
   **Root Analysis**: firm + -ly (in a firm way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `firmus` (strong, stable) via Old French, plus English adverbial `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm firmly pro keeping the team small and focused.
   - They firmly rejected the idea.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organisation remains firmly pro-science in its public communication.
   - The committee is firmly opposed to any weakening of safeguards.

1. **Adverb**: openly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is not hidden; publicly and without trying to conceal one's pro or anti views.
   - Often modifies verbs of saying, behaving, or identifying ("openly support", "openly opposed", "openly pro-European").
   
   **Synonyms**: publicly, visibly, explicitly, transparently, frankly
   
   **Antonyms**: secretly, privately, covertly, quietly, discreetly
   
   **When to Use**: When noting that someone is clear about their stance ("openly pro-innovation", "openly critical").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid where discretion or confidentiality is central; then use "privately" or "behind closed doors".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - openly pro [cause]
   - openly support / oppose
   - openly critical of
   - openly in favour of
   - openly identify as
   
   **Root Analysis**: open + -ly (in an open way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English `open` + `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's openly pro-environment and talks about climate action a lot.
   - He openly admitted he was against the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several leaders now speak openly in favour of pro-democracy reforms.
   - The organisation is openly pro-labour in its public statements.

1. **Adverb**: publicly (type: manner/place)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In public; in a way visible to others beyond a private group; often about declaring pro or anti positions.
   - Typically placed before or after the main verb ("publicly support", "criticised publicly").
   
   **Synonyms**: openly, in public, in the open, before the public
   
   **Antonyms**: privately, behind closed doors, in secret
   
   **When to Use**: Describe announcements, endorsements, or criticisms made where a wide audience can hear ("publicly support this pro-environment bill").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the conversation happens in a small confidential setting, even if several people are present.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - publicly support / oppose
   - publicly declare
   - publicly distance oneself
   - publicly commit to
   - publicly identify as pro-[X]
   
   **Root Analysis**: public + -ly (in a public way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `publicus` via French into English, plus `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The company publicly came out as pro-remote work.
   - She publicly backed the pro-democracy protesters.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The minister publicly reaffirmed the government's pro-science stance.
   - Shareholders publicly expressed support for the pro-innovation strategy.

1. **Adverb**: explicitly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a clear and direct way, leaving no doubt; often used when positions are clearly stated as pro or anti.
   - Typically modifies verbs of saying or stating ("explicitly support", "explicitly oppose").
   
   **Synonyms**: clearly, directly, unambiguously, plainly, unequivocally
   
   **Antonyms**: implicitly, indirectly, vaguely, ambiguously
   
   **When to Use**: Highlight that a document, policy, or speaker clearly names a stance ("explicitly pro-human-rights", "explicitly against") rather than hinting.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the meaning is intentionally vague; then "implicitly" or "indirectly" may be more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - explicitly state
   - explicitly support / oppose
   - explicitly pro-[X]
   - explicitly in favour of
   - explicitly rule out
   
   **Root Analysis**: explicit + -ly (in an explicit way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `explicitus` (unfolded, clear) + `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The policy never explicitly says it's pro-worker, but it's clear from the details.
   - He explicitly said he's pro this merger.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The treaty explicitly recognises the right to form pro-labour organisations.
   - The charter explicitly commits the company to a pro-environment path.

1. **Adverb**: wholeheartedly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With complete sincerity and enthusiasm; used to show very strong pro support.
   - Often placed before verbs of support ("wholeheartedly support", "wholeheartedly endorse").
   
   **Synonyms**: fully, completely, enthusiastically, unreservedly, strongly
   
   **Antonyms**: reluctantly, partially, half-heartedly, conditionally
   
   **When to Use**: When describing warm, enthusiastic support for a person, proposal, or cause ("wholeheartedly pro-democracy").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in highly neutral or technical writing; it adds emotional colouring that may feel out of place.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wholeheartedly support
   - wholeheartedly endorse
   - wholeheartedly agree
   - wholeheartedly back
   - be wholeheartedly pro-[X]
   
   **Root Analysis**: whole + heart + -ed + -ly (with the whole heart)
   
   **Etymology**: From Middle English `hol` (whole) + `heart` + `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I wholeheartedly support her pro-science campaign.
   - The team is wholeheartedly pro trying this experiment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board wholeheartedly endorsed the pro-innovation strategy.
   - Participants expressed wholeheartedly positive views on the pro-social programme.

1. **Adverb**: actively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that involves practical action rather than passive belief; used with pro and anti campaigning.
   - Common before verbs ("actively support", "actively campaign for", "actively lobby against").
   
   **Synonyms**: energetically, vigorously, proactively, hands-on, practically
   
   **Antonyms**: passively, reluctantly, minimally, superficially
   
   **When to Use**: To differentiate between simply holding a pro view and taking action for it ("actively pro-environment", "actively support pro-labour reforms").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the involvement is purely symbolic; then use "nominally" or "formally" instead.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - actively support / oppose
   - actively campaign for
   - actively lobby for
   - actively promote
   - actively pro-[X]
   
   **Root Analysis**: active + -ly (in an active way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `activus` (active) via French.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He doesn't just say he's pro-democracy—he actively volunteers.
   - They're actively campaigning for pro-environment laws.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Civil society groups actively promoted pro-democracy norms.
   - The organisation is actively pro-labour in its bargaining strategy.

1. **Adverb**: unapologetically (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Without apology or regret; used when someone is confidently and visibly pro something even if it is controversial.
   - Often placed before adjectives or verbs ("unapologetically pro-market", "unapologetically support").
   
   **Synonyms**: boldly, proudly, unashamedly, openly, defiantly (context-dependent)
   
   **Antonyms**: apologetically, cautiously, defensively, hesitantly
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to emphasise that a stance is held confidently despite criticism ("unapologetically pro-technology").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in strictly neutral reporting; it adds evaluative flavour and can sound judgmental.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unapologetically pro-[X]
   - unapologetically support
   - unapologetically defend
   - unapologetically advocate
   - unapologetically pro-business / pro-labour
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + apologetic + -ally (adverbial form)
   
   **Etymology**: From `apology` (Greek `apologia` = defence) with negative prefix and adverbial ending.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's unapologetically pro-environment, even when it's unpopular.
   - The brand is unapologetically pro-technology.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The party's platform is unapologetically pro-European in tone.
   - Management adopted an unapologetically pro-customer approach to service recovery.

1. **Adverb**: ideologically (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way driven by a system of ideas or beliefs; used when support is based on ideology rather than short-term interest.
   - Often modifies adjectives like "pro-market" or "pro-labour" ("ideologically pro-market").
   
   **Synonyms**: doctrinally, philosophically, on principle, value-driven
   
   **Antonyms**: pragmatically, practically, opportunistically, tactically
   
   **When to Use**: When highlighting that a pro or anti stance comes from underlying ideology ("ideologically pro-democracy even when costly").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for purely pragmatic decisions not rooted in broader beliefs.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ideologically pro-[X]
   - ideologically committed to
   - ideologically aligned with
   - ideologically opposed to
   - ideologically driven
   
   **Root Analysis**: ideology + -ical + -ly (in an ideological way)
   
   **Etymology**: From French `idéologie` + English suffixes; common in political science.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's ideologically pro-market, even when it hurts him personally.
   - They're ideologically against any pro-labour reform.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Some parties remain ideologically pro-integration despite short-term electoral costs.
   - The organisation is ideologically pro-science and resists politicisation of evidence.

1. **Adverb**: principally (type: degree/sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Mainly; chiefly; used to signal that support is mostly for a particular pro aspect.
   - Often appears in stance sentences ("principally pro-environment", "principally concerned with").
   
   **Synonyms**: mainly, chiefly, primarily, largely, predominantly
   
   **Antonyms**: marginally, secondarily, incidentally, partly
   
   **When to Use**: In more formal or written registers when explaining what a pro position focuses on ("principally pro-consumer", "principally pro-democracy in foreign policy").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech where "mainly" or "mostly" sounds more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - principally pro-[X]
   - principally concerned with
   - principally focused on
   - principally in favour of
   - principally opposed to
   
   **Root Analysis**: principal + -ly (in a principal way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `principalis` (chief, main) + `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The group is mainly pro-environment and pro-social issues.
   - He's principally pro-innovation rather than pro-control.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The coalition is principally pro-labour but increasingly open to pro-business reforms.
   - The policy is principally pro-consumer, with secondary benefits for small firms.

1. **Adverb**: cautiously (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a careful way that avoids risk; often used when someone is mildly pro or anti but still hesitant.
   - Typically modifies verbs about support or optimism ("cautiously pro", "cautiously optimistic").
   
   **Synonyms**: carefully, guardedly, tentatively, warily, prudently
   
   **Antonyms**: boldly, aggressively, wholeheartedly, recklessly
   
   **When to Use**: When a stance is positive but not fully committed ("cautiously pro-market reforms", "cautiously in favour").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the person is clearly either fully committed or clearly opposed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cautiously pro-[X]
   - cautiously optimistic
   - cautiously in favour of
   - cautiously supportive
   - move cautiously towards
   
   **Root Analysis**: cautious + -ly (in a cautious way)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `cavere` (to take care) via `cautio` (caution) + `-ly`.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm cautiously pro this change—we need more data.
   - She's cautiously supportive of the pro-environment plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Investors remain cautiously pro-technology, citing regulatory uncertainty.
   - Stakeholders are cautiously in favour of the pro-market reform package.
