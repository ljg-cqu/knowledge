1. **Verb**: support (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To agree with and help a person, idea, or policy; to be pro something in a practical or emotional way.
   - *(Transitive)* "support [proposal/person]"; *(intransitive)* "I support" used with context.
   
   **Synonyms**: back, endorse, favour, stand behind, uphold
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, resist, reject, undermine, obstruct
   
   **When to Use**: Very general verb for expressing a pro stance ("support pro-democracy reforms", "support this pro-environment campaign"). Suitable for most registers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid where you mean "financially support" specifically—in that case clarify ("fund", "sponsor").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - support a proposal
   - support democracy
   - strongly support / weakly support
   - support pro-[X] policies
   - support someone's stance
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (from below) + port (carry)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French `suporter` meaning "to carry from below"; extended to emotional and political backing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I support the pro-environment plan.
   - Most of us support keeping a strongly pro-customer policy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A clear majority of respondents support the pro-science recommendation.
   - Several professional bodies publicly support the pro-labour amendments.

1. **Verb**: back (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give support to someone or something, often in a decisive or strategic way.
   - *(Transitive)* Common in headlines and political talk.
   
   **Synonyms**: support, endorse, champion, side with, get behind
   
   **Antonyms**: block, oppose, stand against, withdraw support
   
   **When to Use**: When highlighting that a person or organisation takes the pro side with some influence ("back a pro-market candidate"). Informal to semi-formal.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal legal or academic texts where "support" or "endorse" is more neutral.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - back a proposal
   - back a pro-democracy campaign
   - back a candidate
   - back pro-[X] reforms
   - back down (note: different meaning)
   
   **Root Analysis**: from noun `back` (rear part) used metaphorically as "stand behind".
   
   **Etymology**: Old English `bæc`; verb sense "to support" is metaphorical extension.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you going to back the pro-environment option?
   - The union backed the pro-labour package.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several major donors backed the pro-democracy coalition.
   - The committee unanimously backed the pro-customer recommendation.

1. **Verb**: endorse (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To officially state that you support someone or something; to put your name behind a pro side.
   - *(Transitive)* Often used with candidates, policies, or documents.
   
   **Synonyms**: approve, back, support, recommend, sign off on
   
   **Antonyms**: condemn, disapprove, repudiate, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Formal situations where an organisation, expert, or influencer expresses pro support ("endorse a pro-science statement").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual speech where "support" or "be for" is simpler.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - endorse a candidate
   - endorse a policy
   - formally endorse
   - endorse the pro-environment plan
   - endorse a pro-democracy statement
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (on) + dors (back) → literally "put on the back" as a signature
   
   **Etymology**: From Medieval Latin `indorsare` (to write on the back); later "approve" or "back".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Big names have endorsed this pro-innovation project.
   - The group endorsed the pro-labour deal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The association unanimously endorsed the pro-science guidelines.
   - Several NGOs endorsed the joint pro-democracy declaration.

1. **Verb**: advocate (for) (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To publicly recommend or argue strongly in favour of a particular cause, policy, or group.
   - *(Intransitive)* "advocate for [X]"; *(transitive)* more formal "advocate [policy]".
   
   **Synonyms**: campaign for, argue for, press for, champion, lobby for
   
   **Antonyms**: argue against, oppose, criticise, attack
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasise active, often professional, pro work ("advocate for pro-consumer regulation"). Common in NGOs, law, and policy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for private, low-key support; use "support" in that case.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advocate for change
   - advocate for pro-environment policies
   - advocate a pro-labour approach
   - advocate strongly for
   - advocate on behalf of
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + voc (call) + -ate (verb)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `advocare` (to call to one's aid); legal sense extended to general campaigning.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She advocates for pro-social education in schools.
   - They actively advocate for pro-democracy reforms.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organisation advocates a distinctly pro-consumer regulatory framework.
   - Several scholars advocate for a more pro-labour interpretation of the law.

1. **Verb**: champion (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To fight for, speak up for, or lead support for a cause; to be a visible pro leader.
   - *(Transitive)* Often used with abstract nouns.
   
   **Synonyms**: spearhead, lead, promote, defend, stand up for
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, ignore, undermine, work against
   
   **When to Use**: When someone is energetically pro and pushing an idea forward ("champion pro-environment reforms").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for minimal or passive support; use "support" instead.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - champion a cause
   - champion pro-democracy values
   - champion pro-science education
   - champion workers' rights
   - self-styled champion of
   
   **Root Analysis**: from noun `champion` (winner, defender) used as verb.
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French `champion` (warrior); verb sense "to fight for" emerged later.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She champions a very pro-customer culture.
   - He likes to champion pro-technology ideas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organisation champions pro-democracy norms in international forums.
   - Several leaders championed a strongly pro-environment agenda.

1. **Verb**: promote (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To encourage or support the growth or acceptance of something; to push a pro message.
   - *(Transitive)* Common in marketing and policy contexts.
   
   **Synonyms**: encourage, foster, further, advance, advocate
   
   **Antonyms**: hinder, discourage, suppress, undermine
   
   **When to Use**: For active steps that move a pro idea into wider adoption ("promote pro-social behaviour", "promote a pro-science culture").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid where the action is neutral or purely descriptive.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - promote democracy
   - promote pro-environment behaviour
   - promote pro-social norms
   - promote a pro-customer mindset
   - promote pro-innovation culture
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + mot (move) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `promovere` (to move forward).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The campaign promotes pro-environment habits.
   - Our leaders really promote a pro-learning culture.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The programme aims to promote pro-social attitudes among students.
   - Policies were introduced to promote a pro-innovation ecosystem.

1. **Verb**: lobby (for) (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To try to influence officials to support your pro position on a law or policy.
   - *(Intransitive)* "lobby for/against"; *(transitive)* "lobby someone to do something".
   
   **Synonyms**: press, campaign, advocate, petition, push
   
   **Antonyms**: accept, acquiesce, comply without influence
   
   **When to Use**: Describe organised attempts to move decision-makers to the pro side ("lobby for pro-labour reforms", "lobby against pro-business deregulation").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very informal contexts where "ask" or "push" would be more appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lobby for / against
   - lobby hard for
   - lobby lawmakers
   - lobby in favour of
   - corporate lobby
   
   **Root Analysis**: from noun `lobby` (hall where meetings with politicians take place).
   
   **Etymology**: Political sense developed in 19th-century English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Activists are lobbying hard for pro-environment changes.
   - They lobbied the city council to adopt a pro-bike policy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Industry groups lobbied intensively for pro-business tax measures.
   - Trade unions lobbied in favour of pro-labour amendments.

1. **Verb**: campaign (for/against) (intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To organise a series of planned activities to achieve a political or social goal; to work actively on the pro or anti side.
   - *(Intransitive)* followed by "for" or "against".
   
   **Synonyms**: crusade, organise, mobilise, canvass, agitate
   
   **Antonyms**: remain inactive, stay neutral, disengage
   
   **When to Use**: For extended pro efforts around elections, referendums, or social causes ("campaign for pro-democracy reforms").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when actions are one-off or informal; use "argue" or "push" instead.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - campaign for / against
   - campaign on a platform of
   - campaign in favour of
   - campaign as a pro-[X] party
   - grassroots campaign
   
   **Root Analysis**: camp + -aign (related to military campaign); metaphorical for political struggle.
   
   **Etymology**: From French `campagne` (military campaign).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're campaigning for a strongly pro-environment law.
   - She campaigned hard on a pro-education platform.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Civil-society groups campaigned for a pro-democracy constitution.
   - The party campaigned on an explicitly pro-labour agenda.

1. **Verb**: side with (phrasal, intransitive + prep)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To choose to support one person or group in an argument; to place yourself in the pro camp for that side.
   - *(Phrasal verb)* "side with [someone/something]".
   
   **Synonyms**: support, back, align with, take the side of, stand with
   
   **Antonyms**: side against, oppose, stand against, break with
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasising choosing between camps ("side with the pro-reform group").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid where neutrality is emphasised; then use "stay neutral" or "remain impartial".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - side with the pro-[X] camp
   - side with reformers
   - side with management / workers
   - refuse to side with either
   - tend to side with
   
   **Root Analysis**: `side` (one surface, one party) + `with` (preposition showing alignment)
   
   **Etymology**: English phrasal verb formed by metaphorical extension of "side".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I usually side with the more pro-environment option.
   - The public largely sided with the pro-democracy protesters.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smaller parties chose to side with the pro-integration bloc.
   - Media coverage appeared to side with the pro-labour coalition.

1. **Verb**: oppose (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be against something and try to stop it; to take the con side when others are pro.
   - *(Transitive)* "oppose the bill", "oppose pro-market reforms".
   
   **Synonyms**: resist, contest, fight, stand against, object to
   
   **Antonyms**: support, back, endorse, favour
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral verb for describing those on the anti side when others are pro ("oppose the pro-business agenda").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts where disagreement is minor; then use "question" or "raise concerns".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - oppose the proposal
   - strongly oppose
   - oppose pro-[X] reforms
   - openly oppose
   - oppose on principle
   
   **Root Analysis**: ob- (against) + pos (place)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `opponere` (to set against).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Some people strongly oppose the pro-market reform.
   - I don't oppose it, but I'm not fully pro either.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several unions openly oppose the pro-business provisions.
   - A minority of legislators continue to oppose the pro-democracy package.
