1. **Noun**: proponent (C, pl: proponents)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who actively supports or argues in favour of an idea, policy, system, or change.
   - *(Countable)* Often contrasted with "opponent" in debates (proponents vs opponents).
   
   **Synonyms**: supporter, advocate, champion, backer, defender
   
   **Antonyms**: opponent, critic, detractor, adversary, challenger
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal contexts to label people who are clearly pro a proposal or viewpoint ("proponents of the reform").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech where "supporter" or "fan" might sound more natural; avoid when the person is neutral or undecided.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proponent of democracy
   - proponent of reform
   - leading proponent
   - strong proponent of
   - proponents and opponents
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward, for) + pon (put, place) + -ent (one who)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `proponere` (to put forward) via `proponent` in legal and rhetorical Latin; adopted into English to mean "one who argues for".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's a big proponent of pro-environment policies.
   - Many proponents of remote work say it boosts productivity.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Proponents of the pro-democracy movement called for new elections.
   - The leading proponents of the policy argue that it is both pro-growth and pro-labour.

1. **Noun**: advocate (C, pl: advocates)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who publicly supports or speaks in favour of a cause, policy, or group.
   - *(Countable)* Frequently used in social, legal, and political contexts.
   
   **Synonyms**: campaigner, supporter, proponent, champion, defender
   
   **Antonyms**: critic, opponent, adversary, detractor
   
   **When to Use**: Describe someone who is actively and visibly pro a cause ("human-rights advocate", "pro-science advocate").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when someone simply holds an opinion quietly; reserve it for people who advocate publicly.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advocate for
   - advocates and critics
   - consumer advocate
   - pro-democracy advocate
   - advocate of reform
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + voc (call) + -ate (person)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `advocatus` (one called to aid, legal pleader); broadened to any active supporter.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's a strong advocate for pro-social education.
   - Many tech advocates are very pro-open-source.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Pro-labour advocates raised concerns about the impact on workers' rights.
   - Environmental advocates remain firmly pro stricter emissions standards.

1. **Noun**: supporter (C, pl: supporters)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who approves of and often actively helps a person, cause, or team.
   - *(Countable)* Neutral, widely used label for people who are pro something.
   
   **Synonyms**: backer, fan, follower, sympathiser, ally
   
   **Antonyms**: opponent, rival, critic, enemy
   
   **When to Use**: Broadly, whenever referring to people on the pro side ("supporters of the bill", "supporters and opponents").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal legal or academic contexts where more specific roles ("proponent", "advocate") are needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supporters of the reform
   - pro-democracy supporters
   - strong supporter of
   - long-time supporter
   - core supporters
   
   **Root Analysis**: support (to hold up) + -er (person who)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French `suporter`; in English, used for both emotional and material backing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's a big supporter of pro-environment causes.
   - The team's supporters are very pro attacking football.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Supporters of the pro-market package argue that it will boost competitiveness.
   - Pro-democracy supporters organised peaceful demonstrations in the capital.

1. **Noun**: lobby (C, pl: lobbies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An organised group that tries to influence decision-makers in favour of particular interests; a pro side with structured influence.
   - *(Countable)* Often used about business, industry, or issue-based groups.
   
   **Synonyms**: interest group, pressure group, advocacy group, bloc
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct single antonym; contrasted with opposing lobby or public interest groups)
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing organised pro efforts to shape law or policy ("the pro-technology lobby", "the pro-environment lobby").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid to describe informal or one-off influence; reserve it for sustained, organised efforts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pro-business lobby
   - powerful lobby
   - industry lobby
   - lobby for / against
   - lobbying by the [sector] lobby
   
   **Root Analysis**: Originally from `lobby` (hallway where people speak to politicians), extended to group meaning.
   
   **Etymology**: 17th-century English; by the 19th century came to mean groups seeking to influence legislation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The tech lobby is very pro-innovation and wants lighter rules.
   - There's a strong pro-environment lobby in this city.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The pro-labour lobby campaigned for stronger worker protections.
   - A coalition of pro-consumer lobbies opposed the proposed merger.

1. **Noun**: stance (C, pl: stances)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A publicly stated opinion or position on an issue; can be pro, neutral, or anti.
   - *(Countable)* Often modified by adjectives like "pro-environment", "firm", "neutral".
   
   **Synonyms**: position, standpoint, view, attitude, posture
   
   **Antonyms**: indecision, no position, neutrality (in some contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing how clear or committed someone's pro or anti position is ("a strongly pro-democracy stance").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech where "view" or "take" might be simpler.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pro-[issue] stance
   - adopt / take a stance
   - firm stance
   - shift in stance
   - official stance
   
   **Root Analysis**: stand + -ce (noun-forming suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Italian `stanza` (standing place) via French and English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's your stance—are you pro or against this change?
   - The team has a pretty pro-remote-work stance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organisation adopted a clearly pro-science stance on vaccination.
   - Public statements show a gradual shift from neutral to pro-environment stances.

1. **Noun**: camp (C, pl: camps)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A group of people who share the same opinion, especially in a political or organisational split (pro camp vs anti camp).
   - *(Countable)* Often used metaphorically rather than literally.
   
   **Synonyms**: side, faction, bloc, wing, group
   
   **Antonyms**: (contextual: opposing camp, rival faction, other side)
   
   **When to Use**: When issues split people into opposing pro and anti sides ("the pro-reform camp", "the anti-reform camp").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when positions are diverse and not easily grouped into two opposing camps.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the pro camp
   - the anti camp
   - in the pro-[X] camp
   - both camps
   - camps for and against
   
   **Root Analysis**: From physical camp (group living in tents) extended metaphorically to groups with shared views.
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `campus` (field); political sense developed in 19th-century English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm in the pro-change camp on this one.
   - The team is split into pro and anti camps.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Negotiations stalled as the pro-reform and anti-reform camps hardened their positions.
   - Stakeholders generally fell into three camps: strongly pro, cautiously pro, and opposed.

1. **Noun**: coalition (C, pl: coalitions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A temporary alliance of groups or individuals who join together to pursue a shared goal, often on the pro side of a specific issue.
   - *(Countable)* Common in politics and advocacy.
   
   **Synonyms**: alliance, bloc, partnership, front, grouping
   
   **Antonyms**: fragmentation, division, split, opposition (as rival coalition)
   
   **When to Use**: For collaborative pro efforts across different organisations or parties ("pro-democracy coalition", "pro-environment coalition").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing a single organisation acting alone.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pro-democracy coalition
   - pro-environment coalition
   - broad coalition of supporters
   - cross-party coalition
   - coalition for reform
   
   **Root Analysis**: co- (together) + al (nourish, grow) → coalition (growing together)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin `coalescere` (to grow together) via French.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Several groups formed a pro-environment coalition.
   - There's a broad pro-labour coalition behind this bill.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A cross-party pro-democracy coalition emerged in response to the proposed changes.
   - The pro-consumer coalition coordinated messaging across multiple sectors.

1. **Noun**: agenda (C, pl: agendas)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A list of things to be done; more broadly, an underlying set of priorities or goals, often with a clear pro-business, pro-labour, or pro-environment angle.
   - *(Countable)* Frequently modified by `pro-` labels.
   
   **Synonyms**: programme, platform, plan, priorities, course of action
   
   **Antonyms**: aimlessness, lack of direction; counter-agenda (rival priorities)
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the overall direction or priorities behind pro positions ("pro-innovation agenda", "pro-consumer agenda").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid implying hidden motives where there are none; "agenda" can sound negative in some contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pro-business agenda
   - pro-environment agenda
   - pro-labour agenda
   - hidden agenda
   - policy agenda
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin `agenda` (things to be done), plural of `agendum` (thing to be done)
   
   **Etymology**: Borrowed from Medieval Latin into English; widened from meeting lists to broader political or strategic priorities.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new mayor has a clearly pro-environment agenda.
   - Some people think the media has a pro-business agenda.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The government set out a pro-growth agenda focused on investment and innovation.
   - Civil society actors pushed for a more explicitly pro-democracy agenda.

1. **Noun**: platform (C, pl: platforms)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In politics, the set of main policies and values a party or candidate stands for; can be strongly pro or anti specific issues.
   - *(Countable)* Also used for issue-based platforms ("pro-democracy platform").
   
   **Synonyms**: manifesto, programme, policy package, stance, agenda
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonym; contrasted with rival platform)
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about structured sets of pro positions ("pro-labour platform", "pro-market platform").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in non-political contexts where "product" or "service" platform has a technical meaning unless clearly specified.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pro-labour platform
   - pro-environment platform
   - pro-European platform
   - run on a platform of
   - campaign platform
   
   **Root Analysis**: plat (flat) + form (shape); metaphorical extension.
   
   **Etymology**: Originally a physical stage for speaking; later "platform" came to mean the set of views you speak from.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She ran on a strongly pro-education platform.
   - The party's platform is very pro-science.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The coalition negotiated a joint pro-democracy platform.
   - Voters tended to favour platforms that combined pro-market reforms with pro-social protections.

1. **Noun**: bloc (C, pl: blocs)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A group of countries, parties, or voters that act together, often on the same pro side of an issue.
   - *(Countable)* Common in international relations and parliamentary politics.
   
   **Synonyms**: alliance, coalition, group, camp, voting bloc
   
   **Antonyms**: fragmentation, splinter group, divided front
   
   **When to Use**: Describe organised pro alignments ("pro-European bloc", "pro-reform voting bloc").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when actors vote independently or where there is no consistent pattern.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pro-European bloc
   - pro-reform bloc
   - voting bloc
   - core bloc of supporters
   - bloc that is strongly pro-[X]
   
   **Root Analysis**: From French `bloc` (block, group)
   
   **Etymology**: Borrowed into English in 19th–20th century to describe political and economic groupings (e.g. Eastern bloc).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a big pro-market bloc in parliament.
   - Young voters form a strong pro-environment bloc.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A pro-integration bloc emerged within the legislature.
   - The pro-labour voting bloc proved decisive in passing the bill.
