1. **Functional Expression**: Does this apply across [all/different] [categories]? (function: checking scope/clarifying)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Requesting clarification about whether something is universally applicable or limited to specific cases
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Is this true for all [categories]?, Does this hold in all cases?, Is this universal?, Does this extend to all [groups]?
   
   **Antonyms**: Is this specific to [one category]?, Is this limited to certain cases?
   
   **When to Use**: Use in meetings, discussions, or analytical contexts when seeking clarification about scope or breadth of applicability. Essential for ensuring accurate understanding of generalizations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when scope is already clear or specified. Avoid in contexts where questioning seems confrontational without appropriate framing.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Does this hold across all cases?
   - Is this consistent across different groups?
   - Would this apply across the board?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness strategy—shows engagement and careful thinking. Can be softened with "I'm wondering if..." or "Could you clarify whether..."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, it applies universally / across all [categories]
   - No, it's specific to [particular category/case]
   - It applies in most cases, with some exceptions in [X]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Does this discount apply across all products or just certain ones?
   - I'm wondering if this rule applies across all locations.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Does this methodology apply across all demographic subgroups?
   - Could you clarify whether these findings hold across different experimental conditions?

1. **Functional Expression**: Let me get [the message/my point] across (function: expressing communication intent)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Stating intention to communicate something clearly; emphasizing need for successful message transmission
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Let me make this clear, Let me explain, I want to make sure you understand, Let me communicate this effectively
   
   **When to Use**: Use when introducing an important point that requires clear understanding, especially after confusion or when anticipating difficulty in communication. Shows speaker awareness of communication responsibility.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when it might seem condescending or imply listener isn't listening. Don't overuse or it suggests poor initial communication.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let me try to get this across
   - I need to get this point across
   - Let me make sure I get this across clearly
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-saving strategy—acknowledges potential communication difficulty while taking responsibility. Can be softened with "try to" or "make sure."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, please go ahead / I'm listening
   - Sure, I want to understand this clearly
   - Okay, what do you mean?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me get this across: we can't afford to miss the deadline.
   - I'm trying to get the point across that we need more resources.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let me ensure I get this message across clearly: compliance is mandatory.
   - Allow me to get this key concept across before we proceed.

1. **Functional Expression**: We need to reach across [boundaries/divisions] (function: proposing collaboration/unity)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggesting or advocating for overcoming divisions and working together; proposing bridge-building
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (suggesting rather than commanding)
   
   **Synonyms**: We need to bridge divisions, We should work across boundaries, We must overcome divisions, We need to connect different groups
   
   **Antonyms**: We should maintain boundaries, We need to stay within our domain
   
   **When to Use**: Use in organizational, political, or social contexts when advocating for collaboration, unity, or overcoming divisions. Shows inclusive, collaborative mindset.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when maintaining boundaries is professionally appropriate. Don't use without acknowledging legitimate reasons for existing divisions.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We should reach across party lines
   - Let's reach across departmental boundaries
   - It's time to reach across cultural divides
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness and inclusive strategy—appeals to common goals. Can be strengthened with urgency markers ("must," "urgently need to") or softened with modals ("might want to," "could").
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I agree, collaboration is essential
   - Yes, let's explore how we can do that
   - That's challenging, but worth pursuing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We really need to reach across these team divisions and work together.
   - Maybe it's time to reach across to the other department.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative requires reaching across traditional institutional boundaries.
   - We must reach across disciplinary divisions to address this complex challenge.

1. **Functional Expression**: I came across [something] (function: introducing discovered information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Reporting unexpected discovery or encounter; introducing information found by chance
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I found [something], I discovered [something], I stumbled upon [something], I ran into [something]
   
   **When to Use**: Use when introducing information, ideas, or items encountered unexpectedly. Emphasizes serendipitous nature of discovery rather than deliberate search.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when discovery was result of systematic search (use "found" instead). Avoid in very formal reports where chance discovery might undermine credibility.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I just came across some information about X
   - While researching, I came across...
   - You might be interested—I came across...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Indirect introduction strategy—presents information as incidental finding rather than deliberate argument. Can soften potentially challenging information or provide face-saving when correcting.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Oh, interesting! What did you find?
   - Really? Tell me more
   - That's helpful, thank you
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I came across this article that you might find interesting.
   - Hey, I came across an old photo of us from college!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - While reviewing the literature, I came across a relevant study from 2019.
   - During the audit, we came across several discrepancies requiring attention.

1. **Functional Expression**: This trend is evident across [multiple contexts] (function: supporting claim with broad evidence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Strengthening an argument by indicating widespread occurrence or applicability; demonstrating pattern consistency
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: This is observed in all [contexts], This occurs throughout [contexts], This pattern appears in multiple [settings], This is consistent in various [situations]
   
   **Antonyms**: This is limited to [one context], This occurs only in specific cases
   
   **When to Use**: Use in analytical, academic, or professional contexts when supporting arguments with evidence of broad patterns. Strengthens claims by showing consistency across cases.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when evidence is actually limited or inconsistent. Don't use to overstate findings or make unsupported generalizations.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - This pattern is evident across multiple studies
   - The effect is observable across different populations
   - This phenomenon appears across various contexts
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Evidential strategy—builds credibility through breadth of evidence. Strengthened by specific examples or weakened by hedging ("appears to be," "seems to be").
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's compelling evidence
   - Can you provide specific examples?
   - What contexts specifically?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This problem is showing up across all our teams.
   - The same issue is evident across different departments.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This trend is evident across multiple longitudinal studies.
   - The correlation is consistently observed across diverse demographic cohorts.

1. **Functional Expression**: How does this translate across [different contexts]? (function: questioning applicability/seeking clarification)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Inquiring about how something applies or adapts to different situations; checking transferability
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: How does this work in different [contexts]?, How does this apply to various [situations]?, Is this applicable across [different cases]?
   
   **When to Use**: Use in strategic, planning, or analytical discussions when assessing whether approaches, findings, or solutions apply broadly. Shows critical thinking about context-specificity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when context-independence is already established or obvious. Don't use to challenge without genuine interest in answer.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How would this translate across different markets?
   - Does this translate well across cultures?
   - How does this approach translate across age groups?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Critical inquiry strategy—demonstrates analytical thinking while maintaining neutral stance. Can be softened with "I'm curious about..." or strengthened with "We need to understand..."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - It translates well / It adapts effectively
   - It requires some modification for [context]
   - That's a good question; we need to evaluate that
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How does this strategy translate across different customer segments?
   - I'm wondering how this translates across our various locations.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - How do these findings translate across different institutional contexts?
   - We must assess how this methodology translates across diverse populations.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's coordinate across [teams/departments] (function: proposing collaborative action)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggesting joint planning or synchronized action among different groups; proposing integration
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (suggesting/proposing rather than commanding)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's work together across [groups], We should synchronize between [groups], Let's collaborate across [divisions], We need integration between [groups]
   
   **When to Use**: Use in organizational contexts when proposing cross-functional collaboration or alignment. Shows awareness of need for integration and willingness to facilitate it.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when proposing to coordinate others' work without authority. Don't use when independent action is more appropriate than coordination.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We should coordinate across all teams
   - Let's make sure we're coordinating across departments
   - It's important to coordinate across regions
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Collaborative proposal strategy—inclusive "let's" reduces power distance and invites participation. Can be strengthened with urgency ("need to") or necessity ("must").
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Good idea, how should we proceed?
   - Yes, coordination is essential here
   - Who should take the lead on this?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's coordinate across teams to avoid duplicating work.
   - We should probably coordinate across departments on this project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let's establish mechanisms to coordinate across organizational divisions.
   - We propose coordinating across institutional boundaries to achieve shared objectives.
