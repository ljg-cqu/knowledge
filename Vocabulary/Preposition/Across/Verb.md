1. **Verb**: walk (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move by foot at a regular pace, advancing by steps from one side to the other
   - *(Transitive with across)* To traverse on foot; to cross by walking
   
   **Synonyms**: cross (on foot), traverse, go across, move across, step across
   
   **Antonyms**: stay put, remain, stand still
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing pedestrian movement from one side to another, whether literal (street, bridge) or metaphorical (crossing boundaries, stages). Common in everyday conversation and descriptive writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when other modes of transportation are involved (drive across, fly across). Avoid when the action is not primarily about crossing (use "walk along" for parallel movement).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - walk across the street/bridge/room
   - walk across the stage
   - walk across the country/continent
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English wealcan (to roll, move about)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wealcan → Middle English walken, originally meaning "to roll" or "to move about," later specialized to mean moving on foot.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I walk across the campus every morning to get to class.
   - Can you walk across the room and grab that book for me?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Pedestrians must walk across the designated crosswalk for safety.
   - Graduates walk across the stage to receive their diplomas during commencement.

1. **Verb**: spread (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To extend over a wide area or in many directions; to distribute or disperse throughout
   - *(Transitive/Intransitive)* To cause something to cover a larger area or affect more locations
   
   **Synonyms**: disperse, distribute, extend, disseminate, proliferate, diffuse
   
   **Antonyms**: concentrate, gather, consolidate, contain, limit
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing the expansion, distribution, or transmission of something across space, time, or populations. Essential for discussing diseases, information, influence, resources, or physical coverage.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when describing focused or concentrated phenomena. Avoid when precision location is more important than distribution pattern.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - spread across the country/region/world
   - spread across multiple platforms/channels
   - news spreads across social media
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English sprǣdan (to spread out, stretch out)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English sprǣdan, related to Dutch spreiden and German spreiten, Proto-Germanic *spraidjan. Basic meaning of extending or stretching out.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The rumor spread across the school in just a few hours.
   - His smile spread across his face when he heard the good news.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The infection spread across multiple regions before containment measures were implemented.
   - Innovation has spread across industries through collaborative networks and knowledge transfer.

1. **Verb**: stretch (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To extend continuously over a distance or area; to span from one point to another
   - *(Intransitive)* To cover or extend across space or time
   
   **Synonyms**: extend, span, reach, spread, traverse, cross
   
   **Antonyms**: contract, shrink, compress, confine
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing something that extends continuously over a distance, often emphasizing the full extent or breadth. Common for describing physical objects, territories, time periods, and abstract concepts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for disconnected or interrupted coverage (use "scattered across" instead). Avoid when describing discrete movements rather than continuous extension.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stretch across the horizon/sky
   - stretch across miles/kilometers
   - stretch across continents/oceans
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English streccan (to stretch, extend)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English streccan → Middle English strecchen, from Proto-Germanic *strakjanan, meaning to make straight or extend.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The beach stretches across several miles of beautiful coastline.
   - A huge rainbow stretched across the sky after the storm.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The railway network stretches across the entire continental territory.
   - The research project stretches across multiple disciplines and institutions.

1. **Verb**: extend (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To reach, stretch, or continue from one point to another; to cover a specified distance or area
   - *(Transitive/Intransitive)* To cause something to reach across or to span a distance
   
   **Synonyms**: stretch, span, reach, spread, continue, range
   
   **Antonyms**: limit, restrict, confine, contract, reduce
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts when describing coverage, reach, or continuation across space, time, or domains. More formal than "stretch" and emphasizes breadth or scope.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual conversation where simpler verbs like "go across" suffice. Don't use when describing discrete points rather than continuous extension.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - extend across borders/boundaries
   - extend across time periods/generations
   - extend across multiple domains/disciplines
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + tendere (to stretch) → Latin extendere
   
   **Etymology**: Latin extendere (ex- "out" + tendere "to stretch") → Old French estendre → Middle English extenden. Meaning to stretch out or expand.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The fence extends across the entire property line.
   - Her influence extends across the whole organization.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study's implications extend across multiple theoretical frameworks.
   - Trade routes extended across continents, facilitating cultural exchange.

1. **Verb**: drive (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To operate and control a vehicle to travel from one side to another
   - *(Transitive)* To traverse by vehicle; to cross while driving
   
   **Synonyms**: motor across, travel across by car, cross by vehicle, traverse by car
   
   **Antonyms**: walk across, stay stationary
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing vehicle-based crossing or traversing. Common for discussing travel across geographical areas, particularly by car. Can be literal or metaphorical (drive ideas across organizations).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for non-vehicular transportation. Avoid when the focus is on the destination rather than the crossing action.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - drive across the country/state
   - drive across town
   - drive across the bridge
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English drīfan (to drive, force forward)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English drīfan, from Proto-Germanic *drībaną, meaning to push or propel forward. Extended from moving animals to operating vehicles.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're planning to drive across the country this summer.
   - I drove across the bridge to avoid city traffic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Commuters drive across state lines daily for employment opportunities.
   - The campaign aims to drive change across institutional structures.

1. **Verb**: travel (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make a journey from one place to another; to move across distance or territory
   - *(Intransitive/Transitive)* To go from one side or region to another, often covering significant distance
   
   **Synonyms**: journey across, traverse, cross, go across, move across, voyage
   
   **Antonyms**: stay, remain, settle, reside
   
   **When to Use**: Use for any form of long-distance movement across regions, emphasizing the journey aspect. Appropriate for all modes of transportation and both literal and metaphorical contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for very short distances (use "go across" instead). Avoid when specific transportation mode is important to specify (use "drive/fly/sail across" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - travel across the world/globe
   - travel across continents/countries
   - news travels across networks
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English travailen, from Old French travailler (to work, journey)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French travailler → Middle English travailen, originally meaning "to toil" or "to make a laborious journey," from Latin tripaliare (to torture with tripalium, a three-staked instrument).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She loves to travel across Europe during summer break.
   - Sound travels across water much faster than through air.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers travel across the region to collect field data.
   - Information travels across digital networks at unprecedented speeds.

1. **Verb**: distribute (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To spread or disperse throughout an area, group, or system; to allocate among multiple recipients or locations
   - *(Transitive)* To divide and spread things across different places, people, or time periods
   
   **Synonyms**: disperse, spread, allocate, disseminate, apportion, divide
   
   **Antonyms**: concentrate, centralize, gather, consolidate, collect
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts when describing systematic allocation, spreading, or dispersion of resources, information, products, or responsibilities. Common in business, logistics, and analytical contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for casual or natural spreading (use "spread across" instead). Avoid when referring to unintentional or random distribution.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - distribute across channels/platforms
   - distribute across regions/locations
   - evenly distribute across categories
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart) + tribuere (to assign) → Latin distribuere
   
   **Etymology**: Latin distribuere (dis- "apart" + tribuere "to assign/allot") → Old French distribuer → Middle English distributen. Meaning to divide and give out in shares.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They distribute the workload evenly across all team members.
   - The company distributes its products across fifty states.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Resources are distributed across departments according to strategic priorities.
   - The study examined how wealth is distributed across demographic categories.

1. **Verb**: scatter (across)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To throw or distribute things in different directions over a wide area; to disperse irregularly
   - *(Transitive/Intransitive)* To spread in random or irregular patterns across a surface or area
   
   **Synonyms**: disperse, spread, strew, sprinkle, distribute randomly
   
   **Antonyms**: gather, collect, concentrate, cluster, group
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing irregular, random, or uncontrolled distribution across an area. Emphasizes lack of systematic pattern or organization. Common in descriptive and scientific contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when describing organized or systematic distribution (use "distribute across" instead). Avoid when precision or pattern is important.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - scattered across the floor/surface
   - scattered across the landscape/region
   - papers scattered across the desk
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English scateren, possibly from Old English *sceaterian
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English scateren, origin uncertain, possibly related to shatter or from Old Norse skata (to rush about). Meaning to disperse in different directions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His clothes were scattered across the bedroom floor.
   - Islands are scattered across the Pacific Ocean.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Archaeological artifacts were scattered across multiple excavation sites.
   - The population is scattered across remote rural communities.
