1. **Original Expression**: The problem occurs across all departments  
   **Context**: Describing widespread issue in organizational setting
   
   **Core Intent**: To communicate that a problem affects every department without exception
   
   **Casual**: This problem is showing up in every single department  
   *Characteristics*: Phrasal verb "showing up," intensifier "every single," conversational tone  
   *Use when*: Talking with close colleagues, team meetings, informal discussions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The problem affects all departments  
   *Characteristics*: Direct verb "affects," simple construction, clear and professional  
   *Use when*: Standard business communication, cross-departmental emails, routine reports
   
   **Formal**: The issue is pervasive across all organizational divisions  
   *Characteristics*: Elevated vocabulary ("pervasive," "organizational divisions"), abstract noun "issue"  
   *Use when*: Executive reports, formal presentations, official documentation
   
   **Humorous**: This problem has gone on a grand tour of every department—no one's been spared!  
   *Characteristics*: Personification ("gone on a grand tour"), playful exaggeration, exclamation  
   *Use when*: Building rapport with team, lightening mood about serious issue, with familiar colleagues  
   *Avoid when*: Issue has severe consequences, addressing senior leadership, victims are present
   
   **Lively**: This problem is spreading like wildfire through every single department!  
   *Characteristics*: Vivid metaphor ("like wildfire"), energetic tone, emphasis markers  
   *Use when*: Creating urgency, rallying team response, motivating action
   
   **Empathetic**: I know this problem is affecting all of you across every department  
   *Characteristics*: Personal acknowledgment ("I know"), second-person address ("all of you"), validation  
   *Use when*: Acknowledging impact on people, addressing affected staff, showing understanding
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher-status audience requires formal register; peers allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: Close colleagues permit casual/humorous; strangers require formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious problems generally require formal register; minor issues allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect higher formality in business contexts
   - *Medium*: Written reports require more formal language; spoken meetings allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual "showing up" in executive summary—sounds too informal and imprecise
   - Using formal "pervasive across organizational divisions" in team huddle—sounds stilted and distant
   - Using humorous tone when problem has caused significant harm—appears insensitive
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Team standup meeting with peers → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Lively (creates energy without excessive formality)
   - **Situation 2**: Written report to board of directors → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (matches expectations and gravity)
   - **Situation 3**: All-hands meeting addressing frustrated employees → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic (acknowledges impact and validates feelings)
