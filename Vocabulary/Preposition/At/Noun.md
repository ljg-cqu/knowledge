# Nouns Associated with "At"

1. **Noun**: location (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A particular place or position where something is situated or occurs; the action of locating someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: place, position, spot, site, venue
   
   **Antonyms**: nowhere, displacement
   
   **When to Use**: Use when specifying where something exists or happens; common in business (business location), geography, and technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when "place" would be more natural in casual conversation; avoid redundant phrases like "geographic location"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at this location
   - location at the corner
   - determine the location
   - strategic location
   - location services
   
   **Root Analysis**: locat(e) (from Latin "locus" - place) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "locatio" (a placing) → French "location" → English "location" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's your location right now?
   - The restaurant is at a great location downtown.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is situated at a strategic location near major transportation hubs.
   - GPS coordinates indicate the precise location at which the incident occurred.

2. **Noun**: point (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A particular spot, place, or position in space or time
   - A single item or detail in a discussion or argument
   - A unit of scoring, measurement, or value
   
   **Synonyms**: spot, place, moment (temporal), item, detail (argument), score
   
   **Antonyms**: area, region (spatial); whole, entirety (argument)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "at" with "point" for specific locations or moments; extremely versatile noun in English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "On a point" for arguments/discussion; "in point" is archaic
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at this point
   - at some point
   - at the point of
   - starting point
   - turning point
   - make a point
   
   **Root Analysis**: point (from Latin "punctum" - something pricked, dot)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "punctum" (pricked point) → Old French "point" → English "point" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - At this point, I don't really care anymore.
   - Meet me at the point where the two roads cross.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - At this juncture, the evidence suggests a significant correlation.
   - The intersection point at which these variables converge merits further investigation.

3. **Noun**: moment (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A very brief period of time; an instant
   - A particular point in time or an occasion
   - Importance or significance (formal/literary)
   
   **Synonyms**: instant, second, point in time, juncture
   
   **Antonyms**: eternity, age, era
   
   **When to Use**: Use "at" with "moment" for specific points in time; common in temporal expressions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "In a moment" means "soon"; "at the moment" means "right now"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at this moment
   - at the moment
   - at any moment
   - at that moment
   - critical moment
   - defining moment
   
   **Root Analysis**: moment (from Latin "momentum" - movement, instant)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "momentum" (movement, moving power) → Old French "moment" → English "moment" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm busy at the moment, can I call you back?
   - At that moment, everything clicked.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - At this particular moment in history, decisive action is required.
   - The precise moment at which the reaction occurs determines the outcome.

4. **Noun**: glance (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A quick or brief look at something or someone
   
   **Synonyms**: glimpse, peek, look, quick look
   
   **Antonyms**: stare, gaze, scrutiny
   
   **When to Use**: Use with "at" when describing a brief look toward something; very common verb-noun pairing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "at a glance" (idiom meaning "immediately apparent") with "at first glance" (upon initial observation)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at a glance
   - at first glance
   - take a glance at
   - quick glance
   - casual glance
   - exchange glances
   
   **Root Analysis**: glance (possibly from Middle English "glacen" - to glance off)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "glacen" (to strike obliquely, glance off) → extended to vision metaphor (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She took a quick glance at her phone.
   - At first glance, it looks pretty good.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data, at first glance, appears to confirm the hypothesis.
   - A cursory glance at the financial statements reveals several irregularities.

5. **Noun**: rate (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A measure of speed, frequency, or value; the speed at which something happens or the amount per unit
   - A fixed price or charge
   
   **Synonyms**: pace, speed, frequency, price, charge, cost
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms; contextual opposites like "constant" vs. "variable rate")
   
   **When to Use**: Use "at" with "rate" to indicate speed, frequency, or price levels
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "By rate" is incorrect; use "at a rate" or "by the rate of"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at this rate
   - at a rate of
   - at any rate
   - growth rate
   - interest rate
   - exchange rate
   - mortality rate
   
   **Root Analysis**: rate (from Latin "rata" - reckoned, fixed)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "rata" (fixed amount) → Medieval Latin "rata" → Old French "rate" → English "rate" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - At this rate, we'll never finish on time!
   - The taxi charges at a rate of $2 per mile.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The economy is expanding at a rate of 3% annually.
   - At any rate, further research is warranted to validate these preliminary findings.

6. **Noun**: expense (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The cost incurred in or required for something; money spent
   - (plural) Costs incurred in performing one's job
   
   **Synonyms**: cost, expenditure, outlay, charge, price
   
   **Antonyms**: income, profit, revenue, savings
   
   **When to Use**: Use in business, accounting, and financial contexts; "at the expense of" is a key phrasal preposition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Expense" is countable when specific; uncountable when general concept
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at the expense of
   - at great expense
   - business expenses
   - travel expenses
   - spare no expense
   - go to the expense
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + -pense (from Latin "pendere" - to weigh, pay)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "expendere" (to weigh out, pay out) → "expensa" → Old French "expense" → English "expense" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We got the best quality, but at great expense.
   - He lives well at his parents' expense.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Efficiency improvements should not be achieved at the expense of quality.
   - The organization spared no expense in ensuring comprehensive training programs.

7. **Noun**: odds (plural noun)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The chances or likelihood of something happening; the ratio between amounts staked by parties to a bet
   - A state of disagreement or conflict
   
   **Synonyms**: probability, chances, likelihood (sense 1), conflict, disagreement (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: certainty (sense 1), agreement, harmony (sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "at odds" for disagreement/conflict; use "odds" with "against/in favor" for probability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Always plural in modern usage; don't say "an odd" to mean probability
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at odds with
   - against all odds
   - the odds are
   - beat the odds
   - odds and ends
   - at odds over
   
   **Root Analysis**: odd (from Old Norse "oddi" - point of land, triangle, odd number) + -s (plural)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "oddi" (triangle, odd number) → Middle English "odde" → "odds" (unequal things, advantage) (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're completely at odds about where to go on vacation.
   - The odds are pretty good that it'll rain tomorrow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research findings are at odds with prevailing theoretical frameworks.
   - Against all odds, the initiative achieved its objectives ahead of schedule.

8. **Noun**: stake (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something (especially money) that is risked or could be lost or won
   - A share or interest in a business, situation, or outcome
   - A strong wooden post driven into the ground
   
   **Synonyms**: risk, wager, investment, interest, share
   
   **Antonyms**: certainty, guarantee, security
   
   **When to Use**: Use "at stake" to indicate risk or importance; common in business and competitive contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Physical "stake" (post) doesn't use "at stake"; that's for risk/importance only
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at stake
   - high stakes
   - stake in something
   - raise the stakes
   - have a stake in
   - put at stake
   
   **Root Analysis**: stake (from Old English "staca" - pin, stake)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "staca" (pin, post) → metaphorical extension to gambling (money attached to stake) → risk/interest (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a lot at stake in this game.
   - I have a personal stake in seeing this succeed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The credibility of the entire institution is at stake.
   - Stakeholders with significant financial stakes in the outcome voiced concerns.

9. **Noun**: peak (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The highest or most intense point of something; the top of a mountain
   - The maximum level or value
   
   **Synonyms**: summit, pinnacle, apex, zenith, climax, maximum
   
   **Antonyms**: trough, nadir, bottom, minimum, base
   
   **When to Use**: Use "at its peak" or "at the peak of" to indicate maximum point
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "In its peak" is incorrect; use "at its peak"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at its peak
   - at the peak of
   - peak performance
   - reach a peak
   - peak hours
   - mountain peak
   
   **Root Analysis**: peak (possibly from "pike" - pointed)
   
   **Etymology**: Likely from "pike" (pointed weapon/tool) → "peak" (pointed top of mountain) → extended to maximum/highest point (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Traffic is worst at peak hours.
   - He was at the peak of his career when he retired.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The epidemic reached its peak at approximately 2,000 cases per day.
   - At the peak of its influence, the organization comprised over 50,000 members.

10. **Noun**: length (U)
    **Meaning**: 
    - The measurement or extent of something from end to end; the amount of time something lasts
    - The quality of being long
    - A piece or section of something
    
    **Synonyms**: extent, distance, duration, span
    
    **Antonyms**: shortness, brevity, width (perpendicular dimension)
    
    **When to Use**: Use "at length" (phrasal) for thoroughness or eventual occurrence; use "in length" for measurements
    
    **When NOT to Use**: "At length" means thoroughly/eventually, not measurement; for measurement use "in length"
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - at length (thoroughly/eventually)
    - in length (measurement)
    - go to great lengths
    - arm's length
    - the length of
    - entire length
    
    **Root Analysis**: length (from Old English "lengþu" derived from "lang" - long)
    
    **Etymology**: Old English "lengþu" (from "lang" - long) → Middle English "lengthe" → Modern English "length"
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - We discussed it at length last night.
    - The room is 20 feet in length.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The theoretical implications have been examined at length in previous literature.
    - The specimen measures approximately 15 centimeters in length.
