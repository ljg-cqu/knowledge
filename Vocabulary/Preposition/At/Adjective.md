# Adjectives Associated with "At"

1. **Adjective**: good (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the required skill, talent, or proficiency in a particular area; competent or skilled
   
   **Synonyms**: skilled at, proficient at, talented at, capable at, adept at
   
   **Antonyms**: bad at, poor at, terrible at, incompetent at, unskilled at
   
   **When to Use**: Universal adjective for describing competence or skill level in activities, subjects, or tasks
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Good in" for subjects (though "good at" is more common); "good with" for tools/people
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - good at something
   - really good at
   - very good at
   - not very good at
   - surprisingly good at
   
   **Root Analysis**: good (from Old English "gōd")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gōd" (virtuous, desirable, valid) → extended to competence meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not very good at math.
   - She's really good at making people laugh.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students who are good at abstract reasoning tend to excel in theoretical subjects.
   - The candidate demonstrated that she is exceptionally good at strategic planning.

2. **Adjective**: bad (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Lacking skill, ability, or proficiency in a particular area; incompetent or unskilled
   
   **Synonyms**: poor at, terrible at, awful at, incompetent at, hopeless at
   
   **Antonyms**: good at, skilled at, talented at, proficient at, expert at
   
   **When to Use**: Common way to express lack of skill or ability in activities or subjects
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound harsh; consider "not good at" or "struggle with" for politeness
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bad at something
   - really bad at
   - terrible at (intensified)
   - notoriously bad at
   - admittedly bad at
   
   **Root Analysis**: bad (from Old English "bæddel" - hermaphrodite, uncertain origin)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "badde" (inferior, wicked) → extended to incompetence meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really bad at remembering names.
   - He's terrible at keeping secrets.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system proved particularly bad at handling unexpected inputs.
   - Historically, traditional methods have been bad at predicting rare events.

3. **Adjective**: skilled (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having or showing specialized training, knowledge, or ability in a particular area; expert
   
   **Synonyms**: proficient at, expert at, adept at, competent at, accomplished at
   
   **Antonyms**: unskilled at, inexperienced at, incompetent at, amateur at
   
   **When to Use**: More formal than "good at"; use for professional or technical competencies
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Skilled in" is also correct; "skilled with" for tools/equipment
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - skilled at something
   - highly skilled at
   - particularly skilled at
   - become skilled at
   - increasingly skilled at
   
   **Root Analysis**: skill (from Old Norse "skil" - distinction, knowledge) + -ed (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "skil" (knowledge, discernment) → "skill" → "skilled" (having skill)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My dad is really skilled at fixing things.
   - She's become quite skilled at negotiating.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The technician was highly skilled at diagnosing complex mechanical failures.
   - Practitioners skilled at these techniques achieve superior outcomes.

4. **Adjective**: expert (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having comprehensive and authoritative knowledge or skill in a particular area; highly proficient
   
   **Synonyms**: proficient at, skilled at, masterful at, accomplished at, adept at
   
   **Antonyms**: novice at, inexperienced at, amateurish at, incompetent at
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates the highest level of proficiency; use for mastery-level competence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can also be noun; "expert in" for fields of knowledge; "expert at" for activities
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - expert at something
   - become expert at
   - truly expert at
   - widely recognized as expert at
   - grow expert at
   
   **Root Analysis**: expert (from Latin "expertus" - tried, proved) + used as adjective
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "expertus" (tested, tried) → Old French "expert" → English "expert" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's expert at avoiding difficult conversations.
   - My grandmother is expert at making pasta from scratch.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The surgeon is expert at performing minimally invasive procedures.
   - Organizations expert at adaptation demonstrate greater resilience.

5. **Adjective**: surprised (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling or showing unexpected wonder or astonishment about something
   
   **Synonyms**: astonished at, amazed at, shocked at, startled at, taken aback at
   
   **Antonyms**: unsurprised at, expecting, anticipating
   
   **When to Use**: Common emotional adjective; use "at" for the cause of surprise
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Surprised by" also common and interchangeable; "surprised that" for clauses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - surprised at something
   - not surprised at
   - genuinely surprised at
   - pleasantly surprised at
   - hardly surprised at
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (over) + -prise (from Latin "prehendere" - to seize)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "surprendre" (to overtake) → "surprised" (taken unaware) → emotional sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was really surprised at how good the movie was.
   - Nobody was surprised at the result.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers were surprised at the magnitude of the observed effect.
   - The committee expressed itself surprised at the lack of progress.

6. **Adjective**: amazed (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Filled with great surprise or wonder; astonished
   
   **Synonyms**: astonished at, astounded at, stunned at, impressed at, awed at
   
   **Antonyms**: unimpressed at, indifferent to, expecting
   
   **When to Use**: Stronger than "surprised"; indicates intense wonder or admiration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Amazed by" is more common; "amazed at" more British/formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - amazed at something
   - constantly amazed at
   - never cease to be amazed at
   - utterly amazed at
   - continually amazed at
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (intensive) + maze (from Old English "āmasian" - to confuse)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "āmasian" (to confuse) → "amaze" (to bewilder) → "amazed" (filled with wonder)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm always amazed at how fast kids learn.
   - We were amazed at the view from the top.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Scholars remain amazed at the sophistication of ancient astronomical calculations.
   - Observers were amazed at the rapidity with which the transformation occurred.

7. **Adjective**: angry (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Feeling or showing strong annoyance, displeasure, or hostility
   
   **Synonyms**: mad at, furious at, annoyed at, irritated at, enraged at
   
   **Antonyms**: pleased at, happy with, satisfied with, calm about
   
   **When to Use**: "Angry at" (American English) for people/things causing anger; "angry with" (British) for people; "angry about" for situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: British English prefers "angry with" for people; American uses "angry at" for people and things
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - angry at someone/something
   - really angry at
   - get angry at
   - become angry at
   - stay angry at
   
   **Root Analysis**: angry (from Old Norse "angr" - grief, distress) + -y (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "angr" (grief) → Middle English "angry" (troubled) → modern sense (hostile, wrathful)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why are you so angry at me?
   - I'm not angry at you, just disappointed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consumers grew increasingly angry at the company's inadequate response.
   - Stakeholders were understandably angry at the lack of transparency.

8. **Adjective**: adept (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very skilled or proficient at something; expert
   
   **Synonyms**: skilled at, proficient at, expert at, accomplished at, masterful at
   
   **Antonyms**: inept at, clumsy at, unskilled at, incompetent at
   
   **When to Use**: Somewhat formal; indicates high level of skill acquired through practice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More formal than "good at"; may sound pretentious in casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - adept at something
   - become adept at
   - particularly adept at
   - prove adept at
   - grow adept at
   
   **Root Analysis**: adept (from Latin "adeptus" - having attained)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "adipisci" (to attain) → "adeptus" (one who has attained) → English "adept" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's really adept at reading people.
   - He became adept at avoiding the question.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Successful managers are adept at balancing competing priorities.
   - The species has proven remarkably adept at adapting to environmental changes.

9. **Adjective**: proficient (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Competent or skilled in doing or using something; having achieved a high level of competence
   
   **Synonyms**: skilled at, competent at, expert at, accomplished at, capable at
   
   **Antonyms**: incompetent at, inept at, unskilled at, deficient at
   
   **When to Use**: Formal term for high-level competence; common in education and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Proficient in" more common for languages/subjects; "proficient at" for activities/skills
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proficient at something
   - highly proficient at
   - become proficient at
   - increasingly proficient at
   - demonstrate proficiency at
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + fic (from Latin "facere" - to make, do) + -ient (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "proficere" (to make progress) → "proficient" (making progress, skilled) (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After a few months, he was pretty proficient at coding.
   - You need to be proficient at typing for this job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Candidates must demonstrate that they are proficient at statistical analysis.
   - Students who are proficient at self-regulation achieve better academic outcomes.

10. **Adjective**: present (at)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Being in a particular place; in attendance at an event or gathering
    - Existing or occurring now; current
    
    **Synonyms**: attending, in attendance at, there at, in existence, current
    
    **Antonyms**: absent from, away from, missing from
    
    **When to Use**: Use "present at" for physical attendance at events, meetings, or locations
    
    **When NOT to Use**: "Present in" for being within something; "at present" means currently/now
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - present at a meeting/event
    - be present at
    - physically present at
    - those present at
    - not present at
    
    **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + -sent (from Latin "esse" - to be)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "praesent-" (being at hand) → Old French "present" → English "present" (14th century)
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Were you present at the party last night?
    - Only people present at the meeting can vote.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - All stakeholders present at the negotiation concurred with the proposal.
    - The phenomenon was consistently observed by researchers present at the site.
