# Verbs Associated with "At"

1. **Verb**: look (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To direct one's eyes toward something or someone in order to see them; to examine or consider something
   - *(With "at")* To direct one's gaze toward a particular object, person, or place
   
   **Synonyms**: gaze at, stare at, glance at, observe, view, examine
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, avoid seeing
   
   **When to Use**: Universal verb for visual attention; "look at" is essential collocation for directing vision toward specific targets
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Look to" means rely on or turn to; "look for" means search for; "look into" means investigate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look at something/someone
   - take a look at
   - have a look at
   - look at it this way
   - look closely at
   
   **Root Analysis**: look (from Old English "locian" - to see, behold)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "locian" (to see, behold) → Middle English "loken" → Modern English "look"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Look at this funny video I found!
   - Can you look at my essay when you have time?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This study looks at the relationship between diet and cardiovascular health.
   - When we look at the data objectively, a clear pattern emerges.

2. **Verb**: stare (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To look fixedly or intensely at something or someone, typically with eyes wide open
   - *(With "at")* To gaze steadily and intently at a particular target
   
   **Synonyms**: gaze at, gawk at, peer at, fix one's eyes on
   
   **Antonyms**: glance at, ignore, look away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing prolonged, intense, or fixed visual attention; often implies rudeness or fascination
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be perceived as impolite; avoid when neutrality is needed (use "look at" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - stare at someone/something
   - stare blankly at
   - stare intently at
   - stop staring at
   - caught staring at
   
   **Root Analysis**: stare (from Old English "starian" - to stare, gaze)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "starian" (to gaze fixedly) → Middle English "staren" → Modern English "stare"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why are you staring at me like that?
   - He just sat there staring at the wall.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were instructed not to stare at the stimulus for extended periods.
   - The child stared at the painting with unmistakable fascination.

3. **Verb**: point (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To direct someone's attention to something by extending one's finger or an object toward it
   - *(With "at")* To indicate direction or location of something specific
   
   **Synonyms**: indicate, gesture at, direct attention to, single out
   
   **Antonyms**: conceal, hide, obscure
   
   **When to Use**: Common for physical gesture or figurative indication; "point at" requires specific target
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Point to" often interchangeable; "point out" means highlight or mention; pointing at people can be rude in some cultures
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - point at something/someone
   - point directly at
   - point a finger at
   - point and laugh at
   - evidence points at
   
   **Root Analysis**: point (from Latin "punctum" - pricked point)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "punctum" → Old French "point" → verb "pointer" (to point) → English "point" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He pointed at the house on the corner.
   - Don't point at people—it's rude!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The arrow points at the location where the artifact was discovered.
   - Initial evidence points at structural failure as the primary cause.

4. **Verb**: arrive (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To reach a destination or particular place
   - To reach a conclusion, decision, or understanding
   
   **Synonyms**: reach, get to, come to, attain
   
   **Antonyms**: depart from, leave, set out from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "arrive at" for specific locations/points; use "arrive in" for cities/countries; use metaphorically for conclusions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Arrive to" is incorrect; must be "arrive at" or "arrive in"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - arrive at a place
   - arrive at a conclusion
   - arrive at a decision
   - arrive at an agreement
   - arrive at the station
   
   **Root Analysis**: ar- (to) + -rive (from Latin "ripa" - shore)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ad ripam" (to shore) → Old French "ariver" (to land) → English "arrive" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What time did you arrive at the party?
   - We finally arrived at a compromise.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegation arrived at the venue precisely at the scheduled time.
   - Through rigorous analysis, researchers arrived at several significant conclusions.

5. **Verb**: aim (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To point or direct a weapon, object, or remark at a target
   - To intend to achieve something; to direct one's efforts toward a goal
   
   **Synonyms**: target, direct at, point at, intend, strive for
   
   **Antonyms**: miss, avoid, stray from
   
   **When to Use**: Use physically for weapons/objects; use figuratively for goals and intentions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Aim to" + infinitive (goal); "aim at" + noun (target)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - aim at a target
   - aim at someone/something
   - aim directly at
   - take aim at
   - aimed squarely at
   
   **Root Analysis**: aim (from Old French "esmer" - to estimate, aim)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "esmer" (to estimate, reckon) → "aestimare" (Latin) → English "aim" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Aim at the center of the target.
   - His comments were clearly aimed at me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative aims at reducing carbon emissions by 30% within five years.
   - The legislation is aimed at protecting consumer rights.

6. **Verb**: excel (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be exceptionally good at something; to surpass others in achievement or quality
   - *(With "at")* To demonstrate superior skill or ability in a particular area
   
   **Synonyms**: shine at, be skilled at, be proficient at, surpass in, outperform in
   
   **Antonyms**: fail at, struggle with, be poor at
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe superior performance or skill in specific activities or subjects
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Excel in" is also correct and often interchangeable; avoid in contexts requiring humility
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - excel at something
   - excel at sports/academics
   - always excel at
   - excel at tasks
   - naturally excel at
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out, beyond) + -cel (from Latin "cellere" - to rise)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "excellere" (to rise above, surpass) → Old French "exceller" → English "excel" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She really excels at math.
   - He's never excelled at team sports.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students who excel at critical thinking demonstrate superior analytical capabilities.
   - The organization has consistently excelled at innovation and adaptation.

7. **Verb**: laugh (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make sounds and movements expressing amusement, joy, or scorn
   - *(With "at")* To find something or someone amusing; to mock or ridicule
   
   **Synonyms**: chuckle at, giggle at, mock (negative sense), ridicule (negative sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: cry at, sympathize with, take seriously
   
   **When to Use**: Can be positive (finding something funny) or negative (mocking); context determines meaning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Laugh at" can be offensive when directed at people; be careful with tone
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - laugh at a joke
   - laugh at someone
   - don't laugh at me
   - nothing to laugh at
   - laugh at yourself
   
   **Root Analysis**: laugh (from Old English "hlæhhan" - to laugh)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "hlæhhan" (to laugh) → Middle English "laughen" → Modern English "laugh"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone laughed at his jokes.
   - They used to laugh at me in school.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audience laughed appreciatively at the speaker's witty remarks.
   - Historical perspectives that were once laughed at are now accepted as legitimate.

8. **Verb**: work (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be employed or engaged in a particular place or organization
   - To make effort to improve or develop something
   
   **Synonyms**: be employed at, labor at, toil at, strive at
   
   **Antonyms**: rest, be idle, abandon
   
   **When to Use**: Use "work at" for locations/companies or for making effort; "work on" for projects/tasks
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Work on" for tasks/projects; "work for" for employers; "work in" for fields/industries
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - work at a company
   - work at something
   - work hard at
   - work at improving
   - work at relationships
   
   **Root Analysis**: work (from Old English "weorc" - activity, deed)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "weorc" (activity, deed, labor) → Middle English "werk" → Modern English "work"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I work at Google now.
   - You really need to work at your communication skills.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - She has been employed at the institution for fifteen years.
   - Participants were instructed to work at their own pace throughout the study.

9. **Verb**: hint (at)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suggest something indirectly or in a subtle way without stating it explicitly
   
   **Synonyms**: suggest, imply, insinuate, allude to, intimate
   
   **Antonyms**: state directly, declare, announce, specify
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing indirect communication or subtle suggestions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When directness is valued; "hint at" can seem evasive in professional contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hint at something
   - hint strongly at
   - hint at the truth
   - hint at changes
   - broadly hint at
   
   **Root Analysis**: hint (possibly from obsolete "hent" - to grasp)
   
   **Etymology**: Possibly from obsolete "hent" (to grasp, seize) → "hint" (indirect suggestion) (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's been hinting at wanting a new phone.
   - Are you hinting at something?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report hints at potential conflicts of interest without making explicit accusations.
   - Historical documents hint at alternative explanations for the event.

10. **Verb**: marvel (at)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To feel wonder, surprise, or admiration at something remarkable
    
    **Synonyms**: be amazed at, wonder at, admire, be astonished at
    
    **Antonyms**: be indifferent to, ignore, be unimpressed by
    
    **When to Use**: Use to express strong positive surprise or admiration; somewhat literary or formal
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly dramatic in casual contexts; simpler verbs like "love" or "enjoy" may be more natural
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - marvel at something
    - marvel at the sight
    - can only marvel at
    - never cease to marvel at
    - visitors marvel at
    
    **Root Analysis**: marvel (from Latin "mirabilia" - wonderful things)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "mirabilia" (wonderful things) → Old French "merveille" → English "marvel" (14th century)
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - I just marvel at how patient you are with kids.
    - Tourists marvel at the ancient ruins.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Scholars continue to marvel at the sophistication of ancient engineering techniques.
    - One can only marvel at the complexity of biological systems at the molecular level.
