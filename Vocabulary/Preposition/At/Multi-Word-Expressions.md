# Multi-Word Expressions Using "At"

1. **Expression**: at a glance (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - By taking a brief or quick look; immediately upon looking; with a single look
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: immediately, instantly, at first sight, upon looking, right away
   
   **Antonyms**: after careful examination, upon close inspection, after studying
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate something is immediately apparent or can be quickly understood
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "at first glance" (initial impression that may be wrong)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - at first glance (initial impression)
   - at a single glance
   - tell at a glance
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Metaphorical extension of visual perception to mental understanding; "glance" (brief look) + "at a" creates idiomatic sense of immediacy
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + a + glance (from Middle English "glacen")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can tell at a glance that something's wrong.
   - The chart shows the trends at a glance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The executive summary provides key findings at a glance.
   - The dashboard enables users to assess system status at a glance.

2. **Expression**: at odds with (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In disagreement or conflict with someone or something; incompatible or inconsistent with
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: in conflict with, in disagreement with, at variance with, incompatible with
   
   **Antonyms**: in agreement with, consistent with, aligned with, in harmony with
   
   **When to Use**: Use for personal disagreements or logical inconsistencies between ideas/facts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Somewhat informal; in legal contexts use "in conflict with"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - be at odds with
   - find oneself at odds with
   - completely at odds with
   - directly at odds with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Odds" historically meant unequal things; "at odds" emerged from gambling terminology to indicate imbalance or disagreement
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + odds (from "odd" - unequal) + with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're totally at odds about this decision.
   - His actions are at odds with his words.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These findings appear at odds with established theoretical frameworks.
   - The policy remains at odds with international human rights standards.

3. **Expression**: at stake (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At risk of being lost or damaged; in question, especially something important
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: at risk, in jeopardy, on the line, in the balance, endangered
   
   **Antonyms**: secured, guaranteed, safe, protected
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize the importance or risk of something in serious situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound melodramatic for trivial matters; reserve for significant risks
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - be at stake
   - put at stake
   - what's at stake
   - with... at stake
   - much at stake
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: From gambling practice of attaching money to stakes; extended metaphorically to anything risked or in danger
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + stake (Old English "staca" - post)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your reputation is at stake here.
   - There's too much at stake to take risks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With national security at stake, immediate action is imperative.
   - The credibility of the institution is at stake in this matter.

4. **Expression**: at the expense of (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Causing loss, harm, or disadvantage to something else; paid for by; sacrificing
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: at the cost of, to the detriment of, sacrificing, at the sacrifice of
   
   **Antonyms**: to the benefit of, in support of, enhancing
   
   **When to Use**: Use when one thing is gained by sacrificing or harming another
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for neutral financial costs only (use "at the cost of")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - at the expense of something
   - at someone's expense (mockery sense)
   - at great expense to
   - come at the expense of
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Expense" inherently means cost/sacrifice; "at the expense of" conventionally indicates trade-off or sacrifice
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + the + expense (Latin "expensa") + of
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He succeeded, but at the expense of his health.
   - Don't make jokes at my expense!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Economic growth should not be pursued at the expense of environmental sustainability.
   - Efficiency gains were achieved at the expense of service quality.

5. **Expression**: at all costs (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Regardless of what effort, sacrifice, or consequences are involved; no matter what
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: no matter what, whatever it takes, by any means, regardless of cost
   
   **Antonyms**: if convenient, when feasible, cost-permitting
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize absolute necessity or determination; indicates highest priority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Hyperbolic; avoid for minor preferences or flexible situations
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - at any cost (similar meaning)
   - must be avoided at all costs
   - protect at all costs
   - at all costs, avoid...
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Hyperbolic expression emphasizing willingness to pay any price; "all costs" emphasizes totality
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + all + costs (plural of "cost")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We have to win this game at all costs!
   - Avoid him at all costs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Military defeat must be avoided at all costs.
   - The confidentiality of participants must be protected at all costs.

6. **Expression**: at your disposal (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Available for you to use as you wish; under your control or command
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: available to you, at your service, at your command, for your use
   
   **Antonyms**: unavailable, withheld from you, beyond your reach
   
   **When to Use**: Formal expression of availability; common in service contexts and formal offers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly formal in casual conversation; use "available" instead
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - at my/your/his/her disposal
   - place at disposal
   - put at disposal
   - resources at disposal
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Disposal" means control/use; "at disposal" conventionally expresses availability for use
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + possessive + disposal (from Latin "disponere" - to arrange)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm at your disposal if you need any help.
   - All the resources are at your disposal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team places its expertise at your disposal.
   - Comprehensive support services are at the disposal of participants.

7. **Expression**: at a loss (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Uncertain what to say, think, or do; puzzled or confused
   - At a disadvantage; suffering a loss (financial)
   
   **Synonyms**: puzzled, confused, uncertain, perplexed, at a disadvantage
   
   **Antonyms**: certain, confident, knowing what to do, at a profit (financial)
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express confusion, uncertainty, or inability to understand or respond
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context determines meaning (confusion vs. financial loss)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - at a loss for words
   - completely at a loss
   - utterly at a loss
   - sell at a loss (financial)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: From nautical/navigation terminology meaning off course; extended to mental confusion
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + a + loss (Old English "los" - destruction, loss)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm at a loss for what to say.
   - He was completely at a loss about what to do next.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers remain at a loss to explain the anomalous results.
   - The company operated at a loss for three consecutive quarters.

8. **Expression**: at will (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - As and when one pleases; at whatever time or in whatever way desired; freely
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: freely, at pleasure, as desired, whenever one wishes, as one pleases
   
   **Antonyms**: under constraint, by obligation, under restriction, by requirement
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate freedom, voluntary control, or lack of restrictions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Legal term "employment at will" has specific meaning; check context
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - can... at will
   - at will employment (legal term)
   - come and go at will
   - change at will
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Will" means volition/desire; "at will" emphasizes freedom and voluntary choice
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + will (Old English "willa" - desire, intention)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can leave at will—no one's forcing you to stay.
   - He comes and goes at will.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants may withdraw from the study at will without penalty.
   - The contract may be terminated at will by either party.

9. **Expression**: at ease (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Free from worry, awkwardness, or anxiety; relaxed and comfortable
   - Military command to relax from attention position
   
   **Synonyms**: comfortable, relaxed, at peace, comfortable, calm
   
   **Antonyms**: uneasy, anxious, tense, uncomfortable, on edge
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe comfort, relaxation, or lack of anxiety in situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Specific military meaning differs from general comfort sense
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - feel at ease
   - put someone at ease
   - be at ease
   - ill at ease (uncomfortable)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Ease" means comfort/freedom from difficulty; "at ease" is fixed expression for this state
   
   **Root Analysis**: at + ease (from Old French "aise" - comfort, convenience)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She made me feel really at ease.
   - I never feel at ease around him.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The interviewer's manner helped candidates feel at ease.
   - Participants reported feeling more at ease following the intervention.

10. **Expression**: at fault (type: collocation)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Responsible for a mistake, accident, or problem; to blame
    - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed partnership)
    
    **Synonyms**: to blame, responsible, guilty, culpable, at fault
    
    **Antonyms**: blameless, innocent, not responsible, without fault
    
    **When to Use**: Use to assign responsibility for mistakes, accidents, or problems
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can sound accusatory; consider softer alternatives in sensitive situations
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - be at fault
    - who is at fault
    - the party at fault
    - driver at fault
    - entirely at fault
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "Fault" means responsibility for error; "at fault" is conventional expression for blame assignment
    
    **Root Analysis**: at + fault (from Latin "fallere" - to deceive, fail)
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Who's at fault for this mess?
    - I was at fault—I should have checked twice.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The investigation determined that the contractor was at fault.
    - Neither party was found to be exclusively at fault for the dispute.

11. **Expression**: at your fingertips (type: idiom)
    **Meaning**: 
    - Readily available; easily accessible; immediately obtainable
    - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed phrase)
    
    **Synonyms**: readily available, easily accessible, at hand, within reach
    
    **Antonyms**: out of reach, inaccessible, unavailable, hard to access
    
    **When to Use**: Use to emphasize convenience and easy access to information or resources
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Clichéd in technology marketing; consider fresher alternatives
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - have at your fingertips
    - information at your fingertips
    - everything at your fingertips
    - right at your fingertips
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For idioms*: Metaphorical extension of physical proximity of fingers to immediate accessibility
    
    **Root Analysis**: at + possessive + finger + tips
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - With this app, you have everything at your fingertips.
    - All the answers are right at your fingertips.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The database places comprehensive information at researchers' fingertips.
    - Modern technology has placed vast knowledge resources at students' fingertips.

12. **Expression**: at the heart of (type: collocation)
    **Meaning**: 
    - In the most important or central part of something; forming the essential nature of something
    - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed partnership)
    
    **Synonyms**: at the core of, central to, fundamental to, at the center of
    
    **Antonyms**: peripheral to, marginal to, incidental to, on the fringes of
    
    **When to Use**: Use to emphasize centrality or fundamental importance of something
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Can sound clichéd if overused; vary with synonyms
    
    **Common Variations**: 
    - at the heart of the matter
    - lies at the heart of
    - sits at the heart of
    - at the very heart of
    
    **Why This Partnership**: 
    - *For collocations*: "Heart" metaphorically represents center/core; "at the heart of" conventionally expresses centrality
    
    **Root Analysis**: at + the + heart (Old English "heorte") + of
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Trust is at the heart of any good relationship.
    - That's really at the heart of the problem.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Innovation lies at the heart of sustainable economic development.
    - The principle of equality remains at the heart of democratic governance.
