1. **Noun**: front (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) The forward-facing part or side of something; the foremost position
   - (C) The area or position directly before someone or something
   - (C) A false appearance or manner designed to deceive; a façade
   - (U) Boldness or audacity in behavior
   - *Grammatical info*: countable when referring to specific part/position; uncountable when referring to audacity
   
   **Synonyms**: forefront, face, facade, exterior, vanguard
   
   **Antonyms**: back, rear, end, behind
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the forward part of objects, buildings, or positions; indicating false appearances; expressing boldness; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specific technical terms are needed (anterior, facade); avoid confusion between spatial and behavioral meanings
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at the front
   - in front
   - front of the house
   - up front (honest/in advance)
   - put on a front (deceive)
   
   **Root Analysis**: front (borrowed whole from French/Latin)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French front → Latin frons, frontis (forehead, front) → English (13th century) with extended meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Meet me at the front of the building.
   - He's just putting on a front—he's actually really nice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The front of the structure requires reinforcement.
   - The organization presented a united front during negotiations.

1. **Noun**: forefront (C, usually singular)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The leading or most important position; the vanguard
   - The very front part; the foremost position
   - *Grammatical info*: countable but typically used in singular with "the"
   
   **Synonyms**: vanguard, leading edge, cutting edge, van, front line
   
   **Antonyms**: rear, background, periphery, sidelines, back
   
   **When to Use**: Describing leadership positions, innovation, or prominence; indicating cutting-edge status; formal to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Literal spatial descriptions (use "front" instead); overly formal for casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - at the forefront
   - bring to the forefront
   - in the forefront
   - forefront of innovation
   - forefront of attention
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before, in front) + front (forward position)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English (15th century) fore + front, originally military term for front line → metaphorical prominence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's at the forefront of the whole movement.
   - This issue needs to be at the forefront.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution remains at the forefront of cancer research.
   - Environmental concerns are at the forefront of policy discussions.

1. **Noun**: frontage (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C/U) The front part of a building or piece of land, especially the part facing a street
   - (C) The extent or measurement of the front
   - *Grammatical info*: uncountable for general concept; countable for specific instances
   
   **Synonyms**: facade, front, street front, face
   
   **Antonyms**: rear, back, backside
   
   **When to Use**: Real estate, architecture, urban planning contexts; describing building fronts; technical/formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation about buildings (use "front"); non-architectural contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - street frontage
   - road frontage
   - water frontage
   - commercial frontage
   - frontage road
   
   **Root Analysis**: front + -age (noun suffix indicating collection or measure)
   
   **Etymology**: French front + -age suffix → architectural/real estate term (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The house has good street frontage.
   - We want a place with water frontage.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The property features 200 feet of street frontage.
   - Commercial frontage along Main Street commands premium pricing.

1. **Noun**: frontier (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The extreme limit of settled or developed territory; the border between known and unknown
   - (figurative) The advancing edge of knowledge, development, or achievement
   - A border between two countries
   - *Grammatical info*: countable
   
   **Synonyms**: boundary, border, edge, limit, forefront
   
   **Antonyms**: interior, center, heartland, core
   
   **When to Use**: Historical contexts (American frontier); describing cutting-edge fields; geographical borders; neutral to formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Modern spatial descriptions within settled areas; when "border" is more precise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - push back the frontier
   - frontier of knowledge
   - final frontier
   - frontier technology
   - frontier town
   
   **Root Analysis**: front + -ier (French suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French frontiere (border, front line) → English (15th century) with expansion to metaphorical boundaries
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Space is the final frontier.
   - They're exploring the frontiers of AI technology.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research continues to expand the frontiers of human knowledge.
   - The American frontier represented both opportunity and hardship.

1. **Noun**: facade (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The front face of a building, especially an imposing or decorative one
   - (figurative) A false or misleading appearance; an outward show
   - *Grammatical info*: countable; also spelled "façade" with cedilla
   
   **Synonyms**: front, exterior, face, frontage, pretense (figurative)
   
   **Antonyms**: reality, truth, back, interior, authenticity
   
   **When to Use**: Architectural descriptions; discussing false appearances or pretenses; formal to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When literal front without architectural significance is meant; avoid in very casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - building facade
   - maintain a facade
   - behind the facade
   - crumbling facade
   - put up a facade
   
   **Root Analysis**: façade (borrowed from French)
   
   **Etymology**: French façade → Italian facciata (from faccia "face") → architectural and metaphorical front (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Behind that tough facade, he's actually really sensitive.
   - The building's facade is really impressive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The historic facade requires careful restoration.
   - The company's commitment proved to be merely a facade.

1. **Noun**: vanguard (C, usually singular)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The foremost part of an advancing army or other force
   - (figurative) The leaders of a movement or trend; those at the forefront
   - *Grammatical info*: countable but typically used in singular; often with "the"
   
   **Synonyms**: forefront, leading edge, front line, avant-garde, cutting edge
   
   **Antonyms**: rear guard, followers, mainstream, traditionalists, rear
   
   **When to Use**: Describing leadership in movements, trends, or innovation; military contexts; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts (use "leaders" or "at the front"); when mainstream position is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the vanguard
   - vanguard of change
   - at the vanguard
   - vanguard position
   - lead the vanguard
   
   **Root Analysis**: van (shortened from avant, forward) + guard (protection)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French avant-garde (advance guard) → vanguard (15th century) military → metaphorical leadership
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're in the vanguard of the whole green movement.
   - Our school is at the vanguard of education reform.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution has positioned itself in the vanguard of medical innovation.
   - These researchers are in the vanguard of climate science.

1. **Noun**: foreground (C, usually singular)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The part of a view or picture that is nearest to the observer; the front area
   - (figurative) A prominent or important position
   - *Grammatical info*: countable but typically used in singular

   **Antonyms**: background, rear, distance, periphery
   
   **When to Use**: Visual arts, photography, design contexts; discussing prominence or priority; neutral to formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Non-visual spatial descriptions; when "background" is actually meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the foreground
   - bring to the foreground
   - move to the foreground
   - foreground and background
   - foreground focus
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before, in front) + ground (area, surface)
   
   **Etymology**: Dutch voorgrond → English (18th century) artistic term → metaphorical prominence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Who's that in the foreground of the photo?
   - We need to bring this issue to the foreground.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The composition places the subject in the foreground.
   - Social justice concerns have moved to the foreground of institutional priorities.

1. **Noun**: frontline (C, often used attributively)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The military line or position closest to the enemy
   - (figurative) The most important or active position in an activity or field
   - *Grammatical info*: countable; also written as "front line" (two words)
   
   **Synonyms**: front, battlefront, forward position, vanguard, cutting edge
   
   **Antonyms**: rear, behind the lines, support position, periphery
   
   **When to Use**: Military contexts; describing essential workers or critical positions; indicating direct engagement; neutral to formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Non-urgent or support roles; when "forefront" would be more appropriate for leadership
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - on the frontline
   - frontline workers
   - frontline troops
   - frontline position
   - hold the frontline
   
   **Root Analysis**: front + line (military formation)
   
   **Etymology**: Military terminology (WWI era) front + line → metaphorical use for direct engagement positions
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Nurses are frontline workers in the healthcare system.
   - He's on the frontline of climate activism.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Frontline healthcare workers demonstrated exceptional resilience during the pandemic.
   - The organization maintains a presence on the frontline of humanitarian relief.

1. **Noun**: priority (C/U, pl: priorities)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) Something that is more important than other things and should be dealt with first
   - (U) The condition of being more important than something else
   - (U) The right to be dealt with before others
   - *Grammatical info*: countable for specific items; uncountable for the concept
   
   **Synonyms**: precedence, preference, urgency, importance, preeminence
   
   **Antonyms**: afterthought, low priority, unimportant matter, triviality
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing importance, urgency, or order of tasks; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When all items are equally important; avoid overusing "top priority" (dilutes meaning)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - top priority
   - high priority
   - priority list
   - give priority to
   - take priority over
   
   **Root Analysis**: prior (earlier, before) + -ity (noun suffix indicating state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prior (former, earlier) + -ity → priority (precedence in importance or time, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My top priority right now is finishing school.
   - We need to get our priorities straight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Student safety remains the institution's highest priority.
   - Budget allocation should reflect organizational priorities.

1. **Noun**: prominence (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being important, famous, or noticeable
   - A projecting part or feature that sticks out
   - *Grammatical info*: uncountable for importance; countable for physical projection
   
   **Synonyms**: importance, fame, distinction, notability, eminence
   
   **Antonyms**: obscurity, insignificance, unimportance, anonymity
   
   **When to Use**: Describing importance, fame, or visibility; indicating physical projection; formal to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts where simpler words suffice; when precise architectural terms are needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rise to prominence
   - gain prominence
   - position of prominence
   - bring into prominence
   - come to prominence
   
   **Root Analysis**: promin- (to jut out) + -ence (noun suffix indicating state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prominere (pro "forward" + minere "to project") → prominence (jutting forward, notability)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She rose to prominence really quickly in the industry.
   - The issue has gained prominence lately.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author achieved prominence through groundbreaking literary contributions.
   - Environmental issues have gained prominence in public discourse.

1. **Noun**: lead (C, usually singular)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The front or leading position in a race or competition
   - An advantage or margin of superiority
   - (C) A piece of information that helps solve a problem or leads to discovery
   - *Grammatical info*: countable; homophone with "lead" (metal) but different pronunciation
   
   **Synonyms**: advantage, edge, front position, first place, forefront
   
   **Antonyms**: deficit, disadvantage, trailing position, rear, following
   
   **When to Use**: Competitive contexts; investigations; describing advantage or front position; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "leadership" (the role) is meant; watch pronunciation vs. "lead" (metal)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take the lead
   - in the lead
   - hold the lead
   - extend the lead
   - follow someone's lead
   
   **Root Analysis**: lead (from Old English lǣdan, to guide)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English lǣdan (to guide, go before) → noun "lead" (front position, 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're in the lead by three points!
   - Just follow my lead and you'll be fine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate has established a significant lead in polling data.
   - Investigators are pursuing several promising leads.
