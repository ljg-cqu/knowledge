1. **Adjective**: front (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Located at or relating to the forward-facing part or position; situated at the foremost position
   - *(Sense 2)* Most important or prominent; leading
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: forward, foremost, leading, frontal, anterior
   
   **Antonyms**: back, rear, behind, posterior, trailing
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe the forward-facing position of objects, people, or concepts; appropriate in spatial descriptions, rankings, or priority contexts; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing interior or central positions; not appropriate when "rear" or "back" is the correct spatial reference
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - front door
   - front row
   - front page
   - front position
   - front line
   
   **Root Analysis**: front (borrowed whole from French/Latin)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French front → Latin frons, frontis (forehead, front) → English (13th century) as noun, later adjectival use
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I sit in the front seat?
   - The front door is stuck again.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The front facade of the building requires restoration.
   - Front-line workers demonstrated exceptional resilience during the crisis.

1. **Adjective**: forward (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Directed or moving toward the front; oriented toward what is ahead
   - *(Sense 2)* Advanced in development or progress; ahead of schedule
   - *(Sense 3)* Bold or presumptuous in manner; overly familiar
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: front, advancing, progressive, ahead, onward
   
   **Antonyms**: backward, rear, reverse, behind, regressive
   
   **When to Use**: Use for directional movement toward the front; appropriate for progress indicators, spatial orientation, or describing assertive behavior; suitable across registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing stationary position (use "front" instead); sense 3 can be negative, use carefully
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - forward movement
   - forward position
   - forward thinking
   - forward direction
   - forward progress
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before, in front) + -ward (direction suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English foreweard (fore "before" + weard "toward") → forward motion or position
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just move forward a bit so I can see.
   - That was a pretty forward question to ask.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee advocates a forward-looking approach to urban planning.
   - Forward momentum in negotiations has been achieved through compromise.

1. **Adjective**: foremost (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Most prominent, important, or leading in position or rank; first in importance
   - *(Spatial)* Located at the very front; furthest forward
   - *Grammatical info*: non-gradable (already superlative), both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: leading, principal, chief, primary, paramount
   
   **Antonyms**: least important, minor, trailing, hindmost, rearmost
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize the highest importance or most prominent position; formal and semi-formal contexts; suitable for rankings, achievements, or spatial descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where simpler terms suffice; not for equal rankings (already superlative)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - foremost authority
   - foremost position
   - foremost concern
   - world's foremost expert
   - first and foremost
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before, in front) + -most (superlative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English formest (superlative of forma "first") → foremost (influenced by "fore")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Safety is our foremost concern.
   - He's one of the foremost experts on this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution ranks among the world's foremost research centers.
   - Environmental sustainability must be foremost in policy considerations.

1. **Adjective**: frontal (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Of, relating to, or located at the front; involving the front part
   - *(Sense 2)* Direct and straightforward; without concealment
   - *Grammatical info*: non-gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: front, anterior, forward-facing, face-on, head-on
   
   **Antonyms**: rear, posterior, dorsal, indirect, oblique
   
   **When to Use**: Use in technical, medical, or military contexts; appropriate for direct approaches or confrontations; formal to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "front" suffices; be aware of technical meanings in specific fields
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - frontal assault
   - frontal lobe
   - frontal view
   - frontal collision
   - frontal attack
   
   **Root Analysis**: front (front part) + -al (adjectival suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin frontalis (of the forehead) → French frontal → English (medical/military terminology, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The frontal view looks much better.
   - He took a frontal approach to the problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The frontal cortex is responsible for executive functions.
   - A frontal confrontation with the opposition appears inevitable.

1. **Adjective**: leading (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Most important, prominent, or influential; foremost in rank or position
   - *(Sense 2)* Going in front; showing the way
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: foremost, principal, chief, primary, main
   
   **Antonyms**: following, trailing, minor, secondary, subordinate
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe prominence, importance, or position at the front; appropriate for rankings, leadership contexts, or spatial arrangements; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing followers or less important elements; not appropriate for trailing positions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - leading role
   - leading position
   - leading authority
   - leading edge
   - leading figure
   
   **Root Analysis**: lead (to guide, go in front) + -ing (present participle as adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "lead" (Old English lǣdan "to guide") + -ing participial adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's playing the leading role in the school play.
   - Who's the leading candidate right now?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The university is a leading institution in biotechnology research.
   - Leading indicators suggest economic recovery in the coming quarter.

   **Antonyms**: posterior, rear, back, following, subsequent
   
   **When to Use**: Use primarily in medical, anatomical, or scientific contexts; appropriate for technical descriptions; formal register

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anterior position
   - anterior surface
   - anterior chamber
   - anterior cruciate ligament
   - anterior aspect
   
   **Root Analysis**: anteri- (before) + -or (comparative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin anterior (comparative of ante "before") → anatomical/scientific terminology (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used casually; would say "front part" instead)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The anterior portion of the hippocampus shows increased activation.
   - Anterior displacement of the patella was observed during examination.

1. **Adjective**: prominent (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Important, famous, or noticeable; standing out from others
   - *(Spatial)* Projecting outward; protruding or jutting out from a surface
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, both attributive and predicative
   
   **Synonyms**: conspicuous, notable, distinguished, eminent, outstanding
   
   **Antonyms**: inconspicuous, obscure, recessed, insignificant, minor
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe importance, visibility, or physical projection; appropriate for describing people, features, or positions; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing things that are hidden or insignificant; not appropriate for recessed features
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prominent position
   - prominent feature
   - prominent role
   - prominent figure
   - prominent display
   
   **Root Analysis**: promin- (to jut out) + -ent (adjectival suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prominere (pro "forward" + minere "to project") → prominent (jutting forward, notable)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The sign is in a prominent spot where everyone can see it.
   - He has a pretty prominent nose.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author occupies a prominent position in contemporary literature.
   - The building's prominent facade dominates the city skyline.

1. **Adjective**: facing (attributive only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Positioned to look toward or be directed at something; oriented in a particular direction
   - Having the front turned toward a specified direction
   - *Grammatical info*: gradable, attributive only (cannot be used predicatively alone)
   
   **Synonyms**: fronting, overlooking, looking toward, directed at
   
   **Antonyms**: backing onto, turning away from, rear-facing
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe the orientation or direction something faces; common in real estate, architecture, and spatial descriptions; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Cannot be used predicatively without a direction (*incorrect: "The house is facing"*); must specify direction: "facing south"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - south-facing window
   - forward-facing seat
   - ocean-facing room
   - street-facing apartment
   - north-facing slope
   
   **Root Analysis**: face (front part, to be directed toward) + -ing (present participle as adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: From noun "face" (Old French face → Latin facies) + -ing participial adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I want a room facing the beach.
   - Are these forward-facing seats?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The south-facing units receive optimal natural light.
   - Forward-facing cameras provide enhanced driver visibility.

1. **Adjective**: upfront (predicative and attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Honest, direct, and open; straightforward without concealment
   - *(Financial)* Paid or required in advance; at the beginning
   - *Grammatical info*: non-gradable, both attributive and predicative; informal to neutral
   
   **Synonyms**: honest, frank, direct, candid, straightforward
   
   **Antonyms**: evasive, indirect, deceptive, deferred, hidden
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe honesty and directness in communication; or advance payment; appropriate in business and personal contexts; informal to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing (use "forthright" or "advance"); not appropriate for describing physical position
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - upfront cost
   - upfront payment
   - upfront about something
   - upfront and honest
   - upfront fee
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (forward, to the front) + front (front position)
   
   **Etymology**: American English compound (20th century) from "up front" spatial phrase → metaphorical honesty + financial advance meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me be upfront with you about the costs.
   - There's an upfront fee of $100.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract requires upfront payment of 30% of the total amount.
   - Participants were informed upfront about potential risks.

1. **Adjective**: head-on (attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With the front or head facing forward; involving direct confrontation or collision
   - Direct and without evasion; straightforward
   - *Grammatical info*: non-gradable, both attributive and predicative; can also function as adverb
   
   **Synonyms**: frontal, direct, face-to-face, straight-on, direct
   
   **Antonyms**: indirect, oblique, sideways, evasive, glancing
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe direct collisions, confrontations, or approaches; appropriate in accident descriptions, conflict contexts, or problem-solving; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing indirect or subtle approaches; not appropriate for lateral or angled positions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - head-on collision
   - head-on confrontation
   - head-on approach
   - meet head-on
   - tackle head-on
   
   **Root Analysis**: head (front part of body/object) + on (contact, direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound from "head" + "on" (19th century) describing direct frontal impact or approach
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They had a head-on collision at the intersection.
   - Let's just tackle this problem head-on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report advocates a head-on approach to addressing systemic inequalities.
   - Head-on confrontation with these challenges requires comprehensive policy reform.
