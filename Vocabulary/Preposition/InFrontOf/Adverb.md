1. **Adverb**: forward (type: manner/direction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Toward the front or in the direction one is facing; onward in space or time
   - Into a position of prominence or consideration
   - *Grammatical info*: can appear in mid-position or end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: ahead, onward, forth, forwards, in advance
   
   **Antonyms**: backward, back, rearward, behind
   
   **When to Use**: Use to indicate movement or direction toward the front; appropriate for progress, advancement, or spatial movement; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing stationary position (use adjective "front" instead); not for backward movement
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move forward
   - step forward
   - look forward
   - push forward
   - bring forward
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before, in front) + -ward (direction suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English foreweard → forward (direction toward the front)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you move forward a bit?
   - Let's push forward with the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee voted to move forward with the proposal.
   - Research in this field has advanced forward significantly.

1. **Adverb**: ahead (type: place/time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In front; at or to the front in position
   - *(Temporal)* In advance; before the expected time
   - *(Abstract)* In a superior or more advanced position
   - *Grammatical info*: typically end-position or sentence-initial with "ahead of"
   
   **Synonyms**: in front, forward, before, in advance, beforehand
   
   **Antonyms**: behind, back, afterward, later, following
   
   **When to Use**: Use for spatial position in front, temporal advance, or competitive advantage; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simultaneity is meant; not for positions behind or following
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go ahead
   - look ahead
   - think ahead
   - get ahead
   - stay ahead
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + head (front part)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English on hed (on + head) → ahead (at the head/front position)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Go ahead, I'll catch up.
   - She's way ahead of everyone else.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Planning ahead is essential for project success.
   - The institution remains ahead in innovative research methodologies.

1. **Adverb**: forth (type: manner/direction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Forward in position, direction, or time; onward from a starting point
   - Out into view or consideration; into existence or prominence
   - *Grammatical info*: formal/literary; typically end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: forward, onward, ahead, out, outward
   
   **Antonyms**: back, backward, rearward, inward
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or literary contexts for forward movement or bringing into existence; common in fixed phrases; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "forward" instead); sounds archaic in most modern contexts except set phrases
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go forth
   - bring forth
   - set forth
   - put forth
   - back and forth
   
   **Root Analysis**: forth (from Germanic root meaning "forward")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English forþ (forward, onward) → maintained in formal/literary English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used; typically in fixed phrases: "back and forth")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The expedition set forth at dawn.
   - The committee put forth several recommendations for consideration.

1. **Adverb**: forwards (type: manner/direction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Toward the front or in the direction of the front; in the normal direction of movement
   - *(British English variant)* Same as "forward" but more common in British usage
   - *Grammatical info*: end-position; British English preference
   
   **Synonyms**: forward, ahead, onward, to the front
   
   **Antonyms**: backwards, backward, rearward, back
   
   **When to Use**: Use interchangeably with "forward" especially in British English; appropriate for directional movement; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: American English strongly prefers "forward"; avoid confusion by following regional conventions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move forwards
   - lean forwards
   - step forwards
   - look forwards
   - face forwards
   
   **Root Analysis**: forward + -s (adverbial suffix common in British English)
   
   **Etymology**: Forward + -s (directional adverb ending, especially British variant)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Lean forwards so I can see behind you.
   - The car rolled forwards slowly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis moves forwards chronologically through the historical period.
   - Participants were instructed to lean forwards at a 45-degree angle.

1. **Adverb**: upfront (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a direct and honest manner; openly and straightforwardly
   - *(Financial)* As an advance payment; at the beginning
   - *Grammatical info*: typically end-position; informal to neutral register
   
   **Synonyms**: honestly, frankly, openly, candidly, directly
   
   **Antonyms**: evasively, indirectly, deceptively, gradually, later
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe honest, direct communication or advance payment; appropriate in business and personal contexts; informal to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing (use "frankly" or "in advance"); not appropriate for describing physical position as adverb
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pay upfront
   - tell someone upfront
   - be upfront about
   - ask upfront
   - state upfront
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (to the front) + front (forward position)
   
   **Etymology**: American English (20th century) from spatial "up front" → metaphorical honesty and advance payment meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll tell you upfront—this won't be easy.
   - Do I have to pay upfront?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were informed upfront about all procedures.
   - Payment is required upfront before services commence.

1. **Adverb**: frontward (type: direction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Toward the front; in the direction of the front
   - *Grammatical info*: less common variant of "forward"; end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: forward, toward the front, to the front, ahead
   
   **Antonyms**: backward, rearward, toward the back
   
   **When to Use**: Rare usage; primarily in technical or specific directional descriptions; formal contexts where precision is needed
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Generally prefer "forward" in most contexts; "frontward" sounds stilted or overly technical
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move frontward
   - tilt frontward
   - shift frontward
   
   **Root Analysis**: front + -ward (directional suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Front + -ward (directional suffix) → analogous to forward, backward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used; would say "forward" instead)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The specimen was shifted frontward for optimal microscopic examination.
   - Tilt the apparatus frontward to achieve proper alignment.

1. **Adverb**: head-on (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With the front or head facing forward; in direct confrontation or collision
   - Directly and without evasion; straightforwardly
   - *Grammatical info*: end-position or complement position
   
   **Synonyms**: directly, frontally, face-to-face, straight on, frontally
   
   **Antonyms**: indirectly, obliquely, sideways, glancingly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe direct confrontation, collision, or approach; appropriate in accident descriptions, conflict situations, or problem-solving; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing indirect or subtle approaches; not appropriate for lateral movements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - collide head-on
   - meet head-on
   - face head-on
   - confront head-on
   - tackle head-on
   
   **Root Analysis**: head (front part) + on (direction/contact)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound (19th century) describing direct frontal contact or approach
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The two cars hit head-on.
   - Let's face this problem head-on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vehicles collided head-on at the intersection.
   - The organization chose to address criticism head-on through transparent communication.

1. **Adverb**: prominently (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a prominent manner; in a way that is easily seen or noticed
   - In an important or conspicuous position
   - *Grammatical info*: mid-position or end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: conspicuously, noticeably, obviously, visibly, importantly
   
   **Antonyms**: inconspicuously, obscurely, insignificantly, subtly, unobtrusively
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize visibility, importance, or noticeable display; appropriate for describing placement, display, or importance; suitable across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing hidden or subtle elements; not appropriate for understated displays
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - feature prominently
   - display prominently
   - figure prominently
   - appear prominently
   - positioned prominently
   
   **Root Analysis**: prominent (jutting out, important) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prominere (to jut forward) → prominent → prominently (noticeably, importantly)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The sign is displayed prominently at the entrance.
   - Her name appears prominently on the list.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theory features prominently in contemporary cognitive science literature.
   - Environmental concerns figure prominently in the policy framework.

   **Common Phrases**:

   - view frontally
   - approach frontally
   - examine frontally
   
   **Root Analysis**: frontal (of the front) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin frontalis → frontal → frontally (from or toward the front)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used; would say "from the front" instead)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The specimen should be photographed frontally for accurate documentation.
   - The military force approached the position frontally rather than laterally.

1. **Adverb**: anteriorly (type: place/manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an anterior position; toward or at the front
   - In front of another structure (anatomical/technical usage)
   - *Grammatical info*: end-position; highly technical/medical register
   
   **Synonyms**: at the front, in front, forward, frontally
   
   **Antonyms**: posteriorly, at the back, behind, rearward
   
   **When to Use**: Use exclusively in medical, anatomical, or scientific contexts for precise positional descriptions; highly formal/technical register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Never in casual conversation; not appropriate outside technical contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - positioned anteriorly
   - displaced anteriorly
   - located anteriorly
   - tilted anteriorly
   
   **Root Analysis**: anterior (front, before) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin anterior → anteriorly (in a front position) used in medical/scientific terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Not used in casual contexts)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The patella is displaced anteriorly relative to the femoral condyles.
   - The anterior chamber is positioned anteriorly to the lens.
