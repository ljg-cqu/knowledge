1. **Functional Expression**: "Could you move/stand in front of [object/person]?" (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Polite request for someone to reposition themselves to a location ahead of something/someone
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (softened by modal "could")
   
   **Synonyms**: Would you mind standing in front of..., Please position yourself in front of..., Can you move in front of...
   
   **Antonyms**: Could you move behind..., Could you step back from..., Please move away from...
   
   **When to Use**: Organizing people for photos, presentations, or group arrangements; directing traffic flow; teaching positioning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Emergency situations requiring immediate action (too polite/slow); when you lack authority to make requests
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you possibly stand in front of... (more tentative)
   - Would you stand in front of... (slightly more direct)
   - Mind standing in front of... (casual, abbreviated)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Could" softens the request using negative politeness (minimizing imposition); appropriate for strangers or professional contexts
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Sure, no problem" / "Of course" / "Certainly"
   - Declining: "I'd rather not, if that's okay" / "Could someone else do it?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could you move in front of the camera so I can get a better angle?
   - Hey, could you stand in front of Tom for the picture?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you please position yourself in front of the display for the demonstration?
   - Would the speakers kindly stand in front of the podium?

1. **Functional Expression**: "Let me go/stand in front" (function: suggesting/offering)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Volunteer offer to take the front position; suggesting oneself for the forward role
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct offer
   
   **Synonyms**: I'll go first, I can take the front position, Allow me to go ahead, I'll lead
   
   **Antonyms**: You go first, I'll stay back here, Let me follow, I'll go last
   
   **When to Use**: Volunteering for leadership or visible positions; offering to help with navigation; taking initiative
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When someone with higher authority should go first; culturally inappropriate contexts where modesty is valued
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let me just go in front (adding "just" softens)
   - I'll go in front if you want (offering as option)
   - Shall I go in front? (making it a question)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Direct offer using "let me" shows willingness without imposing; demonstrates helpfulness or leadership
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting offer: "Thanks, that would be great" / "Please do" / "Go ahead"
   - Declining offer: "No, it's fine, I'll go" / "That's okay, we can go together"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me go in front—I know where we're going.
   - I'll stand in front since I'm taller anyway.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Allow me to position myself in front to demonstrate the procedure.
   - I would be happy to stand in front for the presentation.

1. **Functional Expression**: "There's something/someone in front of [noun]" (function: informing/warning)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Alerting someone to an obstruction, presence, or object in the forward position
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct information
   
   **Synonyms**: Something is blocking..., There's an obstacle ahead of..., Watch out for something in front of..., [Object] is obstructed
   
   **Antonyms**: The way is clear, Nothing blocking..., All clear in front of...
   
   **When to Use**: Warning of obstacles; explaining why something isn't visible; safety alerts; describing obstructions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When immediate danger requires more urgent language ("Stop!" rather than descriptive statement)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - There's something blocking the front of... (more explicit)
   - Watch out—something's in front of... (adds warning element)
   - Something's right in front of... (emphasizes proximity)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Neutral information statement; can be softened or intensified through tone and additional words
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acknowledgment: "Oh, I see it" / "Thanks for letting me know" / "I'll move it"
   - Request for clarification: "Where exactly?" / "What is it?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hey, there's a car in front of you—don't back up yet!
   - There's someone standing in front of the TV.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please be advised that there is an obstruction in front of the entrance.
   - There appears to be debris positioned in front of the emergency exit.

1. **Functional Expression**: "You're standing/sitting in front of [object/person]" (function: informing/complaining indirectly)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Informing someone of their position; often implies they should move
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect complaint/request (stating fact to prompt action)
   
   **Synonyms**: You're blocking..., You're in the way of..., You're positioned in front of..., I can't see past you
   
   **Antonyms**: You're not in the way, You're fine where you are, Your position is perfect
   
   **When to Use**: When someone is unintentionally blocking; teaching spatial awareness; indirect request to move
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When direct request is more appropriate; can sound passive-aggressive if overused
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You're kind of in front of... (softened with "kind of")
   - You're right in front of... (emphasizes directness of obstruction)
   - Excuse me, but you're in front of... (adds politeness marker)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Indirect request strategy—stating fact to prompt voluntary action without direct command; face-saving for both parties
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Apologetic compliance: "Oh sorry, I'll move!" / "My bad, let me get out of the way"
   - Defensive: "I was here first" / "Can you move instead?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Dude, you're standing right in front of the screen.
   - You're sitting in front of the fan—I can't feel any air!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Excuse me, but you appear to be positioned in front of the display panel.
   - I believe you may be sitting in front of the projection screen.

1. **Functional Expression**: "Can/Could you see [object] in front of you?" (function: checking understanding/awareness)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Verifying someone's ability to perceive something; checking shared reference point
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal (with "could")
   - *Directness*: indirect (question form)
   
   **Synonyms**: Do you see... in front of you?, Are you able to see..., Is [object] visible to you?, Can you spot...
   
   **Antonyms**: (No direct antonyms, but related: Can't you see..., Don't you see...)
   
   **When to Use**: Giving directions or instructions; confirming shared visual reference; troubleshooting visibility issues
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When physical visibility is obviously impossible; can sound condescending if overused
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Do you see... right in front of you? (more direct)
   - Can you make out... in front of you? (implies difficulty seeing)
   - Is... visible in front of you? (more formal phrasing)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Question form as checking strategy; establishes shared frame of reference before proceeding
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Affirmative: "Yes, I see it" / "Got it" / "I can see it now"
   - Negative: "No, I don't see anything" / "Where?" / "I'm not sure what you mean"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you see the remote? It should be right in front of you.
   - Do you see that building in front of you? Turn left there.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you confirm whether the icon appears in front of you on the screen?
   - Can you verify whether the document is displayed in front of you?

1. **Functional Expression**: "I'd like to bring [topic] to the front" (function: suggesting topic priority)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposing to prioritize or highlight a particular topic or issue
   - *Formality*: formal to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect suggestion (using "I'd like")
   
   **Synonyms**: I'd like to prioritize..., Can we focus on..., I suggest we address... first, Let's bring attention to...
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's postpone..., I'd like to set aside..., Can we discuss this later...
   
   **When to Use**: Meeting facilitation; agenda management; redirecting discussion; emphasizing important points
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you lack authority to set priorities; when interrupting would be disruptive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd like to move [topic] to the front of the agenda (more explicit)
   - Can we bring [topic] to the front? (making it a question)
   - I think we should address [topic] up front (more assertive)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Tentative suggestion using "I'd like" minimizes face threat; appropriate for collaborative decision-making
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Good idea" / "Yes, let's do that" / "Absolutely"
   - Negotiation: "Can we finish this first?" / "How about after we cover..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd like to bring up the budget issue right at the front.
   - Can we push that topic to the front of the list?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would like to propose that we address the compliance matter at the forefront of our discussion.
   - May I suggest bringing the strategic initiative to the front of today's agenda?

1. **Functional Expression**: "Keep [object/issue] in front of you" (function: advising/instructing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Advising someone to maintain awareness, focus, or visibility of something
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct advice/instruction
   
   **Synonyms**: Don't lose sight of..., Stay focused on..., Maintain awareness of..., Keep... visible
   
   **Antonyms**: Set aside..., Don't worry about..., Forget about..., Put... behind you
   
   **When to Use**: Teaching; strategic advice; safety instructions; focus coaching
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When someone needs to let go of something; when hyper-focus would be harmful
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Keep [issue] front and center (more emphatic)
   - Always keep... in front of you (emphasizes consistency)
   - Make sure to keep... in front of you (adds compliance emphasis)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Imperative advice form; can be spatial (literal) or metaphorical (maintain awareness)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Compliance: "Will do" / "Got it" / "I'll remember that"
   - Clarification: "What exactly should I focus on?" / "How often?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Keep your phone in front of you so you don't miss the call.
   - Just keep your goals in front of you and you'll be fine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please ensure you keep the safety guidelines in front of you at all times during operation.
   - It is essential to keep regulatory compliance in front of the organization's priorities.

1. **Functional Expression**: "Let's put this issue in front of [person/group]" (function: suggesting action)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposing to present an issue to someone for decision or consideration
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect suggestion (using "let's")
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's present this to..., Let's bring this to..., Let's submit this to..., Let's take this to...
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's handle this ourselves, Let's keep this from..., Let's not involve...
   
   **When to Use**: Escalating issues; seeking higher approval; democratic decision-making; formal procedures
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you have authority to decide yourself; when escalation would be inappropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We should put this in front of... (more assertive)
   - Should we put this in front of...? (making it a question)
   - I suggest putting this in front of... (personal suggestion)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Collaborative "let's" invites joint action; implies issue requires higher-level attention
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Yes, good idea" / "They should know about this" / "Let's do it"
   - Hesitation: "Do we need to?" / "Can we try to solve it first?" / "When?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's put this decision in front of the whole team.
   - We should bring this problem in front of the manager.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I propose we place this matter in front of the board for consideration.
   - Let us submit this proposal in front of the executive committee.

1. **Functional Expression**: "I don't want to get ahead of myself, but..." (function: hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Hedging device to introduce tentative ideas while acknowledging they may be premature
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (heavily hedged)
   
   **Synonyms**: I don't want to jump the gun, but..., This might be premature, but..., It's early to say, but..., Let me think out loud...
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm confident that..., It's definite that..., We can say for certain...
   
   **When to Use**: Introducing early ideas; managing expectations; showing cautiousness; proposing tentative plans
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When certainty is required; when hedging undermines necessary confidence; in crisis decisions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I don't want to get too far ahead, but... (slightly different metaphor)
   - This might be getting ahead of things, but... (more tentative)
   - Without getting ahead of ourselves... (setting group expectation)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-saving hedge; acknowledges possibility of error; invites gentle disagreement
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Encouragement: "No, go ahead, what are you thinking?" / "It's good to plan ahead"
   - Agreement with caution: "You're right, it's early, but let's hear it"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't want to get ahead of myself, but I think we might actually win this.
   - Don't want to get ahead of things, but have you thought about moving?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I do not wish to get ahead of the data, but preliminary results suggest a positive correlation.
   - Without getting ahead of the committee's decision, one might anticipate approval.

1. **Functional Expression**: "Front and center, please" (function: requesting attention/positioning)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Direct request for someone to come to prominent visible position or to pay attention
   - *Formality*: neutral (informal origin but used across contexts)
   - *Directness*: direct command softened by "please"
   
   **Synonyms**: Come to the front, please; Your attention please; Step forward; Come here, please
   
   **Antonyms**: Stay back, please; Return to your seat; Step back
   
   **When to Use**: Teachers calling students; group organizers positioning people; attention-getting in presentations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Without appropriate authority; in very formal ceremonies (use more formal language)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Front and center! (without "please"—more commanding)
   - Come front and center (verb added)
   - I need everyone front and center (expanded instruction)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Theatrical origin; implies importance and visibility; "please" essential for politeness
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Compliance: [Physical movement to front]
   - Clarification: "Me?" / "Everyone?" / "Right now?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Okay, birthday girl, front and center for the cake!
   - Front and center, everyone—time for the group photo!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Award recipients, please come front and center for recognition.
   - Will the keynote speaker please position themselves front and center?
