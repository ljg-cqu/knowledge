# Verbs Related to: Until

1. **Verb**: wait (intransitive/transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To stay in one place or delay action until a particular time or event; to remain inactive or expectant until something happens
   - *(Sense 2)* To serve food or attend to someone's needs
   
   **Synonyms**: stay, remain, hold on, delay, pause, be patient
   
   **Antonyms**: proceed, go ahead, leave, act immediately, rush
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing deliberate inaction or delay until a specified condition, time, or event; extremely common in coordination and scheduling
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply passivity; in contexts requiring action, use "prepare for" or "anticipate" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wait until [time/event], wait for someone/something
   - wait patiently, wait around, can't wait (until)
   - wait it out (until conditions improve), wait up (until someone arrives)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "wæcan" (to watch, be awake)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wæcan" (to watch, be awake) → Middle English "waiten" → Modern English "wait"; original sense of watchful vigilance evolved to expectant delay until event
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Wait until you see what I got!
   - I'll wait here until you get back.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please wait until your name is called.
   - Participants were instructed to wait until the signal was given.

2. **Verb**: postpone (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To delay or reschedule something to a later time; to arrange that something happens at a time later than originally planned until a new date
   
   **Synonyms**: delay, defer, put off, reschedule, push back
   
   **Antonyms**: advance, move up, expedite, hasten, bring forward
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or semi-formal contexts when events, meetings, or deadlines are officially moved to later times; common in business and administration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More formal than "put off" or "delay"; use simpler terms in casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - postpone until [date/event], postpone indefinitely
   - postpone the meeting, postpone a decision, postpone action
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "post" (after) + "ponere" (to place)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "postponere" (to put after, place later) from "post" (after) + "ponere" (to place) → French "postponer" → English "postpone"; literally placing something after until later time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had to postpone the party until next week.
   - Can we postpone this until tomorrow?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board has decided to postpone the vote until additional information is obtained.
   - Implementation has been postponed until regulatory approval is secured.

3. **Verb**: delay (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make something happen at a later time than originally planned; to postpone action until later
   - *(Intransitive)* To be late or slow; to take longer than expected until completion
   
   **Synonyms**: postpone, defer, put off, slow, hold up, stall
   
   **Antonyms**: expedite, hasten, accelerate, rush, advance
   
   **When to Use**: Use for both intentional postponement and unintended slowness; versatile across formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply problems or inefficiency; if postponement is strategic, "postpone" may be more positive
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - delay until [time], delay the start, without delay
   - delay action, delay tactics, delay in [process]
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old French "delaier" (to delay)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "delaier" → Middle English "delayen"; pushing back temporal boundaries until later occurrence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's delay this until we have more info.
   - Don't delay until the last minute!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The decision has been delayed until further consultation occurs.
   - System deployment was delayed until testing protocols were satisfied.

4. **Verb**: continue (intransitive/transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To keep happening, existing, or doing something without stopping; to persist from one point until another
   - *(Sense 2)* To start again after an interruption
   
   **Synonyms**: persist, carry on, proceed, go on, maintain, sustain
   
   **Antonyms**: stop, cease, discontinue, halt, end, terminate
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe ongoing actions or states that extend from a starting point until an endpoint or indefinitely
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Neutral connotation; doesn't indicate whether continuation is positive or negative
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - continue until [end point], continue doing something
   - continue with [activity], continue on, to be continued
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "con-" (together, thoroughly) + "tenere" (to hold)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "continuare" (to join together, make continuous) from "continuus" → French "continuer" → English "continue"; holding together until endpoint
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's continue until we finish this level.
   - The rain continued until morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research will continue until definitive conclusions can be drawn.
   - Operations shall continue until otherwise directed.

5. **Verb**: suspend (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To temporarily stop or interrupt something; to make something inactive for a period until resumption or decision
   - *(Sense 2)* To hang something from above; to keep something in the air
   
   **Synonyms**: halt, pause, discontinue, interrupt, put on hold
   
   **Antonyms**: resume, continue, activate, reinstate, maintain
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts for temporary cessation with intention to potentially resume; common in administration, discipline, and operations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies temporary state; if permanent, use "terminate" or "cancel"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - suspend until [condition/time], suspend operations
   - suspend judgment, suspend disbelief, suspend privileges
   - suspend animation, suspend someone (from position)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "sub-" (from below) + "pendere" (to hang)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "suspendere" (to hang up, interrupt) from "sub" (from below) + "pendere" (to hang); metaphor of hanging something until retrieval
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They suspended him until the investigation is done.
   - Let's suspend this discussion until everyone's here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Operations are suspended until safety compliance is verified.
   - The policy has been suspended pending comprehensive review.

6. **Verb**: persist (intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To continue to exist, happen, or do something despite difficulty or opposition; to endure until change or resolution
   
   **Synonyms**: continue, endure, persevere, carry on, remain, last
   
   **Antonyms**: cease, stop, give up, quit, discontinue, relent
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing continuation despite obstacles; often implies determination or unwanted continuation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be negative (unwanted continuation) or positive (determination); context determines connotation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - persist until [goal/change], persist in doing something
   - persist despite obstacles, problems persist, persist with effort
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "per-" (through, thoroughly) + "sistere" (to stand)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "persistere" (to continue steadfastly, stand firm) from "per" (through) + "sistere" (to stand, cause to stand); standing firm until change
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The problem persisted until we replaced the whole system.
   - If you persist until the end, you'll succeed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Symptoms may persist until appropriate treatment is administered.
   - The disparity persisted until policy interventions were implemented.

7. **Verb**: endure (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suffer or experience something difficult or unpleasant patiently; to tolerate until relief or end
   - *(Intransitive)* To last or continue to exist for a long time
   
   **Synonyms**: bear, tolerate, withstand, suffer, weather, last
   
   **Antonyms**: succumb, surrender, collapse, perish, fail
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing difficulty of continuation or impressive duration; often implies hardship until relief
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Usually implies difficulty; for neutral continuation, use "continue" or "last"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - endure until [relief/end], endure hardship, endure pain
   - endure through [difficulty], can't endure any longer
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "in-" (in) + "durare" (to harden, last)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "indurare" (to make hard) from "in" (in) + "durare" (to harden, last) → Old French "endurer" → English "endure"; hardening oneself until trial ends
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't know how much longer I can endure until this is over.
   - We had to endure the noise until midnight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were required to endure discomfort until the protocol was completed.
   - The structure must endure environmental stress until maintenance can be performed.

8. **Verb**: last (intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To continue to exist, function, or be usable for a period of time; to remain in good condition until expiration or exhaustion
   
   **Synonyms**: endure, continue, persist, remain, survive, hold out
   
   **Antonyms**: expire, end, run out, fail, break down
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe duration of existence, functionality, or supply; often with temporal extents specified
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Neutral about difficulty; if emphasizing hardship, use "endure"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - last until [time], last long, last throughout
   - how long will it last, last the distance, make it last
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "læstan" (to follow, continue)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "læstan" (to follow, continue, endure) → Middle English "lasten"; continuing existence until endpoint
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This battery should last until tomorrow.
   - How long will the food last until we need to shop again?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Provisions are expected to last until resupply arrives.
   - The effect lasts until the medication is metabolized.

9. **Verb**: hold (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To maintain a position, condition, or state; to keep something valid or effective until change
   - *(Sense 2)* To physically grasp or support something
   - *(Sense 3)* To contain or have capacity for something
   
   **Synonyms**: maintain, keep, sustain, preserve (sense 1)
   
   **Antonyms**: release, let go, abandon, change, give up
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing maintenance of states or positions over time; versatile across contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple meanings; ensure context makes intended sense clear
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hold until [time/condition], hold steady, hold firm
   - hold the position, hold out (until relief), hold on (until help arrives)
   - hold true (until proven otherwise)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "healdan" (to hold, possess)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "healdan" (to hold, keep, guard, possess) → Middle English "holden"; maintaining possession or state until release
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you hold this until I get back?
   - Hold on until I figure this out!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agreement holds until either party provides notice.
   - These principles hold true until contradictory evidence emerges.

10. **Verb**: defer (transitive)
    **Meaning**: 
    - To put off or delay something to a later time; to postpone action or decision until more appropriate moment
    - *(Sense 2)* To submit to someone else's judgment or opinion out of respect
    
    **Synonyms**: postpone, delay, put off, shelve, table (sense 1)
    
    **Antonyms**: expedite, advance, hasten, proceed immediately (sense 1)
    
    **When to Use**: Use in formal or professional contexts when decisions or actions are strategically delayed; implies thoughtful postponement
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Quite formal; in casual contexts, use "put off" or "postpone"
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - defer until [later time], defer action, defer a decision
    - defer payment, defer judgment, defer to [authority]
    
    **Root Analysis**: Latin "de-" (away, down) + "ferre" (to carry, bear)
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "differre" (to carry apart, postpone) from "dis" (apart) + "ferre" (to carry) → "defer"; carrying forward until later time
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - Can we defer this conversation until tomorrow?
    - Let's defer the decision until we know more.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The committee has elected to defer consideration until additional data is available.
    - Payment may be deferred until the subsequent fiscal quarter.
