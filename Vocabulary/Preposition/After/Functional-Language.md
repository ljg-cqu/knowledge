1. **Functional Expression**: After you... (function: sequencing/conditional instruction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that one action should follow another; establishes temporal sequence
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens imperative)
   
   **Synonyms**: Once you..., When you've..., Following..., Upon...
   
   **Antonyms**: Before you..., Prior to..., Instead of...
   
   **When to Use**: Instructions, procedures, tutorials, guidance; softens commands by framing them as conditional sequence; works in all contexts from casual to formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When immediate action is required (use direct imperative); when sequence is not critical
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - After you finish/complete/do X, then Y (more explicit sequence)
   - After you've done X (perfect aspect emphasizes completion)
   - Right after you... (emphasizes immediate succession)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Politeness strategy that frames instruction as natural consequence rather than command; negative politeness (minimizes imposition) by giving agency to listener
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Got it. / Understood. (acknowledgment)
   - Okay, what happens after that? (seeking further information)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After you wash the dishes, can you take out the trash?
   - Just sign here, and after you're done, hand it back to me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After you submit your application, you will receive a confirmation email.
   - After you complete the training module, please notify your supervisor.

1. **Functional Expression**: I was thinking, after... (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposes an idea or plan with specific timing; hedges suggestion to reduce imposition
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: very indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: How about after..., What if after..., Maybe after..., Perhaps following...
   
   **Antonyms**: We need to... before, We must... first, You should... instead
   
   **When to Use**: Making tentative suggestions; proposing plans when outcome is uncertain; maintaining flexibility; peer relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need firm commitment; in formal proposals; when authority relationship requires directness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I was thinking maybe after... (adds additional hedge)
   - I thought that after... (past tense suggests prior consideration)
   - I'm thinking after... (present progressive emphasizes current consideration)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Multiple hedging devices ("I was thinking" + "after") minimize face threat; positive politeness by inviting collaboration; leaves room for negotiation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That works for me! / Sounds good! (acceptance)
   - Hmm, what about before/instead? (counter-proposal)
   - I'm not sure... (indirect refusal)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I was thinking, after the movie, we could grab some dinner?
   - I was thinking maybe after you finish that, we could start on the next phase.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I believe that after the Q2 results are finalized, we should schedule a strategic review.
   - I would suggest that after the merger completes, we reassess our organizational structure.

1. **Functional Expression**: After all... (function: justifying/reasoning)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces explanation or justification, often for something that might seem surprising; appeals to obvious or undeniable fact
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (implies conclusion rather than stating it)
   
   **Synonyms**: Considering that..., Given that..., Bear in mind that..., Remember that...
   
   **Antonyms**: Despite..., Even though..., Regardless of...
   
   **When to Use**: Providing justification; appealing to shared knowledge; persuading by citing obvious facts; works in argumentation and explanation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When fact is not obvious or agreed upon; can sound condescending if overused; avoid when appearing to state the obvious would be rude
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - After all, [reason] (comma in writing; pause in speech)
   - [Statement]. After all, [justification]. (sentence-initial for emphasis)
   - [Statement], after all (sentence-final, assumes shared understanding)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Appeals to common ground; assumes agreement on stated fact; can be persuasive but may seem patronizing if misjudged; strengthens argument by citing undeniable truth
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's true. / Fair point. (agreement)
   - Well, yes, but... (qualified agreement)
   - I'm not sure I agree that... (challenging premise)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You should apologize. After all, you were the one who started it.
   - Let's give her a break—after all, it's her first day!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This investment warrants careful consideration. After all, it represents a significant commitment of resources.
   - The policy should be revised. After all, circumstances have changed substantially since its inception.

1. **Functional Expression**: Can we talk about this after...? (function: postponing/deferring)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Requests to delay discussion until later time; combines request with temporal condition
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect request (question form)
   
   **Synonyms**: Could we discuss this later/following...?, Can this wait until after...?, Let's table this until after...
   
   **Antonyms**: We need to talk about this now/immediately, Let's address this before...
   
   **When to Use**: When you need time or different circumstances; managing timing of difficult conversations; establishing boundaries; avoiding interruptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When urgency makes delay inappropriate; when repeatedly postponing erodes trust; when other party has legitimate urgency
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Can we talk about this after [event]? (specific timing)
   - Could we save this conversation for after...? (more formal "could," "save" is gentler)
   - Would it be okay to discuss this after...? (seeking explicit permission)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness (minimizes imposition) through question form; acknowledges importance by proposing specific future time rather than indefinite postponement; maintains control over timing
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, no problem. (acceptance)
   - Actually, it's kind of urgent... (asserting priority)
   - How long are we talking? (negotiating timing)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we talk about this after I finish this call? I want to give you my full attention.
   - Can we save this conversation for after the kids go to bed?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could we schedule this discussion for after the quarterly review? I'd like to have the complete data available.
   - Would it be possible to defer this conversation until after the board meeting?

1. **Functional Expression**: After everything we've been through... (function: appealing to shared history)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Invokes shared experiences to support argument or request; appeals to relationship investment
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (implies rather than states request/expectation)
   
   **Synonyms**: Given our history..., Considering what we've shared..., After all we've done together...
   
   **Antonyms**: Regardless of our past..., Setting aside our history..., This isn't about...
   
   **When to Use**: When appealing to loyalty or shared experience; when history is genuinely relevant; when relationship equity supports request; in persuasion or conflict resolution
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be manipulative if overused; inappropriate when history is irrelevant to current issue; may sound guilt-tripping in wrong context
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - After all we've been through together... (emphasizes collective experience)
   - After everything we've accomplished... (positive framing)
   - After what we've survived... (emphasizes adversity overcome)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness strategy (emphasizes shared in-group membership); can strengthen emotional appeal but risks appearing manipulative; effective when history genuinely supports claim
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're right... (acceptance of obligation)
   - That's not fair/That's manipulative (rejection of appeal)
   - I know, but... (acknowledgment with qualification)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After everything we've been through, I thought you'd at least hear me out.
   - Come on! After all we've done together, you know I've got your back.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given our longstanding partnership and collaborative history, I trust you will consider this request seriously.
   - In light of our successful past collaborations, I believe this proposal merits your careful consideration.

1. **Functional Expression**: Not long after... (function: narrating/sequencing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Indicates that second event followed first event quickly; narrative time marker
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (statement, not request)
   
   **Synonyms**: Shortly after..., Soon after..., A short time later..., Within a brief period...
   
   **Antonyms**: Long after..., Much later..., Years after..., Eventually...
   
   **When to Use**: Storytelling; case studies; incident reports; emphasizing rapid succession of events; creating narrative flow
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact timing is critical (be specific); in contexts requiring precise temporal documentation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Not long after that (refers to previous event)
   - Not long after [specific event] (explicitly names trigger event)
   - Not too long after (even less formal, more conversational)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Narrative discourse marker that manages temporal flow; implies causal or consequential relationship between events; creates cohesion in extended discourse
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What happened then? (continuing narrative)
   - How long exactly? (seeking precision)
   - [Silence—listening to continued narrative] (typical in storytelling)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We moved to Seattle, and not long after that, I got my dream job.
   - She sent the email, and not long after, she got a reply.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy was announced; not long after, its effects became apparent in the market.
   - The initial findings were published in March; not long after, several replications confirmed the results.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's touch base after... (function: scheduling follow-up)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposes reconnecting or communicating at future point; schedules informal check-in
   - *Formality*: informal (business casual)
   - *Directness*: indirect suggestion (let's format)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's reconnect after..., Let's follow up after..., Let's check in after..., We should connect after...
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's finalize this before..., Let's wrap this up now, We need to decide immediately
   
   **When to Use**: Business contexts; scheduling informal meetings; maintaining connections; when formal meeting not required; keeping communication open
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too casual for very formal contexts; when concrete commitment required (use "let's schedule"); outside business/professional contexts may sound jargon-heavy
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's reconnect after... (slightly more personal)
   - Let's circle back after... (similar business jargon)
   - We should touch base after... ("should" slightly more tentative than "let's")
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Business jargon that softens commitment—"touch base" implies brief, informal contact rather than formal meeting; positive politeness through collaborative "let's"; American business culture idiom
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sounds good! / Works for me! (agreement)
   - Sure, I'll send you a calendar invite. (formalizing the arrangement)
   - Actually, can we nail down specifics now? (seeking more commitment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's touch base after the holidays and see where things stand.
   - Touch base with me after you've had a chance to review the proposal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Perhaps we should schedule a formal meeting following the completion of the initial phase.
   - I propose we reconvene after the data analysis is finalized to review findings.

1. **Functional Expression**: After careful consideration... (function: announcing decision)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Prefaces decision or conclusion; emphasizes thoughtfulness of decision-making process
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct (announces decision clearly)
   
   **Synonyms**: Upon reflection..., Having thoroughly considered..., After due deliberation..., Following careful review...
   
   **Antonyms**: Without thinking..., Hastily..., Impulsively..., On a whim...
   
   **When to Use**: Announcing important decisions; conveying deliberation; adding weight to conclusions; formal communications; when justifying potentially controversial decisions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual contexts; can sound pompous for trivial decisions; may appear defensive if overused
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - After careful thought... (slightly less formal)
   - After much consideration... (emphasizes extended deliberation)
   - Following careful analysis... (more technical/analytical frame)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Discourse marker that signals transition from deliberation to decision; adds legitimacy by emphasizing process; face-saving device that preempts criticism; formal register marker
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - We understand. / Thank you for considering all factors. (acceptance)
   - Can you explain your reasoning? (seeking justification)
   - I respectfully disagree. (formal disagreement)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - So I thought about it for a while, and here's what I've decided...
   - After thinking it through, I'm going to pass on this one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After careful consideration of all submitted proposals, we have selected Vendor A.
   - Following careful review of the evidence, the committee has reached the following conclusion.

1. **Functional Expression**: After all is said and done... (function: concluding/summarizing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces final conclusion after considering all factors; idiomatic phrase meaning "ultimately" or "in the end"
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (sets up conclusion rather than stating it immediately)
   
   **Synonyms**: In the end..., Ultimately..., When all is considered..., At the end of the day...
   
   **Antonyms**: Initially..., At first..., Before considering...
   
   **When to Use**: Summarizing discussions; drawing conclusions; emphasizing what matters most; reflective contexts; endings of presentations or arguments
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for very formal academic or legal writing; can be overused cliché; avoid in openings (it signals closure)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - When all is said and done (variation with "when")
   - At the end of the day (similar idiom, more British)
   - In the final analysis (more formal alternative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Idiomatic discourse marker signaling transition to conclusion; implies consideration of multiple perspectives; suggests cutting through complexity to core truth
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're right. / True. (agreement with conclusion)
   - I see it differently. (disagreement with conclusion)
   - That's what really matters. (affirming the summary)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After all is said and done, what matters most is that everyone's safe.
   - When all is said and done, we did our best—that's all we can do.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the final analysis, the project achieved its primary objectives despite several challenges.
   - Ultimately, the evidence supports the proposed hypothesis.

   - *Directness*: indirect (avoids specific timeline)

   - There'll be a gap/delay after... (more explicit about interruption)

1. **Functional Expression**: After what happened... (function: referencing shared knowledge)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Refers to known event without explicit description; assumes shared context and knowledge
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral

   **When to Use**: Among people with shared context; when event is sensitive and euphemism is appropriate; creating cohesion through reference; when explicit mention would be awkward

   **Common Variations**: 
   - After what happened last time... (specifies timing)

1. **Functional Expression**: Look after... (function: requesting attention/care for timing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request to check, monitor, or handle something following specific point; assigns responsibility with timing
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (imperative form, though "look after" softens it)
   
   **Synonyms**: Check on... after, Monitor... following, Handle... after, Take care of... after
   
   **Antonyms**: Ignore..., Neglect..., Leave alone...
   
   **When to Use**: Delegating tasks; requesting ongoing attention; care-taking contexts; when monitoring or maintenance needed after event
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too casual for very formal delegation; "look after" in sense of "care for" may not always fit (use "monitor," "check," "handle" instead)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you look after... after (adding "could you" makes request more polite)
   - Make sure to look after... after (emphasizes importance)
   - Keep an eye on... after (similar meaning, slightly more casual)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Look after" softens imperative with care-focused language; implies ongoing responsibility not just one-time action; can be used for both people and things
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Will do. / Sure thing. (acceptance)
   - For how long? (clarifying scope)
   - Can someone else do it? (declining/deflecting)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you look after the dog after we leave for vacation?
   - Look after the store after I head out, okay?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please monitor the system performance following the update.
   - Kindly oversee the transition process after the changeover is complete.
