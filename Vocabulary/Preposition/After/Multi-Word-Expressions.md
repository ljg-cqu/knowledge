1. **Expression**: after all (type: discourse marker/idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Despite what was thought or expected; used to introduce a fact that should be considered
   - Considering everything; when everything is taken into account
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: considering everything, all things considered, when you think about it, in the end, ultimately
   
   **Antonyms**: despite everything, even so, regardless, nevertheless
   
   **When to Use**: Justifying decisions or opinions; reminding of important facts; persuasive contexts; emphasizing what should have been obvious; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound patronizing if overused; avoid when stating genuinely new information; may imply criticism
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - After all, [reason/fact]
   - [Statement], after all
   - After all that/this
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: "After" (following consideration of) + "all" (everything) → idiomatic meaning of "considering everything" or "despite expectations"
   - Historical development from literal "after considering all factors" to discourse marker
   
   **Root Analysis**: after (Old English "æfter") + all (Old English "eall")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You should cut her some slack—after all, it's her first week.
   - I decided to go after all, even though I was tired.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal merits serious consideration. After all, it addresses a critical gap in current policy.
   - This investment, after all, represents the organization's largest commitment to date.

1. **Expression**: after hours (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - After the normal working or opening times; outside regular business hours
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: after closing time, outside business hours, off hours, after work
   
   **Antonyms**: during business hours, during working hours, in office hours
   
   **When to Use**: Business contexts; discussing availability, access, or activities outside normal schedules; workplace communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context must make "hours" reference clear; specify type of hours if ambiguous
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - after-hours (adjective form, hyphenated)
   - after business hours
   - after office hours
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocation*: "After" naturally partners with "hours" in business contexts; "hours" specifically means "business/operating hours" in this fixed expression
   - Conventional usage in business and institutional contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: after + hours (business operating times)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Want to grab drinks after hours?
   - I'll swing by after hours when the office is closed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - For urgent matters after hours, please contact the emergency line.
   - After-hours access requires special authorization and badge credentials.

1. **Expression**: day after day (type: binomial/intensifying phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Continuously for many days; repeatedly over an extended period; every single day
   - Emphasizes repetition and sometimes monotony or persistence
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: daily, every day, continuously, constantly, repeatedly, relentlessly
   
   **Antonyms**: occasionally, sporadically, once in a while, intermittently
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing persistence, endurance, or monotonous repetition; both positive (dedication) and negative (tedium) contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When variation or irregularity exists; can imply complaint or weariness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - week after week
   - month after month
   - year after year
   - hour after hour
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocation*: Repetitive structure emphasizes relentless continuation; "after" connects identical time units to stress unbroken sequence
   - Pattern extends to other time units with same emphatic function
   
   **Root Analysis**: day + after + day (repetition for emphasis)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Day after day, I'm stuck in this same boring routine.
   - She practiced day after day until she finally nailed it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Day after day, researchers meticulously recorded observations without significant variation.
   - The organization faced mounting pressure day after day as the crisis intensified.

1. **Expression**: one after another / one after the other (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In succession; sequentially without pause or gap; continuously in series
   - Emphasizes uninterrupted sequence or rapid succession
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: successively, consecutively, in succession, in sequence, back-to-back
   
   **Antonyms**: simultaneously, all at once, sporadically, intermittently
   
   **When to Use**: Describing sequential events; emphasizing continuous flow or overwhelming series; narrative and descriptive contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When events are not truly sequential or when simultaneous
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - one right after another (emphasizes immediacy)
   - one thing after another (often implies problems)
   - one by one (similar but emphasizes individual attention)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrase*: "One after another/the other" creates rhythm emphasizing sequence; highly conventional expression for succession
   - Grammatically fixed with specific article choices
   
   **Root Analysis**: one + after + another/the other
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Problems just kept coming, one after another!
   - We binge-watched the episodes one after the other all weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Witnesses testified one after another, each corroborating the previous account.
   - The company announced layoffs in three divisions, one after the other, over six months.

1. **Expression**: after the fact (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - After something has happened; retrospectively; too late to prevent or change
   - Often implies missed opportunity or hindsight
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: retrospectively, in hindsight, belatedly, too late, after the event
   
   **Antonyms**: beforehand, in advance, proactively, ahead of time, preventively
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing missed opportunities; hindsight analysis; legal contexts (accessory after the fact); regret or learning from experience
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When timing was appropriate; avoid when assigning blame unfairly
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - after the fact (most common)
   - long after the fact
   - only after the fact
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: "Fact" represents "the event that happened"; "after the fact" = after the event occurred; legal terminology origin
   - Strong fixedness—cannot substitute "fact" with synonyms
   
   **Root Analysis**: after + the + fact (Latin "factum" = thing done, deed)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's easy to criticize after the fact when you weren't even there!
   - We realized after the fact that we should have brought umbrellas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regulatory compliance audits conducted after the fact revealed systemic deficiencies.
   - The board's intervention, occurring after the fact, could not prevent the reputational damage.

1. **Expression**: look after (type: phrasal verb)

   - Take care of; be responsible for the well-being of someone or something
   - Monitor or maintain something
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: take care of, care for, watch over, tend to, mind
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, abandon, ignore, disregard

   **Common Variations**: 
   - look after oneself (be self-sufficient)
   - be looked after (passive construction)
   - looking after (progressive form)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Look" + "after" creates meaning beyond component parts; "after" adds sense of ongoing care and responsibility
   - Cannot be literal "looking in the direction behind something"
   
   **Root Analysis**: look + after (phrasal verb with idiomatic meaning)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you look after my dog while I'm on vacation?
   - She's perfectly capable of looking after herself!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The custodian is responsible for looking after the facility during evening hours.
   - Appointed trustees will look after the estate until the heir reaches majority.

1. **Expression**: take after (type: phrasal verb)

   - Inherit characteristics from family member
   - *Fixedness*: strong

   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for very formal contexts; primarily about family relationships (though can extend to mentorship)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - takes after (present tense)
   - took after (past tense, less common)
   - really take after (intensifier)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Take" + "after" = inherit qualities from someone you come after/follow; metaphorical extension of following/succession
   - Fixed partnership—cannot substitute other prepositions
   
   **Root Analysis**: take + after (idiomatic phrasal verb)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You really take after your mom—you have the same laugh!
   - He takes after his dad; they're both super stubborn.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The child appears to take after her mother in both temperament and aptitude.
   - In his leadership style, he clearly takes after his mentor.

   **When to Use**: Discussing pursuit of goals, people, or opportunities; competitive contexts; confrontational situations; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound aggressive in some contexts; clarify meaning if ambiguous
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - going after (progressive form)
   - went after (past tense)
   - go after someone/something
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Go" + "after" creates directional meaning of pursuit; "after" adds goal-oriented trajectory
   - Multiple related meanings all involving pursuit or targeting
   
   **Root Analysis**: go + after (directional phrasal verb)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If you want that promotion, you gotta go after it!
   - She went after him in the meeting—it was brutal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company is strategically going after the emerging markets segment.
   - Prosecutors are going after the defendants on multiple charges.
