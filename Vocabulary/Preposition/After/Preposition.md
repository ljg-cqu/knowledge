1. **Preposition**: after (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Following in time; later than; subsequent to a specified time or event
   - *(Spatial)* Behind in place or position; following in a physical sequence
   - *(Abstract)* In pursuit or quest of; seeking; concerning or about
   - *(Abstract)* In imitation of the style or manner of; in honor of
   
   **Synonyms**: following (temporal/spatial), subsequent to (formal temporal), behind (spatial), in pursuit of (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: before (temporal), prior to (temporal), ahead of (spatial), preceding
   
   **When to Use**: Establishing temporal relationships (most common); indicating spatial position; showing pursuit or imitation; all registers from casual to formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "after" (preposition/conjunction) with "afterward" (adverb); avoid ambiguity between meanings; be clear about temporal vs. spatial sense
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: after [time expression], after [event noun], after [person]
   - **Verb + prep**: come after, go after, look after, take after, ask after
   - **Adjective + prep**: named after, modeled after
   - **Noun + prep**: the day after, the year after, time after time
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "æfter" (following in place or time)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "æfter" (behind, following, later) → Proto-Germanic "*afterō" (behind, later) → Indo-European "*apo-" (off, away)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Want to hang out after work?
   - She ran after the bus but missed it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After careful consideration, the committee approved the proposal.
   - The policy was implemented after extensive consultation with stakeholders.

1. **Preposition**: following (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Coming after or as a result of; subsequent to
   - Occurring after a particular time or event
   
   **Synonyms**: after, subsequent to, in the wake of, as a result of
   
   **Antonyms**: before, preceding, prior to, ahead of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal alternative to "after"; business writing; academic contexts; emphasizes consequence or sequence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly formal in casual speech; "after" is usually preferred in everyday language
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: following [noun phrase], following [event]
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (following is not used in phrasal verbs as "after" is)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: in the days following, in the weeks following
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow + -ing (present participle functioning as preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "folgian" (to follow, accompany, pursue) + "-ing" → developed prepositional use in Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the meeting, let's discuss this.
   - Right after graduation, I started my new job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Following the merger, organizational restructuring was initiated.
   - Following the committee's recommendation, new protocols were established.

1. **Preposition**: subsequent to (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Following in time; after; occurring later than
   - Coming after in order or succession
   
   **Synonyms**: after, following, in the aftermath of, later than
   
   **Antonyms**: prior to, before, preceding, earlier than
   
   **When to Use**: Very formal writing; legal documents; academic papers; when extremely formal tone is required
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for most contexts; "after" or "following" usually preferred; can sound pretentious
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: subsequent to [noun phrase], subsequent to [event]
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A (used as preposition only)
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under, after) + sequ (follow) + -ent + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "subsequens" (following after) → "sub-" (close after) + "sequi" (to follow) + prepositional "to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the meeting, we made changes. (prefer simpler language)
   - Following that incident, things changed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Subsequent to the regulatory changes, compliance audits were mandated.
   - Subsequent to the board's decision, implementation procedures were developed.

1. **Preposition**: upon (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Immediately after; as soon as; at the time of
   - *(Spatial)* On; on top of (archaic or formal)
   - *(Abstract)* As a result of; based on
   
   **Synonyms**: on (spatial), after (temporal), as soon as (temporal), immediately following
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, preceding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing; legal documents; emphasizing immediacy; with gerunds and nouns (not finite clauses)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; can sound archaic; use "on" or "after" in most contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: upon [gerund], upon [noun], upon [possessive + noun]
   - **Verb + prep**: act upon, depend upon, rely upon, embark upon
   - **Adjective + prep**: based upon, contingent upon, dependent upon
   - **Noun + prep**: impact upon, effect upon, influence upon
   
   **Root Analysis**: up + on → combined form
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "uppan" (up on, upon) → "up" + "on" → developed formal temporal sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Right when we arrived, it started raining.
   - As soon as I heard, I called you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Upon completion of the review, findings will be disseminated.
   - Upon receiving notification, immediate action is required.

1. **Preposition**: beyond (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* At or to the further side of; past in space
   - *(Temporal)* Later than; after a specified time
   - *(Abstract)* Outside the scope, range, or understanding of; exceeding
   
   **Synonyms**: past (spatial/temporal), after (temporal), outside (abstract), exceeding (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, within, inside, short of
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating limits exceeded; spatial position past something; time after specified point; metaphorical extension
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "past" or "after" is clearer; "beyond" often implies exceeding limits or boundaries
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: beyond [time], beyond [place], beyond [limit/capacity]
   - **Verb + prep**: go beyond, see beyond, look beyond, extend beyond
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: beyond belief, beyond repair, beyond question
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + yond (yon, yonder = that/there)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "begeondan" (on the other side of) → "be-" (by) + "geondan" (yonder, beyond)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The store is just beyond the traffic light.
   - That's way beyond my skill level!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Extensions beyond the specified deadline require explicit approval.
   - The implications extend beyond the immediate stakeholder group.

1. **Preposition**: past (type: simple)

   - *(Temporal)* Beyond in time; after; later than
   - Going beyond the limits or scope of
   
   **Synonyms**: beyond (spatial/temporal), after (temporal), by (spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, up to, until
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating movement beyond a point; time after specified hour; exceeding age or stage; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "past" (preposition/noun/adjective) with "passed" (verb); context must clarify meaning
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: past [time], past [place], past [age/stage]
   - **Verb + prep**: go past, walk past, drive past, get past

   - **Noun + prep**: half past [hour], quarter past

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Drive past the gas station and turn left.
   - It's already past midnight—we should go!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vehicle proceeded past the checkpoint without stopping.

1. **Preposition**: since (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* From a particular time in the past until now; continuously from a past point
   - *(Causal)* For the reason that; because
   
   **Synonyms**: from (temporal), after (temporal), because (causal), as (causal)
   
   **Antonyms**: until, before (temporal); although, despite (causal)
   
   **When to Use**: Connecting past event to present; establishing starting point of ongoing situation; requires perfect tenses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse temporal and causal meanings; temporal "since" requires specific past reference point
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: since [specific time], since [date], since [event]
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (since functions as conjunction more than in phrasal verbs)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: since childhood, since then, since last year
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English "sithence" → Old English "siþþan"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "siþþan" (afterward, from the time that) → Middle English "sithen" → contracted to "since"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been here since noon.
   - Since you're going anyway, can you grab me some coffee?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Since the implementation of the new policy, compliance has improved.
   - The organization has expanded significantly since its founding in 1995.

1. **Preposition**: throughout (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In every part of; all through a place
   - *(Temporal)* From beginning to end of a period; during the entire time
   
   **Synonyms**: all through, during, across, all over
   
   **Antonyms**: at the beginning of, at the end of, partially
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing completeness across space or time; showing continuous presence; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When action is not truly continuous or comprehensive
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: throughout [time period], throughout [place], throughout [process]
   - **Verb + prep**: spread throughout, continue throughout, remain throughout
   - **Adjective + prep**: prevalent throughout, consistent throughout
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: through + out → compound form
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "thorough-out" → "through" (Old English "þurh") + "out" → combined to emphasize complete extent
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It rained throughout the entire game.
   - There's WiFi throughout the whole building.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The principle was applied consistently throughout the investigation.
   - Data were collected throughout the six-month observation period.

1. **Preposition**: over (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Above; higher than; covering
   - *(Temporal)* During; through the duration of; more than (a period)
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; about; beyond; recovered from
   
   **Synonyms**: above (spatial), during (temporal), about (abstract), more than
   
   **Antonyms**: under, below, beneath, less than
   
   **When to Use**: Spatial relationships; duration; recovery or completion; superiority; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Multiple meanings require clear context; can be ambiguous
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: over [time period], over [surface], over [limit]
   - **Verb + prep**: get over, go over, take over, turn over, think over
   - **Adjective + prep**: over and done with
   - **Noun + prep**: over the years, over time, over the course of
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "ofer" (above, beyond)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "ofer" (above, beyond, across) → Proto-Germanic "*uberi" → Indo-European "*uper" (over, above)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's talk about this over coffee.
   - I'm finally over that breakup.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Over the past decade, significant advances have been achieved.

1. **Preposition**: amid / amidst (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial/Abstract)* Surrounded by; in the middle of; among
   - *(Temporal/Abstract)* During; in the course of; while experiencing
   
   **Synonyms**: among, in the middle of, surrounded by, during
   
   **Antonyms**: outside of, apart from, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing; indicating occurrence during complex circumstances; literary style; both spatial and temporal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; "during" or "while" often simpler
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: amid [circumstances], amid [chaos/confusion], amid [events]
   - **Verb + prep**: occur amid, happen amid, emerge amid
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "on middan" (in the middle)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "on middan" (in the middle) → Middle English "amidde" → "amid" (also "amidst" with added -st)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - During all the chaos, we managed to stay calm.
   - In the middle of the argument, she just left.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The decision was announced amid growing public concern.

1. **Preposition**: in the wake of (type: phrasal preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal/Causal)* Following and as a consequence of; in the aftermath of
   - After and typically as a result of a significant event
   
   **Synonyms**: following, after, subsequent to, in the aftermath of, as a result of
   
   **Antonyms**: prior to, before, leading up to, in anticipation of
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing consequences of significant events; formal writing; journalistic style; implies causation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For routine or neutral events; implies significance and often negative connotation
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in the wake of [significant event], in the wake of [disaster/crisis]
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (used as phrasal preposition)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + the + wake (track left by moving ship) + of
   
   **Etymology**: "wake" (Old English "wacu" - track of ship) → metaphorically "consequences following event" → "in the wake of" (1800s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the scandal, a lot of people quit.
   - Following the accident, they changed the rules.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the wake of the financial crisis, regulatory oversight was substantially enhanced.
   - In the wake of the data breach, cybersecurity protocols were comprehensively revised.
