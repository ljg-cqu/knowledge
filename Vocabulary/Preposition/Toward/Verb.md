# Verb: Toward

1. **Verb**: work toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive phrasal verb) To make efforts or take actions directed at achieving a particular goal or outcome over time; to strive or labor in the direction of accomplishing something
   
   **Synonyms**: strive for, aim for, pursue, work on, labor toward
   
   **Antonyms**: work against, abandon, give up on, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing ongoing efforts directed at goals in professional, personal, educational, or organizational contexts; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When goal has been achieved (use "achieved" or "accomplished"); when effort is minimal or not genuine
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - work toward a goal
   - work toward achieving
   - work toward improvement
   - work actively toward
   - continue working toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: work (Old English "weorc" - labor, deed) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wyrcan" (to work, perform) → "work" combined with "toward" to emphasize directional effort; phrasal verb construction common in English for expressing purposeful action
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're working toward getting everything done by Friday.
   - She's been working toward this promotion for like two years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is working systematically toward establishing comprehensive guidelines.
   - Member states continue working toward consensus on the framework agreement.

2. **Verb**: move toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive/Transitive phrasal verb) To progress or shift in the direction of a particular state, position, or goal; to change position spatially or metaphorically in a specific direction
   
   **Synonyms**: progress toward, advance toward, shift toward, head toward, proceed toward
   
   **Antonyms**: move away from, retreat from, regress from, withdraw from
   
   **When to Use**: Use for describing physical movement, strategic transitions, or progress toward goals; appropriate for spatial, abstract, and metaphorical movement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When arrival or completion is meant (use "reach" or "arrive at"); when movement lacks direction
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move toward agreement
   - move steadily toward
   - move closer toward
   - move quickly toward
   - move collectively toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: move (Latin "movere" - to move) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "movere" (to move, set in motion) → Old French "movoir" → English "move" + "toward" creates directional phrasal verb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's move toward wrapping this up so we can go home.
   - The company's moving toward more flexible work schedules.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The economy is moving toward sustainable recovery following the recession.
   - Negotiations are moving steadily toward resolution.

3. **Verb**: shift toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive/Transitive phrasal verb) To change direction, focus, or emphasis in favor of something different; to transition from one state or approach to another
   
   **Synonyms**: transition to, turn toward, pivot toward, swing toward, change to
   
   **Antonyms**: shift away from, move away from, revert from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing changes in direction, strategy, focus, or trends; appropriate for organizational, political, economic, and social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing stable state without change; implies active change in progress
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - shift toward sustainability
   - shift focus toward
   - shift gradually toward
   - shift dramatically toward
   - shift resources toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: shift (Old English "sciftan" - to arrange, divide) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "sciftan" (to arrange, change) → Middle English "shiften" → Modern English "shift" + "toward" emphasizes directional change
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been shifting toward eating healthier lately.
   - Can we shift toward talking about something more interesting?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consumer preferences are shifting toward sustainable products and services.
   - The regulatory framework is shifting toward greater environmental protection.

4. **Verb**: lean toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To have a slight preference, inclination, or tendency in favor of something; to incline in the direction of a particular option or choice
   
   **Synonyms**: incline toward, favor, tend toward, prefer, be disposed toward
   
   **Antonyms**: lean away from, oppose, disfavor, be against
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing tentative preferences or inclinations in decision-making; appropriate when choice isn't final or preference is slight
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When preference is strong or decision is firm (use "choose" or "strongly prefer"); "lean toward" implies openness to changing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lean toward option A
   - lean heavily toward
   - lean slightly toward
   - tend to lean toward
   - lean more toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: lean (Old English "hlinian" - to recline) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "hlinian" (to recline, bend) → "lean" (to incline) + "toward" creates metaphorical phrasal verb for preference
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm leaning toward just staying home tonight.
   - We're all kind of leaning toward the cheaper option.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee appears to be leaning toward accepting the revised proposal.
   - Evidence suggests the findings lean toward supporting the alternative hypothesis.

5. **Verb**: contribute toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive/Transitive phrasal verb) To give money, effort, ideas, or resources that help achieve or move in the direction of a goal; to play a part in causing or bringing about something
   
   **Synonyms**: contribute to, help toward, aid in, support, assist with
   
   **Antonyms**: detract from, hinder, impede, undermine
   
   **When to Use**: Use when acknowledging partial causation or supportive role in achieving outcomes; appropriate for collaborative efforts and multiple contributing factors
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing sole causation (use "cause" or "achieve"); both "contribute toward" and "contribute to" are correct with subtle differences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contribute toward goals
   - contribute significantly toward
   - contribute positively toward
   - contribute toward achieving
   - contribute toward costs
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + tribute (Latin "tribuere" - to assign) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "contribuere" (to bring together, add to) → English "contribute" + "toward" emphasizes directional contribution
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone chipping in would contribute toward getting the gift.
   - Your feedback really contributes toward making things better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Multiple factors contribute significantly toward observed improvements in outcomes.
   - These initiatives contribute materially toward organizational strategic objectives.

6. **Verb**: progress toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To move forward or advance in the direction of achieving goals or reaching destinations; to develop or improve in a specific direction
   
   **Synonyms**: advance toward, proceed toward, move toward, develop toward
   
   **Antonyms**: regress from, retreat from, decline from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing measurable forward movement or development toward objectives; appropriate in project management and goal-tracking
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no actual advancement occurs; when goal is reached (use "achieved" or "reached")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - progress steadily toward
   - progress toward completion
   - progress toward goals
   - progress rapidly toward
   - continue progressing toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + gress (Latin "gradi" - to step) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "progredi" (to go forward) → "progress" + "toward" creates phrasal verb emphasizing directional advancement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're progressing toward getting this done, slowly but surely.
   - How are you progressing toward your fitness goals?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project is progressing toward completion according to established timelines.
   - Research is progressing systematically toward definitive conclusions.

7. **Verb**: strive toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To make great efforts or struggle in the direction of achieving something; to endeavor earnestly toward accomplishing goals
   
   **Synonyms**: work toward, aim for, struggle toward, endeavor toward, fight for
   
   **Antonyms**: give up on, abandon, cease striving for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing determined effort and struggle in pursuit of goals; implies difficulty or challenge in achievement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal or literary; in casual contexts "work toward" or "try to achieve" may be more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strive toward excellence
   - strive toward perfection
   - strive toward achieving
   - strive continually toward
   - strive collectively toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: strive (Old French "estriver" - to quarrel, compete) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "estriver" (to quarrel, strive) → Middle English "striven" → "strive" + "toward" emphasizes directional determined effort
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're all striving toward the same goal here.
   - Keep striving toward being your best self.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization strives continually toward operational excellence.
   - Stakeholders must strive collectively toward achieving sustainable outcomes.

8. **Verb**: head toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To move or proceed in the direction of a destination; to be going or directed toward something
   - *(Sense 2)* To develop or trend in a particular direction (often negative)
   
   **Synonyms**: move toward, go toward, proceed toward, make for, be bound for
   
   **Antonyms**: head away from, retreat from, leave from
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical movement toward destinations or metaphorical progression toward states; appropriate for spatial and abstract direction
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More casual than "proceed toward"; can imply negative outcomes when used metaphorically ("heading toward disaster")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - head toward the exit
   - head toward disaster
   - head directly toward
   - head slowly toward
   - head straight toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: head (noun used as verb) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: "Head" (Old English "hēafod" - top, chief part) used as verb meaning "to go in direction of" + "toward"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should probably head toward the car before it gets dark.
   - Things are heading toward a breaking point if we don't fix this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Indicators suggest the economy is heading toward recovery.
   - Current trends are heading toward unsustainable resource depletion.

9. **Verb**: trend toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To show a general tendency or pattern of movement in a particular direction; to exhibit a directional pattern over time
   
   **Synonyms**: tend toward, move toward, shift toward, lean toward, gravitate toward
   
   **Antonyms**: trend away from, diverge from, move away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing observable patterns, tendencies, or gradual directional changes based on data or evidence; appropriate in analytical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When change is complete rather than ongoing; requires supporting evidence or data; "trend" as verb more formal than as noun
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - trend toward automation
   - trend increasingly toward
   - trend toward extremes
   - trend gradually toward
   - currently trending toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: trend (Old English "trendan" - to turn, roll) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "trendan" (to turn, revolve) → modern meaning of directional tendency + "toward" specifies direction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everything's trending toward being more automated these days.
   - Public opinion is trending toward supporting the changes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Economic indicators are trending toward sustained growth in the sector.
   - Research findings trend toward confirming the initial hypothesis.

10. **Verb**: push toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive phrasal verb) To make strong, forceful efforts to achieve something; to actively promote or drive movement in the direction of goals
   
   **Synonyms**: drive toward, press toward, force toward, propel toward, campaign for
   
   **Antonyms**: resist, oppose, hold back from, pull away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing active, determined, sometimes aggressive effort; appropriate for vigorous campaigns or forceful advancement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When effort is gentle or tentative; "push" implies force that may sound aggressive or inappropriate in some contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - push hard toward
   - push aggressively toward
   - push forward toward
   - push relentlessly toward
   - push toward change
   
   **Root Analysis**: push (Middle English "possen" from Old French) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "poulser" → Middle English "possen" → "push" (to press forward) + "toward" emphasizes directional force
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're really pushing toward getting this done by the deadline.
   - Let's push toward finishing the main features first.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The administration is pushing aggressively toward comprehensive policy reform.
   - Advocacy groups continue pushing toward legislative change.

11. **Verb**: orient toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive phrasal verb) To direct, align, or position something toward a particular goal, purpose, or audience; to design or adjust focus in a specific direction
   
   **Synonyms**: direct toward, aim at, focus on, target to, align with
   
   **Antonyms**: orient away from, misdirect, misalign
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing intentional alignment or directional positioning; appropriate in strategic planning, design, and targeting contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal or technical; more common as past participle "oriented toward" than as active verb
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - orient toward customers
   - orient toward goals
   - orient services toward
   - orient strategy toward
   - orient efforts toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: orient (Latin "oriens" - rising, east) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "oriens" (east, rising sun) → French "orienter" (to face east) → English "orient" (to align) + "toward"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you orient your presentation more toward beginners?
   - We need to orient our efforts toward what actually matters.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization should orient its strategy toward emerging market opportunities.
   - Programs must be oriented toward addressing stakeholder needs effectively.

12. **Verb**: gesture toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To make a movement or signal indicating direction toward something; to point or motion in the direction of something
   - *(Sense 2)* To make a symbolic action that suggests or indicates something (metaphorical)
   
   **Synonyms**: motion toward, point toward, indicate, signal toward, suggest
   
   **Antonyms**: gesture away from, dismiss, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Use literally for physical gestures or metaphorically for symbolic actions that indicate without fully addressing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing verbal statements (use "mention" or "say"); when action is substantial rather than merely symbolic
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - gesture vaguely toward
   - gesture toward the door
   - gesture broadly toward
   - merely gesture toward
   - gesture toward solutions
   
   **Root Analysis**: gesture (Latin "gestura" - bearing) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "gestura" (manner of action, bearing) → "gesture" (expressive movement) + "toward" indicates direction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He just gestured toward the exit without saying anything.
   - The changes only gesture toward fixing the real problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy gestures toward reform without implementing substantive changes.
   - The proposal gestures toward addressing stakeholder concerns but lacks specifics.

13. **Verb**: gravitate toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To be drawn or attracted toward something; to tend to move in the direction of or show preference for something naturally
   
   **Synonyms**: be drawn to, be attracted to, tend toward, incline toward, lean toward
   
   **Antonyms**: gravitate away from, avoid, shun, be repelled by
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing natural attraction or tendency toward things, people, or ideas; implies automatic or instinctive movement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies natural, often unconscious tendency; not appropriate for deliberate, conscious choices
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - naturally gravitate toward
   - tend to gravitate toward
   - gravitate toward similar
   - gravitate toward comfort
   - gravitate toward familiar
   
   **Root Analysis**: gravit- (Latin "gravis" - heavy) + -ate + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "gravitas" (weight) → "gravitate" (to be attracted by gravity) → metaphorical use for attraction + "toward"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - People tend to gravitate toward others who are like them.
   - I always gravitate toward the chocolate options on the menu.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consumers naturally gravitate toward brands that align with their values.
   - Students gravitate toward educational approaches that match their learning styles.

14. **Verb**: look toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To direct one's gaze or attention toward something; to face in a particular direction
   - *(Sense 2)* To consider, anticipate, or plan for future events or goals
   
   **Synonyms**: face toward (spatial), consider, anticipate (future), plan for, direct attention to
   
   **Antonyms**: look away from, ignore, disregard
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical direction of sight or metaphorical future planning; appropriate in spatial and forward-thinking contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can mean literal looking or figurative planning; context must clarify meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look toward the future
   - look toward solutions
   - look toward expansion
   - look toward alternatives
   - look toward improvement
   
   **Root Analysis**: look (Old English "lōcian" - to see) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "lōcian" (to see, behold) → "look" + "toward" creates phrasal verb for directional sight or consideration
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone's looking toward the weekend already.
   - We should look toward other options if this doesn't work out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Strategic planning requires looking toward long-term sustainability.
   - Stakeholders are looking toward the next phase of implementation.

15. **Verb**: build toward
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive phrasal verb) To develop or progress gradually in the direction of a climax, goal, or outcome; to accumulate or increase toward something
   
   **Synonyms**: work toward, develop toward, progress toward, lead up to, culminate in
   
   **Antonyms**: decline toward, diminish toward, wind down from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing gradual development or accumulation toward outcomes; often used for narrative, musical, or process development
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies gradual, sustained development; not appropriate for sudden or immediate achievements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - build gradually toward
   - build steadily toward
   - build toward a climax
   - build toward conclusion
   - build momentum toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: build (Old English "byldan" - to construct) + toward
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "byldan" (to construct) → "build" (to develop, construct) + "toward" emphasizes directional development
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The tension's really building toward something big happening.
   - We're building toward launching the product next month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The narrative builds systematically toward its dramatic conclusion.
   - Evidence is building toward support for the theoretical framework.
