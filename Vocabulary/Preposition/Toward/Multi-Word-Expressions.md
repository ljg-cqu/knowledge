# Multi-Word Expressions: Toward

1. **Expression**: work toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make efforts or take actions directed at achieving a particular goal or outcome over time
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: strive for, aim for, pursue, work on achieving, move toward achieving
   
   **Antonyms**: work against, abandon, give up on, move away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing ongoing efforts toward goals in professional, personal, or organizational contexts; universally appropriate across registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When goal has been achieved (use "achieved" or "accomplished"); when effort is minimal or insincere
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - working toward (progressive form)
   - worked toward (past form)
   - work actively toward (intensified)
   - continue working toward (emphasizes persistence)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Work" naturally combines with "toward" because work represents effort/action and "toward" indicates directional purpose; the preposition "toward" (not "to") emphasizes ongoing process rather than completion
   
   **Root Analysis**: work (Old English "weorc") + toward (Old English "tōweard" - in the direction of)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're working toward getting the house ready before they visit.
   - I've been working toward this promotion for two years now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization is working toward achieving carbon neutrality by 2030.
   - Member states continue working toward consensus on the framework agreement.

2. **Expression**: step toward (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An action or measure that represents progress or movement in the direction of achieving a goal
   - *Fixedness*: strong (common and natural partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: move toward, advance toward, progress toward, measure for, action toward
   
   **Antonyms**: step backward, step away from, retreat from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing incremental progress or positive actions contributing to larger goals; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing complete achievement (use "achievement of"); when action is insignificant or misleading
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - steps toward (plural for multiple actions)
   - first step toward (indicating beginning)
   - significant step toward (emphasizing importance)
   - small step toward (acknowledging limited scope)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Step" metaphorically represents incremental movement, naturally pairing with "toward" to indicate directional progress; emphasizes gradual rather than immediate achievement
   
   **Root Analysis**: step (Old English "stæpe") + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Getting that interview was a big step toward landing the job.
   - It's just a small step toward fixing the problem, but it helps.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This legislation represents a significant step toward comprehensive healthcare reform.
   - The agreement constitutes an important step toward regional stability.

3. **Expression**: move toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To progress or shift in the direction of a particular state, position, or goal
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very common and natural)
   
   **Synonyms**: progress toward, shift toward, advance toward, head toward, transition toward
   
   **Antonyms**: move away from, retreat from, regress from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing transitions, changes in direction, or progress toward goals; appropriate for physical movement, abstract change, or strategic shifts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When arrival or completion is meant (use "reach" or "achieve"); when movement is random rather than directional
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - moving toward (progressive emphasizing ongoing action)
   - moved toward (past, completed shift)
   - move steadily toward (emphasizes consistent progress)
   - move quickly toward (emphasizes speed)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Move" inherently requires directional complement; "toward" provides that direction without implying completion (unlike "to"), making it ideal for expressing progressive change
   
   **Root Analysis**: move (Latin "movere" - to move) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The company is moving toward more flexible work arrangements.
   - Let's move toward wrapping this up so we can go home.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The economy is moving toward sustainable recovery following the recession.
   - Policy is gradually moving toward greater environmental protection standards.

4. **Expression**: attitude toward (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - One's feelings, opinions, or general disposition regarding a particular person, thing, or situation
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: stance on, position on, view of, perspective on, feeling about
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (descriptive phrase without direct opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing perspectives, dispositions, or feelings about topics; appropriate in psychological, social, and evaluative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing action rather than attitude; note that "attitude toward" is more common than "attitude about" in formal English
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - attitudes toward (plural for multiple perspectives or topics)
   - positive/negative attitude toward
   - changing attitude toward
   - general attitude toward
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Attitude" represents mental stance or disposition that is inherently directional—it must be "toward" something; "toward" indicates the target of the attitude
   
   **Root Analysis**: attitude (Latin "aptitudo" - fitness, posture) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's everyone's attitude toward working overtime this week?
   - His attitude toward the whole situation has really improved.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines public attitudes toward climate change mitigation strategies.
   - Employee attitudes toward organizational change significantly impact implementation success.

5. **Expression**: shift toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To change direction, focus, or emphasis in favor of something different
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly frequent partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: move toward, transition to, turn toward, pivot toward, swing toward
   
   **Antonyms**: shift away from, move away from, abandon
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing changes in direction, strategy, focus, or trends; appropriate for organizational, political, economic, and social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing stable conditions without change; when change is complete (use "shifted to" for completed transitions)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - shifting toward (progressive, ongoing change)
   - shifted toward (past, completed change)
   - gradual shift toward (emphasizes pace)
   - significant shift toward (emphasizes magnitude)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Shift" implies directional change that naturally requires a direction indicator; "toward" provides direction while maintaining sense of ongoing transition
   
   **Root Analysis**: shift (Old English "sciftan" - to arrange, divide) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been shifting toward eating healthier lately.
   - The conversation's shifting toward something more interesting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consumer preferences are shifting toward sustainable products.
   - Economic policy is shifting toward greater fiscal conservatism.

6. **Expression**: progress toward (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Forward movement or advancement in the direction of achieving a goal or reaching a destination
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: advancement toward, movement toward, development toward, strides toward
   
   **Antonyms**: regression from, setback in, movement away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing measurable advancement toward objectives; appropriate in project management, goal tracking, and performance contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no actual advancement has occurred; when goal is reached (use "achievement" or "accomplishment")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - make progress toward (verb form)
   - significant progress toward
   - slow progress toward
   - steady progress toward
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Progress" inherently requires a goal or direction; "toward" specifies that direction while maintaining that the goal hasn't been fully reached
   
   **Root Analysis**: progress (Latin "progressus" - a going forward) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're making real progress toward finishing the project.
   - Any progress toward solving this problem would be great.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative has demonstrated substantial progress toward stated objectives.
   - Research indicates significant progress toward developing effective treatments.

7. **Expression**: with an eye toward (type: idiomatic prepositional phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With consideration for, attention to, or intention regarding a future goal or outcome
   - Literal meaning vs. Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: Literal "eye" (organ of sight) becomes figurative "attention/consideration"; idiom means "considering future possibilities"
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiomatic expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: with a view to, considering, in anticipation of, with [goal] in mind, looking ahead to
   
   **Antonyms**: without regard to, ignoring, overlooking
   
   **When to Use**: Use when explaining rationale for actions based on future considerations; appropriate in strategic, planning, and forward-looking contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal or business-like; in casual contexts "thinking about" or "with [goal] in mind" may be more natural
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - with one eye toward (suggests partial attention)
   - keeping an eye toward (emphasizes ongoing attention)
   - with both eyes toward (emphasizes full focus)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Metaphor of vision/sight representing mental attention and foresight; "eye" has long been used metaphorically for attention and consideration in English; the idiom developed from literal "watching for" to figurative "planning for"
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + an + eye (Old English "ēage") + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're saving money with an eye toward maybe buying a bigger place.
   - I'm taking these classes with an eye toward changing careers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy was designed with an eye toward long-term fiscal sustainability.
   - Investments are made with an eye toward positioning for future market opportunities.

8. **Expression**: trend toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To show a general tendency or pattern of movement in a particular direction
   - *Fixedness*: medium (common in analytical contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: tend toward, move toward, shift toward, lean toward, gravitate toward
   
   **Antonyms**: trend away from, move away from, diverge from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing observable patterns, tendencies, or gradual directional changes; appropriate in data analysis, forecasting, and trend reporting
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When there's no supporting data or evidence; when change is complete rather than ongoing; "trend" as verb more common in formal analysis
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - trending toward (progressive form)
   - trended toward (past form)
   - trends toward (present, habitual)
   - clear trend toward (emphasizes certainty)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Trend" indicates directional tendency that requires specification; "toward" provides that direction while maintaining sense that movement is ongoing and not complete
   
   **Root Analysis**: trend (Old English "trendan" - to turn, roll) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everything seems to be trending toward more automation.
   - Public opinion is trending toward supporting the changes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Economic indicators are trending toward sustained growth.
   - Research findings trend toward confirming the initial hypothesis.

9. **Expression**: lean toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have a slight preference, inclination, or tendency in favor of something; to be somewhat inclined toward a particular option
   - *Fixedness*: strong (common idiomatic usage)
   
   **Synonyms**: incline toward, favor, prefer, be inclined toward, tend toward
   
   **Antonyms**: lean away from, favor against, be opposed to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when expressing tentative preferences or slight inclinations; appropriate in decision-making contexts where choice isn't final
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When preference is strong or decision is firm (use "choose" or "prefer strongly"); "lean toward" implies openness to changing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - leaning toward (progressive, current state)
   - leaned toward (past preference)
   - lean slightly/heavily toward (modifies degree)
   - I'm leaning toward (first person expression)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Physical metaphor of leaning (tilting body weight) represents mental inclination; "toward" naturally follows directional verb "lean" to indicate direction of preference
   
   **Root Analysis**: lean (Old English "hlinian" - to recline, rest) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm leaning toward the cheaper option, but I haven't decided yet.
   - We're all kind of leaning toward postponing the trip.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee appears to be leaning toward accepting the revised proposal.
   - Current evidence suggests the findings lean toward supporting the alternative hypothesis.

10. **Expression**: contribute toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To help achieve or play a part in making progress toward a goal; to give money, effort, or resources in support of something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: help toward, aid in, support, assist with, work toward
   
   **Antonyms**: detract from, hinder, work against, undermine
   
   **When to Use**: Use when acknowledging partial causation or supportive role in achieving outcomes; appropriate for collaborative efforts and multiple contributing factors
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing sole causation (use "cause" or "achieve"); note that both "contribute toward" and "contribute to" are acceptable, with subtle differences (toward emphasizes direction, to emphasizes result)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - contributing toward (progressive form)
   - contributed toward (past form)
   - significantly contribute toward
   - help contribute toward
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Contribute" inherently implies adding to something larger; "toward" specifies the goal or purpose of the contribution while maintaining that achievement requires multiple factors
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + tribute (Latin "tribuere" - to assign, pay) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone chipping in a few dollars would really contribute toward getting the gift.
   - Your feedback contributes toward making the product better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These factors significantly contribute toward observed improvements in outcomes.
   - The initiative contributes toward achieving organizational strategic objectives.

11. **Expression**: gesture toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make a symbolic action or movement that indicates, suggests, or points in the direction of something
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard physical and metaphorical usage)
   
   **Synonyms**: motion toward, indicate, point toward, suggest, signal toward
   
   **Antonyms**: gesture away from, dismiss, reject
   
   **When to Use**: Use literally for physical gestures or metaphorically for symbolic actions; appropriate for describing both concrete pointing and abstract indication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing verbal statement (use "mention" or "say"); when action is substantial rather than symbolic
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - gesturing toward (progressive form)
   - gestured toward (past form)
   - gesture vaguely toward
   - gesture broadly toward
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Gesture" represents directional movement or indication that requires a target; "toward" naturally complements the directional nature of gestures
   
   **Root Analysis**: gesture (Latin "gestura" - bearing, behavior) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He gestured toward the door, so I figured we should leave.
   - The changes are really just gestures toward addressing the problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal gestures toward comprehensive reform without providing specific mechanisms.
   - The policy represents a gesture toward stakeholder concerns regarding transparency.

12. **Expression**: oriented toward (type: adjectival phrase / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Designed for, directed at, or focused on a particular purpose, audience, or goal
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard descriptive phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: directed toward, aimed at, focused on, targeted to, designed for
   
   **Antonyms**: oriented away from, not designed for, unsuited for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing the intended purpose, target audience, or design philosophy of programs, products, or strategies
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual contexts, simpler alternatives like "for" or "aimed at" may be more natural
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - customer-oriented toward (compound construction)
   - goal-oriented toward
   - market-oriented toward
   - specifically oriented toward
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Oriented" derives from "orient" (to align, face a direction); it naturally takes "toward" to specify what something is aligned with or designed for
   
   **Root Analysis**: orient (Latin "oriens" - rising, east) + -ed + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This app is totally oriented toward beginners who don't know tech.
   - The whole program is oriented toward helping people find jobs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The curriculum is strategically oriented toward developing critical thinking competencies.
   - Services are specifically oriented toward addressing needs of underserved populations.

13. **Expression**: push toward (type: phrasal verb / collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make strong, forceful efforts to achieve something; to actively promote or drive movement toward a goal
   - *Fixedness*: strong (common in dynamic contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: drive toward, press toward, work hard toward, strive for, campaign for
   
   **Antonyms**: resist, oppose, hold back from, pull away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing active, determined effort or advocacy; appropriate when describing vigorous campaigns, strong initiatives, or forceful advancement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When effort is gentle or tentative; "push" implies force that may sound aggressive in some contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - pushing toward (progressive form)
   - pushed toward (past form)
   - push hard toward (emphasizes intensity)
   - push aggressively toward
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Push" represents forceful action requiring a direction; "toward" provides that direction while suggesting the goal hasn't been reached despite strong effort
   
   **Root Analysis**: push (Middle English "possen" from Old French) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're really pushing toward getting this done by Friday.
   - Everyone's pushing toward making the deadline somehow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The administration is pushing toward comprehensive policy reform.
   - Advocacy groups continue pushing toward legislative change.

14. **Expression**: bias toward (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A tendency, inclination, or prejudice in favor of something; systematic favoritism or preference
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard psychological/evaluative term)
   
   **Synonyms**: preference for, inclination toward, prejudice in favor of, tendency toward, partiality to
   
   **Antonyms**: bias against, prejudice against, opposition to
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing systematic preferences or tendencies in evaluation, decision-making, or judgment; appropriate in analytical, psychological, and critical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Bias" often carries negative connotation of unfairness; for neutral preference, consider "preference for" or "tendency toward"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - biases toward (plural)
   - strong bias toward
   - unconscious bias toward
   - systematic bias toward
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Bias" represents directional tendency or skew that must have a target; "toward" indicates the direction of favoritism (while "against" indicates negative bias)
   
   **Root Analysis**: bias (Old French "biais" - oblique, slanting) + toward
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think the teacher has a bias toward students who speak up in class.
   - There's definitely a bias toward hiring people from certain schools.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The algorithm demonstrates systematic bias toward specific demographic groups.
   - Research reveals cognitive biases toward confirming pre-existing beliefs.
