# Formality: Toward

1. **Original Expression**: working toward a solution  
   **Context**: Describing efforts to resolve a problem or achieve resolution
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate that one is actively making efforts to find or achieve a solution
   
   **Casual**: We're trying to figure this out  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary, informal verb "figure out", implicit direction  
   *Use when*: Friends, close colleagues, relaxed conversations about problems
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We're working toward a solution  
   *Characteristics*: "Working toward" shows active effort, neutral professional tone, goal-oriented language  
   *Use when*: General workplace, client updates, team meetings, standard business communication
   
   **Formal**: We are endeavoring to achieve resolution  
   *Characteristics*: "Endeavoring" is formal verb, "achieve resolution" is formal noun phrase, complete sentences without contractions  
   *Use when*: Official reports, senior management, external stakeholders, formal documentation
   
   **Humorous**: We're stumbling in the general direction of fixing this  
   *Characteristics*: Self-deprecating humor, "stumbling" suggests difficulty, downplays seriousness  
   *Use when*: Building rapport with colleagues, lightening mood during challenges  
   *Avoid when*: Client-facing communication, crisis situations, when stakeholders need confidence
   
   **Lively**: We're pushing hard to crack this thing!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic language "pushing hard", informal "crack this thing", exclamation shows enthusiasm  
   *Use when*: Motivating teams, energizing discussions, showing determination
   
   **Empathetic**: We understand this is difficult and we're actively working toward finding the right solution for you  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges difficulty, "for you" personalizes, "actively" emphasizes commitment  
   *Use when*: Customer service, handling complaints, supporting team members through challenges
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher power distance requires "formal" (endeavoring to achieve); equal status allows "casual" (trying to figure out)
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/clients require "semi-formal" or "formal"; close colleagues allow "casual"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious problems require "semi-formal" minimum; light issues allow "casual"
   - *Cultural Context*: Anglo-American business culture accepts "working toward"; more formal cultures prefer "endeavoring to achieve"
   - *Medium*: Written reports favor "formal"; verbal updates allow "semi-formal" or "casual"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "trying to figure out" with executives or clients (too casual and suggests uncertainty)
   - Using "endeavoring to achieve" with peers (sounds pretentious and creates distance)
   - Omitting acknowledgment of difficulty when stakeholders are frustrated (seems dismissive)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American English tends toward more casual registers than British English in business contexts; Asian cultures often expect more formal language when addressing problems
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Email to frustrated client about service issue → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic/Semi-Formal - "We understand your frustration and are working toward a solution"
   - **Situation 2**: Team standup meeting about bug fix → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Lively - "We're pushing to figure this out by Friday"
   - **Situation 3**: Board presentation about strategic challenge → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "We are endeavoring to achieve resolution through systematic implementation"

2. **Original Expression**: moving toward agreement  
   **Context**: Describing progress in negotiations or consensus-building
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate that parties are making progress toward reaching agreement or consensus
   
   **Casual**: Getting closer to a deal  
   *Characteristics*: "Getting closer" is informal spatial metaphor, "deal" is casual business term  
   *Use when*: Internal team discussions, informal negotiations, close business relationships
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Moving toward agreement  
   *Characteristics*: "Moving toward" shows progressive action, "agreement" is neutral formal term, professional tone  
   *Use when*: General business negotiations, stakeholder updates, professional communications
   
   **Formal**: Progressing toward consensus  
   *Characteristics*: "Progressing" is formal verb, "consensus" more formal than "agreement", implies deliberative process  
   *Use when*: Diplomatic contexts, formal negotiations, official statements, academic/institutional settings
   
   **Humorous**: We're almost on the same page—just a few typos to fix  
   *Characteristics*: "Same page" idiom, extended metaphor with "typos", downplays remaining differences  
   *Use when*: Friendly negotiations, easing tension, after major progress has been made  
   *Avoid when*: High-stakes negotiations, when parties are still far apart, formal diplomatic settings
   
   **Lively**: We're getting so close—momentum is really building!  
   *Characteristics*: "So close" shows enthusiasm, "momentum" suggests energy, exclamation emphasizes excitement  
   *Use when*: Motivating negotiation teams, celebrating progress, building positive energy
   
   **Empathetic**: I can see we're finding common ground, which is encouraging for all of us  
   *Characteristics*: "I can see" personalizes observation, "common ground" is collaborative, "encouraging for all" validates everyone  
   *Use when*: Mediating conflicts, facilitating difficult discussions, acknowledging all parties' efforts
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Diplomatic or high-level negotiations require "formal" (progressing toward consensus)
   - *Social Distance*: External formal negotiations need "semi-formal"; internal discussions allow "casual"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes negotiations require "formal"; routine business deals allow "casual"
   - *Cultural Context*: Asian and European formal business cultures prefer "progressing toward consensus"
   - *Medium*: Press releases and official statements require "formal"; internal emails allow "semi-formal"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "getting closer to a deal" in diplomatic contexts (too casual and transactional)
   - Using "progressing toward consensus" in routine business meetings (overly formal and stiff)
   - Using humorous variants before substantial progress is made (seems premature and dismissive)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Consensus" is highly valued in East Asian business cultures; "deal" is more transactional and American
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: International treaty negotiations press statement → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "Parties are progressing toward consensus on key provisions"
   - **Situation 2**: Sales negotiation update to team → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Lively - "We're getting close—they've agreed on price!"
   - **Situation 3**: Mediating conflict between departments → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "I can see both teams finding common ground on priorities"

3. **Original Expression**: attitude toward change  
   **Context**: Describing someone's perspective or disposition regarding change or transformation
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate how someone feels about or approaches change
   
   **Casual**: How someone feels about change  
   *Characteristics*: Simple language, "feels about" is conversational, straightforward  
   *Use when*: Everyday conversations, informal discussions about people or situations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Attitude toward change  
   *Characteristics*: "Attitude toward" is standard professional construction, neutral and clear  
   *Use when*: Performance reviews, organizational assessments, general professional discussions
   
   **Formal**: Disposition regarding transformation  
   *Characteristics*: "Disposition" more formal than "attitude", "regarding" more formal than "toward", "transformation" elevated from "change"  
   *Use when*: Academic writing, formal reports, research papers, executive communications
   
   **Humorous**: Whether someone's a "change champion" or a "status quo soldier"  
   *Characteristics*: Playful labeling, alliteration, military metaphor creates categories  
   *Use when*: Change management presentations to build engagement, team discussions about organizational dynamics  
   *Avoid when*: Evaluating individuals formally, when people are anxious about change, in official HR documents
   
   **Lively**: How pumped (or not) people are about shaking things up!  
   *Characteristics*: "Pumped" is energetic slang, "shaking things up" is dynamic idiom, parenthetical acknowledges variation  
   *Use when*: Energizing change initiatives, youth-oriented organizations, startup cultures
   
   **Empathetic**: How comfortable people feel with the upcoming changes and what support they need  
   *Characteristics*: "Comfortable" acknowledges emotional aspect, "feel" validates experience, "support they need" shows care  
   *Use when*: Change management, supporting teams through transitions, addressing change resistance
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Evaluating senior leaders requires "formal" (disposition regarding); discussing peers allows "casual"
   - *Social Distance*: HR documentation requires "semi-formal"; informal team discussions allow "casual"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Major organizational transformation requires "semi-formal" minimum; minor changes allow "casual"
   - *Cultural Context*: Academic and European contexts prefer "disposition regarding transformation"
   - *Medium*: Published research requires "formal"; internal emails allow "semi-formal"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "how someone feels" in formal assessments (too casual and imprecise for evaluation)
   - Using "disposition regarding transformation" in casual team discussions (creates unnecessary distance)
   - Failing to acknowledge emotional aspects when discussing change resistance (seems cold)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American culture values "change champions"; some cultures view rapid change more skeptically
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Academic paper on organizational behavior → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "Employee disposition regarding organizational transformation"
   - **Situation 2**: Team meeting about new process → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Empathetic - "Let's talk about how everyone feels about these changes"
   - **Situation 3**: Change management presentation to staff → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively - "We need everyone pumped about this transformation!"

4. **Original Expression**: step toward improvement  
   **Context**: Describing an action or measure that contributes to making something better
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate that an action represents progress in the direction of improvement
   
   **Casual**: Move to make things better  
   *Characteristics*: "Move" is informal for "step", "make things better" is conversational phrase  
   *Use when*: Everyday discussions, casual team updates, informal feedback
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Step toward improvement  
   *Characteristics*: "Step" suggests incremental progress, "toward" shows direction, "improvement" is clear goal  
   *Use when*: Professional communications, project updates, business presentations, performance discussions
   
   **Formal**: Measure undertaken to advance quality enhancement  
   *Characteristics*: "Measure undertaken" is formal construction, "advance" is elevated verb, "quality enhancement" more formal than "improvement"  
   *Use when*: Official reports, policy documents, executive communications, regulatory submissions
   
   **Humorous**: Baby step in the right direction (even if it's a tiny one!)  
   *Characteristics*: "Baby step" self-deprecates scale, parenthetical acknowledges limitations, maintains optimism  
   *Use when*: Acknowledging small wins, maintaining morale during slow progress, building team spirit  
   *Avoid when*: Major initiatives that deserve full credit, when stakeholders expect significant progress
   
   **Lively**: Exciting move forward to level things up!  
   *Characteristics*: "Exciting" shows enthusiasm, "move forward" is dynamic, "level up" gaming metaphor energizes  
   *Use when*: Celebrating progress, energizing teams, building momentum for change
   
   **Empathetic**: Meaningful step forward that will make a real difference  
   *Characteristics*: "Meaningful" validates significance, "real difference" emphasizes impact, acknowledges importance to people  
   *Use when*: Announcing improvements that address pain points, validating team efforts, recognizing impact on stakeholders
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Board reports require "formal" (measure to advance); team updates allow "semi-formal"
   - *Social Distance*: External stakeholder communications need "semi-formal" or "formal"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Major strategic initiatives require "semi-formal" minimum; routine improvements allow "casual"
   - *Cultural Context*: Government and regulatory contexts require "formal"
   - *Medium*: Official documentation requires "formal"; presentations allow "semi-formal"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "baby step" when announcing significant initiatives (undercuts achievement and demotivates)
   - Using "measure undertaken to advance quality enhancement" in team meetings (sounds bureaucratic and alienating)
   - Overstating significance of minor improvements with "exciting move forward" (loses credibility)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American culture celebrates visible progress; some cultures prefer understated acknowledgment
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Government policy announcement → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "Measure undertaken to advance quality enhancement in public services"
   - **Situation 2**: Team retrospective acknowledging slow progress → **Appropriate Variant**: Humorous - "Hey, it's a baby step, but we're moving!"
   - **Situation 3**: Announcing fix for major customer complaint → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "This is a meaningful step that will make a real difference"

5. **Original Expression**: progress toward goals  
   **Context**: Describing advancement in achieving objectives or targets
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate that one is making forward movement in the direction of achieving established goals
   
   **Casual**: Getting closer to what we want  
   *Characteristics*: "Getting closer" is informal spatial metaphor, "what we want" is conversational  
   *Use when*: Informal team check-ins, casual updates, friendly conversations about objectives
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Progress toward goals  
   *Characteristics*: "Progress" is standard noun, "toward goals" clearly indicates direction, professional and clear  
   *Use when*: Project status reports, performance reviews, stakeholder updates, general business communications
   
   **Formal**: Advancement in achieving strategic objectives  
   *Characteristics*: "Advancement" more formal than "progress", "achieving" suggests active accomplishment, "strategic objectives" more elevated than "goals"  
   *Use when*: Executive reports, board presentations, annual reports, formal strategy documents
   
   **Humorous**: Actually checking things off the list (imagine that!)  
   *Characteristics*: "Actually" suggests surprise, "checking off list" is relatable metaphor, parenthetical adds self-deprecating humor  
   *Use when*: Team celebrations of progress, acknowledging pleasant surprises, building camaraderie  
   *Avoid when*: Formal reporting, when progress is expected not surprising, external stakeholders
   
   **Lively**: Crushing our targets and pushing forward!  
   *Characteristics*: "Crushing" is energetic slang, "pushing forward" shows momentum, exclamation conveys excitement  
   *Use when*: Celebrating wins, motivating teams, building competitive energy, sales environments
   
   **Empathetic**: Making meaningful progress that reflects everyone's hard work and dedication  
   *Characteristics*: "Meaningful" validates importance, explicitly acknowledges team effort, "dedication" honors commitment  
   *Use when*: Recognizing team achievements, acknowledging effort during difficult periods, building team morale
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Reporting to executives requires "formal" (advancement in achieving objectives)
   - *Social Distance*: Internal team communications allow "casual" or "semi-formal"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Strategic initiatives require "semi-formal" or "formal"; routine work allows "casual"
   - *Cultural Context*: Corporate and institutional settings prefer "progress toward goals" or more formal
   - *Medium*: Written formal reports require "formal"; verbal updates allow flexibility
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "getting closer to what we want" in formal reports (too vague and casual)
   - Using "advancement in achieving strategic objectives" with team (sounds pompous and disconnected)
   - Celebrating with "crushing targets" when goals were barely met (loses credibility)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: American workplace culture emphasizes celebrating progress visibly; some cultures prefer modest acknowledgment
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Quarterly board presentation → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "Significant advancement in achieving strategic objectives for the fiscal year"
   - **Situation 2**: Team standup meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Lively - "We're crushing our sprint goals!"
   - **Situation 3**: Team recognition email after difficult quarter → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "Real progress that reflects everyone's dedication during challenges"

6. **Original Expression**: shift toward sustainability  
   **Context**: Describing a change or transition in the direction of more sustainable practices
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate that there is a directional change toward more environmentally or economically sustainable approaches
   
   **Casual**: Going greener / Being more eco-friendly  
   *Characteristics*: "Going green" is popular idiom, "eco-friendly" is accessible term, action-oriented  
   *Use when*: Consumer communications, casual workplace discussions, social media content
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Shift toward sustainability  
   *Characteristics*: "Shift" indicates change, "toward" shows direction, "sustainability" is professional standard term  
   *Use when*: Corporate communications, business strategy documents, stakeholder reports, professional presentations
   
   **Formal**: Transition to environmentally sustainable practices  
   *Characteristics*: "Transition" more formal than "shift", "environmentally sustainable practices" fully specified and formal  
   *Use when*: Official policy documents, regulatory filings, academic papers, formal institutional communications
   
   **Humorous**: Finally joining the 21st century and caring about the planet  
   *Characteristics*: "Finally" self-deprecates slow adoption, "21st century" implies being late to movement, slightly sardonic  
   *Use when*: Internal communications acknowledging late adoption, building rapport through self-awareness  
   *Avoid when*: External sustainability reporting, when greenwashing concerns exist, formal stakeholder communications
   
   **Lively**: Jumping on the green bandwagon in a big way!  
   *Characteristics*: "Jumping on" shows enthusiasm, "green bandwagon" acknowledges trend, "big way" emphasizes commitment  
   *Use when*: Marketing materials, consumer-facing announcements, energizing internal initiatives
   
   **Empathetic**: Taking meaningful steps toward sustainability because it matters to all of us  
   *Characteristics*: "Meaningful steps" validates significance, "matters to all of us" creates shared values, inclusive language  
   *Use when*: Community engagement, addressing stakeholder concerns about environment, values-driven communications
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Regulatory reporting requires "formal" (transition to sustainable practices)
   - *Social Distance*: Consumer marketing allows "casual" (going greener); B2B requires "semi-formal"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Climate crisis discourse requires serious tone; routine efficiency improvements allow lighter treatment
   - *Cultural Context*: European audiences expect more formal, serious treatment of sustainability
   - *Medium*: Official reports require "semi-formal" or "formal"; social media allows "casual"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "going greener" in serious environmental policy documents (too casual for gravity of issue)
   - Using humor about sustainability when audience is environmentally passionate (seems flippant)
   - Using "transition to environmentally sustainable practices" in consumer marketing (too formal and alienating)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: European and Scandinavian cultures take sustainability very seriously; casual treatment may offend
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Annual sustainability report → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "Comprehensive transition to environmentally sustainable operational practices"
   - **Situation 2**: Social media announcement about new initiative → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Lively - "We're going greener with our new packaging!"
   - **Situation 3**: Town hall addressing employee concerns → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "Taking meaningful steps toward sustainability because we know it matters"
