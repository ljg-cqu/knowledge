1. **Functional Expression**: I'll show you around (function: offering/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offering to guide someone through a place, pointing out features and locations
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but friendly
   
   **Synonyms**: Let me give you a tour, I'll take you around, I can guide you through, Let me orient you
   
   **Antonyms**: You're on your own, Find your own way, I can't help you
   
   **When to Use**: Welcoming new people to a location (home, office, school, facility); shows hospitality and helpfulness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you don't have time or authority to provide tours; when self-guided exploration is preferred
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let me show you around
   - Would you like me to show you around?
   - I can show you around if you'd like
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness strategy showing willingness to help; creates welcoming atmosphere; shows host role
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That would be great, thank you!
   - That's very kind, but I'm fine exploring on my own
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Is this your first time here?" B: "Yes!" A: "I'll show you around—it's easy to get lost."
   - Come on, I'll show you around the neighborhood. There's a great coffee shop nearby.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Welcome to the facility. I'll show you around and introduce you to the team.
   - The HR representative will show you around the campus during your orientation.

1. **Functional Expression**: It's around [time/amount] (function: estimating)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Giving an approximate time, number, or amount rather than exact figures
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (avoids precision)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's about..., It's approximately..., It's roughly..., I'd say around...
   
   **Antonyms**: It's exactly..., It's precisely..., It's [specific number]
   
   **When to Use**: When you don't know or don't need to provide exact figures; making estimates; casual conversation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precision is required (schedules, bills, measurements); formal reports; legal contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It should be around...
   - I'd guess around...
   - Probably around...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedging strategy to avoid commitment to exact figures; shows uncertainty or approximation; maintains flexibility
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, that works for me
   - Can you be more specific?
   - Around what time exactly?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "What time should we meet?" B: "It's around 3 o'clock now, so how about 4?"
   - A: "How much does it cost?" B: "It's around fifty bucks, maybe a bit more."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The meeting will commence around 2:00 PM, pending final confirmations.
   - Initial estimates suggest costs will be around $50,000, subject to detailed analysis.

1. **Functional Expression**: Stop messing around (function: commanding/criticizing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Telling someone to stop wasting time or behaving foolishly and start being serious
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Stop fooling around, Get serious, Focus, Cut it out, Knock it off
   
   **Antonyms**: Keep having fun, Relax, Take your time
   
   **When to Use**: Informal situations where someone needs to become serious or productive; parents to children; peers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal professional settings; with superiors or authority figures; can sound rude or dismissive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Quit messing around
   - Stop fooling around
   - Enough messing around
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-threatening act; asserts authority; can damage relationship if used inappropriately; appropriate with equal or lower status
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, okay, sorry
   - I was just having fun
   - Fine, I'll get back to work
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: *playing video games* B: "Stop messing around! We have work to do."
   - Mom: "Stop messing around and finish your homework!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts*

1. **Functional Expression**: What are you getting around to? (function: inquiring)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asking indirectly what someone's main point or purpose is; requesting clarification
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite way of asking for clarity)
   
   **Synonyms**: What's your point?, What are you trying to say?, Where are you going with this?, What do you mean?
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms)
   
   **When to Use**: When someone is taking too long to make their point; semi-polite way to ask for clarity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With superiors (too direct); very formal contexts; can seem impatient
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What are you getting at?
   - What's your point?
   - Where are you going with this?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Mild face-threatening act; suggests impatience; requests speaker to be more direct; use with caution
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sorry, let me clarify...
   - My point is...
   - I'm trying to say that...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Well, you know, I was thinking about the project, and..." B: "What are you getting around to?"
   - What are you getting around to? Just tell me directly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you clarify your main point? (more appropriate formal version)

1. **Functional Expression**: Is [person] around? (function: inquiring about presence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asking if someone is present or available at a location
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Is [person] here?, Is [person] available?, Is [person] in?, Can I speak to [person]?
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms)
   
   **When to Use**: Casual inquiries about availability; phone calls; dropping by someone's location
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal professional contexts (use "Is [person] available?" instead)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Is [person] here?
   - Is [person] in?
   - Do you know if [person] is around?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Neutral politeness; implies casual need to see/speak with someone; less formal than scheduled appointments
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, they're in their office
   - No, they left about an hour ago
   - Let me check / I'll go get them
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Is Mike around?" B: "Yeah, he's in the break room."
   - Hey, is your sister around? I wanted to ask her something.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Is Dr. Johnson around? I need to discuss the quarterly report.
   - Could you tell me if the manager is around today?

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's not beat around the bush (function: requesting directness)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggesting that both parties communicate directly without avoiding the main issue
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (ironically, by requesting directness)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's be direct, Let's get to the point, Let's speak plainly, Let's be straightforward
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's ease into this, Let's be diplomatic, Let's approach this carefully
   
   **When to Use**: When wanting frank discussion; cutting through politeness to address core issues; semi-professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When diplomacy is essential; with culturally indirect communicators; very formal contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's get straight to the point
   - I'll be direct
   - To put it bluntly
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals shift to direct communication; can be face-threatening; shows value for efficiency over politeness; use cautiously
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, fair enough
   - Alright, here's the situation...
   - I appreciate your directness
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's not beat around the bush—do you want to break up or not?
   - A: "Well, you see..." B: "Let's not beat around the bush. What happened?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let's not beat around the bush: the company is facing significant financial challenges.
   - To speak plainly, without beating around the bush, we need to restructure immediately.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'll be around (function: stating availability)

   - Indicating that you will be present or available in a location for a period of time

   **Synonyms**: I'll be here, I'll be available, You can find me here, I'll be nearby
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm leaving, I won't be here, I'll be gone
   
   **When to Use**: Offering availability for questions or assistance; letting others know you're accessible
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need to specify exact times or commitments (too vague)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll be around all day
   - I'll be around if you need me
   - I'll stick around
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows willingness to help; creates open-door atmosphere; maintains flexibility without specific commitment
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks, I might take you up on that
   - Good to know
   - I'll find you if I need anything
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll be around all weekend if you want to hang out.
   - Don't worry, I'll be around if you need help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'll be around the office this afternoon should any questions arise.
   - The technical support team will be around throughout the implementation phase.

1. **Functional Expression**: Come around anytime (function: inviting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Extending an open invitation to visit without specifying exact times
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct invitation
   
   **Synonyms**: Drop by anytime, Visit whenever you like, You're always welcome, Stop by anytime
   
   **Antonyms**: Call before visiting, By appointment only, Don't come uninvited
   
   **When to Use**: Close relationships; showing hospitality; creating open, welcoming atmosphere
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you actually want advance notice; professional contexts requiring appointments; with people you're not close to
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Stop by anytime
   - You're welcome anytime
   - Drop by whenever
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness showing closeness and trust; may be polite formula rather than literal invitation; cultural variations in interpretation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks, I will!
   - That's very kind of you
   - I appreciate that
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We love having you—come around anytime!
   - If you're ever in the neighborhood, come around anytime for coffee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; in formal contexts use:* Please feel free to schedule a visit at your convenience.

1. **Functional Expression**: How about we meet around [time]? (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposing an approximate meeting time, leaving flexibility
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (softened suggestion)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's meet around..., Shall we meet approximately at..., What if we meet around...
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's meet at exactly..., We must meet at..., The meeting is at...
   
   **When to Use**: Casual planning; when exact timing isn't critical; friendly proposals
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal appointments requiring punctuality; professional schedules; when precision matters
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What about meeting around...?
   - We could meet around...
   - Let's say around...?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Hedging reduces imposition; shows flexibility; invites negotiation; maintains informality
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sounds good
   - Can we make it a bit earlier/later?
   - That works for me
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How about we meet around 6 for dinner?
   - Let's grab coffee around noon tomorrow?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Perhaps we could schedule the meeting for around 2 PM, pending your availability.
   - I suggest we convene around midday to discuss the proposal.

1. **Functional Expression**: You'll come around (to my way of thinking) (function: predicting/persuading)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing confidence that someone will eventually change their opinion to agree with yours
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (prediction rather than direct persuasion)
   
   **Synonyms**: You'll change your mind, You'll see I'm right, You'll agree eventually, You'll be convinced
   
   **Antonyms**: You'll never agree, We'll have to disagree, You're set in your ways
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing confidence in eventual agreement; after unsuccessful persuasion; showing patience
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound condescending or dismissive; avoid with superiors or in serious disagreements
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You'll come around eventually
   - You'll see things my way
   - Give it time, you'll come around
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Can be face-threatening if dismissive of other's current opinion; shows confidence or stubbornness; implies patience
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - We'll see
   - Don't count on it
   - Maybe, but I doubt it
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You hate mushrooms now, but trust me, you'll come around.
   - A: "I'm never watching that show." B: "You'll come around—everyone does!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Initial resistance is natural, but stakeholders typically come around once they see the benefits.
   - The board will come around to supporting the initiative once they review the data.
