1. **Expression**: around the clock (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - All day and all night without stopping; continuously for 24 hours
   - *Literal meaning*: Moving around the face of a clock
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Continuously, non-stop
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: 24/7, non-stop, continuously, all day and night, round-the-clock
   
   **Antonyms**: part-time, occasionally, intermittently
   
   **When to Use**: Describing continuous operations, services, or work; appropriate in professional and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May be considered informal in highly academic writing; use "continuously" or "24-hour" instead
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - round-the-clock (British, hyphenated as adjective)
   - 24/7 (modern equivalent)
   - day and night
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originates from the continuous movement of clock hands making a complete circle, symbolizing uninterrupted time passage
   
   **Root Analysis**: "around" (circular motion) + "clock" (timepiece) → continuous time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The store is open around the clock, so you can shop whenever.
   - We've been working around the clock to finish this project on time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The emergency hotline operates around the clock to ensure immediate assistance.
   - Medical staff provided around-the-clock care for patients in critical condition.

1. **Expression**: get around to (type: phrasal verb collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To finally do something that you have been intending to do for some time
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: finally do, eventually do, manage to do, find time to do
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid, postpone, neglect, put off
   
   **When to Use**: Informal and semi-formal contexts to describe delayed actions; very common in everyday speech
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too casual for formal reports or academic writing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get round to (British English)
   - never get around to
   - finally get around to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Get around to" combines the sense of moving past obstacles (around) with reaching (to) an intended action
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (accomplish) + around (past obstacles) + to (reaching a goal)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I keep meaning to call him, but I haven't gotten around to it yet.
   - When are you going to get around to fixing that leaky faucet?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has yet to get around to addressing the budget concerns raised last quarter.
   - Many homeowners fail to get around to necessary maintenance until problems become severe.

1. **Expression**: turn around (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To rotate 180 degrees to face the opposite direction
   - To reverse or change a situation from negative to positive
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: reverse direction, spin around, rotate, face the other way; (figurative) reverse, transform, improve
   
   **Antonyms**: continue forward, maintain course, keep going
   
   **When to Use**: Both literal physical rotation and figurative business/situation improvement; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Clear from context which meaning intended
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - turnaround (noun form - single word)
   - turn round (British)
   - turn it around
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Combines turning motion with circular/complete reversal (around), suggesting complete directional change
   
   **Root Analysis**: turn (rotate) + around (circular, complete) → complete reversal
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Turn around and look at this amazing sunset!
   - The new manager really turned the company around.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The CEO's strategic initiatives successfully turned around the organization's financial performance.
   - Participants were instructed to turn around and face the presentation screen.

1. **Expression**: around the corner (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very near in space; just past the next corner
   - Very near in time; about to happen soon
   - *Literal meaning*: Located just past a street corner
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Coming soon, imminent
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: nearby, close by, approaching, imminent, coming soon, just ahead
   
   **Antonyms**: far away, distant, in the distant future
   
   **When to Use**: Both literal locations and figurative future events; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous—clarify whether spatial or temporal meaning
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - just around the corner
   - right around the corner
   - (right) round the corner (British)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originally literal (something close by, past a corner), extended metaphorically to time proximity
   
   **Root Analysis**: around (nearby) + corner (turning point) → close proximity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The coffee shop is just around the corner—two minutes away.
   - Summer vacation is right around the corner!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regulatory changes around the corner, firms must prepare compliance strategies.
   - The research facility is conveniently located around the corner from the university campus.

1. **Expression**: revolve around (type: phrasal verb collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move in a circular orbit around something
   - To have something as the main focus or most important element
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: center on, focus on, be about, orbit, circle around
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, ignore, be peripheral to
   
   **When to Use**: Both literal astronomical motion and figurative focus/importance; formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Clear which meaning (literal vs. figurative) from context
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - revolves around
   - revolving around
   - revolved around
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Revolve" (circular motion) naturally partners with "around" (circular position) for both literal orbits and metaphorical centrality
   
   **Root Analysis**: revolve (rotate) + around (circular path) → circular motion or central focus
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His whole life revolves around basketball—he never thinks about anything else.
   - The Earth revolves around the Sun once a year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research methodology revolves around qualitative case study analysis.
   - The planets revolve around their respective stars in elliptical orbits.

1. **Expression**: beat around the bush (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To avoid talking about something directly or clearly; to approach a topic indirectly
   - *Literal meaning*: Beating bushes to flush out game birds while hunting
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Avoiding direct discussion
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: avoid the issue, be evasive, talk indirectly, skirt the issue, hedge
   
   **Antonyms**: be direct, get to the point, speak plainly, cut to the chase
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to semi-formal contexts to criticize or note indirect communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for very formal academic or legal writing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - beating about the bush (British)
   - stop beating around the bush
   - without beating around the bush
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: From hunting practice of beating bushes to drive out game rather than approaching directly; first recorded 1570s
   
   **Root Analysis**: beat (strike) + around (circular, indirect) + bush → indirect approach
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stop beating around the bush and tell me what you really think!
   - He beat around the bush for ten minutes before finally asking for a raise.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Rather than beating around the bush, the report directly addresses the organization's financial challenges.
   - The diplomat's statement avoided beating around the bush, clearly outlining policy positions.

1. **Expression**: show someone around (type: phrasal verb collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To guide someone through a place, pointing out interesting or important features
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: give a tour, guide through, take someone through, orient someone
   
   **Antonyms**: leave unguided, let someone explore alone
   
   **When to Use**: Welcoming visitors, new employees, guests to a location; formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (Generally appropriate in all contexts)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - show around
   - give someone a tour
   - take someone around
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Show" (present, display) combines with "around" (through various areas) to indicate guided exploration of a space
   
   **Root Analysis**: show (display) + someone + around (through the area) → guided tour
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me show you around the apartment—kitchen's through here.
   - Could someone show the new guy around the office?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The HR manager will show new employees around the facility during orientation.
   - Our guide showed us around the historical district, explaining architectural features.

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stop messing around and get back to work!

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts*

1. **Expression**: all around (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In every direction or aspect; considering everything
   - On all sides; surrounding completely
   - *Fixedness*: medium
   
   **Synonyms**: on all sides, everywhere, in all aspects, overall, generally
   
   **Antonyms**: in one area only, partially, incompletely
   
   **When to Use**: Describing comprehensive situations, complete surrounding, or overall assessment; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specificity is needed rather than generalization
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - all-around (hyphenated as adjective, US)
   - all-round (British)
   - from all around
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "All" (complete) intensifies "around" (every direction) to emphasize totality or comprehensiveness
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (complete) + around (every direction) → complete coverage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's an all-around great person—smart, funny, and kind.
   - People came from all around to see the concert.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate demonstrated all-around excellence in technical and interpersonal competencies.
   - The policy aims to improve all-around performance across organizational divisions.

1. **Expression**: run around (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move quickly from place to place, often in a busy or hurried way
   - To spend time with someone romantically (informal)
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: rush about, hurry around, be busy, dash around
   
   **Antonyms**: stay still, remain in place, be idle
   
   **When to Use**: Describing busy activity or rushed movement; informal to semi-formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Second (romantic) meaning is very informal and can be dated slang
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - running around
   - run round (British)
   - run around like a headless chicken (idiom)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Run" (move quickly) with "around" (various places) suggests busy, multi-directional movement
   
   **Root Analysis**: run (move quickly) + around (in various directions) → busy movement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been running around all day doing errands—I'm exhausted!
   - The kids are running around the playground screaming with joy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Staff members have been running around attempting to meet the deadline.
   - Children run around the recreational area during supervised outdoor time.
