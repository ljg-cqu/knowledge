1. **Verb**: revolve around
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To move in a circular orbit around a central point
   - (Intransitive, figurative) To have something as the main focus or central theme; to be primarily concerned with
   
   **Synonyms**: orbit, circle, rotate around; (figurative) center on, focus on, be about, concern
   
   **Antonyms**: diverge from, move away from; (figurative) ignore, disregard, be peripheral to
   
   **When to Use**: Both literal astronomical/physical motion and figurative discussions of focus or importance; appropriate in formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ensure context makes clear whether literal or figurative meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - revolve around the sun
   - life revolves around
   - discussion revolves around
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + volve (roll, turn) + around (circular path)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "revolvere" (roll back) → Old French → English; combined with "around" for directional specificity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My whole schedule revolves around my kids' activities.
   - The moon revolves around Earth every 27 days.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research methodology revolves around three central hypotheses.
   - Planetary bodies revolve around their host stars due to gravitational forces.

1. **Verb**: turn around
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To rotate one's body or change direction to face the opposite way
   - (Transitive) To reverse a declining situation; to cause improvement in a negative situation
   
   **Synonyms**: rotate, spin around, face the other direction; (figurative) reverse, transform, improve, revitalize
   
   **Antonyms**: continue forward, maintain direction; (figurative) worsen, decline, deteriorate
   
   **When to Use**: Describing physical rotation or business/organizational improvement; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be specific about which meaning when context is ambiguous
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - turn around quickly
   - turn around a company
   - turn around and face
   
   **Root Analysis**: turn (rotate) + around (complete circular motion)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "turnian" + "around" → Middle English combination for complete rotation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Turn around so I can zip up your dress.
   - The new coach really turned the team around this season.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants should turn around to view the visual presentation.
   - Strategic restructuring successfully turned around the organization's declining revenue.

1. **Verb**: look around

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look around the room
   - look around for options
   - just looking around (shopping context)
   
   **Root Analysis**: look (direct gaze) + around (in multiple directions)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "lōcian" + around → examining multiple directions or options
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I looked around but couldn't find my keys anywhere.
   - We're just looking around, not ready to buy yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Visitors are encouraged to look around the exhibition at their leisure.
   - Researchers looked around for alternative methodological approaches before proceeding.

1. **Verb**: walk around
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To move about an area on foot; to stroll through or explore by walking
   - (Transitive) To avoid or bypass an obstacle by going around it
   
   **Synonyms**: stroll around, wander around, explore on foot; (with object) circumnavigate, bypass, go around
   
   **Antonyms**: stay in place, remain seated; (with object) go through, confront directly
   
   **When to Use**: Casual exploration, exercise, or avoiding obstacles; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing purposeful, direct walking to a destination
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - walk around the park
   - walk around the city
   - walk around the problem
   
   **Root Analysis**: walk (move on foot) + around (in various directions or circularly)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wealcan" + around → aimless or circular walking
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's walk around downtown and see what's new.
   - You can walk around that puddle if you want to keep your shoes clean.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were instructed to walk around the facility for orientation purposes.
   - The pedestrian path allows visitors to walk around the historic monument.

1. **Verb**: come around
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To visit someone's home or location
   - (Intransitive) To change one's opinion or attitude; to be persuaded
   - (Intransitive) To regain consciousness or recover from illness
   - (Intransitive) To occur or arrive cyclically (of events or time periods)
   
   **Synonyms**: visit, drop by; change one's mind, be convinced; wake up, recover; recur, arrive
   
   **Antonyms**: stay away; remain stubborn, refuse; (no clear antonym for recovery); never arrive
   
   **When to Use**: Multiple contexts depending on meaning; informal to semi-formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous—context must clarify which meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - come around to visit
   - come around to someone's way of thinking
   - come around eventually (events)
   
   **Root Analysis**: come (move toward) + around (to a position or state)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "cuman" + around → arriving at a position or state
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Come around sometime and we'll have dinner.
   - She'll come around—just give her time to think about it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When the holidays come around, consumer spending typically increases.
   - The patient came around after receiving appropriate treatment.

   **Common Phrases**:

   **Root Analysis**: go (move) + around (circularly or past)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gān" + around → circular or distributing movement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's plenty of pizza—enough to go around for everyone.
   - That story's been going around the office all week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Resources must be carefully managed to ensure sufficient supplies go around.
   - Information goes around the network through established communication channels.

1. **Verb**: move around
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To change position frequently; to not stay in one place
   - (Transitive) To rearrange objects in different positions
   
   **Synonyms**: relocate frequently, shift positions, circulate; rearrange, reposition, shuffle
   
   **Antonyms**: stay put, remain stationary; leave in place, keep fixed
   
   **When to Use**: Physical movement or rearrangement; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing permanent relocation (use "move to" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move around the room
   - move around furniture
   - move around a lot (lifestyle)
   
   **Root Analysis**: move (change position) + around (in various places)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "movere" → Old French "movoir" + around → changing positions
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stop moving around so much—you're making me dizzy!
   - Let's move these chairs around to make more space.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees may move around the collaborative workspace as needed.
   - The study examined how people move around urban environments throughout the day.

1. **Verb**: hang around
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To spend time in a place without specific purpose; to loiter
   - (Intransitive) To wait or remain in a place
   - (Intransitive, informal) To spend time with someone regularly
   
   **Synonyms**: loiter, linger, wait around; (with people) spend time with, associate with, hang out
   
   **Antonyms**: leave promptly, move on, depart; avoid, stay away from
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts describing casual presence or socializing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for formal writing or professional contexts; can have negative connotation of wasting time
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hang around the house
   - hang around with friends
   - don't hang around
   
   **Root Analysis**: hang (remain suspended/present) + around (in an area)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "hengja" + around → 20th century informal usage for lingering
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We just hung around the mall all afternoon.
   - I don't like the people he hangs around with.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts* The regulation prohibits individuals from hanging around restricted areas without authorization.
