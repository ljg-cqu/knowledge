1. **Adjective**: surrounding (attributive and predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Located or existing on all sides of something; encircling or encompassing
   
   **Synonyms**: encircling, neighboring, adjacent, nearby, encompassing
   
   **Antonyms**: distant, remote, far, separate
   
   **When to Use**: Describing things located around a central point or object; formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to things far away or unconnected spatially
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - surrounding area
   - surrounding environment
   - surrounding landscape
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (over, around) + round (circular) + -ing (adjective suffix) → encircling
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "surround" (French "suronder" → Middle English) → present participle as adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check out the surrounding area before you buy the house.
   - The surrounding mountains make this town incredibly scenic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research examined environmental impacts on the surrounding ecosystem.
   - Development plans must consider effects on the surrounding community.

1. **Adjective**: circular (attributive and predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the form or shape of a circle; round
   - *(Sense 2)* Moving around in a circle or following a circular route
   - *(Sense 3)* Reasoning that uses its conclusion as one of its premises (circular reasoning)
   
   **Synonyms**: round, ring-shaped, curved; (movement) revolving, rotating; (reasoning) self-referential
   
   **Antonyms**: linear, straight, angular, square
   
   **When to Use**: Describing shapes, movements, or logical patterns; formal and technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "circuitous" (indirect); be clear about which meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - circular motion
   - circular path
   - circular reasoning
   
   **Root Analysis**: circul- (circle) + -ar (relating to) → relating to circles
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "circularis" (circular) → Late Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The kids ran around the circular track at the park.
   - That's circular reasoning—you're using your conclusion to prove itself!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The device operates through circular motion of internal components.
   - The argument contains circular logic and lacks independent verification.

1. **Adjective**: all-around (attributive, US) / all-round (attributive, British) (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having many different skills or uses; versatile in multiple areas
   - Considering or including all aspects; comprehensive
   
   **Synonyms**: versatile, multifaceted, well-rounded, comprehensive, complete
   
   **Antonyms**: specialized, limited, narrow, one-dimensional
   
   **When to Use**: Describing people with multiple abilities or comprehensive solutions; informal to formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specific expertise rather than versatility is valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - all-around athlete
   - all-around good person
   - all-around performance
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (complete) + around (in every direction) → comprehensive in all aspects
   
   **Etymology**: American English 19th century → compound adjective indicating completeness
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's an all-around great person—smart, funny, and kind.
   - He's the team's best all-around player.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate demonstrated all-around excellence in technical and interpersonal competencies.
   - The program provides all-around development for participants.

1. **Adjective**: approximate (attributive and predicative, gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Close to the actual but not completely accurate or exact; estimated
   
   **Synonyms**: rough, estimated, close, near, inexact
   
   **Antonyms**: exact, precise, accurate, specific
   
   **When to Use**: Describing estimates, rough calculations, or near values; formal and semi-formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precision is required or in contexts demanding exact measurements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approximate value
   - approximate location
   - approximate time
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to, toward) + proxim- (nearest) + -ate (having quality of) → near to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "approximatus" (brought near) → Late Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the approximate cost? I just need a ballpark figure.
   - The approximate distance is around 50 miles.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The approximate date of manufacture is 1950, based on available records.
   - Researchers calculated approximate values using statistical modeling.

1. **Adjective**: peripheral (attributive and predicative, gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to or situated on the edge or periphery of something
   - *(Sense 2)* Of secondary importance; not central to the main subject
   
   **Synonyms**: outer, outlying, marginal; (importance) minor, secondary, incidental
   
   **Antonyms**: central, core, fundamental, essential
   
   **When to Use**: Technical, medical, or formal discussions of location or importance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "outer" or "around the edges"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - peripheral vision
   - peripheral area
   - peripheral issue
   
   **Root Analysis**: peripher- (circumference, around) + -al (relating to) → relating to outer area
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "periphereia" (circumference) → Latin → English 19th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I saw something move in my peripheral vision.
   - That's a peripheral concern—let's focus on the main issue.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Peripheral blood samples were collected for analysis.
   - The research addresses peripheral considerations beyond the central hypothesis.

1. **Adjective**: nearby (attributive and predicative, gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not far away in distance; close by
   
   **Synonyms**: close, near, adjacent, neighboring, close by
   
   **Antonyms**: distant, far, remote, faraway
   
   **When to Use**: Describing proximity; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precise distance measurements are needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nearby area
   - nearby location
   - nearby residents
   
   **Root Analysis**: near (close in distance) + by (at, beside) → close proximity
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "near" + "by" → compound form 16th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a great restaurant nearby—want to try it?
   - The kids play at the nearby park every afternoon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Nearby residents were evacuated as a precautionary measure.
   - The facility is conveniently located near nearby transportation hubs.

1. **Adjective**: ambient (attributive, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to the immediate surroundings of something; environmental
   - *(Sense 2, music/sound)* Creating or enhancing atmosphere through subtle background presence
   
   **Synonyms**: surrounding, environmental, atmospheric, background
   
   **Antonyms**: direct, focused, targeted
   
   **When to Use**: Technical or scientific contexts about environmental conditions; music/art discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too technical for everyday conversation; use "room temperature" or "background" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ambient temperature
   - ambient light
   - ambient noise
   
   **Root Analysis**: ambi- (around, on both sides) + -ent (having quality) → surrounding quality
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "ambire" (go around) → "ambient" (surrounding) 16th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The ambient lighting in this room is perfect—not too bright.
   - I like ambient music for studying—it's not distracting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Measurements were taken at ambient temperature and pressure conditions.
   - The ambient noise level exceeded recommended exposure limits.

1. **Adjective**: neighboring (attributive, US) / neighbouring (attributive, British) (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Situated next to or very near something; adjacent
   
   **Synonyms**: adjacent, nearby, bordering, adjoining, surrounding
   
   **Antonyms**: distant, remote, separate, isolated
   
   **When to Use**: Describing proximity of places, regions, or objects; formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When things are far apart or completely separate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - neighboring countries
   - neighboring buildings
   - neighboring communities
   
   **Root Analysis**: neighbor (one living nearby) + -ing (adjective suffix) → being nearby
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "neahgebur" (near-dweller) → "neighbor" → "neighboring" as adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We can hear everything from the neighboring apartment.
   - The neighboring towns have a big rivalry.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Diplomatic relations with neighboring nations remain stable.
   - The project will impact neighboring communities through increased traffic.

1. **Adjective**: encompassing (attributive and predicative, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Including or containing a wide range of things; comprehensive
   - Surrounding or enclosing completely
   
   **Synonyms**: comprehensive, inclusive, all-embracing, wide-ranging; (spatial) surrounding, enclosing
   
   **Antonyms**: narrow, limited, exclusive, restricted
   
   **When to Use**: Formal descriptions of comprehensive scope or complete surrounding
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "covering everything" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - encompassing view
   - encompassing range
   - all-encompassing
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (in, within) + compass (around, circle) + -ing → containing within circle
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "encompass" (surround, contain) 16th century → participial adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The view from the tower is encompassing—you can see for miles.
   - She has an encompassing knowledge of the subject.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study provides an encompassing analysis of contemporary educational practices.
   - The policy framework is encompassing, addressing multiple dimensions of the issue.

1. **Adjective**: rotational (attributive, non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to or characterized by rotation around an axis
   - Occurring in turns or cycles; alternating
   
   **Synonyms**: rotating, revolving, cyclical; (sequence) alternating, sequential
   
   **Antonyms**: stationary, fixed, permanent
   
   **When to Use**: Technical, scientific, or organizational contexts about rotation or cycles
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too technical for everyday contexts; use "rotating" or "taking turns"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rotational motion
   - rotational force
   - rotational basis
   
   **Root Analysis**: rotation (turning) + -al (relating to) → relating to rotation
   
   **Etymology**: From "rotation" + adjectival suffix, 19th century technical usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The tire has rotational balance issues—feels wobbly.
   - We work on a rotational schedule, changing shifts every month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The device generates energy through rotational kinetic forces.
   - Staff assignments follow a rotational model to ensure skill development.
