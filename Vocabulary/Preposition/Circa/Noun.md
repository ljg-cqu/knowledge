1. **Noun**: approximation (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) A value, figure, or estimate that is nearly but not exactly correct
   - (U) The act or process of estimating something approximately
   
   **Synonyms**: estimate, rough calculation, ballpark figure, estimation, guesstimate (informal)
   
   **Antonyms**: exact figure, precise measurement, accurate calculation
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing estimates, rough calculations, or values that are close to but not exactly accurate; appropriate in mathematical, scientific, historical, and planning contexts; often used alongside "circa" to indicate temporal or numerical approximations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when exact figures are required or available; inappropriate in legal or financial contexts requiring precision; don't use when the value is known with certainty
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rough approximation
   - close approximation
   - approximation of circa [number/date]
   - provide an approximation
   - accurate approximation (somewhat oxymoronic but used)
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to, toward) + proxim- (nearest) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin → approximationem (a drawing near) → French "approximation" → English (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's just an approximation—circa 100 people, maybe more.
   - Give me a rough approximation of when it was built.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The chronological approximation places the event circa 1350 CE.
   - This figure represents a conservative approximation of circa 500,000 specimens.

1. **Noun**: estimate (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An approximate calculation or judgment of the value, number, quantity, or extent of something
   - A rough assessment of the age or date of something
   
   **Synonyms**: approximation, assessment, calculation, evaluation, rough figure
   
   **Antonyms**: exact figure, precise measurement, definite amount
   
   **When to Use**: Use when providing calculated guesses or rough figures; appropriate in business, planning, historical, and academic contexts; frequently paired with "circa" when discussing dates or quantities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when precise figures are known; inappropriate in contexts requiring guaranteed accuracy; don't present estimates as facts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - conservative estimate
   - rough estimate
   - estimate of circa [number/date]
   - provide an estimate
   - preliminary estimate
   
   **Root Analysis**: estim(ate) + -ate (noun suffix from verb)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → aestimare (to value) → "estimate" as noun (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My estimate is circa 50 people showed up.
   - That's just a ballpark estimate—circa $1,000.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Current estimates place the population at circa 250,000 during the classical period.
   - Archaeological estimates date the structure to circa 800 BCE.

1. **Noun**: date (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A particular day, month, and year when an event occurred or is scheduled to occur
   - The period of time when something existed or was created
   
   **Synonyms**: time, point in time, period, year, time period
   
   **Antonyms**: timelessness, unknown period
   
   **When to Use**: Use when specifying or discussing the time when something happened, was created, or existed; appropriate in all contexts from casual to formal; frequently modified by "circa" when the exact date is uncertain
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using alone when approximation needs to be indicated (use "circa" + date); don't use when referring to romantic appointments
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approximate date
   - estimated date of circa
   - date of circa [year]
   - establish a date
   - uncertain date
   
   **Root Analysis**: date (from Latin "data" = given)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → data (given) → Medieval Latin "data" (date on a letter) → Old French → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The date's uncertain, but it's circa 1850.
   - What's the date on that coin? Circa 300 BC?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The construction date is established at circa 1480 through dendrochronological analysis.
   - The manuscript bears no explicit date but is attributed to circa 1320 CE.

1. **Noun**: period (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A length or portion of time characterized by particular events, conditions, or phenomena
   - An era or epoch in history, art, or culture
   
   **Synonyms**: era, epoch, age, time, span, interval
   
   **Antonyms**: instant, moment, point in time
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing extended time spans, historical eras, artistic movements, or cultural phases; appropriate in historical, academic, and cultural contexts; often modified by "circa" to indicate approximate dating
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to precise points in time rather than spans; inappropriate for very short durations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - historical period
   - period circa [date range]
   - flourished during the period circa
   - dated to the period circa
   - Classical period (circa 500-323 BCE)
   
   **Root Analysis**: peri- (around, near) + -od (way, path)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek → periodos (cycle, period of time) → Latin "periodus" → Old French "periode" → Middle English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This was painted during the Baroque period, circa 1650.
   - He lived in the Victorian period, circa 1880s.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The artifact dates to the Late Bronze Age period, circa 1200-1000 BCE.
   - The author flourished during the Romantic period, circa 1800-1820.

1. **Noun**: era (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A long and distinct period of history with particular features or events
   - A major division of geological or historical time
   
   **Synonyms**: age, epoch, period, time, generation
   
   **Antonyms**: moment, instant, brief period
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing major historical divisions, significant time periods, or transformative phases; appropriate in historical, geological, and cultural contexts; modified by "circa" when approximate dating is needed
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for short time spans; inappropriate when discussing minor or insignificant periods
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - historical era
   - era circa [date]
   - golden era circa
   - post-war era
   - modern era
   
   **Root Analysis**: era (from Latin "aera" = counters, items in a calculation)
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin → aera (epoch, era, number) → Spanish/Portuguese "era" → English (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's from the jazz era, circa 1920s.
   - The Victorian era lasted circa 1837 to 1901.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The Hellenistic era, circa 323-31 BCE, witnessed significant cultural synthesis.
   - The manuscript originates from the Carolingian era, circa 750-900 CE.

1. **Noun**: timeframe (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A period of time during which something occurs or is planned to occur
   - The temporal boundaries within which an event or process takes place
   
   **Synonyms**: time period, time span, duration, interval, window
   
   **Antonyms**: indefinite period, timeless span
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing the duration or temporal boundaries of events, projects, or historical phenomena; appropriate in planning, project management, historical, and academic contexts; can be modified by "circa" for approximate temporal boundaries
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to specific points in time rather than spans; inappropriate in very casual contexts (use "time" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - narrow timeframe
   - broad timeframe
   - timeframe of circa [date range]
   - within the timeframe circa
   - estimated timeframe
   
   **Root Analysis**: time + frame (boundary, structure)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound word → "time" (Old English "tima") + "frame" (Old English "framian" = to benefit, make progress) → modern compound (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The timeframe we're looking at is circa 1900 to 1920.
   - What's the timeframe for this project? Circa six months?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The archaeological evidence suggests a construction timeframe of circa 1450-1480 CE.
   - The manuscript's production can be confined to a timeframe of circa 1320-1340.
