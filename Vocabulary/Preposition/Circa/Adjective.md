1. **Adjective**: approximate
   **Meaning**: 
   - Close to the actual but not completely accurate or exact; estimated or rough rather than precise
   - Nearly correct or exact
   
   **Synonyms**: rough, estimated, inexact, close, near, ballpark
   
   **Antonyms**: exact, precise, accurate, definite, specific
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing values, dates, measurements, or calculations that are close to but not exactly correct; appropriate in contexts where precision is unavailable or unnecessary; frequently paired with dates modified by "circa"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when exact figures are known and should be stated; inappropriate in legal, financial, or scientific contexts requiring precision
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approximate date of circa [year]
   - approximate figure
   - approximate location
   - approximate value of circa
   - approximate timeframe
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to, toward) + proxim- (nearest) + -ate (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → ad- (to) + proximus (nearest) → Late Latin "approximatus" → "approximate" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The approximate date is circa 1850.
   - I can give you an approximate figure—circa $500.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The approximate construction date is established at circa 1475 through stylistic analysis.
   - Demographic studies provide approximate population figures of circa 1.2 million.

1. **Adjective**: estimated
   **Meaning**: 
   - Roughly calculated or judged; assessed approximately rather than measured precisely
   - Based on judgment or calculation rather than exact measurement
   
   **Synonyms**: approximated, calculated, assessed, reckoned, projected
   
   **Antonyms**: measured, exact, precise, confirmed, verified
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing values, dates, or quantities that have been calculated or judged rather than precisely measured; appropriate in academic, planning, historical, and scientific contexts; commonly used with "circa" dates
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when actual measurements exist; inappropriate when implying confirmed or verified information; don't use interchangeably with "proven" or "confirmed"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - estimated date of circa
   - estimated population circa
   - estimated cost of circa
   - estimated age circa
   - estimated completion circa
   
   **Root Analysis**: estim(ate) + -ed (past participle/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "estimate" (Latin aestimare) + -ed → adjective form (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The estimated cost is circa $10,000.
   - The estimated crowd was circa 5,000 people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The estimated construction date is circa 1340 CE based on archaeological evidence.
   - The estimated population of Constantinople was circa 500,000 during the 6th century.

1. **Adjective**: uncertain
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not able to be relied on; not known or definite
   - Not definitely known, established, or fixed
   
   **Synonyms**: unclear, indefinite, undetermined, ambiguous, doubtful
   
   **Antonyms**: certain, definite, clear, established, confirmed
   
   **When to Use**: Use when the exact date, value, or fact is not known with confidence; appropriate in scholarly, historical, and academic contexts where evidence is incomplete; often precedes dates modified by "circa"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when information is actually known or verifiable; inappropriate when you want to convey confidence; don't use when "unknown" would be more accurate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - uncertain date
   - uncertain origins circa
   - remains uncertain
   - highly uncertain
   - uncertain estimate of circa
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + certain (from Latin "certus" = determined, fixed)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → certus (determined) → Old French "certain" → Middle English + prefix "un-" → "uncertain" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The date's uncertain, but it's circa 1900.
   - The origin is uncertain—somewhere circa 500 BC.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manuscript's provenance remains uncertain, though it is dated circa 1450.
   - The exact population is uncertain; estimates suggest circa 300,000 inhabitants.

1. **Adjective**: undated
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not marked with or assigned a date
   - Having no specific date attached or known
   
   **Synonyms**: dateless, timeless, not dated, without date
   
   **Antonyms**: dated, time-stamped, marked with a date
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing documents, artifacts, artworks, or objects that lack explicit dating; appropriate in archival, museum, and scholarly contexts; often followed by estimates using "circa"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when a date exists but is simply not visible; inappropriate when the object is clearly from a known period; don't use for modern items with manufacturing dates
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - undated manuscript
   - undated but attributed to circa
   - undated letter
   - undated photograph
   - undated artifact from circa
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + dat(e) + -ed (past participle/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: "un-" (Old English) + "date" (Latin data) + "-ed" → "undated" (modern formation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's an undated photo, but probably circa 1920s.
   - The letter is undated but written circa 1850.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manuscript remains undated but is attributed to circa 1380 on paleographic grounds.
   - This undated ceramic vessel is tentatively assigned to circa 200 BCE.

1. **Adjective**: tentative
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not certain or fixed; provisional or uncertain
   - Subject to further confirmation or revision
   
   **Synonyms**: provisional, preliminary, uncertain, hesitant, conditional
   
   **Antonyms**: definite, certain, confirmed, final, conclusive
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing dates, conclusions, or assessments that are subject to change or require further verification; appropriate in academic, scholarly, and professional contexts; often precedes "circa" dates that may be revised
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when information is confirmed or final; inappropriate when conveying confidence in findings; don't use for well-established facts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tentative date of circa
   - tentative conclusion
   - tentative attribution to circa
   - tentative estimate
   - tentative identification
   
   **Root Analysis**: tent(ative) from Latin "tentare" (to try, test) + -ive (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin → tentativus (trying, testing) → French "tentatif" → English "tentative" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They gave a tentative date of circa 1950.
   - It's a tentative guess—circa 100 people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A tentative chronology places the site's occupation at circa 1200-1400 CE.
   - The tentative attribution dates the painting to circa 1510, pending further analysis.

1. **Adjective**: rough
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not exact or detailed; approximate or preliminary
   - Lacking refinement or precision
   
   **Synonyms**: approximate, ballpark, preliminary, crude, inexact
   
   **Antonyms**: precise, exact, detailed, refined, accurate
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing estimates, calculations, or dates that are very approximate and subject to significant error; appropriate in informal planning and preliminary discussions; often used before "estimate" paired with "circa"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in formal academic or professional writing where "approximate" is preferred; inappropriate when implying careful calculation; don't use when precision exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rough estimate of circa
   - rough calculation
   - rough figure
   - rough date of circa
   - rough approximation
   
   **Root Analysis**: rough (from Old English "ruh" = rough, coarse)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → ruh (rough, coarse, hairy) → Middle English "rough" → extended figurative meaning to "approximate" (modern usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Give me a rough date—circa 1800?
   - That's a rough estimate, circa 50 people.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A rough chronological framework places the phase circa 800-1000 CE.
   - Preliminary analysis provides a rough date of circa 1450 for the manuscript.
