# Into - Preposition Usage

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Movement or direction from outside to inside a space, area, or container; expressing entry or penetration
   - *(Temporal)* Continuing through a period of time; extending to or past a point in time
   - *(Abstract)* Change of state, form, or condition; transformation or conversion; involvement or engagement in an activity or situation
   
   **Synonyms**: to (in some contexts), inside, within, toward the interior of
   
   **Antonyms**: out of, from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Use "into" to express movement from outside to inside, transformation of state, or deep involvement/engagement; common in both physical and metaphorical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "into" with "in to" (two separate words); avoid using "into" for static location (use "in" instead); not used for destination without entry (use "to")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into the room, into pieces, into consideration, into effect, into practice
   - **Verb + prep**: walk into, run into, look into, get into, turn into, translate into, divide into, break into, bump into
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare with adjectives in standard usage)
   - **Noun + prep**: entry into, insight into, inquiry into, investigation into, transition into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (combining two prepositions to express motion toward the interior)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "intō" → Middle English "into" (combining "in" and "to" to emphasize directional movement with entry)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I walked into the coffee shop and saw my friend sitting there.
   - She's really into hiking these days—goes out every weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will look into the matter and provide recommendations by next month.
   - The research findings translate into practical applications for clinical practice.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Contact or collision with something; expressing impact
   - *(Abstract)* Division or separation into parts or categories
   
   **Synonyms**: against (for collision), to (for division contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, clear of
   
   **When to Use**: Use "into" for physical collisions or when dividing/breaking things apart; common in both literal and figurative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For gentle contact, use "against" or "on"; for combination (opposite of division), use other prepositions like "together"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into the wall, into groups, into sections, into categories
   - **Verb + prep**: crash into, bump into, slam into, divide into, split into, break into, cut into, separate into
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not commonly used)
   - **Noun + prep**: collision into, division into, separation into, breakdown into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "intō" → expressing movement toward and impact/penetration
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He wasn't paying attention and walked right into the door.
   - Let's split into smaller groups for the discussion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vehicle crashed into the barrier at high speed.
   - The data were divided into three distinct categories for analysis.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Deep interest, enthusiasm, or involvement in something; expressing passion or engagement
   - *(Abstract)* Investigation, examination, or research of a topic
   
   **Synonyms**: interested in, engaged with, absorbed in, passionate about
   
   **Antonyms**: indifferent to, disengaged from, uninterested in
   
   **When to Use**: Use "into" to express enthusiasm or when describing investigation/research activities; very common in conversational English for hobbies and interests
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For mild interest, "interested in" may be more appropriate; avoid in very formal academic writing where "engaged in research on" might be preferred
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into music, into sports, into technology, into the details
   - **Verb + prep**: be into, get into, look into, delve into, inquire into, investigate into
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: interest into (rare), inquiry into, investigation into, research into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (metaphorical extension of physical entry)
   
   **Etymology**: Extension from physical entry to mental/emotional engagement (20th century colloquial development)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really into jazz music lately—it's all I listen to.
   - Are you into video games at all?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The commission is looking into allegations of financial misconduct.
   - This research delves into the complexities of urban planning in developing nations.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Transformation or conversion from one form, state, or condition to another
   - *(Mathematical)* Division operation expressing how many times one number fits into another
   
   **Synonyms**: to (in transformation contexts), toward (for gradual change)
   
   **Antonyms**: from, out of (for reverse transformation)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "into" for describing changes of state, conversions, or transformations; essential for expressing causative change
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For describing static states, use "as" or "in the form of"; don't use for comparisons (use "to")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into a butterfly, into liquid, into reality, into law, into action
   - **Verb + prep**: turn into, transform into, change into, convert into, develop into, evolve into, grow into, translate into
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: transformation into, conversion into, change into, development into, evolution into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (expressing directional movement toward a new state)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical extension from physical entry to state transformation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The caterpillar turned into a beautiful butterfly.
   - She's grown into a confident young woman.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal was subsequently translated into legislative action.
   - Water converts into steam when heated to 100 degrees Celsius.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Extending or continuing past a particular time or into a time period
   - *(Abstract)* Reaching deep or far into something (time, space, or abstract domain)
   
   **Synonyms**: through, past, beyond (for temporal extension)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, until (for time boundaries)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "into" to describe continuation past a time point or deep penetration into a temporal/spatial domain
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For precise time boundaries, use "until"; for starting points, use "from"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into the night, into the future, into next week, into the evening, into late hours
   - **Verb + prep**: extend into, continue into, stretch into, reach into, carry into
   - **Adjective + prep**: far into, deep into, well into
   - **Noun + prep**: extension into, continuation into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (expressing movement toward/within a temporal space)
   
   **Etymology**: Extension of spatial "entry" concept to temporal domains
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We talked well into the night and didn't notice the time.
   - This project is going to stretch into next month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The negotiations extended into the early morning hours.
   - The implications of this policy will reach far into the future.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Coming to possess, obtain, or acquire something; expressing achievement or attainment
   - *(Abstract)* Being obligated, committed, or bound by something
   
   **Synonyms**: to (in some contexts), toward possession/obligation
   
   **Antonyms**: out of, free from (for obligations)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "into" for describing acquisition of positions, states, or obligations; common in expressions about careers, relationships, and commitments
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simple possession without entry/transition, use "have" or other verbs
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into debt, into trouble, into a contract, into an agreement, into office
   - **Verb + prep**: get into, come into (inheritance), enter into, fall into, run into (debt)
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: entry into (a profession), descent into, fall into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (expressing movement toward a state or condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical extension from physical entry to abstract states and conditions
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He got into trouble for skipping class.
   - They fell into debt after buying the house.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The parties entered into a binding agreement on the specified terms.
   - Upon his election, the president came into office with an ambitious agenda.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning or regarding a particular subject; about or relating to
   - *(Spatial/Abstract)* Penetrating beneath the surface to examine or understand something deeply
   
   **Synonyms**: about, regarding, concerning, on (all for "concerning" meaning)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from (metaphorically), superficially (as adverb contrast)
   
   **When to Use**: Use "into" with verbs of investigation, inquiry, or examination; emphasizes depth of engagement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For casual mention or reference, use "about"; for formal academic citations, use "regarding" or "concerning"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into the matter, into the case, into the situation, into the problem
   - **Verb + prep**: look into, inquire into, investigate into, delve into, dig into, go into (details)
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not standard)
   - **Noun + prep**: inquiry into, investigation into, insight into, research into, probe into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (metaphor: entering deeply into a subject)
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical extension from physical penetration to intellectual/investigative depth
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll look into that problem and get back to you.
   - Let's not go into all the details right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The panel conducted a thorough inquiry into the alleged violations.
   - This study provides valuable insight into the mechanisms underlying the phenomenon.
