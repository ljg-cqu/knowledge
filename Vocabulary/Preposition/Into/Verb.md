# Into - Phrasal Verbs

1. **Verb**: run into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To meet someone unexpectedly or by chance (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To collide with something while moving (transitive)
   - *(Sense 3)* To encounter a problem, difficulty, or obstacle (transitive)
   - *(Sense 4)* To reach or total a particular amount (intransitive, usually with costs or numbers)
   
   **Synonyms**: bump into, encounter, meet by chance, collide with, come across, face (depending on sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid, miss, bypass, steer clear of
   
   **When to Use**: Use for unexpected meetings, physical collisions, encountering problems, or when costs/amounts reach high levels; common in both casual and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For planned meetings, use "meet with"; for intentional contact, use "hit" or "strike"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - run into trouble
   - run into difficulties
   - run into an old friend
   - run into debt
   - costs run into millions
   
   **Root Analysis**: run (Old English "rinnan") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "rinnan" (to flow, run) + "into" → Middle English phrasal development for various encounter meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I ran into Sarah at the grocery store yesterday—what a surprise!
   - Be careful not to run into that wall while backing up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project has run into significant budgetary constraints.
   - Renovation costs are expected to run into hundreds of thousands of dollars.

1. **Verb**: look into
   **Meaning**: 
   - To investigate, examine, or research something; to inquire about or study a matter in detail (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: investigate, examine, research, explore, inquire into, study, check out
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, dismiss, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing investigation or examination of issues, problems, or topics; appropriate for formal and informal contexts; very common in professional/business English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For casual glancing or quick checking, use "check"; for legal/official investigation, "investigate" alone may be more formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look into the matter
   - look into the possibility
   - look into options
   - look into allegations
   - thoroughly look into
   
   **Root Analysis**: look (Old English "lōcian") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "lōcian" (to see, gaze) + "into" → metaphorical extension from physical sight to intellectual examination
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll look into that issue and let you know what I find.
   - We should look into getting a new car soon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will look into the allegations and report its findings.
   - Management is looking into various cost-reduction strategies for the coming fiscal year.

1. **Verb**: get into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To enter a place, vehicle, or enclosed space (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To be admitted to or accepted by an institution, program, or organization (transitive)
   - *(Sense 3)* To become involved in or start doing an activity, hobby, or habit (transitive)
   - *(Sense 4)* To begin discussing or dealing with a topic (transitive)
   - *(Sense 5)* To enter a particular state or condition, often negative (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: enter, board (for vehicles), join, start, begin, become involved in, fall into
   
   **Antonyms**: get out of, exit, leave, avoid, quit
   
   **When to Use**: Versatile phrasal verb for physical entry, admission, involvement, or state changes; very common in everyday English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For formal writing about admission, use "gain admission to"; for exit, use "get out of" or "leave"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get into trouble
   - get into a car
   - get into college
   - get into the habit
   - get into details
   - get into shape
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (Old Norse "geta") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse "geta" (to obtain, reach) + "into" → versatile phrasal verb developing multiple related meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She got into Yale—her parents are so proud!
   - I've really gotten into cooking lately; it's become my favorite hobby.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before we get into the details of the proposal, let me provide some background.
   - The company got into financial difficulties following the market downturn.

1. **Verb**: turn into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To transform or change from one form, state, or thing into another (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To enter by turning, as into a street or driveway (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: transform into, change into, become, convert into, evolve into, develop into
   
   **Antonyms**: remain, stay as, revert from
   
   **When to Use**: Use for transformations, metamorphoses, or changes of state; can be literal (physical change) or figurative (character development, situation changes)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For gradual evolution without clear transformation, use "develop into"; for mechanical conversion, "convert" alone may be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - turn into a nightmare
   - turn into reality
   - turn water into ice
   - turn into a butterfly
   - turn left into the street
   
   **Root Analysis**: turn (Old English "turnian") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "turnian" (to rotate, change) + "into" → emphasis on directional change or transformation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The nice weather suddenly turned into a terrible storm.
   - Turn into the next driveway on your right.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The caterpillar undergoes metamorphosis to turn into a butterfly.
   - The prototype was successfully turned into a commercial product.

1. **Verb**: break into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To enter a building, car, or place illegally or by force (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To suddenly begin an activity, especially singing, laughing, or running (transitive)
   - *(Sense 3)* To successfully enter a profession, market, or field (transitive)
   - *(Sense 4)* To interrupt or interject oneself into something (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: burglarize, force entry (sense 1), burst into (sense 2), enter (sense 3), interrupt (sense 4)
   
   **Antonyms**: exit, leave, stay out of, respect boundaries
   
   **When to Use**: Use for forced/illegal entry, sudden actions, or entering competitive fields; meaning depends heavily on context
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For legal entry, use "enter"; for gradual career development, use "enter" or "join"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - break into a house
   - break into song
   - break into laughter
   - break into the market
   - break into applause
   
   **Root Analysis**: break (Old English "brecan") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "brecan" (to shatter, rupture) + "into" → extension from physical breaking to sudden actions and market entry
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Someone broke into my car last night and stole my laptop.
   - When she heard the news, she broke into tears.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company is attempting to break into the Asian market.
   - Burglars broke into the facility despite enhanced security measures.

1. **Verb**: go into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To enter a place, room, or building (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To discuss or explain something in detail (transitive)
   - *(Sense 3)* To join or enter a profession, field, or state (transitive)
   - *(Sense 4)* To be used for or invested in something (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: enter, discuss in detail, elaborate on, pursue (career), invest in
   
   **Antonyms**: exit, leave, avoid, summarize briefly
   
   **When to Use**: Versatile phrasal verb for physical entry, detailed discussion, career choices, or resource allocation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For surface-level mention, use "mention" or "touch on"; for exit, use "go out of" or "leave"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go into detail
   - go into business
   - go into effect
   - go into debt
   - go into politics
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (Old English "gān") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gān" (to move, proceed) + "into" → basic motion verb with directional preposition
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's not go into all that right now—it's too complicated.
   - He went into teaching after graduating from college.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new regulations will go into effect on January 1st.
   - Substantial resources went into developing this innovative technology.

1. **Verb**: bump into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To meet someone by chance or unexpectedly (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To collide with something or someone accidentally (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: run into, encounter, meet by chance, collide with, hit accidentally
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid, miss, plan to meet
   
   **When to Use**: Common in casual conversation for unexpected meetings or accidental physical contact; slightly more informal than "run into"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For planned meetings, use "meet"; for formal writing, consider "encounter" or "meet unexpectedly"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bump into an old friend
   - bump into someone on the street
   - bump into the furniture
   - literally bump into
   
   **Root Analysis**: bump (imitative origin) + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Imitative origin (sound of collision) + "into" → 16th century development for accidental contact, later extended to social meetings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I bumped into Tom at the mall yesterday—haven't seen him in years!
   - Watch out, you're about to bump into that table.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - During the conference, I had the opportunity to bump into several colleagues from previous collaborations.
   - The vehicle accidentally bumped into the barrier during parking.

1. **Verb**: come into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To inherit or receive money, property, or possessions (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To begin to have or acquire a quality, state, or condition (transitive)
   - *(Sense 3)* To enter a place or situation (transitive)
   - *(Sense 4)* To become relevant or applicable (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: inherit, acquire, receive, obtain, gain, enter
   
   **Antonyms**: lose, give away, forfeit, exit
   
   **When to Use**: Use for inheritance, acquisition of qualities/states, or entering situations; often implies passive receipt rather than active pursuit
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For active earning or achievement, use "earn" or "achieve"; for giving, use "come out of"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - come into money
   - come into effect
   - come into play
   - come into being
   - come into power
   - come into one's own
   
   **Root Analysis**: come (Old English "cuman") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "cuman" (to move toward, arrive) + "into" → emphasis on arrival at a state or possession
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She came into a fortune when her aunt passed away.
   - He's really come into his own as a leader lately.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legislation will come into effect at the beginning of the fiscal year.
   - Several factors come into play when evaluating this complex situation.

1. **Verb**: fall into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To enter or drop into a lower place, hole, or trap (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To enter a state or condition, often negative or unintentionally (transitive)
   - *(Sense 3)* To be classified or categorized as belonging to a group (transitive)
   - *(Sense 4)* To begin doing something easily or naturally (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: drop into, descend into, enter (a state), be classified as, slip into
   
   **Antonyms**: rise from, emerge from, avoid, climb out of
   
   **When to Use**: Often implies unintentional or gradual entry into states, categories, or situations; can be literal (physical falling) or figurative
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For intentional entry, use "enter into"; for positive states, other verbs may be more appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fall into debt
   - fall into place
   - fall into a trap
   - fall into a routine
   - fall into disrepair
   - fall into categories
   
   **Root Analysis**: fall (Old English "feallan") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "feallan" (to drop from height) + "into" → extension from physical falling to metaphorical descent into states
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We just fell into a routine of having coffee together every morning.
   - He fell into the pool while trying to grab his phone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The property has fallen into disrepair due to years of neglect.
   - These phenomena fall into three distinct categories based on their characteristics.

1. **Verb**: dive into
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To jump or plunge headfirst into water (transitive)
   - *(Sense 2)* To start doing something enthusiastically or energetically, especially without hesitation (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: plunge into, jump into, throw oneself into, immerse oneself in, tackle enthusiastically
   
   **Antonyms**: hesitate, avoid, withdraw from, approach cautiously
   
   **When to Use**: Use for enthusiastic or vigorous entry into activities, topics, or situations; implies full commitment and energy
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For cautious or gradual approaches, use "ease into" or "gradually approach"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - dive into work
   - dive into a book
   - dive into the pool
   - dive into details
   - dive into a project
   
   **Root Analysis**: dive (Middle English "diven") + into (compound preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "diven" (to plunge) + "into" → extension from physical diving to metaphorical immersion in activities
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm ready to dive into this new video game!
   - She dove into the pool from the high board.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let us now dive into the specifics of the proposed methodology.
   - The team dove into the project with remarkable enthusiasm and dedication.
