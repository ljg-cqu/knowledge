# Into - Multi-Word Expressions

1. **Expression**: take into account (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To consider or include something when making a decision or judgment; to factor something into one's thinking
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, factor in, bear in mind, take into consideration, allow for
   
   **Antonyms**: overlook, ignore, disregard, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: Use in decision-making contexts, planning, evaluation, or analysis; appropriate for both professional and casual contexts; very common in business and academic English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very informal contexts, simpler phrases like "think about" may be more natural
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - take something/this/that into account
   - take into consideration
   - taking into account (present participle, often sentence-initial)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Take" pairs with "into account" as a fixed expression emphasizing the incorporation of information into one's mental reckoning; "account" here refers to one's calculation or assessment
   
   **Root Analysis**: take (Old English "tacan") + into + account (Old French "acont")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to take the weather into account when planning your trip.
   - I didn't take into account how long it would actually take.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis takes into account multiple variables affecting market performance.
   - Taking into account the budgetary constraints, we recommend the following approach.

1. **Expression**: put into practice (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To apply or implement ideas, theories, or plans in real situations; to execute or carry out something learned or planned
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: implement, apply, execute, carry out, put into action, actualize
   
   **Antonyms**: theorize, plan only, leave unimplemented
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing the implementation of ideas, skills, or plans; common in educational, professional, and self-improvement contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For instant actions without prior planning, use simpler verbs like "do" or "act"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - put theory into practice
   - put learning into practice
   - put ideas into practice
   - put into action
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: The phrase bridges the gap between abstract concepts and concrete reality; "practice" here contrasts with "theory"
   
   **Root Analysis**: put (Old English "putian") + into + practice (Greek "praktikos" → Latin "practica")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I learned a lot in the workshop, but now I need to put it into practice.
   - It's time to put your training into practice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The recommendations must be put into practice to achieve the desired outcomes.
   - Participants were encouraged to put the learned techniques into practice immediately.

1. **Expression**: come into effect (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To begin to be enforced or implemented; to become officially active or operative (typically for laws, rules, policies)
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: become effective, take effect, be implemented, be enacted, become operational
   
   **Antonyms**: expire, be repealed, cease, become void
   
   **When to Use**: Use for laws, regulations, policies, contracts, or agreements that transition from approved to active status; primarily formal/professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For informal arrangements, use simpler language like "start" or "begin"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - go into effect
   - enter into effect
   - bring into effect
   - when it comes into effect
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Legal and administrative language; "effect" here means "operation" or "force"; the phrase marks the transition from dormant to active status
   
   **Root Analysis**: come + into + effect (Latin "effectus" - accomplishment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new parking rules come into effect next week.
   - When does this policy actually come into effect?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The amendments will come into effect on the first day of the following fiscal year.
   - The treaty came into effect upon ratification by all signatory nations.

1. **Expression**: fall into place (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: Objects falling into their correct positions
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: For elements of a situation to become organized, clear, or resolved; for things to work out naturally and successfully
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: work out, come together, be resolved, become clear, make sense
   
   **Antonyms**: fall apart, become confused, unravel, break down
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing situations where confusion resolves or plans succeed naturally; implies organic resolution without forced effort; common in narrative and reflective contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For situations requiring active problem-solving, this phrase understates effort; use "solve" or "work through" instead
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - everything falls into place
   - things fall into place
   - suddenly fell into place
   - finally fall into place
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Metaphor from puzzle pieces fitting together; suggests natural order emerging from chaos; originated from mechanical/puzzle-solving contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: fall + into + place (each word contributes to the spatial metaphor)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Once I understood the basics, everything else just fell into place.
   - Don't worry, things will fall into place eventually.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As the research progressed, the various components of the theory fell into place.
   - The strategic elements fell into place following the reorganization.

1. **Expression**: look into the matter (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To investigate or examine a particular issue, problem, or situation; to conduct an inquiry
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: investigate the issue, examine the situation, inquire into the matter, research the problem
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore the matter, dismiss the issue, overlook the problem
   
   **When to Use**: Common in professional, business, and formal correspondence when promising to investigate something; signals commitment to follow-up
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual contexts, "check it out" is more natural; avoid overuse as it can sound evasive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - look into the issue
   - look into the situation
   - look into the problem
   - look into the case
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Matter" is formal/neutral terminology for an issue requiring attention; pairs naturally with investigative verbs
   
   **Root Analysis**: look + into + matter (Latin "materia" - subject)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for letting me know—I'll look into the matter right away.
   - Can someone look into this matter and figure out what happened?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Management will look into the matter and respond within five business days.
   - The committee has been asked to look into the matter thoroughly.

1. **Expression**: turn into a nightmare (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To transform from a good or neutral situation into a very difficult, stressful, or problematic one; to deteriorate dramatically
   - *Fixedness*: medium (can vary object: disaster, success, reality, etc.)
   
   **Synonyms**: become a disaster, go wrong, deteriorate badly, become problematic
   
   **Antonyms**: turn out well, go smoothly, work out perfectly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express dramatic negative transformation; common in storytelling, complaints, and describing past experiences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For minor inconveniences, this is hyperbolic; for positive transformations, use "turn into a dream"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - turn into a disaster
   - turn into a mess
   - turn into a dream
   - turn into reality
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Nightmare" is a powerful metaphor for unpleasant experiences; "turn into" signals transformation; together they emphasize unexpected negative change
   
   **Root Analysis**: turn + into + nightmare (Old English "niht" + "mære" - night demon)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our relaxing vacation turned into a nightmare when we all got food poisoning.
   - The simple project turned into a nightmare with all these complications.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The acquisition turned into a nightmare due to unforeseen regulatory complications.
   - What began as routine maintenance turned into a nightmare scenario requiring extensive repairs.

1. **Expression**: get into trouble (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To find oneself in a problematic situation; to face negative consequences for one's actions; to experience difficulties
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: face problems, encounter difficulties, land in trouble, get into hot water
   
   **Antonyms**: stay out of trouble, avoid problems, keep safe
   
   **When to Use**: Common for describing consequences of mistakes, rule-breaking, or risky decisions; used for both serious and minor problems
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For positive challenges or opportunities, use "take on" or "embrace"; "trouble" is inherently negative
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get into serious trouble
   - get someone into trouble
   - get into hot water
   - get into debt (similar pattern)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Get into" implies entry into a state; "trouble" is a common abstract destination; the phrase emphasizes the process of entering an undesirable state
   
   **Root Analysis**: get + into + trouble (Old French "trubler" - to disturb)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If you skip class again, you're going to get into trouble.
   - He got into trouble for using his phone during the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company got into trouble with regulators over its financial reporting practices.
   - Organizations that fail to comply may get into serious legal trouble.

1. **Expression**: come into play (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To become relevant, active, or influential in a situation; to begin to have an effect or importance
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: become relevant, become active, take effect, enter the picture, become a factor
   
   **Antonyms**: become irrelevant, fade away, cease to matter
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing factors, forces, or elements that become relevant or influential; common in analysis, explanation, and strategic discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For things that were always relevant, use "matter" or "are important" instead
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - come into play when...
   - bring into play
   - various factors come into play
   - skills come into play
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Play" here means "action" or "operation"; the phrase suggests elements entering the active area of relevance, like players entering a game
   
   **Root Analysis**: come + into + play (Old English "pleg" - activity)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your experience will really come into play when we start the project.
   - That's where your negotiation skills come into play.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Multiple variables come into play when evaluating investment opportunities.
   - Cultural considerations come into play in international business transactions.

1. **Expression**: break into a smile (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suddenly begin smiling, especially after being serious or neutral; to transition from not smiling to smiling
   - *Fixedness*: medium (can vary: laughter, tears, song, applause, sweat)
   
   **Synonyms**: burst into a smile, suddenly smile, begin to smile
   
   **Antonyms**: stop smiling, become serious, frown
   
   **When to Use**: Use in narrative or descriptive writing to emphasize the suddenness or unexpectedness of an emotional expression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For gradual or unsurprising emotional changes, simpler verbs suffice
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - break into laughter
   - break into tears
   - break into song
   - break into applause
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Break into" emphasizes sudden onset; commonly used with visible emotional expressions and sounds; suggests breaking through a previous state
   
   **Root Analysis**: break + into + smile (Old English "smerian" - related to wonder)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When she heard the news, she broke into a huge smile.
   - He tried to stay serious but eventually broke into a smile.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Upon receiving the award, the recipient broke into a smile of genuine gratitude.
   - The audience broke into enthusiastic applause following the performance.

1. **Expression**: take into consideration (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To think carefully about something; to regard as a relevant factor when making a decision or judgment
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: take into account, consider, factor in, bear in mind, weigh, contemplate
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, ignore, overlook, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: Very similar to "take into account" but slightly more formal; common in official decision-making, legal contexts, and formal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech, use simpler alternatives like "think about" or "remember"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - take something into consideration
   - taking into consideration
   - take all factors into consideration
   - taken into consideration
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Consideration" implies careful, deliberate thought; "take into" suggests incorporating something into one's mental process
   
   **Root Analysis**: take + into + consideration (Latin "considerare" - to observe carefully)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We'll take your feedback into consideration when making the final decision.
   - Please take into consideration that some people have allergies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court will take all evidence into consideration before rendering a verdict.
   - When evaluating candidates, we take into consideration both experience and potential.

1. **Expression**: fly into a rage (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suddenly become extremely angry; to have a sudden, intense outburst of anger
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: become furious, lose one's temper, explode with anger, become enraged
   
   **Antonyms**: calm down, stay composed, remain peaceful
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe sudden, intense anger; emphasizes the speed and intensity of the emotional change; often used in narrative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For mild irritation or gradual anger, use "get annoyed" or "become angry"; this phrase is for dramatic anger
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fly into a fury
   - fly into a panic
   - fly into a temper
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Fly" suggests rapid, uncontrolled movement; "into a rage" describes the destination state; together they emphasize explosive emotion
   
   **Root Analysis**: fly + into + rage (Old French "rage" - madness)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My dad flew into a rage when he saw the damage to his car.
   - She flies into a rage whenever anyone criticizes her work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manager reportedly flew into a rage during the board meeting.
   - Historical accounts indicate the king would fly into a rage at the slightest provocation.

1. **Expression**: put into words (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To express thoughts, feelings, or ideas verbally or in writing; to articulate something that is difficult to express
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: express, articulate, verbalize, phrase, formulate
   
   **Antonyms**: leave unsaid, keep to oneself, remain silent about
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing the challenge of expressing complex emotions, abstract ideas, or ineffable experiences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simple statements or easy-to-express ideas, use "say" or "explain"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - hard to put into words
   - difficult to put into words
   - can't put into words
   - put feelings into words
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Emphasizes the transformation of internal, abstract thoughts/feelings into external, concrete language; "words" represents the medium of expression
   
   **Root Analysis**: put + into + words (Old English "word" - speech)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's hard to put into words how grateful I am.
   - I can't really put into words what I'm feeling right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The experience was so profound it was difficult to put into words.
   - Participants were asked to put their observations into words using the provided framework.
