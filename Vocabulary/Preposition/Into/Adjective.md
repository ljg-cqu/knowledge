# Into - Related Adjectives

1. **Adjective**: deep (comp: deeper, sup: deepest)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Extending far down from the surface or far in from the edge; penetrating far into something (gradable)
   - *(Sense 2)* Profound in quality or intensity; thorough or extreme
   - *(Sense 3)* Fully absorbed or immersed in an activity or state
   
   **Synonyms**: profound, thorough, far-reaching, intense, comprehensive, immersed
   
   **Antonyms**: shallow, superficial, surface-level, cursory, brief
   
   **When to Use**: With "into" in phrases like "deep into the night" or "dive deep into"; emphasizes extent or intensity of penetration or engagement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Deep into" requires appropriate context; don't use for shallow engagement or surface activities
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deep into the night
   - deep into thought
   - dive deep into
   - delve deep into
   - far/well deep into
   
   **Root Analysis**: deep (Old English "deop" - extending far down)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "deop" (profound, awful, mysterious) → Germanic roots → English with both physical and metaphorical depth meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We talked deep into the night and didn't notice the time passing.
   - I'm getting deep into this new book series—can't put it down.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The discussion extended deep into the evening as delegates sought consensus.
   - Research that delves deep into the underlying mechanisms is critically needed.

1. **Adjective**: serious (non-gradable in some uses, gradable in others)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Significant or severe in nature; causing concern or requiring attention (gradable)
   - *(Sense 2)* Earnest or sincere rather than joking or casual
   - *(Sense 3)* Demanding careful thought or application; not trivial
   
   **Synonyms**: severe, significant, grave, major, substantial, considerable
   
   **Antonyms**: minor, trivial, insignificant, light, casual, inconsequential
   
   **When to Use**: Commonly modifies "trouble," "debt," "difficulties" in "into" phrases; intensifies the severity of negative states
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "serious" to describe positive outcomes; it emphasizes gravity and concern
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get into serious trouble
   - fall into serious debt
   - run into serious problems
   - serious consideration
   - serious consequences
   
   **Root Analysis**: seri (from Latin serius - grave, earnest) + -ous (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "serius" (weighty, important) → French "sérieux" → English; maintained meaning of gravity and importance
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You're going to get into serious trouble if you keep doing that.
   - They fell into serious debt after the business failed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company has run into serious financial difficulties requiring immediate intervention.
   - Failure to comply may result in serious legal consequences.

1. **Adjective**: full (comp: fuller, sup: fullest)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Complete; with nothing left out; entire or comprehensive (can be gradable or non-gradable depending on context)
   - *(Sense 2)* Maximum in amount, degree, or extent
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, total, thorough, comprehensive, entire, maximum
   
   **Antonyms**: partial, incomplete, limited, restricted, abbreviated
   
   **When to Use**: In phrases like "go into full detail" or "put into full effect"; emphasizes completeness and thoroughness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when partial or limited action is intended; "full" commits to completeness
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go into full detail
   - put into full effect
   - come into full bloom/flower
   - swing into full production
   - burst into full flame
   
   **Root Analysis**: full (Old English "full" - containing all it can hold)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "full" → Germanic roots → maintained meaning of completeness and maximality
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't want to go into full detail, but basically it didn't work out.
   - The plan goes into full effect starting Monday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy was put into full effect following board approval.
   - The initiative will swing into full operation in the second quarter.

1. **Adjective**: hot (comp: hotter, sup: hottest)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having a high temperature; very warm (gradable)
   - *(Sense 2)* Causing trouble or difficulty; dangerous or risky (informal, idiomatic)
   - *(Sense 3)* Currently popular, exciting, or trendy
   
   **Synonyms**: heated, warm, burning; troubled, difficult (in idiomatic sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: cold, cool; safe, trouble-free, comfortable
   
   **When to Use**: In idiom "get into hot water" meaning to get into trouble; figurative use emphasizing discomfort or danger
   
   **When NOT to Use**: This idiomatic meaning is informal; in formal writing, use "get into trouble" or "face difficulties"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get into hot water
   - in hot water
   - hot topic
   - hot pursuit
   - hot under the collar
   
   **Root Analysis**: hot (Old English "hat" - having high temperature)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "hat" → Germanic roots → metaphorical extension to trouble/danger from physical heat/discomfort
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He got into hot water with his boss for missing the deadline.
   - You're going to get into hot water if you don't follow the rules.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The executive found himself in hot water following the controversial decision. (semi-formal, better to use "faced criticism" in very formal contexts)
   - The company got into trouble with regulators over compliance issues. (preferred formal alternative)

1. **Adjective**: greater (comparative of "great", no superlative in these uses)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Larger in amount, size, or importance; more extensive or significant (comparative, always attributive in "into greater" phrases)
   - Used to indicate increase or expansion in scale or scope
   
   **Synonyms**: larger, more extensive, more significant, broader, wider, increased
   
   **Antonyms**: lesser, smaller, reduced, decreased, diminished
   
   **When to Use**: In phrases about expansion or increase; "develop into greater" or "grow into greater"; emphasizes upward progression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Greater" is comparative; requires implicit or explicit comparison; don't use for neutral descriptions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - develop into something greater
   - grow into greater responsibility
   - expand into greater markets
   - evolve into greater complexity
   - blossom into something greater
   
   **Root Analysis**: great (Old English "grēat" - big, thick) + -er (comparative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "grēat" → comparative form indicating increased magnitude or importance
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This small project could grow into something much greater.
   - She's developed into a greater leader than anyone expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative has evolved into a greater undertaking than initially anticipated.
   - The organization seeks to expand into greater markets in the Asia-Pacific region.

1. **Adjective**: considerable (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Notably large in size, amount, or extent; substantial or significant enough to merit attention (non-gradable; already expresses degree)
   
   **Synonyms**: substantial, significant, notable, extensive, large, appreciable
   
   **Antonyms**: negligible, insignificant, small, minor, trivial
   
   **When to Use**: Commonly modifies nouns in "into" phrases expressing magnitude; "go into considerable detail," "fall into considerable debt"; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Non-gradable; don't say "very considerable" or "more considerable"; use "more significant" or "greater" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go into considerable detail
   - fall into considerable debt
   - put considerable effort into
   - considerable attention
   - considerable resources
   
   **Root Analysis**: consider (Latin "considerare") + -able (adjective suffix meaning "worthy of")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "considerare" (to observe carefully) → French → English; meaning evolved to "worthy of consideration" then "substantial"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The project went into considerable detail about the historical background.
   - They fell into considerable debt trying to save the business.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report goes into considerable detail regarding methodological considerations.
   - The organization has fallen into considerable financial difficulties.

1. **Adjective**: financial (non-gradable, attributive only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to money, banking, investments, or management of funds; pertaining to finance (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: monetary, fiscal, economic, pecuniary, budgetary
   
   **Antonyms**: non-financial, non-monetary (though these are more limiting terms than true antonyms)
   
   **When to Use**: Commonly modifies "trouble," "difficulties," "debt" in "into" phrases; specifies the type of problem as money-related
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Non-gradable; don't say "very financial" or "more financial"; it either relates to finance or doesn't
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get into financial trouble
   - fall into financial difficulty
   - run into financial problems
   - financial debt
   - financial crisis
   
   **Root Analysis**: financ (from Latin finis - end, settlement) + -ial (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "finis" (end) → Old French "finer" (to end, settle a debt) → "finance" → English adjective form
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The company got into financial trouble and had to lay people off.
   - They ran into financial problems after unexpected medical bills.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization encountered significant financial difficulties during the recession.
   - Several institutions have fallen into financial distress requiring governmental intervention.

1. **Adjective**: further (comparative form, no base or superlative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Additional; more; supplementary to what already exists (comparative but used without explicit comparison)
   - *(Sense 2)* More distant in space or time; extending beyond
   
   **Synonyms**: additional, more, extra, supplementary, added, extended
   
   **Antonyms**: less, reduced, fewer, limited
   
   **When to Use**: In phrases like "go into further detail" or "delve further into"; indicates increased depth or additional information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Often interchangeable with "farther" for physical distance, but "further" preferred for abstract/metaphorical extension
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go into further detail
   - look further into
   - delve further into
   - further investigation
   - further consideration
   
   **Root Analysis**: further (Old English "furthor" - to a greater degree)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "furthor" (more forward, to a greater extent) → comparative form that functions independently
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's not go into further detail right now—we can discuss it later.
   - I'll look further into the problem and get back to you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee recommends further investigation into the alleged violations.
   - We will not go into further detail at this juncture pending additional data.

1. **Adjective**: practical (non-gradable in most uses)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to actual use or action rather than theory or ideas; concerned with what is useful or effective (non-gradable)
   - *(Sense 2)* Suitable for a particular purpose; functional and sensible
   
   **Synonyms**: applied, pragmatic, functional, useful, workable, realistic
   
   **Antonyms**: theoretical, impractical, idealistic, abstract, unusable
   
   **When to Use**: Modifies "application," "use," "effect" in "into" phrases; emphasizes real-world implementation versus theory
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Generally non-gradable; don't say "very practical" in "put into practical application" (redundant)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - put into practical application
   - translate into practical terms
   - put into practical use
   - practical consideration
   - practical effect
   
   **Root Analysis**: pract (from Greek praktikos - active) + -ical (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "praktikos" (fit for action) → Latin "practicus" → English with emphasis on application over theory
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's time to put these ideas into practical use.
   - Can we translate this theory into practical terms?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings must be translated into practical applications for clinical settings.
   - These recommendations can be put into practical effect immediately.

1. **Adjective**: immediate (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Occurring or done at once; instant without delay (non-gradable)
   - *(Sense 2)* Nearest in time, space, or relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: instant, instantaneous, prompt, swift, direct, right away
   
   **Antonyms**: delayed, postponed, deferred, eventual, gradual
   
   **When to Use**: Modifies "effect," "action," "implementation" in "into" phrases; emphasizes lack of delay
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Non-gradable; don't say "very immediate" or "more immediate"; use "faster" or "sooner" for comparison
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - put into immediate effect
   - spring into immediate action
   - come into immediate effect
   - immediate implementation
   - immediate response
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (not) + medi (Latin medius - middle) + -ate (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "immediatus" (without anything between) → French → English; emphasizes directness and lack of intermediary
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new rules go into immediate effect, so be careful.
   - We need to put this plan into immediate action.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The emergency measures were put into immediate effect upon declaration.
   - The team sprang into immediate action upon receiving the alert.
