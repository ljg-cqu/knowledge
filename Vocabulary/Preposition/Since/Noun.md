**Note**: "Since" is a preposition and conjunction with no direct noun derivatives. However, the following temporal nouns commonly collocate with "since" in temporal expressions to mark starting points of continuing situations.

1. **Noun**: beginning (C, pl: beginnings)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The point in time or space at which something starts; the first part or earliest stage of something. When used with "since," it marks the absolute origin point of a situation that continues to the present.
   
   **Synonyms**: start, commencement, outset, inception, origin
   
   **Antonyms**: end, conclusion, termination, finish
   
   **When to Use**: Use "beginning" with "since" to emphasize that something has been true from the very start. Appropriate in narrative, descriptions of ongoing situations, and when emphasizing fundamental or original characteristics. Common in both formal and informal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when a specific date or time would be more precise and informative. Don't use when the situation actually began gradually rather than at a clear starting point.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - since the beginning
   - since the very beginning
   - since the beginning of time
   - from the beginning
   - at the beginning
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (intensive prefix) + -gin (Old English *beginnan* = to begin) + -ning (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *beginning* from *beginnan* (to begin) → Middle English *biginning* → Modern "beginning"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been rooting for them since the beginning of the season.
   - Since the beginning, this project has been more work than we expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has maintained these principles since the beginning of its operations in 1985.
   - Since the beginning of the study period, significant trends have emerged in the data.

2. **Noun**: childhood (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The period during which a person is a child; the state or time of being a child. When used with "since," it marks experiences, conditions, or relationships that have persisted from one's early years.
   
   **Synonyms**: youth (partially overlapping), early years, formative years
   
   **Antonyms**: adulthood, maturity
   
   **When to Use**: Use "childhood" with "since" to indicate that something has been true throughout one's life from an early age. Common in biographical narrative, personal histories, and discussions of long-standing traits, relationships, or conditions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the situation began in adolescence or adulthood. Don't use when more precise age specification is needed or appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - since childhood
   - since early childhood
   - since my childhood
   - childhood friend (since childhood)
   - childhood memories
   
   **Root Analysis**: child + -hood (state or condition suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *cild* (child) + *-hād* (condition, state) → Middle English *childhod* → Modern "childhood"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been terrified of spiders since childhood—can't stand them!
   - We've been best friends since childhood, almost thirty years now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The patient has experienced chronic respiratory issues since childhood.
   - The subject demonstrated exceptional musical aptitude since childhood, beginning piano instruction at age four.

3. **Noun**: inception (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The establishment or starting point of an institution, organization, or project; the beginning of something, especially something significant or formal. Used with "since" in formal contexts to mark organizational or project origins.
   
   **Synonyms**: founding, establishment, commencement, creation, initiation
   
   **Antonyms**: dissolution, termination, closure, end
   
   **When to Use**: Use "inception" with "since" in formal, professional, or academic contexts when discussing organizations, programs, policies, or projects. Appropriate for corporate histories, institutional descriptions, and formal documentation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (too formal—use "start" or "beginning"). Don't use for personal experiences or informal situations. Not appropriate for natural processes or phenomena.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - since its inception
   - since the inception of
   - from inception
   - at inception
   - since inception
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *inceptio* = beginning, undertaking (from *incipere* = to begin, undertake)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *incipere* (to begin: *in-* = in, on + *capere* = to take) → *inceptio* → Old French → Middle English → Modern "inception"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *Typically formal; avoid in casual contexts (use "since it started" instead)*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The program has served over 50,000 participants since its inception in 2010.
   - Since inception, the fund has generated consistent returns above the market average.

4. **Noun**: retirement (C/U, pl: retirements when countable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action or fact of leaving one's job and ceasing to work, typically upon reaching a particular age; the period of one's life after leaving employment. Used with "since" to mark significant life transition and subsequent period.
   
   **Synonyms**: leaving work, finishing one's career
   
   **Antonyms**: employment, work, career beginning
   
   **When to Use**: Use "retirement" with "since" to describe changes or continuities that began when someone stopped working. Common in biographical narrative, discussions of lifestyle changes, and descriptions of retirees' activities.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing current workers or those not yet retired. Don't use when early career transitions are the focus.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - since retirement
   - since his/her retirement
   - since retiring
   - retirement age
   - retirement years
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + tire (from Old French *tirer* = to draw) + -ment (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: French *retirer* (to withdraw) → *retirement* (withdrawal from service) → English adoption in 16th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Since retirement, Grandpa's been traveling all over the place!
   - Dad's been so much more relaxed since his retirement last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Since retirement, the former executive has served on numerous nonprofit boards.
   - The demographic has experienced significant lifestyle changes since retirement from active employment.

5. **Noun**: arrival (C/U, pl: arrivals)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action or process of arriving; reaching a destination or particular point. When used with "since," it marks the beginning of someone's presence in a place or position as a temporal reference point.
   
   **Synonyms**: coming, reaching, appearance, advent
   
   **Antonyms**: departure, leaving, exit
   
   **When to Use**: Use "arrival" with "since" to mark changes or developments that began when someone or something came to a place. Common in discussing new employees, residents, or the introduction of new elements into a situation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when continuous presence (rather than the arrival event) is the focus. Don't use when departure is more relevant to the narrative.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - since his/her/their arrival
   - since the arrival of
   - upon arrival
   - since arriving
   - new arrival
   
   **Root Analysis**: ar- (to) + riv- (shore, from Latin *ripa*) + -al (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *ad* (to) + *ripa* (shore) → Old French *ariver* → *arrival* (reaching shore/destination) → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Things have been way more fun around here since your arrival!
   - Since the new baby's arrival, they've barely had time for anything else.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Since her arrival as department head, productivity metrics have improved by twenty-three percent.
   - The institution has undergone substantial transformation since the arrival of the new administration.

6. **Noun**: departure (C/U, pl: departures)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action of leaving a place, position, or role; the act of going away. Used with "since" to mark the beginning of an absence or the changes that followed someone's leaving.
   
   **Synonyms**: leaving, exit, going away, withdrawal
   
   **Antonyms**: arrival, coming, appearance, advent
   
   **When to Use**: Use "departure" with "since" to describe changes, developments, or situations that began after someone left. Common in organizational contexts, discussing former employees or members, and explaining consequent changes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when current presence is more relevant than absence. Don't use when arrival or other events are the appropriate temporal marker.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - since his/her/their departure
   - since the departure of
   - since departing
   - sudden departure
   - after departure
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away) + part- (to go) + -ure (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *departir* (to divide, separate) → *departure* → Middle English *departen* → Modern "departure"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The office has been so quiet since Tom's departure—we really miss him.
   - Since her departure, the team dynamics have totally changed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Since the director's departure, the organization has struggled to maintain strategic coherence.
   - The policy has undergone multiple revisions since the departure of its original architect.
