**Note**: "Since" is a preposition and conjunction with no direct verb derivatives. However, the following verbs commonly appear in present perfect and past perfect constructions with "since" to express actions or states continuing from a past point to the present.

1. **Verb**: to be (irregular: am/is/are, was/were, been)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To exist or live; to occupy a position or state. When used with "since" in present perfect ("has/have been"), it expresses a continuing state or situation from a past point until now.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has been + [adjective/noun/location] + since + [time]
   
   **Synonyms**: to exist, to remain (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: not to be, to cease to exist
   
   **When to Use**: Use present perfect "have/has been" with "since" to describe states, conditions, or locations that began at a specific past time and continue to the present. Essential pattern for expressing duration of ongoing situations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid simple past tense with "since" when describing ongoing situations ("I was here since Monday" ✗). Don't use simple present with temporal "since" ("I am here since Monday" ✗).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have been here/there since
   - has been [adjective] since (has been busy since, has been difficult since)
   - have been in [location] since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *bēon* (to be, exist)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *bēon*, *wesan* → merged forms in Middle English → Modern "be" (highly irregular due to multiple root sources)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been super stressed since the project deadline got moved up.
   - She's been in Tokyo since last summer—having the time of her life!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy has been in effect since January 2020 and will remain until further review.
   - The organization has been a leader in sustainability initiatives since its founding.

2. **Verb**: to live
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have one's home in a particular place; to reside. With "since" in present perfect, it expresses residential continuity from a past point to the present.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has lived + [location] + since + [time]
   
   **Synonyms**: to reside, to dwell, to inhabit
   
   **Antonyms**: to move away from, to leave
   
   **When to Use**: Use "have/has lived" with "since" to express how long you've resided in a place from a specific starting time. One of the most common constructions with "since" in everyday conversation and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid simple past with ongoing residence ("I lived here since 2010" ✗—implies no longer living there). Don't use when residence ended (use "lived... from X to Y" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have lived here/there since
   - has lived in [city/country] since
   - have lived together since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *libban* (to live, be alive)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *libban* → Middle English *liven* → Modern "live"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We've lived in this neighborhood since 2015—love it here!
   - I've lived alone since my roommate moved out last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The family has lived in the residence since 1987, maintaining the property's historical character.
   - The demographic has lived in the region since prior to documented historical records.

3. **Verb**: to work
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be engaged in physical or mental activity to achieve a result; to be employed. With "since," it expresses employment or work continuity from a past starting point.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has worked + [location/field] + since + [time]
   
   **Synonyms**: to labor, to be employed, to toil
   
   **Antonyms**: to rest, to be unemployed, to retire
   
   **When to Use**: Use "have/has worked" with "since" to describe employment duration from a specific starting time. Common in professional contexts, CVs, introductions, and career discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid simple past when employment continues ("I worked here since 2018" ✗). Don't use when employment ended (use past simple with time range instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have worked here/there since
   - has worked for [company] since
   - have worked in [field/industry] since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *wyrcan* (to work, perform)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *wyrcan* → Middle English *werken* → Modern "work"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've worked at this company since right after college—seven years now!
   - She's been working from home since the pandemic started.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Dr. Chen has worked in the field of neuroscience since completing her doctorate in 2005.
   - The employee has worked in various capacities within the organization since 2012.

4. **Verb**: to know
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be aware of through observation, inquiry, or information; to have established a relationship with someone. With "since," it expresses duration of knowledge or acquaintance.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has known + [person/fact] + since + [time]
   
   **Synonyms**: to be aware of, to be acquainted with, to be familiar with
   
   **Antonyms**: to be unaware of, to be ignorant of, to be unfamiliar with
   
   **When to Use**: Use "have/has known" with "since" to describe how long you've been acquainted with someone or aware of something. Common in describing relationships, friendships, and duration of awareness.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid simple present ("I know him since 2010" ✗—common error for non-native speakers). Don't use simple past with continuing acquaintance ("I knew her since childhood" ✗).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have known [person] since
   - have known about [something] since
   - have known each other since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *cnāwan* (to know, recognize)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *cnāwan* → Middle English *knowen* → Modern "know"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've known Sarah since we were in kindergarten together—basically my whole life!
   - We've known about the problem since last week but haven't found a solution yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The parties have known each other since their initial business collaboration in 2015.
   - Researchers have known of this phenomenon since the early 1990s but lacked tools for comprehensive analysis.

5. **Verb**: to change
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make or become different; to undergo transformation. With "since" in present perfect, it expresses transformation that occurred at or after a past time point and whose effects continue.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has changed + since + [time/event]
   
   **Synonyms**: to alter, to transform, to modify, to evolve
   
   **Antonyms**: to stay the same, to remain unchanged, to persist
   
   **When to Use**: Use "have/has changed" with "since" to describe transformations that occurred after a specific time and whose effects are still relevant. Common in comparisons, explanations of current states, and describing development.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when change is not actually evident or when specific unchanged aspects are the focus. Don't use when precise change moment is more important than ongoing state.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have/has changed since
   - have/has changed a lot since
   - everything has changed since
   - hasn't changed since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old French *changier* (to change)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *cambiare* (to exchange) → Old French *changier* → Middle English → Modern "change"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You've changed so much since high school—barely recognized you!
   - Things have really changed around here since the new management took over.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulatory environment has changed substantially since the legislation was enacted.
   - Public perception has changed dramatically since the initial findings were published.

6. **Verb**: to remain (formal variant of "to stay")
   **Meaning**: 
   - To continue to be in the same state or condition; to stay unchanged. With "since," it emphasizes continuity and lack of change from a past point.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has remained + [adjective/noun] + since + [time]
   
   **Synonyms**: to stay, to continue to be, to persist
   
   **Antonyms**: to change, to vary, to fluctuate
   
   **When to Use**: Use "have/has remained" with "since" in formal contexts to emphasize stability, consistency, or lack of change from a specific time. More formal than "have/has stayed" or "have/has been."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "stayed" or "been" instead). Don't use when change is the focus rather than stability.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have/has remained unchanged since
   - have/has remained constant since
   - have/has remained in place since
   - have/has remained committed since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *remanere* (re- = back + manere = to stay)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *remanere* (to stay behind) → Old French *remaindre* → Middle English → Modern "remain"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *More formal; casual speakers typically use "stayed" or "been"*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy has remained in effect since its implementation without significant modification.
   - Interest rates have remained historically low since the financial crisis of 2008.

7. **Verb**: to see
   **Meaning**: 
   - To perceive with the eyes; to meet or visit someone. In negative constructions with "since," commonly expresses duration of not having met someone.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has not seen + [person] + since + [time]
   
   **Synonyms**: to perceive, to meet with, to visit, to encounter
   
   **Antonyms**: to miss, to overlook, to avoid
   
   **When to Use**: Use "haven't/hasn't seen" with "since" to express how long it's been since you last met or encountered someone. Very common in everyday conversation when discussing relationships and contact with people.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid simple past when expressing duration ("I didn't see him since Monday" ✗). Don't confuse visual perception with meeting ("I haven't seen that movie since..." typically means last watched, not last encountered).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - haven't seen [person] since
   - haven't seen each other since
   - has been seen since
   - last seen since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *sēon* (to see, perceive)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *sēon* → Middle English *seen* → Modern "see" (irregular verb)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I haven't seen Mike since that party in June—wonder how he's doing.
   - We haven't seen each other since graduation, but we stay in touch online.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The parties have not seen each other since the conclusion of their business relationship in 2019.
   - The defendant has not been seen since the date of the alleged incident.

8. **Verb**: to continue
   **Meaning**: 
   - To persist in an activity or process; to remain in a state or condition without interruption. With "since," emphasizes ongoing persistence from a past starting point.
   - *(Pattern)* have/has continued + [action/state] + since + [time]
   
   **Synonyms**: to persist, to carry on, to maintain, to keep on
   
   **Antonyms**: to stop, to cease, to discontinue, to halt
   
   **When to Use**: Use "have/has continued" with "since" to emphasize persistence and ongoing nature of actions or states. Particularly effective when continuity itself is notable or surprising.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple present perfect of main verb would be clearer ("have continued to work" vs. "have worked"). Don't use when interruptions have occurred.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have/has continued since
   - have/has continued to [verb] since
   - continue unabated since
   - continued growth since
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *continuare* (to make continuous)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *continuus* (uninterrupted) → *continuare* → Old French *continuer* → Middle English → Modern "continue"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The problems have continued since we first noticed them—getting worse actually.
   - We've continued our weekly calls since she moved away last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The trend has continued unabated since the initial observation in 2015.
   - Economic growth has continued at a moderate pace since the recession ended.
