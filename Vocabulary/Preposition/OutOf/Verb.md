1. **Verb**: run out of
   **Meaning**: 
   - To use all of a supply of something so that there is none left; to exhaust or deplete a resource
   - (Transitive with "out of" prepositional phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: exhaust, deplete, use up, consume, finish
   
   **Antonyms**: stock up on, replenish, acquire, accumulate
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing the process of exhausting supplies, resources, time, or patience. Very common in everyday English for describing depletion. Appropriate in both casual and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when describing something that was never present (use "lack" instead). Avoid when supplies are low but not completely exhausted (use "running low on").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - run out of time
   - run out of money
   - run out of patience
   - run out of ideas
   - run out of gas
   
   **Root Analysis**: run (Old English rinnan, to flow) + out + of
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb formation combining "run" (to flow away) + "out of" (from), meaning to flow away from a container until empty (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We ran out of milk this morning, need to grab some.
   - I'm running out of patience with these constant delays.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has run out of allocated funds for this fiscal year.
   - Participants reported running out of time to complete the assigned tasks.

1. **Verb**: get out of
   **Meaning**: 
   - To leave or exit from a place, vehicle, or situation
   - *(Sense 2)* To avoid or escape from an obligation, commitment, or undesirable situation
   - (Transitive with "out of" prepositional phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: exit, leave, escape (for sense 1); avoid, evade, escape, wriggle out of (for sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: enter, get into, go into (for sense 1); commit to, agree to, accept (for sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Use for physical exit from places/vehicles (sense 1) or for avoiding obligations/commitments (sense 2). Very common phrasal verb in everyday English. Versatile across contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid ambiguity between physical exit and obligation avoidance meanings—context must clarify. Don't use when "leave" or "avoid" alone would be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get out of bed
   - get out of the car
   - get out of town
   - get out of doing something
   - get out of trouble
   - can't get out of it
   
   **Root Analysis**: get (Old Norse geta, to obtain/reach) + out + of
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb combining "get" + "out of" (from inside), meaning to move oneself from inside to outside (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Get out of my room, I'm busy!
   - He's trying to get out of washing dishes again.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please ensure all passengers get out of the vehicle at the designated stop.
   - The contractor is attempting to get out of contractual obligations.

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She came out of the exam looking exhausted.
   - Something good might come out of this disaster.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative came out of extensive stakeholder consultation.

1. **Verb**: make out of
   **Meaning**:

   - (Transitive with "out of" prepositional phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: create from, construct from, fashion from, build from
   
   **Antonyms**: destroy, dismantle, break down
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing creation or construction from specific materials. Common in crafts, construction, and creative contexts. More casual than "construct from" or "fashion from."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal writing, prefer "construct from" or "create from." Don't use when the material relationship isn't clear.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - make something out of nothing
   - make out of wood
   - make out of recycled materials
   - make the best out of
   - make a living out of
   
   **Root Analysis**: make (Old English macian, to construct) + out + of
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb combining "make" + "out of" (from materials), meaning to construct from source material (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She made this cool lamp out of old bottles.
   - He's trying to make a business out of his hobby.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The artisan crafted the sculpture from reclaimed materials. (formal prefers "from")
   - The framework was constructed out of established theoretical principles.

   - pull troops out of

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He pulled out of the competition at the last minute.
   - The company is pulling out of that market.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investor decided to pull out of the partnership agreement.
   - Military forces will begin pulling out of the region next quarter.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

   - The counselor successfully talked the student out of dropping out.

1. **Verb**: snap out of
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suddenly recover from or stop being in an undesirable state (usually negative emotion or condition)
   - (Intransitive with "out of" prepositional phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: recover from, shake off, get over, break free from, pull oneself together
   
   **Antonyms**: sink into, fall into, succumb to, remain in
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing sudden recovery from negative emotional or mental states. Often used imperatively as encouragement. Common in informal contexts about mood or attention.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when gradual recovery rather than sudden change is meant. Don't use insensitively about serious mental health conditions. Can sound dismissive if not used carefully.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - snap out of it
   - snap out of a mood
   - snap out of depression
   - snap out of a daze
   - can't snap out of
   
   **Root Analysis**: snap (Middle Dutch or Low German snappen, to seize quickly) + out + of
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb combining "snap" (sudden action) + "out of" (from state), meaning to suddenly exit a state (20th century, relatively recent)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Snap out of it! We have work to do.
   - I can't seem to snap out of this bad mood.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The market is expected to snap out of the current downturn.
   - Participants were encouraged to employ strategies to snap out of unproductive thought patterns.
