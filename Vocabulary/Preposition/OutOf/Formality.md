1. **Original Expression**: We're out of time  
   **Context**: Expressing that no time remains to complete a task or activity
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate that time has been exhausted or depleted
   
   **Casual**: We're out of time  
   *Characteristics*: Direct, simple preposition "out of", contracted form "we're", straightforward statement  
   *Use when*: With friends, family, casual work settings, informal deadlines
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We have run out of time  
   *Characteristics*: Full verb form "have run out", no contractions, complete sentence structure  
   *Use when*: Professional meetings, colleague interactions, standard business contexts
   
   **Formal**: The allocated time has been exhausted  
   *Characteristics*: Passive construction, formal vocabulary ("allocated," "exhausted"), no colloquialisms  
   *Use when*: Official announcements, formal presentations, written reports, authority figures
   
   **Humorous**: Time's up! We've hit the time wall  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor ("hit the time wall"), exclamatory tone, informal idiom  
   *Use when*: Light-hearted situations, friendly competitions, casual team settings  
   *Avoid when*: Serious deadlines, high-stakes situations, professional presentations
   
   **Lively**: Time's completely gone! We've burned through it all  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic language ("completely gone," "burned through"), emphatic tone, vivid imagery  
   *Use when*: Engaging storytelling, motivational contexts, team rallies
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand time has slipped away from us  
   *Characteristics*: Personal acknowledgment ("I understand"), gentle phrasing ("slipped away"), inclusive "us"  
   *Use when*: Deadline pressure situations, when people feel rushed, acknowledging shared time constraints
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher authority figures require formal ("allocated time has been exhausted"); peers allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers or new acquaintances require semi-formal; close relationships allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Critical deadlines or official time limits require formal; routine matters allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more formal time-related communication in professional settings
   - *Medium*: Written reports require formal; verbal reminders can be semi-formal or casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "We're out of time" in formal written reports sounds too casual and unprofessional
   - Using "The allocated time has been exhausted" in casual conversation sounds pretentious and stiff
   - Failing to acknowledge time pressure empathetically when people are stressed
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Anglo-American cultures generally accept direct time communication; some Asian and Latin American cultures prefer more indirect phrasing when discussing time constraints to preserve harmony
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Project deadline with CEO present → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("The allocated time has been exhausted")
   - **Situation 2**: Board game with friends → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual or Humorous ("We're out of time" or "Time's up!")
   - **Situation 3**: Team struggling with tight deadline → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("I understand time has slipped away from us")
   - **Situation 4**: Standard business meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("We have run out of time")

   **Core Intent**: Communicate that something is absolutely not possible or will not be permitted

   *Characteristics*: Very direct, informal phrases, emphatic rejection, colloquial expressions  
   *Use when*: Close friends, family, very informal settings, peer-level discussions

   *Use when*: Professional discussions, colleague interactions, explaining limitations, manager communications

   **Humorous**: That's about as likely as pigs flying / Not in a million years  
   *Characteristics*: Hyperbolic humor, playful impossibility metaphors, exaggerated expressions  
   *Use when*: Light-hearted rejections, close relationships, humor-appropriate contexts  
   *Avoid when*: Serious proposals, professional settings, situations where feelings might be hurt
   
   **Lively**: Absolutely not! That's completely off the table  
   *Characteristics*: Emphatic language ("absolutely"), energetic rejection, vivid metaphor ("off the table")  
   *Use when*: Strong boundary-setting, energetic discussions, situations requiring clear firmness
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand why you'd suggest that, but I'm afraid it's not possible  
   *Characteristics*: Validation phrase ("I understand"), apologetic frame ("I'm afraid"), gentle but firm rejection  
   *Use when*: Disappointing someone, rejecting well-intentioned suggestions, maintaining relationships while declining
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Rejecting superiors' suggestions requires very diplomatic formal language; rejecting subordinates allows semi-formal
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers or clients require formal, polite rejections; close colleagues allow semi-formal or casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Important proposals require formal, reasoned rejections; minor suggestions allow casual responses
   - *Cultural Context*: High-context cultures (Asian, Middle Eastern) require extremely indirect rejections; Western cultures allow more direct "no"
   - *Medium*: Written rejections require formal or semi-formal; verbal discussions allow more casual approaches
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "No way" when rejecting formal proposals sounds dismissive and unprofessional
   - Using "That option is not viable" with friends sounds unnecessarily formal and distant
   - Rejecting ideas too directly without acknowledging the suggester's intent or explaining reasoning
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Rejection varies enormously across cultures. Anglo-American cultures accept relatively direct "no" with brief explanation. East Asian, Middle Eastern, Latin American, and many other cultures require very indirect refusal, often needing multiple softening strategies and rarely using direct negative language.
