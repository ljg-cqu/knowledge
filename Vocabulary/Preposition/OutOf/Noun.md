1. **Noun**: depletion (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action or process of reducing or exhausting a resource, supply, or quantity
   
   **Synonyms**: exhaustion, reduction, draining, consumption, diminishment
   
   **Antonyms**: replenishment, restoration, accumulation, increase
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or technical contexts when discussing resource management, environmental science, inventory control, or energy/nutrient reduction. Appropriate for scientific, business, and environmental discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "running out" is more natural. Don't use when "reduction" or "decrease" would be clearer and less technical.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - resource depletion
   - ozone depletion
   - depletion of reserves
   - nutrient depletion
   - rapid depletion
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down, away) + -plete (fill, from Latin plere) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deplere (to empty out) → depletion (act of depleting, 19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The depletion of our snack supply happened faster than expected.
   - Energy depletion after that workout was real.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examines soil nutrient depletion in intensive agricultural systems.
   - Ozone depletion remains a significant environmental concern requiring international cooperation.

1. **Noun**: exhaustion (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being completely used up or consumed; extreme tiredness
   - *(Sense 2)* Complete depletion of resources or supplies
   
   **Synonyms**: depletion, fatigue, consumption, weariness, draining
   
   **Antonyms**: replenishment, energy, restoration, renewal
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing complete or near-complete depletion of physical, mental, or material resources. Appropriate for discussing fatigue, resource depletion, or complete consumption. Common in medical, environmental, and business contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when partial depletion is meant (use "tiredness" or "reduction" instead). Don't use for minor fatigue or slight resource reduction.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - physical exhaustion
   - mental exhaustion
   - exhaustion of resources
   - point of exhaustion
   - complete exhaustion
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out, thoroughly) + -haust (draw, from Latin haurire) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin exhaustus (drawn out) → exhaustion (state of being drained, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The exhaustion I felt after finals week was unreal.
   - We're facing exhaustion of our budget before the month ends.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The exhaustion of natural resources poses significant sustainability challenges.
   - Patient exhaustion following intensive treatment requires careful management and recovery protocols.

1. **Noun**: shortage (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A state or situation in which something needed cannot be obtained in sufficient amounts; lack or deficiency
   
   **Synonyms**: scarcity, lack, deficiency, insufficiency, shortfall
   
   **Antonyms**: surplus, abundance, excess, plenty
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing insufficient supply relative to demand or need. Appropriate for economic, supply chain, resource management, and crisis contexts. Common in news reporting and business discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when complete absence is meant (use "absence" or "lack" instead). Don't use when temporary unavailability rather than insufficient quantity is the issue.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - food shortage
   - water shortage
   - labor shortage
   - shortage of supply
   - severe shortage
   
   **Root Analysis**: short (insufficient) + -age (noun suffix indicating condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Short (Old English sceort) + -age → shortage (deficiency in supply, 19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a shortage of good coffee shops in this neighborhood.
   - We're dealing with a shortage of clean socks in this house.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The region is experiencing a critical water shortage due to prolonged drought conditions.
   - The labor shortage has significantly impacted the manufacturing sector's production capacity.

1. **Noun**: scarcity (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being scarce or in short supply; insufficiency or shortage of something
   
   **Synonyms**: shortage, lack, rarity, insufficiency, dearth
   
   **Antonyms**: abundance, plenty, surplus, profusion
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or academic contexts when discussing limited availability relative to demand. Particularly common in economics when discussing resource allocation. Appropriate for environmental, economic, and philosophical discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "shortage" is more natural. Don't use when abundance is temporarily unavailable but not actually scarce.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - scarcity of resources
   - water scarcity
   - economic scarcity
   - artificial scarcity
   - scarcity mindset
   
   **Root Analysis**: scarce (Latin excarpere, to pluck out) + -ity (noun suffix indicating state)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norman French scars (restricted) → scarce + -ity → scarcity (shortage, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The scarcity of parking downtown is annoying.
   - Job scarcity in this town is forcing people to move away.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Water scarcity represents one of the most pressing global challenges of the 21st century.
   - The principle of scarcity is fundamental to economic theory and resource allocation decisions.

1. **Noun**: deficiency (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A lack or shortage of something necessary or required; insufficient amount of an essential element
   
   **Synonyms**: lack, shortage, insufficiency, inadequacy, deficit
   
   **Antonyms**: sufficiency, adequacy, surplus, abundance
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, medical, nutritional, or technical contexts when discussing insufficient amounts of required elements. Particularly common in health and scientific discussions. Appropriate for describing missing essential components.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "lack" or "shortage" is more natural. Don't use when excess rather than shortage is the problem.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vitamin deficiency
   - nutritional deficiency
   - iron deficiency
   - deficiency of resources
   - immune deficiency
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, down) + -fic- (make, do, from Latin facere) + -ency (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deficere (to fail, lack) → deficiency (lack of something essential, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My constant tiredness might be from an iron deficiency.
   - There's a serious deficiency of decent restaurants around here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The patient presents with symptoms consistent with vitamin D deficiency.
   - The audit revealed significant deficiencies in the organization's internal controls.

1. **Noun**: supply (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A stock or amount of something supplied or available for use; the action of providing what is needed
   
   **Synonyms**: stock, reserve, provision, inventory, cache
   
   **Antonyms**: demand, consumption, shortage, depletion
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing available quantities of resources, goods, or materials. Appropriate in business, economics, logistics, and everyday contexts. Common in supply chain, inventory, and resource management discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "provision" or "delivery" would be more specific. Don't use when discussing demand-side issues.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - food supply
   - water supply
   - supply and demand
   - in short supply
   - supply chain
   - run out of supply
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (from below, Latin sub) + -ply (fill, from Latin plere)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplere (to fill up, complete) → Old French supplier → English supply (stock, provision, 15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to stock up on supplies before the storm hits.
   - The supply of snacks runs out fast in this office.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization maintains a strategic supply of essential resources to ensure operational continuity.
   - Global supply chains have been disrupted by recent geopolitical developments.

1. **Noun**: reserves (C, typically plural)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A supply of a commodity not needed for immediate use but available if required; funds or resources kept back for emergencies or future use
   
   **Synonyms**: stock, backup, cache, savings, resources
   
   **Antonyms**: consumption, depletion, expenditure
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing backup supplies, emergency resources, financial savings, or strategic stockpiles. Appropriate in business, military, environmental, and financial contexts. Common in discussing resource management and planning.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing active/current supplies rather than backup resources. Don't use when "savings" or "backup" would be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - financial reserves
   - oil reserves
   - foreign reserves
   - cash reserves
   - emergency reserves
   - depleted reserves
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + serve (Latin servare, to keep/preserve)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reservare (to keep back) → Old French reserver → English reserve (something kept back, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My energy reserves are completely tapped out after that hike.
   - We're dipping into our emergency reserves to cover this expense.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization's financial reserves have been substantially depleted during this fiscal year.
   - Strategic petroleum reserves serve as a buffer against supply disruptions.

1. **Noun**: stock (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The goods or merchandise kept on the premises of a business or available for sale or distribution; a supply accumulated for future use
   
   **Synonyms**: inventory, supply, store, reserves, goods
   
   **Antonyms**: shortage, out-of-stock, depletion
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing retail inventory, warehouse goods, or accumulated supplies available for use. Appropriate in business, retail, and supply management contexts. Common in discussing availability of products or materials.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid confusing with financial "stocks" (shares). Don't use when "inventory" would be more precise in business contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in stock
   - out of stock
   - stock levels
   - restock
   - stock up on
   - stock depletion
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English stocc (trunk, stem, stick)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English stocc (tree trunk) → stock (extended to mean store/supply, as goods were originally tallied on wooden sticks, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're out of stock on that popular item right now.
   - I need to stock up on groceries before the weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Inventory management systems monitor stock levels to prevent depletion.
   - The store maintains adequate stock to meet customer demand during peak periods.

1. **Noun**: inventory (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A complete list of items such as goods in stock or contents of a building; the quantity of goods and materials held in stock
   
   **Synonyms**: stock, supply, goods, reserves, catalog
   
   **Antonyms**: shortage, depletion, out-of-stock
   
   **When to Use**: Use in business, retail, and warehouse contexts when discussing tracked stock quantities. Appropriate for formal business communications, supply chain management, and accounting. More formal and precise than "stock."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "stuff" or "supplies" is more natural. Don't use when a simple list rather than stock management is meant.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inventory levels
   - inventory management
   - take inventory
   - inventory control
   - depleted inventory
   - out of inventory
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + vent (come, from Latin venire) + -ory (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin inventorium (list of goods) → Old French inventoire → English inventory (detailed list of stock, 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to take inventory of what we have before ordering more.
   - The store's inventory of that item is completely gone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company conducts quarterly inventory audits to ensure accurate stock records.
   - Inventory depletion has necessitated expedited procurement procedures.

1. **Noun**: resources (C, typically plural)

   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when more specific terms like "money," "personnel," or "equipment" would be clearer. Don't use vaguely when precision is needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - natural resources
   - human resources
   - financial resources
   - limited resources
   - resource allocation
   - depleted resources
   - run out of resources
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, back) + source (Latin surgere, to rise)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French ressource (relief, recovery) → English resource (means of support, 17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're running out of resources to keep this project going.
   - He has lots of resources to draw on for help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization lacks sufficient resources to implement the proposed initiatives.
   - Natural resource depletion poses significant challenges to sustainable development.

   - patience wearing thin
   - limited patience

   **Examples [Casual]**:

1. **Noun**: proportion (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A part, share, or number considered in comparative relation to a whole; the relationship of one thing to another in terms of quantity, size, or number
   - *(In phrase "out of proportion")* Excessively large or small relative to something else; not appropriately sized
   
   **Synonyms**: ratio, relationship, balance, symmetry, correspondence
   
   **Antonyms**: disproportion, imbalance, asymmetry
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing relative sizes, amounts, or relationships. Particularly common in the phrase "out of proportion" to describe excessive reactions or mismatched scales. Appropriate in formal and technical contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple "percentage" or "amount" would be clearer. Don't use "out of proportion" for minor imbalances.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - out of proportion
   - in proportion to
   - sense of proportion
   - blow out of proportion
   - proportion of resources
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (in relation to) + portion (Latin portio, share)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin proportio (comparative relation) → Old French proportion → English proportion (comparative relationship, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You're blowing this way out of proportion—it's not that big a deal.
   - The cost is completely out of proportion to the value.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The response appears out of proportion to the actual risk presented.
   - The allocation of resources should be in proportion to demonstrated need.
