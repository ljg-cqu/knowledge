1. **Functional Expression**: I'm afraid we're out of [item] (function: apologizing/refusing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to inform someone that something is no longer available; expressing inability to provide something due to depletion
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (uses apologetic frame "I'm afraid")
   
   **Synonyms**: We don't have any [item] left, [Item] is unavailable, We've run out of [item], There's no [item] available
   
   **Antonyms**: We have [item] in stock, [Item] is available, We still have [item]
   
   **When to Use**: Customer service contexts, retail situations, hospitality settings, professional communications where you need to politely decline or inform about unavailability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual settings with friends (too formal), situations requiring urgent direct communication without politeness softeners, when announcing to large groups (use more direct phrasing)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm sorry, we're out of [item] (more apologetic)
   - Unfortunately, we're out of [item] (more formal)
   - We're all out of [item] (more casual, emphasizes completeness)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The phrase "I'm afraid" is a politeness strategy that softens bad news and shows empathy. This is negative politeness (minimizing imposition). Common in British English; Americans often use "I'm sorry" instead.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's okay, do you have [alternative]?
   - When will you have more?
   - Oh, that's too bad. Thanks anyway.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm afraid we're out of coffee. Want some tea instead?
   - Sorry, we're out of that flavor. The chocolate's really good though.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'm afraid we're out of that particular model at present. May I suggest an alternative?
   - I regret to inform you that we're currently out of stock for that item.

1. **Functional Expression**: We're running out of [resource] (function: warning/expressing concern)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Informing others that a resource is being depleted and will soon be exhausted; signaling urgency or need for action
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but not aggressive
   
   **Synonyms**: We're low on [resource], [Resource] is running low, We don't have much [resource] left, We're getting short on [resource]
   
   **Antonyms**: We have plenty of [resource], [Resource] is abundant, We're well-stocked with [resource]
   
   **When to Use**: Project management, team coordination, household management, inventory monitoring, any situation requiring proactive warning about resource depletion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: After resources are completely gone (use "out of" instead), formal written reports (use "depleting" or "diminishing"), when no action can be taken
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We're quickly running out of [resource] (emphasizes speed)
   - We're about to run out of [resource] (emphasizes immediacy)
   - We may run out of [resource] soon (hedges the warning)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression functions as both information-giving and indirect requesting. The continuous aspect "running" implies ongoing process and suggests action is still possible to prevent complete depletion. Often implies "we should do something about this."
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - How much do we have left?
   - I'll order more right away.
   - Do we need to prioritize anything?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hey, we're running out of milk. Can you grab some on your way home?
   - We're running out of time here, need to speed things up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We're running out of budget for this quarter and need to adjust expenditures.
   - The team is running out of capacity to take on additional projects.

1. **Functional Expression**: Could you help us out? We're out of [resource] (function: requesting assistance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Requesting help or resources by explaining a situation of depletion; combining request with justification
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (modal "could" softens request)
   
   **Synonyms**: Can you help? We need [resource], Would you be able to assist? We're short on [resource], Could you spare some [resource]? We've run out
   
   **Antonyms**: We don't need help, We're fully supplied, We're all set
   
   **When to Use**: Asking neighbors, colleagues, or friends for assistance, seeking support from team members, requesting resources from other departments, social borrowing situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal requests to superiors (too casual), emergency situations requiring direct requests, when asking for significant resources (needs more formal structure)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would you mind helping us? We're out of [resource] (more polite)
   - Can you help out? We've run out of [resource] (more casual)
   - I was wondering if you could help—we're out of [resource] (very polite, tentative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines modal question (negative politeness) with explanation/justification. The explanation legitimizes the request and makes it harder to refuse. "Out of" provides concrete reason for needing help. This is a face-saving strategy for both parties.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, what do you need?
   - I'd be happy to help. What's going on?
   - Let me see what I can do.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hey, could you help us out? We're out of paper towels and need to clean this spill.
   - Could you give me a hand? We're completely out of ideas here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would you be able to assist us? We're out of the necessary resources to complete this phase.
   - Could your department help out? We've run out of allocated personnel for this initiative.

1. **Functional Expression**: That's out of [my/our] control (function: declining responsibility/explaining limitations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Explaining that one lacks the power or authority to influence or change a situation; deflecting responsibility appropriately
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but diplomatic
   
   **Synonyms**: I/We can't control that, That's beyond my/our authority, I/We have no power over that, That's not within my/our scope
   
   **Antonyms**: That's within my control, I can handle that, That's my responsibility
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining limitations to customers or colleagues, setting appropriate boundaries, clarifying scope of authority, deflecting unfair blame or expectations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When avoiding legitimate responsibility, when you actually can influence the situation, as an excuse without offering alternatives
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm afraid that's out of my control (more apologetic)
   - That's completely out of our control (emphasizes totality)
   - Unfortunately, that's out of my hands (more idiomatic)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This is a boundary-setting phrase that protects one's face by limiting responsibility appropriately. Should often be followed by what you CAN do or who can help. Overuse can seem like excuse-making. In customer service, pair with empathy and alternatives.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Is there anyone who can help with this?
   - What can be done then?
   - I understand. Thank you for explaining.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry, the delivery time is out of my control—that's up to shipping.
   - Whether we get snow is totally out of our control, so let's just plan for both scenarios.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I understand your concern, but that decision is out of my control. You'll need to speak with management.
   - Regulatory changes are out of our control, but we're adapting our processes accordingly.

1. **Functional Expression**: Out of curiosity, [question]? (function: softening questions/requesting information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introducing a question in a non-threatening way; signaling that the question is optional to answer and motivated by interest rather than judgment
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens potentially intrusive questions)
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm curious, [question]?, Just wondering, [question]?, If you don't mind me asking, [question]?, May I ask [question]?
   
   **Antonyms**: (Direct questions without softeners)
   
   **When to Use**: Asking personal questions, requesting information that might be sensitive, showing interest without demanding answers, casual professional conversations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal interviews or official inquiries, legal or medical contexts requiring direct answers, when you actually need the answer (don't minimize importance)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Out of interest, [question]? (slightly more formal, British)
   - Just out of curiosity, [question]? (more casual, emphasizes casualness)
   - I'm curious, [question]? (removes "out of" structure, more direct)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This is a hedging strategy that gives the respondent permission to decline answering. It frames the question as optional and interest-based rather than demand-based. Very useful for potentially face-threatening questions. Signals the asker has no right to demand the answer.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Answers question]
   - I'd rather not say, actually.
   - Why do you ask?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Out of curiosity, how much did you pay for that car?
   - Just out of curiosity, why did you decide to leave your last job?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Out of curiosity, what prompted your organization to explore this particular approach?
   - May I ask, purely out of curiosity, what factors influenced that decision?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm out of the loop on [topic] (function: admitting lack of information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Explaining that one has not been included in communication or updates about something; admitting ignorance about a situation
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct but not aggressive
   
   **Synonyms**: I haven't been informed about [topic], I don't know anything about [topic], I'm not up to date on [topic], No one's told me about [topic]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm in the loop, I'm fully informed, I'm up to speed, I know all about that
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts when you need information, admitting knowledge gaps to colleagues, explaining why you can't contribute to a discussion, requesting updates
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you should have known (sounds like excuse), very formal contexts (use more formal phrasing), when it might make you look negligent
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I've been out of the loop on [topic] (emphasizes duration)
   - I seem to be out of the loop here (softens admission)
   - I'm completely out of the loop (emphasizes total lack of information)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This idiomatic expression admits knowledge gap while subtly suggesting others have information you don't. Can imply (diplomatically) that you should have been informed. Often functions as indirect request to be included or updated. The metaphor of "loop" (circle of information) is widely understood in business contexts.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Oh, let me catch you up.
   - Sorry, I should have included you.
   - Here's what's been happening...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally out of the loop on this party planning. What's the latest?
   - I'm out of the loop here—when did this change happen?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I appear to be out of the loop regarding these developments. Could you provide an update?
   - I'm out of the loop on that initiative. Perhaps you could bring me up to speed?

1. **Functional Expression**: Get out of [negative situation] (function: suggesting/commanding escape)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Advising or commanding someone to exit or escape from an undesirable situation; recommending removal from problematic circumstances
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct (imperative form is command, but can be softened)
   
   **Synonyms**: Leave [situation], Escape from [situation], Remove yourself from [situation], Exit [situation]
   
   **Antonyms**: Stay in [situation], Remain in [situation], Get into [situation]
   
   **When to Use**: Giving advice to friends or family, suggesting professional changes, warning about dangerous situations, recommending exits from bad relationships or jobs
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the person has legitimate reasons to stay, without understanding full context, in formal counseling (requires more nuanced guidance), when your advice isn't requested
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You should get out of [situation] (softer, suggestive)
   - You need to get out of [situation] (emphatic, stronger)
   - Try to get out of [situation] (tentative, acknowledges difficulty)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The directness varies greatly with intonation and context. Can be caring advice or urgent command. "Get out" suggests active removal of oneself. Often implies the situation is harmful and not likely to improve. Should be accompanied by empathy and practical support. Can be face-threatening if unsolicited.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I know, but it's complicated.
   - You're right, I need to make a change.
   - It's not that simple.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to get out of that relationship—it's not healthy.
   - Get out of there! The building's on fire!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would strongly advise you to extricate yourself from this arrangement.
   - You should consider removing yourself from this professional situation.

1. **Functional Expression**: Out of respect for [person/thing], [action] (function: justifying/explaining)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Explaining that an action is motivated by consideration or regard for someone or something; providing honorable justification
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (provides reasoning rather than direct statement)
   
   **Synonyms**: In deference to [person/thing], To honor [person/thing], In consideration of [person/thing], Because I respect [person/thing]
   
   **Antonyms**: Despite [person/thing], Without regard for [person/thing], Disregarding [person/thing]
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining decisions motivated by respect, justifying actions that might otherwise seem unusual, showing deference to traditions or individuals, professional contexts involving sensitivity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When respect isn't actually the motivation, as manipulation to get others to agree, when the action doesn't genuinely honor what's cited
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Out of respect, [action] (abbreviated when context is clear)
   - Purely out of respect for [person/thing] (emphasizes singular motivation)
   - Out of deep respect for [person/thing] (intensifies respect)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression elevates one's actions by associating them with respect, which is a high-value social concept. Creates positive face for both speaker and the respected person/thing. Can be used to explain seemingly inconvenient choices. Cultural sensitivity: respect hierarchies vary significantly across cultures.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I appreciate that.
   - That's very considerate.
   - Thank you for being so respectful.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Out of respect for grandma, we're keeping the holiday traditions the same this year.
   - I'm not going to that party out of respect for my friend's feelings.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Out of respect for the bereaved family, we will postpone the celebration.
   - Out of respect for the committee's process, I will await their decision before proceeding.

1. **Functional Expression**: [Action] out of necessity (function: justifying/explaining)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Explaining that an action was compelled by circumstances rather than preference; justifying potentially undesirable actions as unavoidable
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (provides justification)
   
   **Synonyms**: Because we had to, Due to circumstances, Because there was no alternative, We had no choice but to [action]
   
   **Antonyms**: By choice, Voluntarily, Out of preference, Willingly
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining difficult decisions, justifying actions that might be criticized, showing that circumstances forced a choice, professional explanations for changes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you actually had choices (dishonest), to avoid responsibility for genuinely bad decisions, without acknowledging the drawbacks of the action
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Out of sheer necessity (emphasizes absolute requirement)
   - Purely out of necessity (emphasizes lack of alternative motivation)
   - Out of economic/practical necessity (specifies type of necessity)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression removes blame by attributing causation to circumstances rather than choice. It's a face-saving strategy that maintains one's autonomy while acknowledging constraints. Often implies "I wouldn't have done this otherwise." Can elicit sympathy. Should be used honestly, as overuse erodes credibility.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I understand, you had to do what you had to do.
   - That must have been difficult.
   - What circumstances led to this?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I moved back in with my parents out of necessity when I lost my job.
   - We're eating ramen every night out of necessity, not because we love it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization restructured out of necessity to remain financially viable.
   - We made these policy changes out of necessity to comply with new regulations.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm getting out of [commitment/activity] (function: announcing withdrawal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Stating intention to exit or withdraw from a commitment, activity, or situation; announcing disengagement
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct (clear statement of intention)
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm leaving [commitment], I'm quitting [activity], I'm withdrawing from [commitment], I'm backing out of [commitment]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm staying in [commitment], I'm continuing with [activity], I'm committed to [activity]
   
   **When to Use**: Announcing personal decisions to friends or colleagues, explaining career or lifestyle changes, informing others of withdrawal from joint activities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal resignations (use proper formal resignation), legal commitments (may require different terminology), when you're unsure of decision (don't announce prematurely)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm trying to get out of [commitment] (suggests difficulty)
   - I need to get out of [commitment] (emphasizes necessity)
   - I want to get out of [commitment] (emphasizes desire)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The continuous aspect "getting" suggests process rather than immediate exit. Can soften the announcement slightly compared to "I'm leaving." May imply external constraints on timing. Often requires follow-up explanation. Can affect relationships, so tone and explanation matter significantly.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Why? What's going on?
   - Is everything okay?
   - When will this happen?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm getting out of teaching—it's just not for me anymore.
   - I need to get out of this lease, the apartment's terrible.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'm in the process of getting out of that partnership arrangement.
   - We're getting out of that market segment due to strategic realignment.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let me get you out of [difficult situation] (function: offering assistance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Offering to help someone escape or be freed from a difficult, awkward, or problematic situation
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct but friendly offer
   
   **Synonyms**: Let me help you with [situation], I'll rescue you from [situation], Let me extract you from [situation], I'll help you escape [situation]
   
   **Antonyms**: You're on your own, You'll have to deal with [situation] yourself
   
   **When to Use**: Offering help to friends or colleagues in awkward situations, professional contexts where you can provide solutions, social rescue situations, mentoring contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you can't actually help (empty promise), in situations where the person needs to handle it themselves (helicopter helping), without assessing if help is wanted
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll get you out of [situation] (more confident, less tentative)
   - Can I get you out of [situation]? (asks permission)
   - Let me help get you out of [situation] (emphasizes collaborative help)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression positions the speaker as capable helper/rescuer. Can build relationship bonds. The metaphor of physical extraction from a place extends to abstract difficulties. Should be offered genuinely with capacity to follow through. In some contexts can seem patronizing if help wasn't requested.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That would be amazing, thank you!
   - I'd really appreciate that.
   - Thanks, but I think I can handle it.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You look stuck talking to that bore. Let me get you out of there with a fake phone call.
   - Let me get you out of washing dishes—you cooked, I'll clean.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I see you're in a difficult contractual position. Let me get you out of that arrangement through proper channels.
   - If you're committed to that meeting, let me get you out of it by representing our department.
