1. **Adjective**: depleted
   **Meaning**: 
   - Reduced or exhausted in quantity, resources, or strength; used up or emptied (gradable, typically predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: exhausted, drained, emptied, diminished, reduced
   
   **Antonyms**: replenished, full, abundant, stocked
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing resources, supplies, energy, or strength that have been significantly reduced or used up. Common in contexts of resource management, energy levels, environmental discussions, and physical/mental states.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts where "empty" would be more appropriate for complete absence. Don't use for temporary shortages that haven't significantly reduced the total supply.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - depleted resources
   - severely depleted
   - depleted energy
   - depleted reserves
   - become depleted
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down, away) + -plete (fill, from Latin plere) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deplere (to empty out) → Middle French déplété → English depleted (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally depleted after that workout, need to rest.
   - Our snack supplies are getting pretty depleted, time to restock.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company's financial reserves have been substantially depleted due to the extended downturn.
   - Natural resources in the region are severely depleted, requiring immediate conservation measures.

1. **Adjective**: exhausted
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely used up or consumed; having no remaining supply (gradable/non-gradable depending on context)
   - *(Sense 2)* Extremely tired; having no energy left (gradable, predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: depleted, drained, spent, consumed, finished
   
   **Antonyms**: replenished, abundant, energized, fresh
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing complete or near-complete depletion of resources, supplies, or energy. Appropriate for both physical resources and abstract concepts like patience or options. Common in environmental, economic, and personal energy contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing partial reduction (use "reduced" or "diminished" instead). Don't use for renewable resources that regenerate quickly.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exhausted supplies
   - completely exhausted
   - exhausted resources
   - exhausted patience
   - become exhausted
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out, thoroughly) + -haust (draw, from Latin haurire) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin exhaustus (drawn out) → English exhausted (16th century, meaning "consumed completely")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're totally exhausted out of ideas for the party theme.
   - My phone battery's exhausted, can I borrow your charger?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has exhausted all viable options for resolving the dispute.
   - Fossil fuel reserves in this sector are nearly exhausted, necessitating alternative energy sources.

1. **Adjective**: empty
   **Meaning**: 
   - Containing nothing; having no contents (non-gradable in absolute sense, but gradable in practical usage: "nearly empty")
   - *(Sense 2)* Lacking substance, meaning, or value (gradable, typically predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: vacant, void, hollow, bare, depleted
   
   **Antonyms**: full, filled, occupied, abundant
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing complete or near-complete absence of contents in containers, spaces, or abstract concepts. Common in everyday descriptions of containers, spaces, feelings, or promises.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using "empty" for partial depletion (use "low" or "depleted" instead). Don't use for abstract concepts when "meaningless" or "hollow" would be more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - empty container
   - completely empty
   - empty promise
   - empty handed
   - run empty
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English æmtig (unoccupied, vacant)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English æmtig (at leisure, vacant) → Middle English empty (lacking contents, 13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The fridge is empty, we're totally out of food.
   - I feel so empty inside after hearing that news.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reservoir was found to be empty, indicating a critical water shortage.
   - The proposal appears to be an empty gesture, lacking substantive commitment.

1. **Adjective**: bereft
   **Meaning**: 
   - Deprived of or lacking something, especially something important or valued; completely without (non-gradable, formal, typically used with "of")
   
   **Synonyms**: devoid, deprived, lacking, stripped, without
   
   **Antonyms**: endowed, blessed, equipped, furnished
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or literary contexts to emphasize complete absence or loss of something important. Particularly effective when describing emotional states or significant deprivations. Often followed by "of".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "without" or "lacking" would be more natural. Don't use for trivial absences. Too formal for everyday speech.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bereft of hope
   - utterly bereft
   - bereft of ideas
   - bereft of resources
   - left bereft
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (thoroughly) + reave (rob, deprive) + -t (past participle suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English bereafian (to deprive) → Middle English bereft (deprived of, robbed, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the breakup, I felt totally bereft of any joy.
   - We're bereft of options now, everything's been tried.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization found itself bereft of leadership during this critical period.
   - The proposal was bereft of any practical implementation strategy.

1. **Adjective**: devoid
   **Meaning**: 
   - Entirely without or lacking; completely free from (non-gradable, formal, always used with "of")
   
   **Synonyms**: lacking, bereft, empty, void, free (from)
   
   **Antonyms**: full, replete, abundant, filled
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing or speech to emphasize complete absence of a quality, characteristic, or substance. Always followed by "of". Common in academic, professional, and literary contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "without" is more natural. Don't use for physical emptiness of containers (use "empty" instead). Not appropriate for partial absence.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - devoid of meaning
   - completely devoid
   - devoid of emotion
   - devoid of merit
   - devoid of substance
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (completely) + void (empty)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French desvuidier (to empty out) → English devoid (completely lacking, 15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His explanation was totally devoid of any logic.
   - The meeting was devoid of any useful information.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manuscript appears devoid of original insights or analytical rigor.
   - The defendant's testimony was devoid of credibility and internal consistency.

1. **Adjective**: lacking
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not having enough of something; deficient in a particular quality or resource (gradable, typically predicative, used with "in")
   
   **Synonyms**: deficient, short, wanting, insufficient, inadequate
   
   **Antonyms**: abundant, sufficient, adequate, rich (in)
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing inadequacy or insufficiency of a quality, resource, or characteristic. Common in critiques, assessments, and descriptions of shortcomings. Often followed by "in".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when complete absence is meant (use "devoid" or "without" instead). Don't use for countable items that are simply absent rather than insufficient.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lacking in confidence
   - sadly lacking
   - lacking in detail
   - lacking in substance
   - seriously lacking
   
   **Root Analysis**: lack (deficiency) + -ing (present participle/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle Dutch laken (to be wanting) → English lack → lacking (deficient, 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm feeling a bit lacking in motivation today.
   - Your essay is lacking in solid examples to back up your points.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal is lacking in methodological rigor and empirical support.
   - The candidate was found lacking in the necessary technical expertise for the position.

1. **Adjective**: short
   **Meaning**:

   **Antonyms**: abundant, plentiful, sufficient, adequate

   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in formal contexts where "insufficient" or "deficient" would be more appropriate. Don't confuse with physical height or length meanings.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - short of money
   - running short
   - short of time
   - fall short
   - in short supply
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English sceort (not long, brief)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English sceort → Middle English short (extended meaning to "insufficient", 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're running a bit short of coffee, need to pick some up.
   - I'm short of cash right now, can I pay you back tomorrow?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The department is short of qualified personnel to meet current demands.
   - The project fell short of its objectives due to insufficient funding.

1. **Adjective**: scarce

   **Synonyms**: rare, limited, sparse, insufficient, short
   
   **Antonyms**: abundant, plentiful, common, ample
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing limited availability relative to demand or need. Common in economic, environmental, and resource management contexts. Effective for emphasizing rarity or insufficiency.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when complete absence is meant (use "absent" or "lacking" instead). Don't use for situations where something is simply hard to find but not actually limited.
   
   **Common Phrases**:

   - scarce resources
   - make oneself scarce
   - in scarce supply
   - scarce commodity
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old French escars (restricted, stingy)

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Jobs are pretty scarce around here right now.
   - Fresh produce is getting scarce out of season.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Clean water resources are becoming increasingly scarce in arid regions.
   - Qualified candidates remain scarce despite extensive recruitment efforts.

1. **Adjective**: insufficient
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not enough in quantity, quality, or degree to meet a need or requirement (gradable, typically predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: inadequate, deficient, lacking, short, scanty
   
   **Antonyms**: sufficient, adequate, ample, abundant
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to describe failure to meet required standards, quantities, or expectations. Common in academic, professional, and official communications. More formal than "not enough".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where "not enough" is more natural. Don't use when excess or oversufficiency is meant.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - insufficient evidence
   - grossly insufficient
   - insufficient funds
   - insufficient resources
   - prove insufficient
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (not) + suf- (under, from sub-) + -fic- (make) + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin insufficiens (not making enough) → Middle French insufficient → English insufficient (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My savings are insufficient to cover the repair costs.
   - The explanation was totally insufficient for understanding the problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The evidence presented was insufficient to support the prosecution's case.
   - Funding levels remain insufficient to address the program's core objectives.
