1. **Functional Expression**: That's so unlike you (function: expressing surprise at uncharacteristic behavior)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express surprise or puzzlement when someone acts differently from their usual character; indicates behavior that contrasts with established patterns
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit statement about character)
   
   **Synonyms**: That's not like you, That's uncharacteristic of you, You don't usually act this way, That's out of character for you
   
   **Antonyms**: That's just like you, That's typical of you, That's so you, That's characteristic
   
   **When to Use**: Commenting on surprising behavior with friends or acquaintances, informal contexts, showing concern or curiosity about deviation from norms, expressing surprise at change
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With strangers (implies established knowledge of character); in very formal settings; when behavior is actually characteristic; can seem judgmental in some contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - That's not like you at all
   - It's so unlike you to [do something]
   - That's very unlike you
   - How unlike you!
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Functions as both observation and mild reproach; shows speaker's familiarity with addressee's typical behavior; can express concern, surprise, or disapproval depending on tone; softened with "so" for emphasis
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I know, right? I surprised myself!"
   - "Yeah, I'm trying something different"
   - "Is it really that unusual?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "I'm thinking of skydiving next month." B: "That's so unlike you—you hate heights!"
   - "Arriving late? That's so unlike you; you're always super punctual."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Such impulsive decision-making is most unlike you, given your typically cautious approach."
   - "This uncharacteristic departure from your usual methodical process is rather unlike you."

1. **Functional Expression**: Unlike [X], I think... (function: expressing contrasting opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce one's differing viewpoint while acknowledging another perspective; politely disagrees by showing how one's opinion is unlike another's
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens disagreement through comparison structure)
   
   **Synonyms**: In contrast to [X], I believe..., Different from [X], my view is..., I disagree with [X] in that..., Whereas [X] thinks...
   
   **Antonyms**: Like [X], I think..., I agree with [X] that..., Similar to [X], I believe..., Along with [X]...
   
   **When to Use**: Academic discussions, professional meetings, polite disagreement, showing respect for alternative views while presenting different perspective; appropriate for written and spoken contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When agreement is meant; very casual contexts may prefer "I disagree" or "I see it differently"; when strong opposition requires more direct language
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Unlike [X], I would argue that...
   - Unlike what [X] suggests, I believe...
   - Unlike [X]'s position, my view is...
   - Unlike the previous speaker, I think...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness strategy minimizing face threat of disagreement; "unlike" frames as difference rather than direct opposition; acknowledges validity of other view while presenting alternative; more diplomatic than direct disagreement
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I see your point, but I still think..."
   - "That's an interesting perspective..."
   - "Could you elaborate on why you see it differently?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Unlike Sarah, I think we should go with the blue design—it's more modern."
   - "Unlike most people here, I actually enjoyed that movie—thought it was creative."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Unlike previous researchers, I contend that the data support an alternative interpretation."
   - "Unlike my colleague's assessment, I believe the methodology requires fundamental revision."

1. **Functional Expression**: What makes X different from Y? / How is X unlike Y? (function: requesting clarification of differences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to ask for explanation of distinctions or contrasts between entities; seeks to understand what makes things unlike each other
   - *Formality*: neutral (appropriate across most contexts)
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit request for information)
   
   **Synonyms**: How does X differ from Y?, What distinguishes X from Y?, What's the difference between X and Y?, In what ways are X and Y different?
   
   **Antonyms**: How are X and Y similar?, What do X and Y have in common?, In what ways are X and Y alike?
   
   **When to Use**: Seeking clarification, educational contexts, discussions requiring precise understanding of differences, professional and academic settings; shows analytical thinking
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When differences are obvious; rhetorical questions (unless for effect); when not genuinely seeking information
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What's different about X compared to Y?
   - How does X compare to Y?
   - What are the differences between X and Y?
   - Can you explain how X differs from Y?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows speaker's desire for precise understanding; implies differences exist but need clarification; appropriate information-seeking strategy; demonstrates critical thinking and attention to detail; "unlike" version more formal than "different"
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Well, the main difference is..."
   - "Unlike Y, X has the following characteristics..."
   - "There are several key distinctions..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "What makes this phone different from the older model? Is it worth upgrading?"
   - "How is this version unlike the first one? I can't really tell."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "What distinguishes this methodology from conventional approaches employed in previous studies?"
   - "How is the proposed framework unlike existing theoretical models in the field?"

1. **Functional Expression**: It's nothing like [X] (function: contradicting false comparison/expectation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to strongly deny similarity or correct mistaken expectations; emphasizes that reality is completely unlike what was suggested or expected
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (emphatic contradiction)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's completely different from [X], It's not at all like [X], It bears no resemblance to [X], It's totally unlike [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: It's just like [X], It's exactly like [X], It's very similar to [X], It's comparable to [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Correcting misconceptions, contradicting false comparisons, expressing disappointment or surprise at reality vs. expectation, informal to semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When similarity actually exists; very formal contexts may prefer "substantially different"; when gentle correction is more appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It's nothing like what I expected
   - It's nothing like they said
   - It's nothing like the original
   - It's nothing like that at all
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Emphatic contradictive function; "nothing" provides absolute negation; often expresses disappointment or surprise; can be used positively (better than expected) or negatively (worse than expected); directness may threaten face if contradicting addressee
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Really? How so?"
   - "In what way is it different?"
   - "Oh, I must have misunderstood then"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The restaurant was nothing like the pictures online—totally disappointing!"
   - "Don't worry, the test was nothing like the practice exam—way easier!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The implementation process was nothing like the preliminary proposal suggested."
   - "Contemporary practices are nothing like historical precedents in this regard."

1. **Functional Expression**: Unlike most/other [people/things], ... (function: distinguishing oneself/something)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to highlight how someone or something differs from the majority or from others; establishes contrast to show distinctiveness or special characteristics
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit self-distinction or differentiation)
   
   **Synonyms**: In contrast to most..., Different from others..., Distinct from the majority..., Not like most...
   
   **Antonyms**: Like most..., Similar to others..., Along with everyone else..., Just like the rest...
   
   **When to Use**: Establishing uniqueness, marketing contexts, explaining distinctive features, academic comparisons, professional differentiation; appropriate across most formal and semi-formal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When similarity is advantage; can sound boastful if overused about oneself; when distinction isn't actually meaningful
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Unlike many [people/things], ...
   - Unlike the majority, ...
   - Unlike typical [category], ...
   - Unlike conventional [approaches/methods], ...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positioning statement establishing difference from norm; can express superiority, uniqueness, or simply factual distinction depending on context and tone; effective for differentiation in competitive contexts; requires justification of claimed difference
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "What makes it different?"
   - "That's interesting—tell me more"
   - "How so?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Unlike most people, I actually enjoy doing taxes—find it relaxing!"
   - "Unlike other coffee shops, this place lets you stay all day without buying more."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Unlike conventional methodologies, this approach integrates stakeholder perspectives throughout."
   - "Unlike most organizations, our institution prioritizes long-term sustainability over immediate returns."

1. **Functional Expression**: If I may say so, that's unlike... (function: offering polite criticism/observation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to politely point out behavior or characteristics that are uncharacteristic or inappropriate; softens criticism by framing as observation of unlikeness
   - *Formality*: formal to very formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (heavy hedging and politeness markers)
   
   **Synonyms**: With respect, that seems uncharacteristic..., If I might observe, that differs from..., Pardon me, but that's not like...
   
   **Antonyms**: That's typical of..., That's just like..., As expected from...
   
   **When to Use**: Professional settings requiring tact, addressing superiors or clients, making delicate observations about behavior, formal diplomatic contexts; heavy politeness required
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts (overly formal); with close friends (unnecessarily distant); when direct feedback is more appropriate; emergencies requiring clear communication
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - If I may observe, that's unlike...
   - If I might say, that differs from...
   - With respect, that's uncharacteristic of...
   - Forgive me, but that's unlike...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Multiple politeness strategies: "if I may" seeks permission; "unlike" frames as neutral observation rather than judgment; negative politeness protecting addressee's face; appropriate for status-sensitive contexts; indirectness allows addressee to save face
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I appreciate your observation"
   - "Thank you for pointing that out"
   - "You're quite right to notice that"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used casually; too formal for typical casual contexts)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "If I may say so, that approach seems unlike your typically methodical decision-making process."
   - "With respect, this recommendation appears unlike the strategic direction previously articulated."

1. **Functional Expression**: It's not unlike... (function: hedging/suggesting similarity with double negative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to suggest similarity while maintaining caution or understatement; double negative creates nuanced comparison that is less direct than stating similarity
   - *Formality*: formal to very formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (litotes—understatement through double negative)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's similar to..., It resembles..., It's comparable to..., It's rather like..., It bears resemblance to...
   
   **Antonyms**: It's completely unlike..., It's totally different from..., It bears no resemblance to...
   
   **When to Use**: Formal academic writing, literary contexts, cautious comparisons, British formal English; when understatement is valued; diplomatic contexts requiring nuance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal/archaic); American informal English; when clear direct statement is preferable; technical writing requiring precision
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It's not unlike what we see in...
   - The situation is not unlike...
   - One might say it's not unlike...
   - The effect is not unlike...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Litotes (rhetorical understatement) suggests similarity while seeming modest or cautious; intellectual/literary register; double negative softer than direct positive assertion; British English more common than American; implies thoughtful comparison
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Indeed, I see the similarity"
   - "An apt comparison"
   - "Quite so"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Less common in casual speech; "It's kind of like..." preferred)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The phenomenon is not unlike that observed in comparable organic systems."
   - "The rhetorical strategy employed here is not unlike classical persuasive techniques."

1. **Functional Expression**: Unlike you to [do something] (function: expressing surprise at behavior)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to comment that an action is uncharacteristic; expresses surprise or mild criticism that behavior differs from normal patterns
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: fairly direct (though softened by focus on behavior rather than person)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's not like you to..., That's uncharacteristic of you to..., You don't usually..., It's unusual for you to...
   
   **Antonyms**: Just like you to..., Typical of you to..., That's so you to...
   
   **When to Use**: Commenting on unusual behavior with people you know well, informal contexts, expressing surprise or concern, friendly observation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With strangers (requires knowledge of typical behavior); very formal settings; when behavior is actually characteristic; can sound critical in wrong tone
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It's unlike you to be so...
   - Most unlike you to...
   - Very unlike you to...
   - How unlike you to...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Presupposes shared knowledge of addressee's typical behavior; can express concern, surprise, or mild reproach; tone crucial for interpretation; implies deviation from positive norm; face-threatening if used critically
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I know, I'm trying to change"
   - "Yeah, it's weird for me too"
   - "Is it really that unusual?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Unlike you to be pessimistic—what's wrong? Usually you're so positive!"
   - "It's unlike you to forget something—you have the best memory!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "It is unlike you to overlook such details, given your characteristic thoroughness."
   - "Most unlike you to respond so hastily, considering your typically measured approach."

1. **Functional Expression**: That's quite unlike anything I've seen/heard (function: expressing strong impression)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express that something is remarkably unique or unusual; emphasizes extraordinary difference from all prior experience
   - *Formality*: neutral to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct (explicit statement of uniqueness)
   
   **Synonyms**: That's completely unique, That's unprecedented in my experience, I've never seen anything like it, That's extraordinary
   
   **Antonyms**: That's typical, I've seen that before, That's nothing special, That's quite common
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing admiration or surprise, commenting on unique experiences, professional assessments, showing genuine impression; can be positive or negative depending on context
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When thing is actually common; when understatement is more appropriate; if can sound patronizing in wrong context
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - That's unlike anything I've experienced
   - I've seen nothing quite like it
   - That's quite unlike my usual experience
   - It's unlike anything in my knowledge
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Emphasizes speaker's experiential knowledge as comparison baseline; "quite" moderates while still emphasizing uniqueness; can express positive admiration or negative surprise; establishes speaker's authority through claimed experience; "anything" creates universal comparison scope
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Really? What makes it so different?"
   - "I'm glad it made an impression"
   - "What specifically stands out to you?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "That performance was quite unlike anything I've seen—absolutely incredible!"
   - "The flavor combination is unlike anything I've tasted—interesting but weird!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The architectural design represents an innovation quite unlike anything currently documented."
   - "This analytical framework is quite unlike anything established in contemporary scholarship."

1. **Functional Expression**: How unlike X to do Y (function: commenting on characteristic/uncharacteristic behavior)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Exclamatory form expressing surprise or irony about someone's behavior; comments on how action contrasts with typical character (can be genuine surprise or sarcastic)
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (exclamation rather than direct statement)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's so uncharacteristic of X to..., X never does Y, That's not like X at all to..., Imagine X doing Y
   
   **Antonyms**: How typical of X to..., Just like X to..., Of course X would...
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing surprise or irony, commenting on behavior with third parties, informal contexts, can be affectionate or critical depending on tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Direct address (might seem insulting); very formal contexts; when sincerity requires direct statement; with strangers
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - How very unlike X to...
   - How unlike X it is to...
   - Most unlike X to...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Exclamatory form adds emotional emphasis; can be genuine surprise or sarcastic; implies shared knowledge of X's character with addressee; tone determines whether affectionate observation or criticism; "how" creates rhetorical emphasis
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I know, right? Very unusual!"
   - "Actually, they do that more than you think"
   - "Maybe they're changing?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "How unlike Sarah to miss a deadline—she's always so reliable!"
   - "How unlike him to help without being asked—what's gotten into him?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "How unlike the committee to reach consensus so swiftly on such contentious matters."
   - "How unlike the organization to implement reforms absent extensive deliberation."
