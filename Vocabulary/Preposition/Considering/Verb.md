1. **Verb**: consider
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To think carefully about something, typically before making a decision; to take into account when making a judgment
   - (Transitive) To regard someone or something as having a specified quality; to believe or think
   
   **Synonyms**: think about, contemplate, deliberate, reflect on, evaluate
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, ignore, dismiss, overlook, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; decision-making contexts; expressing thoughtful evaluation; showing careful judgment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When quick, instinctive action is described; when "think" is simpler and sufficient
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - consider carefully
   - consider the options
   - consider someone/something as
   - take into consideration
   - be considered
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (with/together) + sider (star, from observing stars)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin considerare (to observe carefully, from observing stars for omens) → Old French → English "consider"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm considering switching jobs.
   - Have you considered what I said?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will consider all proposals before making a recommendation.
   - We must consider the long-term implications of this policy decision.

1. **Verb**: contemplate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To think about something deeply and at length; to consider as a possibility
   - (Transitive) To look thoughtfully at something for a long time
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, ponder, reflect on, meditate on, think about
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, disregard, dismiss, act impulsively
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; emphasizing deep or prolonged thought; philosophical or serious consideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "think about"); quick decisions; when simpler words work
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contemplate doing something
   - contemplate the possibilities
   - contemplate the consequences
   - sit and contemplate
   - contemplate one's future
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (intensive) + templ (temple/sacred space for observation) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin contemplari (to observe carefully, from templum: space for observation) → contemplate
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm contemplating a career change.
   - She sat contemplating her next move.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization must contemplate alternative strategies given market disruption.
   - Philosophers have long contemplated the nature of consciousness.

1. **Verb**: deliberate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To think carefully and at length about something; to engage in long and careful consideration
   - Often used of formal group discussions and decision-making
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, ponder, reflect, discuss, debate
   
   **Antonyms**: rush, act hastily, decide impulsively, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; group decision-making; legal/official settings; emphasizing careful consideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal); quick personal decisions; informal discussions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deliberate over/on something
   - deliberate carefully
   - the jury deliberated
   - deliberate at length
   - deliberate before deciding
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (completely) + liber (weigh/balance) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin deliberare (to weigh carefully, consider) → Old French → English "deliberate"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's deliberate on this before we decide.
   - The team deliberated for hours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board will deliberate on the proposed merger at next month's meeting.
   - After deliberating extensively, the committee reached consensus on the recommendation.

1. **Verb**: evaluate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To form an idea of the amount, value, or quality of something; to assess systematically
   
   **Synonyms**: assess, appraise, judge, analyze, examine
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, disregard, accept without question, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and academic contexts; systematic assessment; formal evaluation processes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "check out" or "look at"); informal opinions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - evaluate the options
   - evaluate performance
   - evaluate the evidence
   - evaluate carefully
   - evaluate against criteria
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (out) + valu (value) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: French évaluer (to find the value of) → evaluate
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's evaluate our options before deciding.
   - I need to evaluate whether this is worth it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will evaluate all proposals using standardized criteria.
   - Systematic evaluation of program outcomes is essential for continuous improvement.

1. **Verb**: assess

   **Synonyms**: evaluate, appraise, judge, estimate, gauge
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, disregard, misjudge
   
   **When to Use**: Professional and academic contexts; formal evaluations; systematic reviews; emphasizing judgment

   **Common Phrases**:

   - assess the impact
   - assess performance
   - assess the damage
   
   **Root Analysis**: as- (to) + sess (sit, from sitting as judge)

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to assess the situation before acting.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Verb**: weigh

   **Synonyms**: consider, evaluate, ponder, assess, balance
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, disregard, dismiss, act without thinking
   
   **When to Use**: Decision-making contexts; comparing alternatives; showing balanced consideration; all registers

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - weigh the options
   - weigh the pros and cons
   - weigh carefully
   - weigh up
   - weigh the evidence
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English wegan (to carry, weigh)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wegan (to carry, have weight, measure) → metaphorical use for mental consideration
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm weighing my options before deciding.
   - Let's weigh the pros and cons.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee carefully weighed all alternatives before recommending a course of action.
   - Investors must weigh potential returns against associated risks.

1. **Verb**: ponder
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To think about something carefully, especially before making a decision or reaching a conclusion
   - To consider something deeply and at length
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, contemplate, reflect on, think about, mull over
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, ignore, act hastily, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: Showing thoughtful, prolonged consideration; formal to semi-formal contexts; reflective thinking
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Quick decisions; very casual contexts (use "think about"); action-oriented situations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ponder over something
   - ponder the question
   - sit and ponder
   - ponder deeply
   - ponder the implications
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ponder (weight) + -are (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin ponderare (to weigh, consider) → Old French → English "ponder"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm still pondering what you said.
   - Let me ponder that for a while.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research findings merit careful pondering by the academic community.
   - Policymakers must ponder the societal implications of emerging technologies.

1. **Verb**: reflect (on/upon)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To think deeply or carefully about something; to consider something thoughtfully
   - Often implies looking back on past experiences or reconsidering earlier thoughts
   
   **Synonyms**: think about, contemplate, consider, ponder, meditate on
   
   **Antonyms**: act without thinking, ignore, disregard, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: Showing thoughtful reconsideration; introspective contexts; learning from experience; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Forward-looking planning (use "consider"); physical reflection contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reflect on something
   - reflect upon the past
   - take time to reflect
   - reflect carefully
   - reflect on experience
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + flect (bend)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reflectere (to bend back) → metaphorical use: bending thoughts back on something
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been reflecting on what you said.
   - Take some time to reflect before answering.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report reflects thoughtfully on lessons learned from implementation.
   - Practitioners should regularly reflect upon their professional practices.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Verb**: review
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To consider or examine something again, especially in order to make changes or give an opinion
   - To look back on or reconsider past events, decisions, or information
   
   **Synonyms**: reconsider, re-examine, reassess, evaluate, analyze
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, disregard, accept without question, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; reconsidering decisions; periodic evaluations; systematic assessment
   
   **When NOT to Use**: First-time consideration (use "consider" or "examine"); very casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - review the options
   - review the evidence
   - under review
   - conduct a review
   - review carefully
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again) + view (look at)
   
   **Etymology**: French revoir (to see again) → review (look at again, reconsider)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's review what we decided last time.
   - I'll review my notes before the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will review all submissions before making recommendations.
   - The policy is reviewed annually to ensure continued relevance.

1. **Verb**: analyze / analyse

   **Synonyms**: examine, evaluate, study, investigate, assess

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Verb**: reconsider
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To consider something again, especially in order to change a decision or opinion
   - To think about something again with the possibility of changing one's mind
   
   **Synonyms**: rethink, review, re-examine, think again, revise
   
   **Antonyms**: maintain position, stick with decision, refuse to reconsider, remain firm
   
   **When to Use**: Showing openness to changing mind; responding to new information; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When firmness is important; when reconsidering appears weak; after multiple changes
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reconsider a decision
   - reconsider your position
   - willing to reconsider
   - reconsider carefully
   - might want to reconsider
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again) + consider
   
   **Etymology**: Latin re- (again) + considerare → reconsider (consider again)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Maybe you should reconsider your decision.
   - I'm willing to reconsider if you have new information.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board agreed to reconsider the proposal in light of new evidence.
   - Given stakeholder concerns, management has elected to reconsider the implementation timeline.

1. **Verb**: rethink
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To think about something again, especially in order to change a plan or idea; to reconsider fundamentally
   
   **Synonyms**: reconsider, reassess, re-examine, review, revise
   
   **Antonyms**: maintain approach, stick with plan, refuse to change
   
   **When to Use**: Suggesting fundamental reconsideration; responding to problems; showing flexibility; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Minor adjustments (use "adjust" or "modify"); when stability is valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rethink the strategy
   - rethink the approach
   - need to rethink
   - rethink your position
   - rethink from scratch
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again) + think
   
   **Etymology**: re- (again) + Old English þencan (to think) → rethink
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We might need to rethink this whole plan.
   - I'm rethinking my approach to the problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Market disruption necessitates rethinking traditional business models.
   - The organization must fundamentally rethink its strategic positioning.

1. **Verb**: mull over
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive phrasal verb) To think about something carefully and for a long time; to consider at length
   
   **Synonyms**: ponder, consider, think over, reflect on, contemplate
   
   **Antonyms**: decide quickly, dismiss, ignore, act impulsively
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to semi-formal contexts; showing prolonged consideration; buying time for decision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal writing (use "deliberate" or "consider"); quick decisions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mull it over
   - mull over the options
   - mull things over
   - mull over the decision
   - been mulling over
   
   **Root Analysis**: mull (heat/mix, extended to mental processing) + over (thoroughly)
   
   **Etymology**: Mull (originally: heat/spice wine) → extended metaphorically to "work over" thoughts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me mull it over for a few days.
   - I've been mulling over your suggestion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The executive team is mulling over several strategic alternatives.
   - *"Typically informal; in formal contexts prefer 'deliberating' or 'considering carefully'"*

1. **Verb**: entertain (an idea/thought)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To consider or be willing to consider an idea or suggestion; to allow oneself to think about something as a possibility
   - Often implies the idea is somewhat doubtful or unusual
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, contemplate, think about, give thought to
   
   **Antonyms**: dismiss, reject, refuse to consider, discount
   
   **When to Use**: Showing openness to ideas; formal and semi-formal contexts; considering unconventional options
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When full commitment is implied; very casual contexts; for everyday ideas
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - entertain the idea
   - entertain the possibility
   - entertain a thought
   - entertain the notion
   - willing to entertain
   
   **Root Analysis**: enter- (between) + tain (hold)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inter- (among) + tenere (to hold) → entertain (hold among one's thoughts)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm willing to entertain the idea.
   - Have you entertained the possibility of moving?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is willing to entertain alternative proposals.
   - While unconventional, the approach merits entertaining as a possibility.
