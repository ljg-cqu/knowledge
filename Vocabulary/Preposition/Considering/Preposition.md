1. **Preposition**: considering (type: complex/participial preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Taking into account; bearing in mind; when you think about (a particular fact or circumstance)
   - Used to introduce a factor that should be taken into account when making a judgment or decision
   
   **Synonyms**: given, taking into account, in light of, bearing in mind, in view of
   
   **Antonyms**: disregarding, ignoring, despite, regardless of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal speech and writing; presenting reasoned arguments; acknowledging context or constraints; qualifying statements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation where simpler constructions are more natural; when the factor being considered is self-evident; when overused weakens argumentation
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: considering + noun/noun phrase/pronoun
     * considering the circumstances
     * considering your experience
     * considering the cost
   - **Verb + prep**: Used independently as preposition introducing factor
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: consider (verb) + -ing (present participle form used as preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin considerare (to observe carefully) → consider → considering (participial form acquiring prepositional function)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Considering how late it is, we should probably leave.
   - I did pretty well considering I barely studied.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Considering the economic climate, conservative fiscal policy is advisable.
   - The project achieved substantial success considering the resource constraints.

1. **Preposition**: given (type: participial preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Taking into account; in view of; assuming that something is the case
   - Used to introduce a fact or situation that is accepted as true and forms basis for reasoning
   
   **Synonyms**: considering, in view of, taking into account, with, in light of
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, regardless of, without, ignoring, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; establishing premises; logical arguments; professional and academic writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the premise is disputed or hypothetical (use "if" instead); very casual contexts; when simpler constructions suffice
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: given + noun/noun phrase
     * given the circumstances
     * given the evidence
     * given the budget
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (can be followed by "that" clause: given that...)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: give (verb) + -en (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English giefan (to give) → given (past participle) → prepositional use (what is "given"/established)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Given how tired you are, you should rest.
   - I think we did well given the situation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given the budgetary constraints, we recommend phased implementation.
   - Given current market volatility, a diversified approach is prudent.

1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - in considering context)* Taking into account; having as a feature or circumstance to be considered
   - Used to introduce a factor or condition that accompanies or qualifies a statement
   
   **Synonyms**: considering, given, taking into account, in light of
   
   **Antonyms**: without, lacking, absent, minus
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; introducing accompanying circumstances; showing what factors are present in a situation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When clearer prepositional phrases would be more explicit; when ambiguity with other meanings of "with" could occur
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with + noun phrase describing circumstance
     * with that in mind
     * with all due respect
     * with the benefit of hindsight
   - **Verb + prep**: live with, deal with, cope with (acknowledge/consider)
   - **Adjective + prep**: satisfied with, pleased with (after consideration)
   - **Noun + prep**: problem with, issue with (requiring consideration)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English wið/wiþ (against, toward, with)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wið (against, toward) → Middle English → modern "with" (accompaniment, including consideration of)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With your help, we can finish early.
   - With all this rain, the game might be canceled.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With careful planning, the project can be completed within budget.
   - With the recent policy changes, organizations must update compliance procedures.

1. **Preposition**: in light of (type: complex preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* When you consider or take into account (new information or circumstances); because of
   - Used to introduce information that influences understanding or decision-making
   
   **Synonyms**: considering, given, in view of, taking into account, because of
   
   **Antonyms**: disregarding, ignoring, despite, regardless of, irrespective of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; presenting new information that changes perspective; professional and academic writing; explaining reconsideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal); when simpler "because" is clearer; when context hasn't changed
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in light of + noun phrase
     * in light of recent events
     * in light of the evidence
     * in light of new information
   - **Verb + prep**: reconsider in light of, evaluate in light of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + light (metaphor: illumination/understanding) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical: "light" (illumination/clarity) → "in light of" (with the understanding provided by)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In light of what happened, maybe we should talk.
   - In light of the weather, let's stay indoors.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In light of recent regulatory changes, we have updated compliance procedures.
   - In light of empirical findings, the hypothesis requires revision.

1. **Preposition**: in view of (type: complex preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Considering; taking into account; because of
   - Used to introduce a reason or consideration that explains or justifies something
   
   **Synonyms**: considering, in light of, given, because of, due to
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, regardless of, in spite of, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; explaining rationale; professional writing; showing causal reasoning with consideration emphasis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual speech (too formal); when "because" is simpler and sufficient
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in view of + noun phrase
     * in view of the circumstances
     * in view of recent developments
     * in view of these concerns
   - **Verb + prep**: decide in view of, act in view of
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + view (sight/consideration) + of
   
   **Etymology**: "View" (mental perspective) → "in view of" (within the perspective/consideration of)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In view of the late hour, let's wrap up.
   - In view of how it went last time, I'm hesitant.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In view of declining performance, strategic repositioning is necessary.
   - In view of stakeholder concerns, implementation will be delayed.

1. **Preposition**: regarding (type: participial preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* About; concerning; in respect to; with reference to
   - Used to introduce the subject or topic under consideration
   
   **Synonyms**: concerning, about, with regard to, as to, with respect to
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, apart from, aside from
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; introducing topics; professional correspondence; specifying focus of consideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (use "about"); when simpler words work; when overused creating bureaucratic tone
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: regarding + noun phrase
     * regarding your question
     * regarding the proposal
     * regarding this matter
   - **Verb + prep**: inquire regarding, write regarding, speak regarding
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: questions regarding, concerns regarding, decision regarding
   
   **Root Analysis**: regard (verb: consider) + -ing (present participle)
   
   **Etymology**: French regarder (to look at/consider) → regard → regarding (concerning)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a question regarding your email.
   - What's the status regarding the project?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regarding your inquiry, we are pleased to provide the following information.
   - The committee reached consensus regarding implementation timeline.

1. **Preposition**: concerning (type: participial preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* About; regarding; in relation to; on the subject of
   - Used to introduce the topic or subject being considered or discussed
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, about, with regard to, relating to, as to
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, apart from, unrelated to
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; introducing subjects of consideration; professional communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "about"); when simpler language is preferred
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: concerning + noun phrase
     * concerning your request
     * concerning the allegations
     * concerning this issue
   - **Verb + prep**: write concerning, inquire concerning, speak concerning
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: information concerning, questions concerning, issues concerning
   
   **Root Analysis**: concern (verb: relate to) + -ing (present participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin concernere (to relate to) → concern → concerning (about/regarding)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to talk to you concerning the meeting.
   - What are the rules concerning parking?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board has concerns concerning the proposed timeline.
   - Further information concerning eligibility requirements is available online.

1. **Preposition**: with regard to / with respect to (type: complex preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; about; in connection with; as far as...is concerned
   - Used to introduce the specific topic or aspect under consideration
   
   **Synonyms**: concerning, regarding, about, as for, as to, relating to
   
   **Antonyms**: apart from, aside from, excluding, unrelated to
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; professional writing; specifying topics; transitioning between subjects
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal); when "about" is simpler; when overused creating bureaucratic style
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with regard to + noun phrase
     * with regard to your question
     * with respect to the budget
     * with regard to implementation
   - **Verb + prep**: write with regard to, inquire with respect to
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: questions with regard to, concerns with respect to
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + regard/respect (attention/consideration) + to
   
   **Etymology**: French regard (attention) → "with regard to" (with attention directed toward)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With regard to your question, I'll need to check.
   - With respect to timing, we're flexible.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regard to budget allocation, the committee recommends the following adjustments.
   - With respect to regulatory compliance, all procedures have been updated.

1. **Preposition**: as for (type: complex preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* With regard to; concerning; as far as...is concerned
   - Used to introduce a new topic for consideration or to shift focus to a specific subject
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with regard to, speaking of, about
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, apart from, aside from
   
   **When to Use**: Shifting topics; contrasting subjects; all registers but especially transitional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When smooth topic transitions exist; in very formal writing where more explicit phrases preferred
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as for + noun/pronoun
     * as for me
     * as for the budget
     * as for the timeline
   - **Verb + prep**: Used independently to introduce topics
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the capacity of) + for (with respect to)
   
   **Etymology**: Idiomatic combination: "as" + "for" → "as for" (concerning/regarding)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As for me, I'm staying home.
   - As for the cost, we'll figure it out later.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As for implementation timeline, the committee proposes a phased approach.
   - The first phase is complete; as for subsequent phases, planning is underway.

1. **Preposition**: as to (type: complex preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* With regard to; concerning; about; on the subject of
   - Used to introduce the subject or topic under consideration, often with slight uncertainty
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, about, with respect to, as for
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, apart from, certain about
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; introducing topics with slight tentativeness; professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When certainty should be conveyed; casual conversation (use "about"); when clearer phrases exist
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: as to + noun phrase/wh-clause
     * as to whether
     * as to the reason
     * as to how
   - **Verb + prep**: uncertain as to, unclear as to, question as to
   - **Adjective + prep**: unclear as to, uncertain as to, doubtful as to
   - **Noun + prep**: question as to, doubt as to, uncertainty as to
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the manner of) + to (toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Idiomatic: "as" + "to" → "as to" (with respect to/concerning)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not sure as to why he left.
   - There's some confusion as to the timing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - There remains uncertainty as to whether the proposal will gain approval.
   - The committee requires clarification as to the implementation methodology.

1. **Preposition**: pending (type: participial preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* While awaiting; until; during the time before
   - *(Abstract)* While considering or waiting for decision/resolution
   - Used to indicate something is awaiting consideration or decision
   
   **Synonyms**: awaiting, until, while awaiting, in anticipation of
   
   **Antonyms**: following, after, upon completion of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; legal and business writing; indicating interim status during consideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "while waiting for" or "until"); when clearer temporal words work
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: pending + noun phrase
     * pending review
     * pending approval
     * pending further investigation
   - **Verb + prep**: remain pending, hold pending
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: decision pending, action pending
   
   **Root Analysis**: pend (hang/wait) + -ing (present participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin pendere (to hang) → pending (hanging/awaiting)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everything's on hold pending his decision.
   - The trip is pending approval from my boss.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Implementation is suspended pending board review.
   - The application remains pending further documentation.

1. **Preposition**: barring (type: participial preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Except for; excluding; unless there is/are
   - Used to introduce exceptions to be considered or circumstances that would change a conclusion
   
   **Synonyms**: except for, unless, excluding, save for, short of
   
   **Antonyms**: including, with, considering, plus
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; stating exceptions; conditional statements; planning scenarios
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (use "except" or "unless"); when unconditional statements are needed
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: barring + noun phrase
     * barring unforeseen circumstances
     * barring objections
     * barring complications
   - **Verb + prep**: proceed barring, continue barring
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A
   
   **Root Analysis**: bar (prevent/exclude) + -ing (present participle)
   
   **Etymology**: bar (verb: exclude) → barring (excluding/except for)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We'll be there by 6, barring traffic.
   - Barring any surprises, it should work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Barring unforeseen circumstances, the project will conclude on schedule.
   - The proposal will proceed barring significant stakeholder objections.
