**Task**: Extract adverbs (manner, degree, frequency, time, place) that are essential for language mastery from the source text.

**Definition**: Each entry focuses on one adverb with comprehensive learning information including meaning, usage context, synonyms, antonyms, etymology, and examples across registers.

**Rules**:

**Coverage:**
- Read entire source thoroughly—identify all adverbs that are decision-critical for understanding the text
- Prioritize: adverbs of manner, degree modifiers, discourse markers, sentence adverbs, and adverbs with nuanced meanings
- Extract comprehensively to reflect source richness; apply 80/20 principle (focus on high-impact adverbs)
- Do not skip sections; cover both common and specialized adverbial expressions

**Fidelity:**
- Ground all information in adverbs actually present in source
- Never invent adverbs not in source
- Derive meaning from context when source provides it; supplement with standard dictionary definitions

**Focus:**
- Adverbs of manner (how something happens)
- Adverbs of degree (intensity, extent)
- Sentence adverbs (modify entire sentence, express attitude)
- Linking adverbs (discourse markers, transitions)
- Adverbs with precise or nuanced meanings

1. **Adverb**: particularly (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a higher degree than usual or than others; especially; used to single out one member of a group mentioned with "among" (mid or front position)
   
   **Synonyms**: especially, notably, specifically, in particular, above all
   
   **Antonyms**: generally, typically, ordinarily, commonly
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize that something applies more strongly to a specific group; appropriate in both formal and informal contexts; common in research writing to highlight specific findings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse (weakens emphasis); not appropriate when all members are equally affected; can sound redundant with other emphasizers
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - particularly common among
   - particularly popular among
   - particularly prevalent among
   - particularly effective among
   - particularly noticeable among
   
   **Root Analysis**: particul(ar) (from Latin "particula" meaning "small part") + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "particularis" (of a part) → Middle English "particuler" → "particularly" (adverb form emphasizing specificity)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That game is particularly popular among teenagers right now.
   - The issue is particularly noticeable among older users.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The phenomenon is particularly prevalent among urban populations in developing nations.
   - This approach is particularly effective among patients with comorbid conditions.

1. **Adverb**: increasingly (type: degree/time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - More and more; to a progressively greater degree over time; often used with "among" to show growing prevalence (typically front or mid position)
   
   **Synonyms**: progressively, gradually, more and more, ever more, continually more
   
   **Antonyms**: decreasingly, less and less, diminishingly, progressively less
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe trends showing growth or increase; appropriate in formal and informal contexts; essential in research, journalism, and trend analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing stable or declining trends; not appropriate for sudden changes (use "suddenly" or "rapidly"); common error: using for completed trends (use "increased" past tense instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - increasingly common among
   - increasingly popular among
   - increasingly prevalent among
   - increasingly important among
   - become increasingly [adjective] among
   
   **Root Analysis**: increas(e) (from Latin "increscere" meaning "to grow") + -ing + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "increscere" (grow into, grow upon) → Old French "encreiss-" → Middle English "increase" → "increasingly" (adverb form indicating progressive growth)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That opinion is becoming increasingly common among my friends.
   - The app is increasingly popular among college students.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The practice is increasingly prevalent among multinational corporations.
   - Such methodologies are increasingly employed among researchers in the field.

1. **Adverb**: widely (type: degree/manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Over a wide area or range; by or to many people; extensively; often used with "among" to show broad acceptance or distribution (typically mid position)
   
   **Synonyms**: extensively, broadly, commonly, generally, universally
   
   **Antonyms**: narrowly, rarely, seldom, uncommonly, restrictively
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe broad distribution or acceptance; appropriate in all registers; common in academic writing, journalism, and when emphasizing breadth
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when distribution is actually limited; not appropriate for niche or specialized applications; overstating breadth damages credibility
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - widely accepted among
   - widely recognized among
   - widely used among
   - widely held among
   - widely practiced among
   
   **Root Analysis**: wide (broad, extensive) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wid" (spacious, extensive) → Middle English "wide" → "widely" (adverb form indicating breadth)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That belief is widely held among people in that community.
   - The method is widely used among professionals in the field.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theory is widely accepted among scholars in this discipline.
   - Such practices are widely recognized among industry experts as best practices.

1. **Adverb**: commonly (type: frequency)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very often; frequently; in a way that is usual or typical within a group (typically mid or front position)
   
   **Synonyms**: frequently, often, typically, usually, regularly
   
   **Antonyms**: rarely, seldom, infrequently, uncommonly, occasionally
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe typical or frequent occurrence within a group; appropriate in all registers; essential in technical and scientific writing to describe standard patterns
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing rare or exceptional cases; not appropriate when emphasizing uniqueness; can diminish significance of unusual phenomena
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - commonly found among
   - commonly seen among
   - commonly observed among
   - commonly used among
   - most commonly among
   
   **Root Analysis**: common (from Latin "communis" meaning "shared") + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "communis" (shared by all) → Old French "comun" → Middle English "common" → "commonly" (adverb form indicating frequency)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That problem is commonly seen among first-time users.
   - The practice is commonly followed among people in that profession.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The symptom is most commonly observed among patients in the acute phase.
   - Such strategies are commonly employed among organizations facing similar challenges.

1. **Adverb**: equally (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To the same degree or extent; in equal amounts or proportions; often used with "divide/distribute/share among" (typically mid position)
   
   **Synonyms**: evenly, uniformly, fairly, identically, equivalently
   
   **Antonyms**: unequally, unevenly, disproportionately, unfairly, differently
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe uniform distribution or treatment; appropriate in all registers; essential when discussing fairness, equity, or balanced allocation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when distribution is actually unequal (factual error); not appropriate when proportional (not equal) distribution is intended; common confusion with "proportionally"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - divide equally among
   - distribute equally among
   - share equally among
   - split equally among
   - allocated equally among
   
   **Root Analysis**: equal (from Latin "aequalis" meaning "uniform, identical") + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "aequalis" (equal, uniform) → Old French "egal" → Middle English "equal" → "equally" (adverb form indicating uniformity)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We'll split the cost equally among the five of us.
   - The work should be divided equally among all team members.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Resources will be allocated equally among participating institutions.
   - Voting rights are distributed equally among all shareholders.
