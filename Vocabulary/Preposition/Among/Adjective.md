**Task**: Extract adjectives (descriptive words, attributes, qualifiers) that are essential for language mastery from the source text.

**Definition**: Each entry focuses on one adjective with comprehensive learning information including meaning, usage context, synonyms, antonyms, etymology, and examples across registers.

**Rules**:

**Coverage:**
- Read entire source thoroughly—identify all adjectives that are decision-critical for understanding the text
- Prioritize: precise descriptive adjectives, technical descriptors, evaluative adjectives, and adjectives with nuanced meanings
- Extract comprehensively to reflect source richness; apply 80/20 principle (focus on high-impact adjectives)
- Do not skip sections; cover both common and specialized descriptive vocabulary

**Fidelity:**
- Ground all information in adjectives actually present in source
- Never invent adjectives not in source
- Derive meaning from context when source provides it; supplement with standard dictionary definitions

**Focus:**
- Precise descriptive adjectives with specific meanings
- Evaluative and opinion adjectives
- Technical or domain-specific descriptors
- Adjectives with subtle connotations
- Gradable vs. non-gradable adjectives

**Format:**
- **Adjective**: [base form] (include comparative/superlative if irregular)
- **Meaning**: Collins Dictionary style definition(s)
  - *If adjective has multiple senses, number them clearly*
  - *Use simple, clear language to explain meaning*
  - *Include grammatical information (gradable/non-gradable, attributive/predicative)*

- **When NOT to Use**: Contexts where this adjective would be inappropriate or incorrect

- **Etymology**: Language origin and historical development (brief)

1. **Adjective**: common (among) - frequently used with "among" in comparative contexts
   **Meaning**: 
   - Occurring frequently or existing in large numbers within a particular group (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - *(Sense 2)* Shared by or affecting all members of a group
   
   **Synonyms**: frequent, prevalent, usual, typical, widespread
   
   **Antonyms**: rare, uncommon, unusual, exceptional, unique
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe high frequency or prevalence within a specified group; appropriate in all registers; essential for research writing, medical literature, and comparative analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing truly rare phenomena; not appropriate for unique situations; can sound dismissive when describing serious individual experiences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - common among [group]
   - particularly common among
   - increasingly common among
   - most common among
   - less common among
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together, with) + -mon (from Latin "munis" meaning "public, general")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "communis" (shared by all) → Old French "comun" → Middle English "commune" → Modern English "common"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That kind of thinking is pretty common among teenagers.
   - Anxiety is really common among students during exam season.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This condition is particularly common among elderly populations.
   - The practice is increasingly common among organizations seeking competitive advantage.

1. **Adjective**: popular (among) - standard adjective-preposition collocation
   **Meaning**: 
   - Liked, admired, or enjoyed by many people within a particular group (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: well-liked, favored, beloved, admired, celebrated
   
   **Antonyms**: unpopular, disliked, unfavored, rejected, spurned
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe positive reception or preference within a group; appropriate in all registers; common in marketing, social analysis, and product descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing objective quality (use "effective" or "high-quality"); not appropriate when discussing mandatory or required items; conflating popularity with quality is a common error
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - popular among [group]
   - particularly popular among
   - increasingly popular among
   - highly popular among
   - most popular among
   
   **Root Analysis**: popul- (from Latin "populus" meaning "people") + -ar (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "popularis" (of the people) → Middle French "populaire" → English "popular" (originally meaning "of or from the people," evolved to mean "liked by many")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That show is super popular among college students right now.
   - Korean food has become really popular among my friends.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology is particularly popular among researchers in quantitative fields.
   - This approach has proven popular among policymakers seeking evidence-based interventions.

1. **Adjective**: prevalent (among) - formal descriptor of frequency
   **Meaning**: 
   - Widespread or common in a particular area or at a particular time, especially within a specific group (gradable, primarily predicative but can be attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: widespread, common, extensive, pervasive, rife
   
   **Antonyms**: rare, scarce, uncommon, infrequent, sparse
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal or academic contexts to describe frequency or dominance within populations; appropriate in research, medical, and technical writing; more formal than "common"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (sounds overly formal); not appropriate for positive phenomena you want to celebrate (neutral/negative connotation); use "popular" or "common" instead in informal contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prevalent among [group]
   - particularly prevalent among
   - highly prevalent among
   - most prevalent among
   - become prevalent among
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before, superior) + val- (strong, from Latin "valere") + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praevalent-" (having greater power) → English "prevalent" (16th century, meaning "having superior force or influence," evolved to mean "widespread")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That attitude is pretty prevalent among people in that industry.
   - The problem has become prevalent among younger users.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The condition is highly prevalent among populations with genetic predisposition.
   - Such practices are particularly prevalent among organizations in emerging markets.

1. **Adjective**: rare (among) - opposite of common, used for infrequency
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not occurring very often; uncommon or unusual within a particular group (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: uncommon, unusual, infrequent, scarce, exceptional
   
   **Antonyms**: common, frequent, widespread, prevalent, typical
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe low frequency or exceptional nature within a group; appropriate in all registers; common in medical literature, research, and when highlighting uniqueness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when something is actually common (factual error); not appropriate when "unique" is more precise; understatement can diminish significance
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rare among [group]
   - relatively rare among
   - quite rare among
   - increasingly rare among
   - extremely rare among
   
   **Root Analysis**: rare (from Latin "rarus" meaning "thinly sown, having intervals between")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "rarus" (spaced out, thin) → Old French "rere" → Middle English "rare" → Modern English "rare"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That kind of honesty is pretty rare among politicians these days.
   - It's rare among my friends to find someone who actually enjoys running.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Such outcomes are relatively rare among patients receiving standard treatment protocols.
   - This level of transparency is rare among organizations in this sector.
