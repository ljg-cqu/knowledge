   **Meaning**: 
   - Individual persons or entities that belong to a larger organization, group, or category; often used with "among members"
   
   **Synonyms**: participants, constituents, affiliates, associates, adherents
   
   **Antonyms**: outsiders, non-members, strangers, excluded parties
   
   **When to Use**: Use when referring to individuals who belong to an organization or group; appropriate in all registers; essential in organizational, political, and social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to the collective as a whole (use "membership"); not appropriate for non-affiliated individuals
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - among members
   - among group members
   - among team members
   - popular among members
   - distributed among members
   
   **Root Analysis**: member (from Latin "membrum" meaning "limb, part of the body")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "membrum" (limb, part) → Old French "membre" → Middle English "member" (metaphorical extension from body part to part of organization)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That idea is pretty popular among members of the club.
   - We need to divide the work among all team members.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consensus emerged among members of the committee regarding the proposal.
   - The policy has generated significant support among members of the organization.

1. **Noun**: consensus (U) - agreement within a group
   **Meaning**: 
   - General agreement or collective opinion reached by a group; frequently used with "consensus among"
   
   **Synonyms**: agreement, accord, unanimity, concurrence, common ground
   
   **Antonyms**: disagreement, discord, dissent, conflict, division
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing collective agreement within a group; appropriate in formal and semi-formal contexts; common in political, academic, and organizational discourse
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when agreement is not genuine or complete; not appropriate in casual contexts (use "agreement"); common error: treating as countable noun ("consensuses" is rare)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - consensus among [group]
   - reach consensus among
   - build consensus among
   - lack of consensus among
   - growing consensus among
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + sens- (feel, perceive, from Latin "sentire") + -us (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "consensus" (agreement, accord) from "consentire" (to feel together, agree) → English "consensus" (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's no consensus among my friends about which movie to watch.
   - We finally reached consensus among the group about the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A strong consensus exists among experts regarding the validity of the methodology.
   - The study aimed to identify areas of consensus among stakeholders.

1. **Noun**: distribution (C/U, pl: distributions) - allocation across a group
   **Meaning**: 
   - The way something is divided or spread out among a number of people or entities
   - *(Sense 2)* The process or act of distributing something among multiple recipients
   
   **Synonyms**: allocation, division, apportionment, dispersal, sharing
   
   **Antonyms**: concentration, consolidation, centralization, gathering, accumulation
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing how resources, responsibilities, or characteristics are allocated; appropriate in formal and technical contexts; essential in statistics, economics, and organizational planning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts (use "split" or "divide"); not appropriate when describing delivery of goods (different sense); technical term may be too formal for everyday conversation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - distribution among [groups]
   - fair distribution among
   - equal distribution among
   - uneven distribution among
   - distribution pattern among
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart) + tribut- (assign, from Latin "tribuere") + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "distributio" (division, distribution) from "distribuere" (to divide up) → Middle English "distribution"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The distribution of work among team members seems pretty uneven.
   - We need a fairer distribution of chores among roommates.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined the distribution of wealth among different socioeconomic groups.
   - Analysis revealed significant variation in the distribution among regional populations.

1. **Noun**: prevalence (U) - frequency within a population
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being common, widespread, or frequent within a particular group or area at a specific time
   
   **Synonyms**: commonness, frequency, widespread occurrence, ubiquity, pervasiveness
   
   **Antonyms**: rarity, scarcity, infrequency, uncommonness, absence
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, academic, or medical contexts to describe frequency; appropriate in research writing, epidemiology, and technical analysis; more formal than "commonness"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "how common it is"); not appropriate for describing popularity (use "popularity"); primarily used as uncountable noun
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prevalence among [group]
   - high prevalence among
   - low prevalence among
   - prevalence rate among
   - increasing prevalence among
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before, superior) + val- (be strong, from Latin "valere") + -ence (noun suffix indicating state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "praevalentia" (superior strength) from "praevalere" (have superior power) → English "prevalence" (17th century, medical/statistical usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The prevalence of that attitude among young people is kind of worrying.
   - There's a high prevalence of anxiety among college students.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study documented the prevalence of the condition among elderly populations.
   - Researchers observed significant prevalence variation among demographic subgroups.

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - among peers
   - among one's peers
   - popular among peers
   - respected among peers
   - standing among peers
   
   **Root Analysis**: peer (from Latin "par" meaning "equal")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "par" (equal) → Old French "per" → Middle English "peer" (equal in rank or standing)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's really respected among his peers at work.
   - She stands out among her peers because of her creativity.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The researcher is highly regarded among peers in the scientific community.
   - The methodology has gained acceptance among peers in the field.
