1. **Adjective**: relevant
   **Meaning**: 
   - Closely connected or appropriate to what is being done or considered; pertinent to the matter at hand; having regard to the subject
   - *Gradable adjective; can be used attributively (relevant information) or predicatively (this is relevant)*
   
   **Synonyms**: pertinent, applicable, appropriate, germane, related, material
   
   **Antonyms**: irrelevant, unrelated, inapplicable, immaterial, extraneous
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts when discussing what matters or applies to a particular subject; extremely common in professional and scholarly writing where regard to appropriateness is critical
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simpler words like "related" would be clearer in casual conversation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - relevant information, relevant to, highly relevant
   - directly relevant, particularly relevant, most relevant
   - relevant experience, relevant data, relevant with regard to
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + lev (lift, raise) + -ant (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin relevare (to raise up, relieve) → Medieval Latin relevantem (bearing upon, pertinent) → French relevant → English "relevant" (pertinent, applicable)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is this relevant to what we're discussing with regard to the project?
   - I don't think that's relevant right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All relevant documentation with regard to the proposal has been provided to stakeholders.
   - The committee will review materials relevant to the investigation.

1. **Adjective**: pertinent
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relevant or applicable to a particular matter; appropriate to the situation; having direct bearing with regard to the subject
   - *Gradable adjective; primarily used attributively; more formal than "relevant"*
   
   **Synonyms**: relevant, applicable, appropriate, germane, apt, material
   
   **Antonyms**: irrelevant, impertinent, inapplicable, immaterial, extraneous
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts, especially in legal, academic, and professional writing; more formal than "relevant"; when showing regard to appropriateness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "relevant" would be more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pertinent information, pertinent to, pertinent questions
   - pertinent details, pertinent facts, most pertinent
   - particularly pertinent, pertinent with regard to
   
   **Root Analysis**: per- (through, thoroughly) + tin (hold, keep) + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin pertinere (to relate to, belong to) → pertinentem (relevant) → English "pertinent" (directly related, relevant)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's a pertinent question with regard to our timeline.
   - All pertinent details have been shared.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please provide all pertinent information with regard to the compliance audit.
   - The report contains pertinent observations regarding operational efficiency.

1. **Adjective**: applicable
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relevant or appropriate in a particular situation; capable of being applied; having regard to specific conditions or circumstances
   - *Gradable adjective; often used predicatively with "to"*
   
   **Synonyms**: relevant, appropriate, pertinent, suitable, apropos
   
   **Antonyms**: inapplicable, irrelevant, unsuitable, inappropriate
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when discussing rules, policies, or methods that can be applied to specific situations; common in legal and technical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "relevant" would be simpler and clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - applicable to, applicable law, where applicable
   - not applicable, directly applicable, applicable with regard to
   - broadly applicable, widely applicable
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to, toward) + plic (fold, apply) + -able (capable of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin applicare (to attach to, apply) → applicabilis → English "applicable" (capable of being applied)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is this rule applicable to our situation?
   - The discount is applicable with regard to all purchases over $50.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All applicable regulations with regard to environmental compliance must be observed.
   - The methodology is widely applicable to diverse research contexts.

1. **Adjective**: appropriate
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suitable or proper in the circumstances; having regard to what is fitting or acceptable in a given context
   - *Gradable adjective; can be used attributively or predicatively*
   
   **Synonyms**: suitable, fitting, proper, apt, applicable, relevant
   
   **Antonyms**: inappropriate, unsuitable, improper, unfit, irrelevant
   
   **When to Use**: General contexts when discussing what is suitable or fitting; very common in professional communication regarding decisions and actions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When more specific terms like "relevant" or "applicable" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - appropriate action, appropriate with regard to, deemed appropriate
   - appropriate response, appropriate measures, most appropriate
   - appropriate for, culturally appropriate
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to, toward) + propri (own, proper) + -ate (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin appropriare (to make one's own) → appropriatus → English "appropriate" (suitable, proper)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is this outfit appropriate with regard to the dress code?

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will determine appropriate measures with regard to the compliance violations.
   - Appropriate protocols must be followed with regard to data security.

1. **Adjective**: related
   **Meaning**: 
   - Connected or associated; having regard to a connection or relationship with something else
   - *Non-gradable adjective typically; used attributively or predicatively with "to"*
   
   **Synonyms**: connected, associated, linked, relevant, pertaining, concerning
   
   **Antonyms**: unrelated, disconnected, separate, independent, irrelevant
   
   **When to Use**: General contexts when showing connections between topics; very common in all registers; simpler alternative to "relevant" or "pertinent"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precision requires "relevant" or "pertinent" in formal contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - related to, closely related, directly related
   - related issues, related matters, related with regard to
   - related topics, related concerns
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + late (carry, bring) + -ed (past participle/adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin relatus (brought back, referred to) → relate → English "related" (connected, having reference to)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These are related issues with regard to the project.
   - Everything related to the meeting is in the folder.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All related documentation with regard to the merger has been filed.
   - The study examined factors related to organizational performance.

1. **Adjective**: concerning
   **Meaning**: 
   - Causing anxiety or worry; troubling; giving cause for concern with regard to a situation
   - *Gradable adjective; used attributively or predicatively*
   
   **Synonyms**: worrying, troubling, disturbing, alarming, unsettling
   
   **Antonyms**: reassuring, comforting, encouraging, positive
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and informal contexts when describing situations that cause worry or require attention; shows regard to problematic aspects
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When confusion with the preposition "concerning" (meaning "about") might arise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - concerning development, concerning trend, concerning with regard to
   - deeply concerning, particularly concerning, most concerning
   - concerning issue, concerning behavior
   
   **Root Analysis**: concern (relate to, trouble) + -ing (present participle/adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin concernere (to sift together, relate to) → concern (trouble, worry) → "concerning" (worrying)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's a concerning development with regard to the deadline.
   - His behavior is becoming concerning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings reveal concerning trends with regard to student performance.
   - Several concerning issues have been identified regarding compliance.

1. **Adjective**: germane
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relevant to a subject under consideration; having regard to what is appropriate and pertinent to the discussion
   - *Non-gradable or weakly gradable; primarily used predicatively with "to"; formal*
   
   **Synonyms**: relevant, pertinent, applicable, appropriate, related, material
   
   **Antonyms**: irrelevant, immaterial, extraneous, inappropriate
   
   **When to Use**: Formal academic and professional contexts; legal writing; when emphasizing direct relevance; more literary than "relevant"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "relevant" is clearer and more common
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - germane to, not germane, germane with regard to
   - germane to the discussion, germane to the matter
   - particularly germane
   
   **Root Analysis**: From proper noun German → germane (closely akin, relevant)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin germanus (having the same parents, genuine) → Middle English germane (closely related) → English "germane" (relevant, pertinent)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That point isn't really germane to what we're discussing.
   - Is this germane with regard to our decision?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will consider only testimony germane to the investigation.
   - Arguments not germane to the central thesis have been excluded.

1. **Adjective**: material
   **Meaning**: 
   - Significant; important; relevant and having regard to substantial impact on a matter or decision
   - *(Legal/Technical)* Having real importance or great consequence; essential to the issue at hand
   - *Gradable adjective; used attributively or predicatively*
   
   **Synonyms**: significant, important, relevant, substantial, essential, pertinent
   
   **Antonyms**: immaterial, irrelevant, insignificant, trivial, unimportant
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts, especially legal, financial, and business contexts; when emphasizing something's importance or relevance with regard to decisions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts; when confusion with "material" (physical substance) might arise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - material information, material to, material with regard to
   - material fact, material breach, materially relevant
   - material impact, material difference
   
   **Root Analysis**: materi (matter, substance) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin materialis (of matter) → Late Latin materialis (important) → English "material" (significant, relevant)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is there a material difference with regard to the two options?
   - That's a material point we should consider.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All material facts with regard to the transaction must be disclosed.
   - The change has no material impact regarding operational efficiency.

   **Common Phrases**:

   - respective responsibilities, respective fields, in their respective
   - respective countries
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + spec (look) + -ive (adjective suffix)

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone returned to their respective homes.
   - They each have their respective opinions with regard to the issue.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The parties must fulfill their respective obligations with regard to the contract.

1. **Adjective**: corresponding

   - Similar in character, form, or function; analogous or equivalent; having regard to parallel or matching relationships
   - *Non-gradable or weakly gradable; used attributively or participially*
   
   **Synonyms**: equivalent, matching, analogous, parallel, comparable, related
   
   **Antonyms**: different, contrasting, dissimilar, unrelated
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when indicating parallel or matching elements; common in academic and technical writing regarding comparisons
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precision requires more specific terms like "identical" or "similar"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - corresponding to, corresponding period, corresponding with regard to
   - corresponding increase, corresponding change, corresponding value
   - corresponding section
   
   **Root Analysis**: cor- (together) + respond (answer, match) + -ing (adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin correspondere (to answer together, match) → correspond → English "corresponding" (matching, analogous)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a corresponding increase in demand with regard to the new product.
   - Each problem has a corresponding solution.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Revenue growth shows a corresponding improvement with regard to operational efficiency.
   - The corresponding provisions regarding liability are outlined in Section 7.

1. **Adjective**: due
   **Meaning**: 
   - Appropriate or fitting with regard to the circumstances; proper; required or owed
   - *(Phrase)* Due regard: proper attention or consideration given to something
   - *Non-gradable typically; used attributively or predicatively*
   
   **Synonyms**: appropriate, proper, fitting, suitable, owed, required
   
   **Antonyms**: inappropriate, improper, undue, unfitting
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when discussing what is proper, fitting, or owed; very common in phrases like "due regard," "due consideration," "due diligence"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When clearer alternatives like "proper" or "required" would be better
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - due regard, due consideration, due diligence
   - due attention, due process, with due regard to
   - due respect, give due weight to
   
   **Root Analysis**: From French deu (owed, proper)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin debere (to owe) → Old French deu → English "due" (owed, proper, appropriate)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Give this issue due attention.
   - With all due respect, I disagree.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee gave due regard to all stakeholder concerns.
   - Due consideration has been given to the environmental impact.

1. **Adjective**: tangential
   **Meaning**: 
   - Diverging from a previous course or line; peripheral; only slightly connected or relevant; lacking proper regard to the main subject
   - *Gradable adjective; used attributively or predicatively*
   
   **Synonyms**: peripheral, marginal, incidental, secondary, oblique, digressive
   
   **Antonyms**: relevant, pertinent, central, essential, germane, directly related
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when describing something that is only slightly related or diverges from the main topic; shows lack of direct regard to the central issue
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "off-topic" or "not relevant" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tangential to, tangential relationship, tangential with regard to
   - merely tangential, tangential connection, tangential reference
   - tangential issue
   
   **Root Analysis**: tangent (touching) + -ial (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin tangentem (touching) → tangent → English "tangential" (touching only slightly, peripheral)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's tangential to what we're discussing with regard to the budget.
   - Let's not get tangential here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The objection raises issues merely tangential to the central question.
   - Arguments tangential to the main thesis have been excluded from consideration.
