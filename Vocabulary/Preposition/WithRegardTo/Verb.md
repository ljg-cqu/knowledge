1. **Verb**: regard
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To consider or think of someone or something in a particular way.
   - *(Sense 2)* To look at someone or something attentively; to observe.
   - *(Sense 3)* To have respect or concern for something.
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, view, see, deem, judge
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, ignore, neglect, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: In semi-formal and formal contexts when expressing evaluation, opinion, or respect with regard to a person, thing, or idea.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk where "think" or "see" would be more natural; or when you only mean "look at" physically (then "look at" is clearer).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - regard as, be regarded as, regard with
   - highly regarded, widely regarded, generally regarded
   - in/with regard to (phrasal preposition built from the noun "regard")
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + gard (guard, watch)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *regarder* (to look at, consider) → Middle English *regarden* → English verb "regard".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I regard her as a close friend, especially with regard to how supportive she has been.
   - He's widely regarded as the expert with regard to this topic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee regards this matter as urgent with regard to public safety.
   - These findings are generally regarded as definitive with regard to this question.

1. **Verb**: concern
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To relate to; to be about; to have to do with.
   - *(Sense 2)* To worry or trouble someone; to cause anxiety.
   - *(Sense 3)* To be important to or affect someone.
   
   **Synonyms**: relate to, pertain to, involve, affect (sense 1); worry, trouble, disturb (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: not affect, be irrelevant to; reassure (for sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: When saying that something is about a particular topic (sense 1) or when describing what worries people (sense 2), especially in discussions with regard to risk or responsibility.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When a simpler verb like "be about" or "worry" would be clearer in casual contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - be concerned with, concern oneself with, concern someone
   - as far as X is concerned, concerning this matter
   - issues that concern, matters that concern (someone) with regard to
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + cern (separate, sift)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *concernere* (to sift together, relate to) → Middle French *concerner* → English verb "concern".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This mainly concerns our plans with regard to the summer.
   - It really concerns me with regard to how stressed you look.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy concerns all employees with regard to remote work arrangements.
   - The report concerns issues with regard to long-term financial stability.

1. **Verb**: address
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To deal with or try to solve a problem, question, or issue.
   - *(Sense 2)* To speak or write to someone formally.
   - *(Sense 3)* To direct attention or efforts toward something.
   
   **Synonyms**: tackle, deal with, handle, approach, treat (sense 1)
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, avoid, neglect, evade
   
   **When to Use**: In professional and academic contexts when describing how problems, concerns, or questions are dealt with, especially with regard to plans, risks, or complaints.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very informal speech where "deal with" or "fix" would be more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - address an issue, address a concern, address a question
   - address matters with regard to, adequately address, fail to address
   - address someone formally, address a letter to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + dress (direct, arrange)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *adresser* (to direct, set right) → Middle English *addressen* → English "address" (to direct attention to, deal with).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We really need to address these problems with regard to the schedule.
   - Can we address your concerns with regard to the move?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will address outstanding issues with regard to data security.
   - This section addresses questions with regard to the research methodology.

1. **Verb**: pertain
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To be relevant or applicable to something; to relate to.
   - *(Sense 2)* (Formal) To belong to something as a part or characteristic.
   
   **Synonyms**: relate to, concern, apply to, bear on, refer to
   
   **Antonyms**: not relate to, be irrelevant to
   
   **When to Use**: In formal, especially legal or academic, language when you want to state precisely what rules, documents, or statements relate to with regard to scope.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual speech, where "relate to" or "be about" are simpler.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pertain to, as it pertains to, pertaining to
   - documents pertaining to, matters pertaining to
   - regulations that pertain to, rights that pertain to
   
   **Root Analysis**: per- (through, thoroughly) + tain (hold, keep)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *pertinere* (to reach through, relate to) → Old French *partenir* → Middle English *pertainen* → English "pertain".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These rules don't really pertain to us with regard to the event.
   - Any questions that pertain to the trip should be asked now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulations pertain to all matters with regard to workplace safety.
   - Only documents that pertain to the case with regard to liability will be examined.

1. **Verb**: relate
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To have a connection with or reference to something; to concern.
   - *(Sense 2)* To tell a story or give an account of something.
   - *(Sense 3)* To establish or show a connection between things.
   
   **Synonyms**: connect, concern, pertain to, be linked to (sense 1); recount, narrate (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: disconnect, separate, be unrelated to
   
   **When to Use**: When describing how ideas, facts, or people are connected, especially when mapping how one point relates to another with regard to a problem or argument.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you only mean "tell" (then "tell" or "describe" is clearer), or when you need a more specific technical verb like "correlate".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - relate to, be related to, directly relate to
   - as it relates to, relate back to
   - relate something to something, relate a story
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + late (carry, bring)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *relatus* (carried back, reported) → Middle French *relater* → English "relate" (to tell, to connect).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How does this relate to what you said with regard to deadlines?
   - I can't really relate to that experience.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These findings relate to previous work with regard to language acquisition.
   - The policy relates to all procedures with regard to data retention.

1. **Verb**: refer
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To mention or allude to something.
   - *(Sense 2)* To direct someone to a source of information or authority.
   - *(Sense 3)* To relate to or concern something.
   
   **Synonyms**: mention, allude to, cite (sense 1); direct, send (sense 2); concern, relate to (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: avoid mentioning, omit, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: In academic, professional, and service contexts when pointing to sources, documents, or topics with regard to information or responsibility.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk where "talk about" or "mention" would be more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - refer to, refer back to, referring to
   - please refer to, refer someone to
   - with reference to, as referred to above
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + fer (carry, bear)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *referre* (to carry back, report) → Middle French *référer* → English "refer".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you referring to what I said with regard to the budget?
   - I'll refer you to someone who knows more about this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please refer to Section 3.2 with regard to reporting requirements.
   - The report refers to several studies with regard to climate risk.

1. **Verb**: discuss
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To talk about something with another person or group in order to exchange ideas or reach a decision.
   - *(Sense 2)* To examine or analyze a subject in speech or writing.
   
   **Synonyms**: talk about, debate, examine, consider, review
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, avoid, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: When focusing on conversation or analysis around a topic, especially in meetings and documents with regard to decisions, plans, or problems.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the interaction is one-way ("present", "inform") rather than two-way exchange.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - discuss an issue, discuss a matter, discuss options
   - discuss with regard to, discuss further, discuss in detail
   - discuss whether/what/how
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart) + cuss (shake, strike)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *discutere* (to shake apart, examine) → Middle English *discussen* → English "discuss".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to discuss what to do with regard to the party.
   - Let's discuss your concerns with regard to the move.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report discusses several questions with regard to experimental design.
   - We will discuss options with regard to restructuring at the next meeting.

1. **Verb**: inquire
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To ask for information in a careful or formal way.
   - *(Sense 2)* To investigate something officially.
   
   **Synonyms**: ask, question, investigate, query, seek information
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, disregard, assume
   
   **When to Use**: Mainly in formal or polite contexts when asking about status, details, or reasons with regard to something.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In everyday casual conversation where "ask" is more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inquire about, inquire regarding, inquire into
   - inquire whether, inquire as to
   - make inquiries with regard to
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + quire (seek, ask)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *inquirere* (to seek into, investigate) → Old French *enquerre* → Middle English *enquire/inquire* → English "inquire".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I called to inquire about the job with regard to the starting date.
   - Can I inquire regarding your availability next week?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We write to inquire with regard to the status of our application.
   - The commission will inquire into matters with regard to financial reporting.

1. **Verb**: consider
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To think carefully about something, especially before making a decision.
   - *(Sense 2)* To believe or think that someone or something is a particular way.
   - *(Sense 3)* To take something into account; to have regard to.
   
   **Synonyms**: think about, contemplate, deliberate, regard, deem
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, dismiss, overlook, disregard
   
   **When to Use**: In any context where careful thought or evaluation is needed, especially in decisions with regard to people, plans, or risks.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you simply mean "notice" or "see"; then "notice" or "see" is more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - consider carefully, take into consideration
   - consider doing, consider whether/what/how
   - consider all factors with regard to, be considered
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + sider (star, heavenly body)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *considerare* (to observe carefully, examine the stars) → Old French *considerer* → Middle English *consideren* → English "consider".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Have you considered what I said with regard to changing jobs?
   - I'll consider your idea with regard to the trip.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board will consider all factors with regard to long-term sustainability.
   - Several alternatives were considered with regard to cost and feasibility.

1. **Verb**: clarify
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* To make something clearer or easier to understand by giving more details or a simpler explanation.
   - *(Sense 2)* (Technical) To remove impurities from a liquid or substance.
   
   **Synonyms**: explain, elucidate, illuminate, make clear, spell out
   
   **Antonyms**: confuse, obscure, complicate, muddle
   
   **When to Use**: When improving understanding, especially in professional or academic communication with regard to complex instructions, policies, or arguments.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When a quick, informal "explain" would be more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - clarify a point, clarify the situation, seek clarification
   - clarify what/whether/how, clarify expectations
   - clarify requirements with regard to, clarify roles with regard to
   
   **Root Analysis**: clar- (clear, bright) + -ify (make)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *clarus* (clear) → Late Latin *clarificare* → Old French *clarifier* → Middle English *clarifien* → English "clarify".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you clarify what you meant with regard to the deadline?
   - I just want to clarify things with regard to our plans this weekend.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We request that you clarify the requirements with regard to documentation.
   - The amendment seeks to clarify obligations with regard to data protection.
