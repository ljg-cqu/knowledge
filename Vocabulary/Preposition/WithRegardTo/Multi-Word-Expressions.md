1. **Expression**: with regard to (type: phrasal preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Concerning; about; in relation to (formal way of introducing a topic)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership; words must appear in this order)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, in relation to, as regards
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Formal business correspondence, academic writing, professional reports, official documents when introducing topics for discussion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "about" is more natural; avoid overuse which makes writing unnecessarily formal; text messages and informal emails
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in regard to (slightly less common variant)
   - with regards to (incorrect—"regards" should be singular)
   - as regards (British English formal variant)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Fixed formal construction combining preposition "with" + noun "regard" + preposition "to" to create formal prepositional phrase; conventional usage in formal English establishes this as standard way to introduce topics
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (preposition) + regard (noun: consideration, reference) + to (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With regard to your question, I think we should wait.
   - I have concerns with regard to the timeline.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regard to your inquiry dated November 15, we are pleased to provide the following response.
   - The board has issued new guidelines with regard to executive compensation policies.

1. **Expression**: in connection with (type: phrasal preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Related to; associated with; concerning; often implies causal or associative relationship
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed word order and preposition choices)
   
   **Synonyms**: in relation to, regarding, concerning, associated with, related to
   
   **Antonyms**: unrelated to, separate from, independent of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when emphasizing relationships or connections; legal, journalistic, and official writing; when showing causation or association
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple "about" would be clearer; when no clear connection exists; overly formal for casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in connection to (less common, sometimes considered non-standard)
   - connected with (related phrase, less formal)
   - in conjunction with (related but means "together with" rather than "concerning")
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Preposition "in" + noun "connection" + preposition "with" forms fixed phrase emphasizing relational aspect; frequency of co-occurrence in formal discourse establishes conventional usage
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (preposition) + connection (noun: link, relationship) + with (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm calling in connection with the order I placed yesterday.
   - There are concerns in connection with the new system.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audit was conducted in connection with the annual compliance review process.
   - Several measures have been implemented in connection with strategic quality initiatives.

1. **Expression**: with respect to (type: phrasal preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Concerning; regarding; especially common in academic and technical contexts
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed construction; changing word order breaks meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with regard to, in relation to, as to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Formal academic and technical writing; scientific papers; mathematical and technical documentation; when precision is essential
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; when simpler alternatives suffice; can seem overly formal in standard business writing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in respect of (British English variant)
   - respecting (archaic single-word form)
   - with all due respect to (different meaning: polite disagreement)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Preposition "with" + noun "respect" + preposition "to"; semantic compatibility (respect = consideration/reference); especially strong in academic discourse conventions
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (preposition) + respect (noun: regard, reference, consideration) + to (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With respect to your concerns, I understand where you're coming from.
   - What's the current status with respect to hiring?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The derivative was computed with respect to the independent variable x.
   - With respect to experimental methodology, several modifications have been implemented to enhance validity.

1. **Expression**: take into account (type: verb + preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Consider; give consideration to; factor in when making decisions
   - *Fixedness*: strong (phrasal verb; components work together as unit)
   
   **Synonyms**: consider, factor in, bear in mind, take into consideration, account for
   
   **Antonyms**: overlook, disregard, ignore, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and informal contexts when discussing consideration of factors; business planning; decision-making discussions; analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simpler "consider" would suffice; when precision requires distinguishing from "account for" (explain)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - take into consideration (more formal synonym)
   - take account of (British English variant)
   - taking into account (gerund form common in formal writing)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Verb "take" + preposition "into" + noun "account" forms phrasal verb with idiomatic meaning; semantic development from literal accounting metaphor to figurative consideration
   
   **Root Analysis**: take (verb) + into (preposition: direction) + account (noun: reckoning, consideration)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to take into account the budget constraints.
   - Did you take into account the delivery time?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When evaluating proposals, the committee takes into account multiple criteria with regard to feasibility.
   - Analysis must take into account regional variations concerning demographic factors.

1. **Expression**: give due consideration to (type: verb + adjective + noun + preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Properly and thoroughly think about; provide appropriate attention to; show proper regard for
   - *Fixedness*: medium (core is "give consideration"; "due" adds emphasis but is optional)
   
   **Synonyms**: carefully consider, thoroughly evaluate, properly weigh, give proper attention to
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, overlook, give short shrift to, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts when emphasizing thoroughness and appropriateness of consideration; official decision-making; showing respect for concerns
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts; when simpler "consider carefully" would be clearer; when emphasis on proper regard is unnecessary
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - give due regard to (very similar)
   - give careful consideration to (emphasizes care over propriety)
   - give full consideration to (emphasizes completeness)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Verb "give" + adjective "due" (proper, appropriate) + noun "consideration" + preposition "to"; semantic field of providing proper attention; conventional phrase in formal discourse
   
   **Root Analysis**: give (verb) + due (adjective: appropriate, owed) + consideration (noun: careful thought) + to (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We'll give due consideration to your suggestions.
   - The team gave due consideration to all the options.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board will give due consideration to stakeholder concerns with regard to the proposed changes.
   - Proper methodology requires giving due consideration to potential confounding variables.

1. **Expression**: bear in mind (type: verb + preposition + noun idiomatic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Remember; keep in consideration; maintain awareness of something relevant
   - *Fixedness*: strong (idiomatic phrasal verb; meaning is somewhat non-compositional)
   
   **Synonyms**: remember, keep in mind, don't forget, take into account, consider
   
   **Antonyms**: forget, overlook, disregard, lose sight of
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and informal contexts when reminding about important factors; advice-giving; when directing attention to relevant considerations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing where "consider" might be more appropriate; when urgency requires more direct language
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - keep in mind (more common in American English)
   - bear in memory (archaic, literal sense)
   - keeping in mind (gerund form for written contexts)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Metaphorical extension of "bear" (carry) + "in mind" (in consciousness); semantic development from physical carrying to mental retention; established idiomatic expression
   
   **Root Analysis**: bear (verb: carry, support) + in (preposition) + mind (noun: consciousness, awareness)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Bear in mind that we have a tight deadline.
   - Please bear in mind the budget limitations regarding equipment purchases.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Analysts should bear in mind that correlation does not imply causation with respect to these findings.
   - When interpreting results, one must bear in mind the limitations inherent in observational studies.

1. **Expression**: in light of (type: phrasal preposition idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Considering; taking into account; because of (used to show how new information affects understanding)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiomatic phrase; "light" is metaphorical)
   
   **Synonyms**: considering, given, in view of, taking into account, because of
   
   **Antonyms**: disregarding, ignoring, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts when new information changes interpretation; academic writing; business analysis; when showing how facts influence conclusions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual contexts; when simpler "because of" would be clearer
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in the light of (British English variant with article)
   - in light of the fact that (extended version)
   - considering (simpler single-word alternative)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Metaphor of light illuminating understanding; preposition "in" + noun "light" (metaphorical: illumination, revelation) + preposition "of"; semantic field of understanding and interpretation
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (preposition) + light (noun: illumination→understanding) + of (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In light of the weather forecast, we should reschedule.
   - In light of recent events, we need to reconsider our approach.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In light of these findings, the hypothesis requires revision with regard to causality.
   - Policy recommendations have been adjusted in light of stakeholder feedback concerning implementation feasibility.

1. **Expression**: with reference to (type: phrasal preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Concerning; regarding; referring to (often used when citing documents or prior communication)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed formal construction)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with regard to, referring to, in relation to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Formal business correspondence, especially when referencing previous communications or documents; official letters; when creating clear documentation trail
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts; first communications without prior context; when reference is obvious
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in reference to (variant)
   - referring to (more casual)
   - with regard to (broader, less specific to prior documents)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Preposition "with" + noun "reference" (citation, allusion) + preposition "to"; semantic compatibility emphasizing documentary or conversational links; conventional in business correspondence
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (preposition) + reference (noun: citation, mention) + to (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With reference to your email last week, I've finished the report.
   - With reference to our call yesterday, here's the information you requested.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With reference to your correspondence dated November 20, 2024, we are pleased to provide the requested documentation.
   - With specific reference to clause 3.4 of the agreement, we wish to propose an amendment.

1. **Expression**: pertaining to (type: verb + preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to; concerning; having to do with; about
   - *Fixedness*: strong (formal collocation; archaic verb mainly used in this construction)
   
   **Synonyms**: relating to, concerning, regarding, about, with regard to, in relation to
   
   **Antonyms**: unrelated to, irrelevant to, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and legal contexts; technical writing; when formal register is appropriate; classification and categorization
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "about" or "relating to" would be simpler; when formality seems pretentious
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - relating to (less formal synonym)
   - concerning (synonym without the slightly archaic feel)
   - appertaining to (very archaic legal variant)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Verb "pertain" + preposition "to"; Latin origin (pertinere: to belong to, relate to); conventional usage in formal and legal registers maintains this partnership
   
   **Root Analysis**: pertain (verb: belong to, relate to) + to (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Documents pertaining to the case are in the file.
   - All matters pertaining to budget should be discussed with the manager.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regulations pertaining to environmental compliance must be strictly observed.
   - This section addresses issues pertaining to contractual obligations with regard to performance standards.

1. **Expression**: as far as [X] is concerned (type: fixed phrase idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With regard to; concerning; from the perspective of; regarding the specific aspect mentioned
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiomatic construction; all elements necessary)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, when it comes to, as regards
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Informal and semi-formal contexts when expressing perspective or limiting scope; conversations and informal writing; when emphasizing particular viewpoint
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing; when simpler "regarding" would suffice
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - as far as [X] goes (similar but slightly more casual)
   - where [X] is concerned (variant)
   - when it comes to [X] (more casual alternative)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Fixed expression structure: "as far as" + noun/pronoun + "is concerned"; semantic development from literal distance to figurative scope; idiomatic meaning slightly different from component parts
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (conjunction: to the extent) + far (adjective: extent) + as (conjunction) + [X] + is concerned (verb phrase: is relevant)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As far as the budget is concerned, we're in good shape.
   - As far as I'm concerned, we should accept the offer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As far as regulatory compliance is concerned, all requirements have been met.
   - As far as implementation timelines are concerned, a phased approach is recommended.

1. **Expression**: when it comes to (type: fixed phrase idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Regarding; concerning; in the matter of; used to introduce a specific topic or area
   - *Fixedness*: strong (completely fixed idiomatic phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, as for, with regard to, in terms of
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Informal and semi-formal contexts; conversational writing; when transitioning to specific topics; expressing particular situations or domains
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic or legal writing; when more formal alternatives are expected
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - where [X] is concerned (more formal)
   - as for (similar transition function)
   - as far as [X] is concerned (variant)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Complete fixed expression "when it comes to"; idiomatic meaning (regarding) differs from literal meaning (arriving at); semantic development from physical arrival to topical introduction
   
   **Root Analysis**: when (conjunction: time) + it (pronoun) + comes (verb) + to (preposition) → idiomatic meaning: concerning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When it comes to budget, we need to be more careful.
   - I'm pretty flexible when it comes to scheduling.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - When it comes to regulatory compliance, the organization maintains rigorous standards.
   - The company excels when it comes to customer service with regard to responsiveness.

1. **Expression**: on the subject of (type: phrasal preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Concerning; about; regarding (used to introduce or transition to a topic)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase with clear meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, about, with regard to, as for
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Semi-formal contexts when transitioning to topics; writing and conversation; when explicitly marking topic shifts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing (use "regarding"); when topic is already clear; first mention where context hasn't been established
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - on the topic of (similar)
   - on the matter of (slightly more formal)
   - speaking of (more casual)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Preposition "on" + article "the" + noun "subject" (topic, theme) + preposition "of"; semantic compatibility in topic introduction domain
   
   **Root Analysis**: on (preposition) + the (article) + subject (noun: topic, theme) + of (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - On the subject of deadlines, when do you need this?
   - On the subject of vacation time, have you requested yours?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - On the subject of organizational restructuring, several proposals are under consideration.
   - The committee convened on the subject of policy revisions with regard to remote work.

1. **Expression**: in terms of (type: phrasal preposition collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Regarding; with respect to; concerning (used to specify the aspect or dimension being discussed)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrase; very common collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, as far as [X] is concerned, with regard to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Informal and formal contexts when specifying particular aspects or dimensions; analysis; comparisons; defining parameters
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Overuse (very common phrase that can become filler); when more specific language would be clearer
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in the terms of (incorrect—no article)
   - regarding (simpler formal alternative)
   - as for (different nuance, more topic-shifting)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Preposition "in" + noun "terms" (conditions, respects, aspects) + preposition "of"; semantic compatibility in specification domain; extremely high frequency in modern English
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (preposition) + terms (noun: respects, conditions, aspects) + of (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In terms of cost, this option is better.
   - How are we doing in terms of the timeline?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project succeeded in terms of meeting technical specifications and budget constraints.
   - Performance should be evaluated in terms of both quantitative metrics and qualitative outcomes with regard to stakeholder satisfaction.

   - have regard for (emphasizes respect)
