1. **Noun**: regard (C/U, pl: regards)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* Attention, consideration, or careful thought given to someone or something.
   - *(Sense 2)* Respect or admiration for someone.
   - *(Sense 3)* (Usually plural) Polite greetings or good wishes sent to someone.
   
   **Synonyms**: consideration, attention, respect, esteem, concern
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, neglect, contempt, indifference
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about the level of attention, respect, or thought given to a person, topic, or decision, especially in semi-formal and formal contexts such as emails, reports, and policy discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual conversation where simpler words like "care", "respect", or "thought" would be clearer; or when you only mean "topic" or "subject" (use "subject", "issue", or "matter" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with regard to, in this regard, in regard to
   - pay due regard to, with due regard to, without regard for
   - kind regards, warm regards, best regards
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + gard (guard, watch)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *regarder* (to look at, watch) → Middle English *regarden* → English noun "regard" (attention, consideration, respect).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He has no regard for the rules with regard to safety.
   - Give her my regards when you write with regard to the wedding.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee reached its decision with due regard to all stakeholder interests.
   - With regard to risk, particular regard has been paid to long-term environmental impact.

1. **Noun**: consideration (C/U, pl: considerations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* Careful thought given to something before making a decision.
   - *(Sense 2)* A fact or circumstance that needs to be taken into account.
   - *(Sense 3)* Thoughtfulness and sensitivity toward others.
   
   **Synonyms**: thought, deliberation, reflection, factor, concern
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, neglect, thoughtlessness, indifference
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the process of thinking carefully about something, or the important factors you must weigh, especially in written decision-making with regard to plans, policies, or people.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk where "thought" or "factor" would be more natural; or when referring only to feelings of respect (then "respect" or "regard" may be better).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take into consideration, give consideration to, under consideration
   - important consideration, key consideration, primary consideration
   - with due consideration, out of consideration for
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + sider (star, heavenly body) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *considerare* (to look at closely, examine the stars) → Old French *consideration* → English "consideration" (careful thought, factor to be weighed).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for taking my schedule into consideration with regard to the meeting time.
   - Money is only one consideration when we talk about this with regard to happiness.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several key considerations with regard to data security must be addressed before deployment.
   - The policy was revised after careful consideration with regard to employee feedback.

1. **Noun**: respect (C/U, pl: respects)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A feeling of admiration for someone or something because of their abilities, qualities, or achievements.
   - *(Sense 2)* Due regard for the feelings, wishes, rights, or traditions of others.
   - *(Sense 3)* A particular aspect or detail of something (usually with "in" or "with"), as in "in this respect".
   
   **Synonyms**: esteem, admiration, regard, deference, consideration
   
   **Antonyms**: disrespect, contempt, disdain, scorn
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about admiration, proper treatment of people, or particular aspects of an issue ("in this respect"), often in discussions with regard to ethics, behavior, or policy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you only mean "topic" or "aspect" in neutral, technical language ("aspect" or "dimension" may be more precise).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with respect to, in respect of, in this respect
   - out of respect for, show respect, earn respect
   - due respect, pay your respects
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back, again) + spect (look, see)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *respectus* (regard, consideration) → Old French *respect* → English "respect" (admiration, due regard).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a lot of respect for how she handled everything with regard to her family.
   - In some respects, you're right about the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With respect to employee well-being, the company has shown genuine respect for work–life balance.
   - In this respect, the new procedure improves compliance with regard to data protection.

1. **Noun**: reference (C/U, pl: references)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* The act of mentioning or alluding to something.
   - *(Sense 2)* A source of information that is consulted or cited.
   - *(Sense 3)* A written or spoken statement about someone's character or abilities (e.g. a job reference).
   
   **Synonyms**: mention, allusion, citation, source, recommendation
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: In academic, professional, and administrative contexts when you talk about where information comes from or how something is mentioned with regard to a topic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech where "source" or "mention" is enough.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with reference to, in reference to, make reference to
   - reference material, reference point, reference book
   - provide a reference, check references
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + fer (carry, bear) + -ence (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *referre* (to carry back, report) → Middle French *référence* → English "reference" (mention, citation, recommendation).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Was that a reference to what I said with regard to the budget?
   - Can you give me a reference if I apply for the role?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With reference to your email dated 3 May, we confirm receipt of all supporting documents.
   - The report makes several references with regard to previous studies on the subject.

1. **Noun**: concern (C/U, pl: concerns)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A matter that causes worry or anxiety.
   - *(Sense 2)* A feeling of interest or worry about something or someone.
   - *(Sense 3)* A business or company (formal/old-fashioned).
   
   **Synonyms**: worry, anxiety, matter, issue, interest
   
   **Antonyms**: unconcern, indifference, apathy
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about problems, risks, or issues that people are worried about, especially in discussions with regard to safety, quality, or performance.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you simply mean "topic" without emotional weight; in that case "subject" or "matter" may be better.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - raise concerns, express concern, cause for concern
   - major concern, growing concern, central concern
   - concerns with regard to, concerns regarding, of concern to
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + cern (separate, sift)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *concernere* (to sift together, involve) → Late Latin *concernere* → French *concerner* → English noun "concern" (matter, worry, business).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My main concern with regard to the trip is the cost.
   - Parents have real concerns with regard to screen time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several concerns with regard to data privacy were raised during the review.
   - Environmental concerns with regard to the project must be fully documented.

1. **Noun**: matter (C, pl: matters)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A subject, situation, or problem that is being considered or dealt with.
   - *(Sense 2)* A situation that needs to be resolved or decided.
   - *(Sense 3)* (Informal) The reason for distress or a problem ("What's the matter?").
   
   **Synonyms**: issue, subject, topic, affair, concern
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to the specific subject or case you are dealing with, especially in formal writing with regard to decisions, complaints, or procedures.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In highly technical writing where a more precise term ("case", "instance", "parameter") is required.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with regard to this matter, in this matter, matter of concern
   - urgent matter, serious matter, delicate matter
   - as a matter of fact, no matter, a matter of time
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *materia* (substance, subject) → English "matter".
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *materia* (material, subject) → Old French *matiere* → Middle English *matere* → modern "matter" (subject, affair).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's talk about this matter later with regard to the kids.
   - What's the matter with regard to your mood today?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regard to this matter, the board will issue a written response.
   - Any matters with regard to contract termination must be referred to the legal department.

1. **Noun**: subject (C, pl: subjects)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A person or thing that is being discussed, described, or dealt with.
   - *(Sense 2)* An area of knowledge studied at school or university.
   - *(Sense 3)* A person under the authority of a monarch or state.
   
   **Synonyms**: topic, theme, matter, issue (for sense 1)
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: When identifying what something is about, especially in academic or professional contexts with regard to research, emails, or meetings.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want a more neutral, written label like "topic" in headings, or a more problem-focused word like "issue".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the subject of, on the subject of, subject matter
   - change the subject, sensitive subject, main subject
   - subject line, subject heading
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under) + ject (throw, place)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *subjectus* (placed under, subjected) → Old French *sujet* → Middle English *subject* (topic, person under authority).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's change the subject with regard to this argument.
   - That's a touchy subject, especially with regard to her family.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The subject with regard to today's meeting is long-term strategy.
   - This report addresses several subjects with regard to regulatory reform.

1. **Noun**: topic (C, pl: topics)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A subject or theme of conversation, discussion, or writing.
   - *(Sense 2)* A specific matter dealt with in a text, speech, or meeting.
   
   **Synonyms**: subject, theme, matter, issue, point
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: In almost any context when you want to name what you are talking or writing about, particularly when organizing content with regard to several themes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In highly legal or administrative documents where "matter" or "issue" may sound more appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - topic of conversation, topic of discussion, hot topic
   - introduce a topic, change the topic, stay on topic
   - topics with regard to, related topics
   
   **Root Analysis**: Greek *topos* (place, commonplace) → *topikos* (concerning a place or commonplaces)
   
   **Etymology**: From Greek *topikos* (of a place, of commonplaces) → Latin *topica* → French *topique* → English "topic" (subject for discussion).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's a sensitive topic with regard to my family, so let's skip it.
   - We talked about all kinds of topics with regard to travel.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The workshop will cover three main topics with regard to project management.
   - This chapter introduces key topics with regard to sustainable finance.

1. **Noun**: issue (C, pl: issues)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* An important topic or problem for debate, discussion, or decision.
   - *(Sense 2)* A personal problem or psychological difficulty (informal).
   - *(Sense 3)* Each of a regular series of publications (magazine issue, journal issue).
   
   **Synonyms**: matter, problem, question, concern, subject
   
   **Antonyms**: solution, resolution, answer
   
   **When to Use**: When focusing on problems, disagreements, or controversial topics, especially in professional contexts with regard to policy, risk, or performance.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the situation is neutral and not problematic—then "topic" or "subject" may be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the issue at hand, key issue, contentious issue
   - raise an issue, address an issue, resolve an issue
   - issues with regard to, issues regarding, have issues with
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + ire (go)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *exitus* (outcome, exit) → Old French *issue* → Middle English *issue* (outcome, result) → modern "issue" (problem, matter for discussion).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We really need to fix these issues with regard to the app crashing.
   - I have some issues with regard to how that meeting was run.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several issues with regard to compliance remain unresolved.
   - The report highlights key issues with regard to customer satisfaction.

1. **Noun**: question (C, pl: questions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* A sentence or phrase used to ask for information.
   - *(Sense 2)* A matter that needs discussion or decision; an issue.
   - *(Sense 3)* Doubt or uncertainty about something.
   
   **Synonyms**: query, inquiry, issue, matter, problem (for sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: answer, solution, certainty
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about what someone asks or what needs to be settled, especially in contexts where people have questions with regard to a plan, rule, or decision.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you mean "problem" in the sense of something going wrong; use "problem" or "issue" instead.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - question with regard to, question regarding, raise a question
   - answer a question, open question, without question
   - call into question, beyond question
   
   **Root Analysis**: quaest- (seek, ask) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *quaestionem* (a seeking, inquiry) → Old French *question* → Middle English *questioun* → modern "question".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a quick question with regard to the homework.
   - That brings up another question with regard to timing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several questions with regard to the methodology have been raised.
   - The question with regard to jurisdiction remains unresolved at this stage.
