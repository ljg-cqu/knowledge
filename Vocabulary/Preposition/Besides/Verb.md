1. **Verb**: add
   **Meaning**: 
   - To join or unite (something) to something else so as to increase the size, number, or amount; to put together with something else
   - (transitive) To include (something) as part of a total
   
   **Synonyms**: include, append, attach, incorporate, supplement
   
   **Antonyms**: subtract, remove, deduct, take away, exclude
   
   **When to Use**: Use "add" for increasing or augmenting; when putting things together; in mathematical contexts; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "subtract" or "remove" is meant; be clear about what is being added to what; not for replacement (use "substitute")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - add to [something]
   - add up (to)
   - add on
   - add value
   - add insult to injury
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "addere" (ad- = to + -dere = put) → "to put to"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "addere" (to give to, join to) → Old French "ajouter" → Middle English "adden" → emphasizing joining or increase
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you add some milk to my coffee?
   - Besides that, we need to add decorations to the list.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study adds significant insights to the existing literature.
   - Please add your signature to the document to indicate approval.

2. **Verb**: include
   **Meaning**: 
   - To comprise or contain as part of a whole; to have as a component
   - To make someone or something part of a group or collection
   
   **Synonyms**: incorporate, encompass, comprise, contain, cover
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, leave out, remove, eliminate
   
   **When to Use**: Use "include" when something is part of a larger whole; when listing members of a category; emphasizes being part of; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "exclude" is meant; be clear about what is including what; doesn't imply addition (already part of)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - include in [something]
   - including [examples]
   - be included
   - include as part of
   - price includes
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + clud (close, shut) + -e → "to shut in"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "includere" (to shut in, enclose) → Old French → Middle English → emphasizing containment
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The price includes breakfast and dinner.
   - Besides John, who else should we include on the guest list?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis includes data from multiple sources.
   - Participants included individuals from diverse demographic backgrounds.

3. **Verb**: incorporate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To include (something) as part of a whole; to combine (ingredients) into one substance
   - To take in or contain as part of a structure
   
   **Synonyms**: include, integrate, assimilate, blend, merge
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, separate, segregate, isolate, extract
   
   **When to Use**: Use "incorporate" in formal contexts; when combining things into unified whole; emphasizes integration rather than mere addition; common in academic and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; avoid when simple "add" or "include" is clearer; implies thorough mixing or integration
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - incorporate into [something]
   - incorporate elements/features
   - incorporate feedback
   - incorporate changes
   - fully incorporated
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + corpor (body) + -ate (verb) → "to embody, put into a body"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "incorporare" (unite into one body) → Late Latin → English → emphasizing unified integration
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Somewhat formal) Try to incorporate some exercise into your daily routine.
   - (More natural) Try to add some exercise to your daily routine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The revised proposal incorporates feedback from multiple stakeholders.
   - The curriculum incorporates both theoretical and practical components.

4. **Verb**: supplement
   **Meaning**: 
   - To add an extra element or amount to something; to provide what is needed to make something complete
   - To add to something to enhance or complete it
   
   **Synonyms**: add to, complement, augment, enhance, boost
   
   **Antonyms**: reduce, diminish, deplete, weaken
   
   **When to Use**: Use "supplement" when adding something to improve or complete; in formal contexts; when emphasizing enhancement rather than mere addition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when replacement rather than addition is meant; not for independent additions; implies original is incomplete or insufficient
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supplement with [something]
   - supplement income
   - supplement diet
   - supplement existing [something]
   - supplement information
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (under, from below) + ple (fill) + -ment (verb) → "to fill up from below"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "supplere" (to fill up, make full) → "supplement" → emphasizing completion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I supplement my income with freelance work.
   - She supplements her diet with vitamins.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The textbook is supplemented with online resources and interactive modules.
   - Existing programs will be supplemented with additional funding.

5. **Verb**: exclude
   **Meaning**: 
   - To deny access to or bar from a place, group, or privilege; to keep out
   - To remove from consideration; to leave out
   
   **Synonyms**: omit, leave out, bar, eliminate, prohibit
   
   **Antonyms**: include, incorporate, admit, welcome, embrace
   
   **When to Use**: Use "exclude" when intentionally keeping something/someone out; when removing from consideration; emphasizes deliberate omission; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when inclusion is meant; can carry negative connotation; be clear about what is being excluded
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exclude from [something]
   - exclude possibility/option
   - deliberately exclude
   - cannot exclude
   - exclude on the basis of
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + clud (close, shut) + -e → "to shut out"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "excludere" (to shut out, keep out) → Old French → English → emphasizing keeping out
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Why did you exclude me from the party?
   - Besides Tom, we excluded anyone who came late.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants with prior conditions were excluded from the study.
   - The analysis excludes outliers that may skew the results.

6. **Verb**: omit
   **Meaning**: 
   - To leave out or exclude, either intentionally or forgetfully; to fail to include
   - To fail to do something
   
   **Synonyms**: leave out, exclude, skip, drop, neglect
   
   **Antonyms**: include, add, insert, incorporate
   
   **When to Use**: Use "omit" in formal contexts; when something is left out (intentionally or accidentally); in writing and editing contexts; more formal than "leave out"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in casual speech; avoid when active exclusion (not passive omission) is emphasized; somewhat formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - omit from [something]
   - omit details/information
   - omit to mention
   - accidentally omit
   - deliberately omit
   
   **Root Analysis**: ob- (away) + mitt (send) → "to send away, let go"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "omittere" (to let go, disregard) → Old French → English → emphasizing leaving out
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Somewhat formal) Don't omit any important details.
   - (More natural) Don't leave out any important details.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several variables were omitted from the final analysis.
   - The report inadvertently omitted crucial information regarding costs.

7. **Verb**: encompass
   **Meaning**: 
   - To include comprehensively; to contain or comprise
   - To surround or cover completely
   
   **Synonyms**: include, comprise, contain, embrace, cover
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, leave out, narrow
   
   **When to Use**: Use "encompass" in formal contexts; when something includes a wide range; emphasizes comprehensive inclusion; common in academic and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; avoid when simple "include" is clearer; implies breadth or comprehensive coverage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - encompass a range/variety
   - encompass all aspects
   - broadly encompass
   - encompass within [something]
   - encompass both [X and Y]
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (in, into) + compass (circle, range) → "to put in a circle, surround"
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "compassen" (to surround) → "encompass" → emphasizing comprehensive inclusion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Formal) The project encompasses many different areas.
   - (More natural) The project includes many different areas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study encompasses five years of longitudinal data collection.
   - The curriculum encompasses both theoretical foundations and practical applications.

8. **Verb**: comprise
   **Meaning**: 
   - To consist of; to be made up of; to include or contain
   - (Note: Traditional usage: "the whole comprises the parts," not "the parts comprise the whole")
   
   **Synonyms**: consist of, include, contain, encompass, constitute
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, lack
   
   **When to Use**: Use "comprise" in formal writing; when indicating what parts make up a whole; prefer "consist of" if unsure; traditional grammar: whole comprises parts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Often misused with "of" (should be "comprise" not "comprise of"); avoid if uncertain about proper usage; somewhat formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - comprise [parts]
   - be comprised of (modern usage, traditionally incorrect)
   - comprise the majority
   - comprise elements/components
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together, with) + prise (from prehendere = seize, grasp) → "to grasp together"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "comprehendere" → Old French "compris" → English "comprise" → emphasizing inclusion of parts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Uncommon) The team comprises five members. (formal)
   - (More natural) The team has five members.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee comprises representatives from each department.
   - The dataset comprises 10,000 individual observations.

9. **Verb**: extend
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make something longer, larger, or more comprehensive; to cause to cover a wider area or scope
   - To offer or make available; to reach or stretch
   
   **Synonyms**: expand, enlarge, prolong, broaden, stretch
   
   **Antonyms**: reduce, shorten, curtail, contract, limit
   
   **When to Use**: Use "extend" for making something larger or longer; when offering or giving; emphasizes expansion or reaching; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "shorten" or "reduce" is meant; be clear about direction of extension; not always synonymous with "add"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - extend to [something/someone]
   - extend deadline/timeframe
   - extend invitation/offer
   - extend beyond [something]
   - extend coverage/scope
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + tend (stretch) → "to stretch out"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "extendere" (to stretch out, spread) → Old French → English → emphasizing outward expansion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we extend the deadline by a week?
   - I'd like to extend an invitation to you for dinner.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The benefits extend to all eligible employees.
   - The research findings extend beyond the immediate scope of the study.

10. **Verb**: append
    **Meaning**: 
    - To add (something) as an attachment or supplement; to attach to the end
    - To add (text or data) to the end of a document or file
    
    **Synonyms**: attach, add, affix, adjoin, annex
    
    **Antonyms**: remove, detach, separate, delete
    
    **When to Use**: Use "append" in formal or technical contexts; when adding something to the end; common in computing and document preparation; implies attachment rather than integration
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Too formal/technical for casual speech; avoid when "add" is clearer; implies position at end; not for integrated additions
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - append to [document/file]
    - append signature/information
    - append at the end
    - append data
    - append notes
    
    **Root Analysis**: ap- (to, toward) + pend (hang) → "to hang to"
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "appendere" (to hang upon, attach) → Old French → English → emphasizing attachment
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - (Formal) Please append your signature to the form.
    - (More natural) Please sign at the bottom of the form.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - Additional documentation was appended to the final report.
    - The researcher appended methodological notes to each chapter.

11. **Verb**: augment
    **Meaning**: 
    - To make (something) greater by adding to it; to increase or enlarge
    - To supplement or enhance
    
    **Synonyms**: increase, enhance, supplement, boost, expand
    
    **Antonyms**: reduce, diminish, decrease, lessen, curtail
    
    **When to Use**: Use "augment" in formal contexts; when emphasizing improvement through addition; in academic, professional, or technical writing; implies strategic enhancement
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; avoid when simple "increase" or "add to" is clearer; somewhat literary or technical
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - augment with [something]
    - augment income/resources
    - augment reality (AR - augmented reality)
    - augment capabilities
    - significantly augment
    
    **Root Analysis**: aug- (increase, grow) + -ment (verb) → "to cause to grow"
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "augere" (to increase, enlarge) → Old French "augmenter" → English → emphasizing enhancement
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - (Formal) He augments his salary with consulting work.
    - (More natural) He adds to his salary with consulting work.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The program is designed to augment existing support services.
    - Artificial intelligence can augment human decision-making capabilities.

12. **Verb**: complement
    **Meaning**: 
    - To add to (something) in a way that enhances or improves it; to make complete
    - To go well with; to form a pleasing combination with
    
    **Synonyms**: supplement, enhance, complete, accompany, match
    
    **Antonyms**: contradict, clash with, conflict with, detract from
    
    **When to Use**: Use "complement" when things enhance each other; when emphasizing synergy; don't confuse with "compliment" (praise); appropriate in all registers
    
    **When NOT to Use**: Spelling confusion with "compliment"; avoid when items don't enhance each other; not for simple addition without enhancement
    
    **Common Phrases**: 
    - complement each other
    - complement with [something]
    - complement perfectly
    - complement skills/strengths
    - naturally complement
    
    **Root Analysis**: com- (together, completely) + ple (fill) + -ment (verb) → "to fill completely together"
    
    **Etymology**: Latin "complere" (to fill up, complete) → "complement" → emphasizing completion through combination
    
    **Examples [Casual]**: 
    - This wine complements the cheese perfectly.
    - Your skills complement mine really well.
    
    **Examples [Formal]**: 
    - The quantitative findings complement the qualitative analysis.
    - These approaches complement rather than contradict each other.
