# Verbs: Pending

1. **Verb**: await
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To wait for; to expect; to be in store for; to remain in readiness or expectation for something
   
   **Synonyms**: wait for, expect, anticipate, look forward to, be in readiness for
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, dismiss, disregard, avoid
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts about waiting for decisions or events; written communication; academic and business writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "wait for" instead); when simpler language is more appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - await approval
   - await a decision
   - await confirmation
   - eagerly await
   - patiently await
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (at, to) + wait (Old French waitier, to watch)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "awaitier" (to watch for) → Middle English "awaiten" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're awaiting word from the boss on this.
   - Still awaiting the results from last week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee awaits final determination from the regulatory authority.
   - All stakeholders await resolution of the pending compliance matters.

1. **Verb**: pend
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To remain undecided or unsettled; to be awaiting decision or settlement; to hang in suspense (archaic/formal)
   
   **Synonyms**: remain undecided, hang in the balance, be unresolved, remain outstanding
   
   **Antonyms**: be resolved, be settled, be decided, be concluded
   
   **When to Use**: Very formal or legal writing; literary contexts; often used as adjective "pending" more commonly than verb
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Modern casual or even standard professional writing (archaic); use "remain pending" or "be pending" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pend before the court (legal)
   - let it pend
   - while matters pend
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin pendere (to hang, weigh)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "pendere" (to hang, be suspended) → English "pend" (17th century), now largely archaic
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *"Rarely used in casual speech; better: 'The issue is still pending'"*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The matter shall pend until such time as all parties are prepared to proceed.
   - While the appeal pends, the lower court's decision remains in effect.

1. **Verb**: delay
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To make something late or slow; to postpone; to put off until a later time
   - (Intransitive) To be late or slow; to linger; to hesitate
   
   **Synonyms**: postpone, defer, put off, hold up, set back
   
   **Antonyms**: expedite, accelerate, hasten, advance, rush
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing postponements or slowdowns; explaining why things are pending; professional and casual contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing permanent cancellation (use "cancel" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - delay the decision
   - delay implementation
   - without delay
   - delay approval
   - delay pending review
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away from) + lay (Old French laier, to leave)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "delaier" (to put off) → Middle English "delayen" (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They delayed the announcement pending final approval.
   - We need to delay the launch until everything's ready.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has elected to delay final determination pending receipt of additional documentation.
   - Implementation has been delayed pending resolution of outstanding compliance issues.

1. **Verb**: postpone
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To arrange for something to take place at a time later than originally scheduled; to defer; to put off to a future time
   
   **Synonyms**: defer, delay, put off, reschedule, table
   
   **Antonyms**: advance, bring forward, expedite, proceed with immediately
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts about rescheduling; professional communication about changed timelines
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When cancellation rather than rescheduling is meant; very casual contexts (use "put off")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - postpone indefinitely
   - postpone the meeting
   - postpone pending further information
   - postpone until further notice
   - decide to postpone
   
   **Root Analysis**: post- (after) + pone (put, place)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "postponere" (to put after) → English "postpone" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're postponing the launch pending final approval.
   - Let's postpone the decision until we have more info.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board has elected to postpone final determination pending comprehensive review.
   - Implementation shall be postponed indefinitely pending resolution of regulatory concerns.

1. **Verb**: defer
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To put off to a later time; to postpone; to delay (formal)
   - (Intransitive) To yield to another's opinion or wish out of respect (different sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: postpone, delay, put off, shelve temporarily, table
   
   **Antonyms**: expedite, advance, accelerate, proceed with
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts about postponements; professional and academic writing; business contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "put off" or "postpone"); when referring to deferring to authority (different meaning)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - defer the decision
   - defer pending review
   - defer to a later date
   - defer consideration
   - defer implementation
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, from) + fer (carry, bear)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "differre" (to postpone, differ) → Old French → English "defer" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They deferred the vote pending more discussion.
   - We should defer this decision until next meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has deferred final determination pending receipt of additional evidence.
   - Consideration of the proposal has been deferred pending comprehensive stakeholder consultation.

1. **Verb**: suspend
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To temporarily stop or interrupt something; to defer or postpone; to hold in abeyance; to make inactive for a period
   
   **Synonyms**: halt temporarily, pause, discontinue temporarily, put on hold, interrupt
   
   **Antonyms**: resume, continue, activate, reinstate, proceed
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts about temporary halts; administrative actions; professional communication about paused activities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Permanent stops (use "terminate" or "cancel"); very casual contexts (use "put on hold")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - suspend operations
   - suspend pending review
   - suspend temporarily
   - suspend judgment
   - suspend activities
   
   **Root Analysis**: sus- (from below) + pend (hang)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "suspendere" (to hang up) → Old French → English "suspend" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They suspended the program pending investigation.
   - We're suspending new enrollments until we sort this out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Operations have been suspended pending resolution of outstanding compliance matters.
   - The board has elected to suspend implementation pending comprehensive risk assessment.

1. **Verb**: resolve
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To find a solution to; to settle or solve; to make a firm decision about; to bring to a conclusion
   - (Intransitive) To make a definite and serious decision to do something
   
   **Synonyms**: settle, solve, conclude, determine, decide
   
   **Antonyms**: leave unresolved, complicate, defer, postpone, leave pending
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts about solving problems or making decisions; formal communication about conclusions; all registers discussing solutions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When problems are ongoing (use "address" or "work on")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - resolve the issue
   - resolve pending matters
   - resolve quickly
   - fully resolve
   - resolve outstanding items
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, thoroughly) + solve (loosen, dissolve)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "resolvere" (to loosen, dissolve) → English "resolve" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to resolve these pending issues ASAP.
   - Let's resolve this before moving forward.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is working diligently to resolve all pending compliance matters.
   - Immediate action is required to resolve the outstanding discrepancies currently pending review.

1. **Verb**: finalize
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To complete or put into final form; to bring to a conclusion; to make final or definitive
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, conclude, finish, wrap up, bring to completion
   
   **Antonyms**: initiate, begin, leave incomplete, leave pending, postpone
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts about completing processes; discussing final stages of pending matters; business and administrative communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts where "finish" is simpler; when process is just beginning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - finalize the decision
   - finalize pending arrangements
   - finalize the contract
   - finalize details
   - yet to be finalized
   
   **Root Analysis**: final + -ize (verb suffix: make into)
   
   **Etymology**: "Final" (Latin finalis, from finis "end") + -ize → "finalize" (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to finalize this pending deal soon.
   - Once we finalize the details, we can start.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract cannot be finalized until all pending contingencies are resolved.
   - The committee will convene to finalize the decision currently pending deliberation.

1. **Verb**: expedite
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To speed up the progress or process of; to make something happen more quickly; to accelerate
   
   **Synonyms**: accelerate, hasten, speed up, fast-track, rush
   
   **Antonyms**: delay, slow down, hinder, obstruct, postpone
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts requesting faster processing; business communication about urgency; administrative requests
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "speed up"); when normal pace is acceptable
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - expedite the process
   - expedite pending matters
   - expedite approval
   - expedite resolution
   - request to expedite
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + ped (foot) + -ite (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "expedire" (to free from shackles, literally "free the feet") → English "expedite" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we expedite this pending approval?
   - We need to expedite the review process.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has agreed to expedite consideration of the pending application given time constraints.
   - Efforts are being made to expedite resolution of all outstanding compliance matters.

1. **Verb**: deliberate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To think carefully and often slowly about a decision; to discuss and consider carefully; to engage in long consideration
   - (Transitive) To consider or discuss carefully (less common)
   
   **Synonyms**: consider carefully, ponder, reflect on, discuss thoughtfully, weigh options
   
   **Antonyms**: rush to judgment, act impulsively, decide hastily, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts about decision-making processes; legal and committee contexts; professional discussions emphasizing thoroughness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Quick decisions; casual contexts (use "think about"); when speed is valued over thoroughness
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - deliberate on the matter
   - deliberate carefully
   - continue to deliberate
   - deliberate pending issues
   - deliberate before deciding
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (thoroughly) + liber (weigh, balance) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "deliberare" (to weigh carefully) → English "deliberate" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The committee is still deliberating on the proposal.
   - Let's deliberate a bit more before we decide.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board continues to deliberate on the pending matters requiring executive determination.
   - The committee shall deliberate thoroughly before rendering a final decision on the outstanding applications.

1. **Verb**: process
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To perform a series of operations on something in order to change or preserve it; to deal with something according to established procedures; to handle administratively
   
   **Synonyms**: handle, deal with, work through, manage, execute
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, neglect, leave unattended, bypass
   
   **When to Use**: Administrative and professional contexts about handling applications or requests; technical contexts about operations; business procedures
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no systematic procedure is involved; very casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - process the application
   - pending processing
   - process pending requests
   - process in order
   - currently being processed
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + cess (go)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "processus" (a going forward) → Old French → English "process" (14th century as noun, 19th century as verb)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're still processing my application.
   - How long does it take to process pending orders?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All pending applications will be processed in order of submission date.
   - The department is currently processing a substantial backlog of pending requests.

1. **Verb**: approve
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To officially agree to or accept as satisfactory; to give formal consent to; to sanction or authorize
   
   **Synonyms**: authorize, sanction, endorse, ratify, consent to
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, deny, disapprove, refuse, veto
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts about authorization; formal decision-making; administrative and management communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Expressing personal opinion (use "like" or "support"); when rejection has occurred
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approve the request
   - pending approval
   - approve pending items
   - formally approve
   - approve or reject
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to) + prove (test, demonstrate good)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "approbare" (to prove, assent to) → Old French "aprover" → English "approve" (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The boss still needs to approve the pending request.
   - Once they approve it, we can start.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will convene to approve or deny all pending applications.
   - Executive management must approve the proposal before implementation may commence.

1. **Verb**: follow up
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Phrasal verb, intransitive/transitive) To pursue an action or matter further; to check on progress; to take additional action after initial contact; to pursue to a conclusion
   
   **Synonyms**: check on, pursue further, investigate further, chase up, track progress
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, drop, abandon, neglect, leave unattended
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts about tracking pending matters; project management; customer service; business communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: First contact (use "contact" or "reach out"); when no previous interaction exists
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - follow up on pending items
   - follow up with someone
   - follow up pending approval
   - need to follow up
   - regular follow-up
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow + up (directional particle emphasizing pursuit)
   
   **Etymology**: "Follow" (Old English folgian) + "up" → phrasal verb emerged early 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to follow up on that pending approval.
   - Can you follow up with them about the status?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The department will follow up on all pending compliance matters within the specified timeframe.
   - Staff are required to follow up systematically on outstanding applications pending final determination.

1. **Verb**: escalate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To refer to a higher authority or level; to increase in intensity or scope; to raise to a higher level of importance or urgency
   
   **Synonyms**: elevate, refer upward, raise to higher level, intensify, amplify
   
   **Antonyms**: de-escalate, downgrade, reduce, lower priority, resolve at current level
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts about raising issues to higher management; project management; discussing priority increases; formal business communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Routine matters not requiring higher authority; casual contexts where simpler terms work
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - escalate the issue
   - escalate pending matters
   - escalate to management
   - need to escalate
   - escalate for resolution
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out, up) + scala (ladder) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "escalator" → back-formation "escalate" (20th century), meaning to rise as if on an escalator
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should escalate this pending issue to the boss.
   - If it's not resolved soon, I'm going to escalate.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The matter will be escalated to executive management if pending issues remain unresolved.
   - Protocol requires that long-standing pending matters be escalated for expedited resolution.

1. **Verb**: hold
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To keep in a particular state or position; to maintain; to keep back; to delay or suspend temporarily
   - (Intransitive) To remain valid or in effect
   
   **Synonyms**: keep, maintain, suspend, pause, retain
   
   **Antonyms**: release, proceed, continue, advance, let go
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts about suspending or maintaining states; discussing temporary pauses; business communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Permanent states (use more permanent verbs); physical holding (different sense)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hold pending approval
   - put on hold
   - hold the decision
   - hold in abeyance
   - hold temporarily
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "healdan" (to grasp, keep)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "healdan" → Middle English "holden" → Modern English "hold"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's hold this pending more information.
   - The project is on hold pending funding approval.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has elected to hold the matter pending receipt of additional documentation.
   - Implementation shall be held in abeyance pending resolution of outstanding regulatory concerns.
