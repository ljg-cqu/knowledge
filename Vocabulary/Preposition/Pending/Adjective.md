# Adjectives: Pending

1. **Adjective**: pending (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Awaiting decision, settlement, or completion; not yet decided or resolved; remaining undecided or in a state of uncertainty until a future event or action occurs
   - *Grammatical*: Typically used predicatively (after linking verbs) or attributively (before nouns); non-gradable
   
   **Synonyms**: awaiting, unresolved, undecided, outstanding, in abeyance
   
   **Antonyms**: resolved, settled, completed, finalized, concluded
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts discussing matters awaiting resolution, legal/administrative situations, project management, formal communication about incomplete tasks
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations where simpler terms like "waiting" would suffice; avoid when discussing completed matters
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pending approval
   - pending investigation
   - pending decision
   - pending outcome
   - matters pending
   
   **Root Analysis**: pend (hang, weigh) + -ing (present participle/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin pendere (to hang, weigh) → Old French pendre → English "pending" (15th century), originally meaning "hanging, suspended"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My promotion is still pending - they haven't made a decision yet.
   - There are a few things pending on my to-do list that I need to finish.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The application remains pending review by the committee.
   - All pending matters will be addressed at the next board meeting.

1. **Adjective**: unresolved (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not settled, solved, or concluded; remaining in a state of uncertainty or disagreement without a definitive solution or outcome
   - *Grammatical*: Both attributive and predicative use; non-gradable
   
   **Synonyms**: unsettled, undecided, pending, outstanding, open
   
   **Antonyms**: resolved, settled, solved, concluded, finalized
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing problems, conflicts, or issues that lack closure; professional and academic contexts; emotional or interpersonal matters
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual situations where "still working on it" is more natural; avoid in contexts requiring optimistic framing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unresolved issues
   - unresolved conflict
   - unresolved questions
   - remain unresolved
   - leave matters unresolved
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + resolve (settle, solve) + -ed (past participle/adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin resolvere (to loosen, solve) → un- prefix added in English (17th century) to create negative form
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We still have some unresolved issues from our last meeting.
   - That argument between them remains unresolved.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Several unresolved questions remain regarding the implementation timeline.
   - The unresolved dispute continues to affect bilateral relations.

1. **Adjective**: outstanding (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Sense 1)* Not yet paid, settled, or resolved; remaining to be dealt with or completed
   - *(Sense 2)* Exceptionally good or remarkable (note: completely different meaning)
   - *Grammatical*: Attributive and predicative; context determines which sense applies
   
   **Synonyms**: unpaid (for debts), pending, unresolved, remaining, due
   
   **Antonyms**: settled, paid, resolved, completed, concluded
   
   **When to Use**: Financial contexts (outstanding payments, debts), professional settings (outstanding tasks), formal communication about incomplete obligations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid ambiguity - clarify context since "outstanding" has positive connotations in other uses; not for casual conversation about minor delays
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - outstanding balance
   - outstanding payment
   - outstanding obligations
   - outstanding matters
   - outstanding issues
   
   **Root Analysis**: out- (beyond) + stand (remain firm) + -ing (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English standan → Middle English outstanding (literally "standing out"), meaning both "projecting" and "remaining unpaid" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a few outstanding bills I need to pay this week.
   - There are still some outstanding tasks on my list.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The outstanding balance must be settled within 30 days.
   - Several outstanding issues require immediate attention from the committee.

1. **Adjective**: imminent (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - About to happen very soon; impending in the immediate future, often with an element of urgency or inevitability
   - *Grammatical*: Typically attributive; non-gradable (something is either imminent or not)
   
   **Synonyms**: impending, forthcoming, approaching, near, looming
   
   **Antonyms**: distant, remote, far-off, unlikely, postponed
   
   **When to Use**: Situations requiring urgency or warning; formal announcements; contexts where timing is critical and action is needed soon
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Routine scheduled events without urgency; distant future events; casual planning conversations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - imminent danger
   - imminent threat
   - imminent arrival
   - imminent release
   - imminent deadline
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (in, upon) + -min- (project, threaten) + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin imminere (to overhang, threaten) → English "imminent" (16th century), literally meaning "hanging over"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The storm is imminent - we should head inside now.
   - Her arrival is imminent; she texted she's almost here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company faces imminent financial difficulties if corrective action is not taken.
   - Due to imminent security threats, additional protocols have been implemented.

1. **Adjective**: overdue (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Past the scheduled, expected, or required time; delayed beyond the deadline or appointed time; should have been completed or arrived already
   - *Grammatical*: Predicative and attributive use; non-gradable in strict sense but can be modified by "long overdue"
   
   **Synonyms**: late, delayed, belated, tardy, past due
   
   **Antonyms**: early, timely, punctual, on-time, ahead of schedule
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing missed deadlines, late payments, delayed deliveries, situations where punctuality matters; professional and administrative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Situations where "late" sounds too harsh; avoid when discussing future items (use "upcoming" or "due" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overdue payment
   - overdue library books
   - long overdue
   - overdue for review
   - overdue conversation
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (beyond, exceeding) + due (owed, expected)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound of "over" + "due" (17th century), meaning "past the time when due or expected"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This conversation is long overdue - we should have talked weeks ago.
   - My library books are overdue; I need to return them ASAP.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report is overdue by three business days and requires immediate submission.
   - Payment is overdue, and late fees will be assessed accordingly.

1. **Adjective**: undecided (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not having reached a decision; uncertain or unsettled; lacking a definite conclusion or choice; in a state of indecision
   - *Grammatical*: Predicative and attributive; non-gradable
   
   **Synonyms**: uncertain, unsure, irresolute, undetermined, uncommitted
   
   **Antonyms**: decided, certain, determined, resolved, definite
   
   **When to Use**: Describing states of indecision, ongoing deliberations, voters/consumers who haven't made choices, matters awaiting decision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Situations requiring confident language; avoid when discussing finalized matters
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - remain undecided
   - undecided voters
   - undecided question
   - still undecided
   - undecided about
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + decide (settle, determine) + -ed (past participle/adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin decidere (to cut off, determine) → un- prefix added in English (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm still undecided about which restaurant to choose for dinner.
   - She's undecided on whether to accept the job offer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The matter remains undecided pending further consultation with stakeholders.
   - Approximately 15% of voters remain undecided ahead of the election.

1. **Adjective**: tentative (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not fully agreed upon, arranged, or certain; provisional and subject to change; done without confidence or as an experiment
   - *Grammatical*: Attributive and predicative; gradable (more tentative, most tentative)
   
   **Synonyms**: provisional, preliminary, conditional, uncertain, hesitant
   
   **Antonyms**: definite, final, confirmed, certain, decisive
   
   **When to Use**: Scheduling discussions, preliminary plans, proposals open to revision, expressing uncertainty about arrangements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Situations requiring firm commitment; final decisions; contexts where confidence is essential
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tentative agreement
   - tentative schedule
   - tentative plan
   - tentative conclusion
   - tentative steps
   
   **Root Analysis**: tent- (try, test) + -ative (adjective suffix indicating tendency)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin tentare (to try, test) → Medieval Latin tentativus → English "tentative" (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We have a tentative plan to meet Friday, but let me confirm tomorrow.
   - I made a tentative reservation, but I can cancel if needed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has reached a tentative agreement subject to final approval.
   - A tentative schedule has been proposed for the conference proceedings.

1. **Adjective**: incomplete (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not having all necessary parts or elements; unfinished, partial; lacking in fullness or entirety; not brought to completion
   - *Grammatical*: Attributive and predicative; non-gradable in standard use
   
   **Synonyms**: unfinished, partial, fragmentary, deficient, uncompleted
   
   **Antonyms**: complete, finished, whole, entire, comprehensive
   
   **When to Use**: Academic contexts (incomplete grades), project status reports, describing partial information or unfinished work
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Contexts where "in progress" sounds more positive; avoid when implying failure vs. ongoing work
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - incomplete information
   - incomplete data
   - incomplete application
   - remain incomplete
   - incomplete records
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (not) + complete (whole, finished)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin complere (to fill up) → in- prefix added in English (14th century) to create negative form
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My homework is incomplete - I still have two problems left.
   - The instructions feel incomplete; I'm missing some steps.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The application was rejected due to incomplete documentation.
   - The dataset remains incomplete, requiring additional data collection efforts.
