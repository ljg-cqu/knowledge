# Formality Variants: Pending

1. **Original Expression**: We're still waiting to hear back about the application.  
   **Context**: Status inquiry about pending decision
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate that a decision has not yet been received
   
   **Casual**: Still waiting to hear back about the application.  
   *Characteristics*: Contraction, simple present continuous, conversational tone  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, close colleagues, informal updates
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We are awaiting a response regarding the application.  
   *Characteristics*: No contraction, "awaiting" instead of "waiting", professional vocabulary  
   *Use when*: Standard workplace communication, professional emails, neutral business contexts
   
   **Formal**: The application remains pending review, and we await notification of the outcome.  
   *Characteristics*: Passive construction, formal vocabulary ("remains pending review"), complex sentence structure  
   *Use when*: Official correspondence, reports to senior management, external stakeholder communication
   
   **Humorous**: We're in application limbo - somewhere between hope and despair!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor ("limbo"), exaggerated contrast  
   *Use when*: Close colleagues who share frustration, informal team communication, stress relief  
   *Avoid when*: Communicating with decision-makers, formal contexts, when outcome is truly serious
   
   **Lively**: Still on the edge of our seats waiting for that application decision!  
   *Characteristics*: Vivid imagery ("edge of our seats"), exclamation mark for energy  
   *Use when*: Team updates, enthusiastic contexts, expressing shared anticipation
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand you're still waiting for news about the application - the uncertainty must be difficult.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of emotional impact ("uncertainty must be difficult"), "I understand" validation  
   *Use when*: Supporting someone anxious about pending decision, counseling contexts, showing care
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status (boss, client) requires formal; peers allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/external parties require formal; close relationships allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes decisions require formal; routine matters allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect formal language in all professional contexts
   - *Medium*: Written communication tends more formal than verbal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using casual "waiting to hear back" in formal reports (sounds unprofessional)
   - Over-formalizing in peer communication ("The matter remains pending" to close colleague sounds stilted)
   - Using humor about pending job applications when speaking to anxious applicants (insensitive)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: North American business culture accepts semi-formal in most workplace situations; European/Asian contexts may expect more formal language
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Email to external client about contract status → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "The contract approval remains pending executive review"
   - **Situation 2**: Slack message to team about project approval → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "Still waiting to hear if the project got approved"
   - **Situation 3**: Supporting colleague worried about promotion → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "I know the wait is stressful while your promotion is pending"

1. **Original Expression**: The issue hasn't been resolved yet.  
   **Context**: Status update on unresolved problem
   
   **Core Intent**: Inform that a problem remains outstanding
   
   **Casual**: The issue isn't fixed yet.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary ("fixed"), contraction, brief  
   *Use when*: Informal team communication, quick updates, casual status checks
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The issue remains unresolved at this time.  
   *Characteristics*: "Remains unresolved" (professional vocabulary), "at this time" (formal time marker)  
   *Use when*: Status reports, professional emails, client updates, standard business communication
   
   **Formal**: The matter remains outstanding and has not yet been brought to resolution.  
   *Characteristics*: "Matter" instead of "issue", passive voice, formal construction  
   *Use when*: Official reports, senior management communication, legal/compliance contexts
   
   **Humorous**: The issue is still alive and kicking - stubbornly refusing to be solved!  
   *Characteristics*: Personification ("alive and kicking"), playful tone, light exaggeration  
   *Use when*: Team morale contexts, venting frustration with trusted colleagues, informal retrospectives  
   *Avoid when*: Client communication, formal reporting, when issue has serious consequences
   
   **Lively**: The issue is hanging in there - we're working hard to nail it down!  
   *Characteristics*: Active metaphors, energetic tone, action-oriented language  
   *Use when*: Team updates emphasizing effort, motivational contexts, progress meetings
   
   **Empathetic**: I realize this unresolved issue continues to cause frustration - we're committed to finding a solution.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of impact ("cause frustration"), commitment statement  
   *Use when*: Responding to frustrated stakeholders, customer service, acknowledging ongoing problems
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Reporting to superiors requires formal; peer updates allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: External stakeholders require semi-formal or formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Critical issues require formal tone; minor issues allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Technical teams often accept casual; executive communication requires formal
   - *Medium*: Incident reports require formal; chat messages allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being too casual about serious unresolved issues to affected stakeholders (appears dismissive)
   - Over-formalizing routine bug reports to development team (sounds bureaucratic)
   - Using humor about unresolved security issues (inappropriate given severity)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In high-accountability cultures (e.g., Germany, Japan), unresolved issues always require formal treatment
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Executive summary for board → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "Three critical matters remain outstanding from Q3"
   - **Situation 2**: Team standup about bug → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "That login bug isn't fixed yet"
   - **Situation 3**: Email to frustrated customer → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "I understand this ongoing issue is frustrating; we're prioritizing a resolution"

1. **Original Expression**: We need to follow up on this soon.  
   **Context**: Reminder about pending action item
   
   **Core Intent**: Express urgency about addressing pending matter
   
   **Casual**: We should check on this soon.  
   *Characteristics*: "Should" (softer modal), simple verb "check", conversational  
   *Use when*: Informal reminders, peer-to-peer communication, friendly nudges
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We need to follow up on this matter in the near future.  
   *Characteristics*: "Follow up" (professional phrasal verb), "this matter" (professional), "near future" (formal time reference)  
   *Use when*: Professional reminders, project management, inter-departmental communication
   
   **Formal**: It is imperative that we address this outstanding matter expeditiously.  
   *Characteristics*: "Imperative" (strong formal urgency), "expeditiously" (formal adverb), complex structure  
   *Use when*: Urgent executive communication, compliance matters, legal deadlines
   
   **Humorous**: We better poke this bear before it hibernates completely!  
   *Characteristics*: Animal metaphor, playful imagery, informal idiom  
   *Use when*: Close team members, lightening mood about overdue tasks, informal project meetings  
   *Avoid when*: External communication, serious/urgent matters, formal project management
   
   **Lively**: Time to jump on this before it slips through the cracks!  
   *Characteristics*: Action verb "jump", vivid metaphor, energetic urgency  
   *Use when*: Team motivation, action-oriented meetings, creating momentum
   
   **Empathetic**: I know you have a lot pending - shall we prioritize following up on this together?  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of workload, collaborative offer ("together"), gentle inquiry  
   *Use when*: Supporting overwhelmed colleagues, collaborative problem-solving, managing up
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Reminding superiors requires tactful semi-formal; reminding subordinates allows direct casual
   - *Social Distance*: External partners require semi-formal; close team allows casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Urgent/critical requires formal; routine follow-ups allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct cultures (US, Germany) accept direct reminders; indirect cultures (Japan, Korea) require softer approach
   - *Medium*: Calendar reminders can be casual; formal emails require semi-formal or formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being too casual when reminding senior stakeholders ("We should check on this" to CEO sounds presumptuous)
   - Using formal imperatives with peers ("It is imperative" sounds commanding)
   - Using humor to remind about genuinely urgent matters (undermines seriousness)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: High-context cultures require more indirect reminders; low-context cultures prefer direct action language
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Reminding executive about pending decision → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal/Formal - "We need to address this pending matter to meet the compliance deadline"
   - **Situation 2**: Team member about overdue task → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "Hey, we should follow up on that vendor thing soon"
   - **Situation 3**: Colleague with heavy workload → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "I know things are busy - can I help follow up on any pending items?"

1. **Original Expression**: The decision is still pending.  
   **Context**: Status communication about undecided matter
   
   **Core Intent**: State that no decision has been made yet
   
   **Casual**: Still no decision yet.  
   *Characteristics*: Brief, "no decision" (plain language), conversational  
   *Use when*: Quick status updates, informal conversations, text messages
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The decision is currently pending.  
   *Characteristics*: "Currently" (professional time marker), complete sentence, neutral tone  
   *Use when*: Status reports, professional emails, project updates, standard business communication
   
   **Formal**: The decision remains pending deliberation by the relevant authorities.  
   *Characteristics*: "Remains pending deliberation" (formal construction), "relevant authorities" (formal reference)  
   *Use when*: Official communications, board reports, legal contexts, external stakeholder updates
   
   **Humorous**: Still stuck in decision purgatory!  
   *Characteristics*: Religious metaphor ("purgatory"), exaggeration, exclamation  
   *Use when*: Close colleagues, venting frustration, informal team discussions  
   *Avoid when*: Formal contexts, religious sensitivities, communicating with decision-makers
   
   **Lively**: The decision ball is still up in the air - waiting for it to drop!  
   *Characteristics*: Sports metaphor, dynamic imagery, anticipatory tone  
   *Use when*: Team updates, creating engagement, expressing shared anticipation
   
   **Empathetic**: I know the pending decision creates uncertainty - I'm here if you need to talk about it.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of emotional impact, supportive offer  
   *Use when*: Supporting someone affected by pending decision, counseling situations, showing care
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Informing superiors about pending decisions requires formal; peer updates allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: External parties require semi-formal or formal; internal team allows casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes decisions require formal reporting; routine decisions allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Hierarchical cultures expect formal language about pending leadership decisions
   - *Medium*: Written formal reports require formal; verbal updates allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "still no decision" in executive summary (too casual for formal reporting)
   - Over-formalizing routine decision updates to team ("remains pending deliberation" sounds pompous)
   - Making jokes about pending decisions that significantly impact people's lives (insensitive)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In cultures valuing hierarchical decision-making, pending decisions always require respectful formal language
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Board report on strategic initiative → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "The strategic initiative remains pending executive committee approval"
   - **Situation 2**: Team chat about feature approval → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "Still no decision on the new feature"
   - **Situation 3**: Employee anxious about promotion → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "I understand waiting for the promotion decision is stressful"

1. **Original Expression**: Can you give me an update on the status?  
   **Context**: Requesting information about pending matter
   
   **Core Intent**: Ask for current status information
   
   **Casual**: What's the latest on this?  
   *Characteristics*: Informal question structure, "what's" contraction, "latest" (casual status marker)  
   *Use when*: Close colleagues, informal check-ins, quick status requests
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Could you provide an update on the current status?  
   *Characteristics*: "Could you" (polite modal), "provide" (professional verb), "current status" (business terminology)  
   *Use when*: Professional emails, inter-departmental requests, standard business inquiries
   
   **Formal**: I would appreciate receiving a status update regarding this pending matter at your earliest convenience.  
   *Characteristics*: "I would appreciate" (formal politeness), "at your earliest convenience" (formal time reference), complex structure  
   *Use when*: Formal requests to senior staff, external stakeholder communication, official inquiries
   
   **Humorous**: Any intel on whether this thing has moved out of the "pending" zone?  
   *Characteristics*: Casual vocabulary ("intel", "thing"), playful framing ("pending zone")  
   *Use when*: Close team members, lightening routine status checks, informal project communication  
   *Avoid when*: Formal requests, external communication, when urgency is high
   
   **Lively**: Dying to know - has there been any movement on this?  
   *Characteristics*: "Dying to know" (enthusiastic expression), energetic tone, anticipatory language  
   *Use when*: Expressing enthusiasm, team members who share interest, informal updates
   
   **Empathetic**: I don't want to pressure you, but when you have a moment, could you share any updates on the status?  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of burden ("don't want to pressure"), "when you have a moment" (respectful of time)  
   *Use when*: Requesting from busy colleagues, sensitive situations, collaborative relationships
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Requesting from superiors requires formal/deferential; requesting from subordinates allows casual/direct
   - *Social Distance*: External vendors require semi-formal; close colleagues allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Urgent matters allow more direct requests; routine checks require polite framing
   - *Cultural Context*: High-power-distance cultures require more deferential requests
   - *Medium*: Email requests tend more formal than in-person/chat requests
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "What's the latest?" to senior executives (too casual, appears presumptuous)
   - Over-formalizing peer status checks ("I would appreciate receiving..." sounds stilted)
   - Using humor when requesting urgent status updates (undermines urgency)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Indirect communication cultures (East Asian) prefer very polite, deferential requests; direct cultures (Western) accept straightforward asks
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Requesting status from external vendor → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal - "Could you please provide an update on the pending delivery?"
   - **Situation 2**: Quick check with teammate → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "Hey, what's the latest on that approval?"
   - **Situation 3**: Asking overworked colleague → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "No rush, but when you get a chance, any update on the pending review?"

1. **Original Expression**: This has been waiting for approval for too long.  
   **Context**: Expressing concern about delay
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicate frustration or concern about extended pending status
   
   **Casual**: This has been sitting there forever waiting for approval.  
   *Characteristics*: "Sitting there" (informal), "forever" (hyperbolic), conversational complaint  
   *Use when*: Venting to peers, informal frustration expression, close colleague discussions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: This matter has been pending approval for an extended period.  
   *Characteristics*: "Matter" (professional), "extended period" (formal time reference), neutral complaint  
   *Use when*: Escalation emails, status reports highlighting delays, professional concern expression
   
   **Formal**: The approval process has exceeded reasonable timeframes and requires immediate attention.  
   *Characteristics*: "Exceeded reasonable timeframes" (formal assessment), "requires immediate attention" (formal urgency), authoritative tone  
   *Use when*: Executive escalation, formal complaints, project governance issues
   
   **Humorous**: This approval request is growing a beard at this point!  
   *Characteristics*: Absurd visual metaphor, playful exaggeration  
   *Use when*: Close team humor, venting frustration lightheartedly, informal retrospectives  
   *Avoid when*: Formal escalations, external communication, genuinely urgent situations
   
   **Lively**: We've been waiting ages for this approval - time to light a fire under it!  
   *Characteristics*: "Ages" (emphatic duration), "light a fire" (energetic action metaphor)  
   *Use when*: Motivating action, team discussions about pushing approvals, creating urgency
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand approvals take time, but this extended wait is impacting the team's work - can we explore expediting it?  
   *Characteristics*: Validation ("I understand"), impact statement, collaborative solution-seeking  
   *Use when*: Escalating to busy approvers, balancing urgency with empathy, collaborative problem-solving
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Complaining to superiors about their delays requires diplomatic formal; complaining about peer delays allows semi-formal
   - *Social Distance*: Internal escalations allow semi-formal; external require formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Project-blocking delays require formal escalation; minor delays allow casual mention
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct complaint cultures accept clear problem statements; indirect cultures require face-saving approaches
   - *Medium*: Written escalations require formal; verbal frustration can be casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being too casual when escalating to the person causing delay ("sitting there forever" to senior manager sounds disrespectful)
   - Over-formalizing when venting to peers (kills camaraderie)
   - Using humor in escalation to executives (undermines seriousness of complaint)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Face-saving cultures require indirect expression of delay concerns; direct cultures accept straightforward problem identification
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Escalating to executive about critical blocked approval → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal - "The approval has exceeded reasonable timeframes and now blocks critical deliverables"
   - **Situation 2**: Venting to teammate about slow approval → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "This has been sitting in approvals forever"
   - **Situation 3**: Following up with busy manager → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "I know approvals pile up, but this pending item is blocking our next phase - can we prioritize it?"

1. **Original Expression**: I'll let you know once it's finalized.  
   **Context**: Promise to update when pending matter is resolved
   
   **Core Intent**: Commit to providing update when decision/resolution occurs
   
   **Casual**: I'll ping you when it's done.  
   *Characteristics*: "Ping" (informal tech verb), "done" (casual completion marker), brief  
   *Use when*: Team communication, instant messaging, informal commitments
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I will notify you once the matter is finalized.  
   *Characteristics*: "Notify" (professional verb), "the matter" (formal reference), "finalized" (professional completion)  
   *Use when*: Professional emails, client communication, standard business commitments
   
   **Formal**: You will be formally notified upon resolution of this pending matter.  
   *Characteristics*: Passive voice, "formally notified" (official language), "upon resolution" (formal timing)  
   *Use when*: Official communications, legal contexts, executive commitments, external stakeholder communication
   
   **Humorous**: I'll sound the alarm when this finally escapes from pending prison!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor ("pending prison"), "sound the alarm" (dramatic imagery)  
   *Use when*: Close colleagues, shared frustration with delays, informal team communication  
   *Avoid when*: Professional commitments to stakeholders, formal project management, serious matters
   
   **Lively**: You'll be the first to know the moment this gets the green light!  
   *Characteristics*: "First to know" (priority language), "green light" (energetic approval metaphor), exclamation  
   *Use when*: Enthusiastic updates, prioritizing recipient, creating positive anticipation
   
   **Empathetic**: I know you're waiting anxiously - I promise to reach out the moment I have news.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of anxiety, "promise" (strong personal commitment), "the moment" (emphasizing speed)  
   *Use when*: Supporting anxious stakeholders, situations where delay causes stress, personal commitments
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Committing to superiors requires formal; committing to peers allows casual
   - *Social Distance*: External parties require semi-formal or formal; close colleagues allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes matters require formal commitments; routine updates allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect formal commitment language in all professional contexts
   - *Medium*: Written commitments tend more formal; verbal can be casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "I'll ping you" in formal client communication (too casual)
   - Over-formalizing peer communications ("You will be formally notified" sounds bureaucratic to teammates)
   - Using humor when someone is genuinely anxious about pending outcome (insensitive)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: High-trust cultures accept casual commitment language; low-trust or formal cultures require explicit formal commitments
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Committing to client about contract status → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal - "I will notify you immediately once the contract is finalized"
   - **Situation 2**: Telling teammate about code review → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual - "I'll ping you when the review is done"
   - **Situation 3**: Supporting anxious job applicant → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic - "I know the wait is hard - I'll reach out the moment we have a decision"
