1. **Functional Expression**: I'd rather [X] than [Y] (function: expressing preference)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express a strong preference for one option over another; indicates which alternative the speaker prefers when forced to choose
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I prefer [X] to [Y], I'd prefer [X] over [Y], I'd choose [X] instead of [Y], [X] would be better than [Y] for me
   
   **Antonyms**: I'd prefer [Y] to [X], [Y] would be better than [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing personal preferences clearly; declining options politely; making choices between alternatives; everyday decision-making contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal writing (use "I would prefer"); when expressing preferences about serious matters that affect others (may sound too casual)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I would rather [X] than [Y] (more formal)
   - I'd much rather [X] than [Y] (stronger preference)
   - I'd sooner [X] than [Y] (more emphatic, sometimes hyperbolic)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This construction uses indirect preference statement to soften what could be a direct refusal. Common in negative politeness strategies where speaker protects their own face while declining.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That's fine, we can do [X] then
   - Fair enough, [X] it is
   - Sure, no problem
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd rather stay home than go to that party.
   - I'd much rather walk than take the bus in this weather.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would rather postpone the meeting than proceed without adequate preparation.
   - The committee would rather delay implementation than risk procedural errors.

1. **Functional Expression**: It's better to [X] than (to) [Y] (function: advising/recommending)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to give advice or recommendations by comparing two courses of action and indicating which is superior
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: moderately direct (softened by impersonal "it")
   
   **Synonyms**: You should [X] rather than [Y], [X] is preferable to [Y], [X] would be wiser than [Y], I recommend [X] over [Y]
   
   **Antonyms**: It's worse to [X] than [Y], You shouldn't [X] instead of [Y]
   
   **When to Use**: Giving advice or recommendations; presenting best practices; teaching or mentoring contexts; explaining optimal choices
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When giving direct commands (too soft); when cultural context requires more indirect advice; when your authority to advise is questionable
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It's always better to [X] than (to) [Y] (universal advice)
   - It's usually better to [X] than (to) [Y] (general recommendation)
   - It would be better to [X] than (to) [Y] (conditional, softer)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Impersonal construction "it's better" reduces imposition and face-threat compared to "you should." Common in both positive politeness (offering helpful advice) and negative politeness (not imposing directly).
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're right, I'll do [X]
   - Good point, thanks for the advice
   - I hadn't thought of it that way
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's better to ask for forgiveness than permission, right?
   - It's way better to be honest than lie about it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - It is better to address concerns proactively than to respond reactively to crises.
   - In risk management, it is better to prevent issues than to remediate consequences.

1. **Functional Expression**: Would you rather [X] than [Y]? (function: asking about preference)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to inquire about someone's preference between two options; asks which alternative they would choose
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct question but polite
   
   **Synonyms**: Do you prefer [X] or [Y]?, Which would you prefer: [X] or [Y]?, Would you prefer [X] over [Y]?
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (questions don't have direct antonyms)
   
   **When to Use**: Asking about preferences politely; decision-making discussions; offering choices to others; social conversations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal surveys or research (use "Do you prefer"); when someone has already stated their preference clearly
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would you rather [X] or [Y]? (alternative structure, equally common)
   - Would you prefer [X] to [Y]? (more formal)
   - Do you want [X] or [Y]? (more direct, less formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Modal "would" creates hypothetical frame that reduces imposition. Common in negative politeness to give addressee freedom to choose without pressure.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I'd rather [X], actually
   - Definitely [X] over [Y]
   - I don't really have a preference
   - Either works for me
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would you rather eat pizza than Chinese food tonight?
   - Would you rather hang out here than go to the movies?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would you rather schedule the meeting for Tuesday than Thursday?
   - Would stakeholders rather receive monthly reports than quarterly summaries?

1. **Functional Expression**: More [adjective/adverb] than necessary/needed (function: criticizing excess)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to criticize or point out that something exceeds appropriate, required, or optimal levels
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: moderately direct (criticism softened by comparison to standard)
   
   **Synonyms**: excessively [adjective], overly [adjective], too [adjective], unnecessarily [adjective]
   
   **Antonyms**: less [adjective] than necessary, insufficiently [adjective], not [adjective] enough
   
   **When to Use**: Providing feedback on excess; critiquing approaches diplomatically; identifying inefficiencies; expressing that something is overdone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When direct criticism is more appropriate and expected; when hedging reduces necessary clarity; in emergency situations requiring immediate correction
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - More [adjective] than required (formal, technical contexts)
   - More [adjective] than it needs to be (casual explanation)
   - More [adjective] than you need to be (can sound critical of person, use carefully)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Comparison to standard ("necessary," "needed") makes criticism more objective and less personal. Negative politeness strategy that protects face by framing as deviation from standard rather than personal failing.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You're right, I can dial it back
   - Fair point, I'll simplify
   - Maybe I overdid it
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is way more complicated than necessary.
   - You're being more defensive than you need to be.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal includes more detail than necessary for preliminary approval.
   - The intervention proved more invasive than required for desired outcomes.

1. **Functional Expression**: No more than / No less than (function: setting exact standards/limits)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to specify precise limits, expectations, or standards; indicates exact boundary that should not be exceeded or fallen short of
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: exactly [amount], precisely [amount], at most [amount] / at least [amount]
   
   **Antonyms**: at least / at most (respectively)
   
   **When to Use**: Setting clear expectations; establishing rules or guidelines; technical specifications; formal instructions; legal or contractual language
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (can sound overly precise or rigid); when flexibility is important to maintain
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Not more than [amount] (alternative phrasing)
   - Not less than [amount] (alternative phrasing)
   - [Amount] maximum / [Amount] minimum (more concise)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Provides clear boundaries and removes ambiguity. Common in institutional language, rules, and regulations where precision is essential. Face-neutral as it references objective standards.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Understood, exactly [amount]
   - Got it, I'll keep it to [amount]
   - Noted, [amount] is the limit
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You should spend no more than an hour on this.
   - Give me no less than three options to choose from.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applications should contain no more than 500 words.
   - Participants must be no less than 18 years of age.

1. **Functional Expression**: Rather than [X], [Y] (function: contrasting alternatives)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to present a chosen option by contrasting it with a rejected alternative; emphasizes what was done instead of another possibility
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: instead of [X], [Y], in place of [X], [Y], as opposed to [X], [Y]
   
   **Antonyms**: in addition to [X], [Y] (additive rather than contrastive)
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining decisions or choices; academic writing; formal explanations; highlighting contrasts between approaches
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (use "instead of"); when simple coordination is clearer than contrast
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Rather than [verb]-ing, [subject verb] (gerund form)
   - Instead of [X], [Y] (less formal)
   - As opposed to [X], [Y] (equally formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Formal connector that frames decision-making and choices. Common in academic and professional writing to show deliberate selection of one option over another. Face-neutral as it presents objective contrasts.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That makes sense
   - Good choice
   - I see why you went that route
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Rather than complain, I just fixed it myself.
   - We decided to stay home rather than go out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Rather than implementing immediate changes, the committee recommended further investigation.
   - The organization opted for incremental reform rather than comprehensive restructuring.

1. **Functional Expression**: Nothing/No one [comparison] than [X] (function: emphasizing superlative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to emphasize that something/someone is the ultimate or extreme example; states that no alternative surpasses the specified instance
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct and emphatic
   
   **Synonyms**: [X] is the [superlative adjective], no [noun] is [comparative] than [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: everything/everyone [comparison] than [X] (opposite extreme)
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing ultimate status; expressing strong opinions; making absolute statements; praise or criticism
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exaggeration might reduce credibility; formal academic writing (use superlatives); when precision matters more than emphasis
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Nothing is [comparative] than [X] (standard form)
   - There's nothing [comparative] than [X] (existential construction)
   - No one [comparative] than [X] (for people)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Emphatic construction that makes absolute claim. Can function as strong praise (positive politeness) or strong criticism. Logically expresses superlative through negative + comparative structure.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Absolutely agree
   - That's definitely true
   - You're not wrong
   - I don't know about that... (disagreement)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Nothing's better than sleeping in on Saturday.
   - No one works harder than my mom.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Nothing proves more effective than direct stakeholder engagement.
   - No intervention demonstrates greater impact than early childhood education.
