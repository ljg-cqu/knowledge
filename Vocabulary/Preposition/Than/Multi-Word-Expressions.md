1. **Expression**: better late than never (type: idiom/proverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - It is better to do something late or delayed than not to do it at all; arriving or completing something belatedly is preferable to total failure or absence
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiomatic expression, proverb)
   
   **Synonyms**: at least you did it, tardiness beats absence, delayed completion is better than none
   
   **Antonyms**: better never than late (ironic reversal), punctuality matters, timing is everything
   
   **When to Use**: Consoling someone about delays; rationalizing lateness; accepting delayed outcomes; casual to semi-formal contexts; when emphasizing completion over timing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Situations where punctuality is critical (medical emergencies, legal deadlines); when it sounds like excuse-making; very formal writing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Late is better than never
   - Better to be late than not show up
   - At least it's done, even if late
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Ancient proverb dating to 300 BCE (Dionysius of Halicarnassus, "History of Early Rome"); became English proverb by late 14th century; expresses universal value of persistence and completion despite delay
   
   **Root Analysis**: Comparative construction "better...than" + temporal contrast "late/never"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I know I'm two hours late, but better late than never, right?
   - She finally got her degree at 40 – better late than never!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - While the report arrived past the deadline, the principle of "better late than never" applies given its comprehensive nature.
   - Implementation occurred behind schedule, though better late than never in addressing critical vulnerabilities.

1. **Expression**: easier said than done (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something sounds simple or straightforward when described but is actually difficult to accomplish in practice; acknowledges gap between theory and execution
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiomatic expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: harder than it sounds, looks easy but isn't, theory versus practice, not as simple as it seems
   
   **Antonyms**: as easy as it sounds, straightforward in practice, simple to execute
   
   **When to Use**: Acknowledging difficulty of tasks; tempering optimistic suggestions; expressing realistic assessment of challenges; explaining why something hasn't been completed
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When it sounds like excuse-making without attempting; as preemptive refusal (can seem defeatist); when encouragement is more appropriate than caution
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - That's easier said than done
   - Sounds easier than it actually is
   - Easy to say, hard to do
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Common expression arising from universal human experience of theory-practice gap; contrasts ease of verbal description with difficulty of actual execution; first recorded 15th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: Comparative "easier...than" + passive constructions "said/done"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Just save more money!" Yeah, easier said than done.
   - Fixing that bug sounds simple, but it's easier said than done.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - While policy reform appears straightforward, implementation proves easier said than done.
   - Organizational transformation, though conceptually clear, remains easier said than done given institutional inertia.

   **Common Variations**: 
   - Do more harm than good
   - Cause more harm than good
   - More harmful than helpful
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Common evaluative phrase contrasting harm and good as opposing outcomes; binary ethical/practical assessment framework; reflects cost-benefit analysis language; strongly preferred collocation
   
   **Root Analysis**: Quantifier "more" + contrasting evaluative nouns "harm/good" + "than"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That diet does more harm than good, honestly.
   - His advice caused more harm than good.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Expression**: actions speak louder than words (type: proverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - What people do is more significant and revealing than what they say; behavior demonstrates true intentions and character better than verbal promises
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed proverb)
   
   **Synonyms**: deeds matter more than promises, show don't tell, walk the talk, behavior reveals character
   
   **Antonyms**: words matter most, communication is key, verbal commitment counts, promises have power
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing importance of behavior over promises; calling for concrete action; critiquing empty rhetoric; leadership and integrity discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When verbal communication genuinely matters (therapy, negotiation); when dismissing important communication; situations where words are the appropriate action
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Action speaks louder than words (singular)
   - Deeds speak louder than words
   - What you do matters more than what you say
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Ancient proverb (various cultural versions) emphasizing behavioral evidence over verbal claims; contrasts action and speech as evidence of character; entered English as proverb by 17th century; attributed to various sources including Lincoln
   
   **Root Analysis**: Metaphorical comparative "speak louder than" + contrasting forms of expression "actions/words"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He keeps promising to help, but actions speak louder than words.
   - Stop talking about it and do it – actions speak louder than words!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizational culture is shaped by leadership behavior; indeed, actions speak louder than words.
   - As the adage reminds us, actions speak louder than words in demonstrating commitment.

1. **Expression**: more often than not (type: fixed phrase/frequency expression)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Usually; in the majority of cases; describes something that happens frequently but not always
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed frequency expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: usually, typically, generally, most of the time, in most cases, frequently
   
   **Antonyms**: rarely, seldom, infrequently, occasionally, more often not than (rare reversal)
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing typical patterns or tendencies; acknowledging exceptions while asserting general truth; hedging absolute claims; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precise frequency is needed (use percentages); when "usually" would be simpler and clearer; when pattern is truly universal (use "always")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - More times than not
   - More frequently than not
   - Often as not (slightly different meaning: about half the time)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Fixed frequency expression using comparative to indicate majority without claiming universality; hedging device that acknowledges exceptions; strongly preferred collocation for expressing "usually" with nuance
   
   **Root Analysis**: Frequency adverb "more often" + "than" + negative "not"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - More often than not, he's late to meetings.
   - She wins more often than not when they play chess.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research outcomes, more often than not, support the initial hypothesis.
   - Policy implementation, more often than not, encounters unforeseen challenges.
