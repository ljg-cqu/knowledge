1. **Adverb**: more (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a greater extent, degree, or frequency when compared to something else
   - Used to form comparative degree of adjectives and adverbs
   
   **Synonyms**: further, additionally, to a greater extent, increasingly
   
   **Antonyms**: less, fewer, to a lesser extent, decreasingly
   
   **When to Use**: Forming comparatives with multi-syllable adjectives/adverbs; emphasizing increased degree or quantity; expressing preference
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With one-syllable adjectives that take -er suffix (e.g., "more big" is incorrect, use "bigger")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - more than enough
   - more than ever
   - more than expected
   - more often than
   - more quickly than
   
   **Root Analysis**: more (from Old English "māra", comparative of "much")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "māra" → Middle English "more" (comparative degree marker, 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like this one more than that one.
   - She exercises more than I do.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants demonstrated more substantial improvement than control groups.
   - The intervention proved more effective than conventional approaches.

1. **Adverb**: less (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a smaller extent, degree, or frequency when compared to something else
   - Used to form comparative degree indicating reduction or decrease
   
   **Synonyms**: to a lesser extent, not as much, decreasingly, minimally
   
   **Antonyms**: more, to a greater extent, increasingly, additionally
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing reduced degree or amount; forming comparatives to indicate decrease; making negative comparisons
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With countable nouns in formal writing (use "fewer"); when "not as [adjective]" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - less than ideal
   - less than expected
   - less frequently than
   - less often than
   - much less than
   
   **Root Analysis**: less (from Old English "læssa", comparative of "little")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "læssa" → Middle English "lesse" → "less" (comparative, 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I go there way less than I used to.
   - This costs less than the other one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention demonstrated less significant impact than hypothesized.
   - Outcomes varied less than statistical models predicted.

1. **Adverb**: faster (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a greater speed or in a shorter time when compared to something else
   - More quickly or swiftly in action, movement, or progress
   
   **Synonyms**: more quickly, more rapidly, more swiftly, more speedily, sooner
   
   **Antonyms**: slower, more slowly, more gradually, more leisurely
   
   **When to Use**: Comparing speed of actions, processes, or movements; discussing efficiency or velocity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to frequency (use "more often"); when discussing time duration alone (use "sooner")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - run faster than
   - work faster than
   - grow faster than
   - move faster than
   - happen faster than expected
   
   **Root Analysis**: fast (quickly) + -er (comparative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "fæste" (firmly, quickly) → Middle English "faster" (comparative, 13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She types way faster than me.
   - Time passes faster than I thought.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Technology evolves faster than regulatory frameworks adapt.
   - Markets responded faster than economic models anticipated.

1. **Adverb**: slower (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a reduced speed or taking more time when compared to something else
   - Less quickly or swiftly in action, movement, or progress
   
   **Synonyms**: more slowly, more gradually, more leisurely, less rapidly
   
   **Antonyms**: faster, more quickly, more rapidly, more swiftly
   
   **When to Use**: Comparing reduced speed or pace; describing delayed or cautious processes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to lateness rather than speed (use "later"); in contexts where it might imply cognitive impairment offensively
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move slower than
   - progress slower than
   - happen slower than expected
   - work slower than
   - develop slower than
   
   **Root Analysis**: slow (not fast) + -ly (adverb marker) → slower (comparative)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "slāwe" → Middle English "slowly" → "slower" (comparative adverb, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He walks way slower than everyone else.
   - Things are moving slower than usual.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Implementation proceeded slower than project timelines specified.
   - Adoption rates increased slower than industry benchmarks suggested.

1. **Adverb**: better (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a more satisfactory, suitable, or effective way when compared to something else
   - To a greater degree of excellence, quality, or success
   
   **Synonyms**: more effectively, more successfully, more skillfully, more satisfactorily
   
   **Antonyms**: worse, less effectively, more poorly, less successfully
   
   **When to Use**: Comparing quality of performance, actions, or outcomes; expressing improvement; indicating preference
   
   **When NOT to Use**: With adjectives (use "better" as adjective); when "more well" would be grammatically consistent (though "better" is standard)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - perform better than
   - work better than
   - know better than
   - do better than
   - feel better than
   
   **Root Analysis**: bet- (good) + -ter (comparative suffix) → better (irregular comparative of "well")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bet" (comparative of "wel") → Middle English "better" (adverb, 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She plays guitar way better than I do.
   - This works better than the old method.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants performed significantly better than baseline assessments indicated.
   - The algorithm functions demonstrably better than legacy systems.

1. **Adverb**: worse (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a less satisfactory, suitable, or effective way when compared to something else
   - To a lesser degree of quality, success, or favorable outcome
   
   **Synonyms**: more poorly, less effectively, less successfully, more badly
   
   **Antonyms**: better, more effectively, more successfully, more skillfully
   
   **When to Use**: Comparing inferior quality of performance or outcomes; describing deterioration; making negative assessments
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When constructive feedback requires softer language; with adjectives (use "worse" as adjective)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - perform worse than
   - do worse than
   - fare worse than
   - feel worse than
   - behave worse than
   
   **Root Analysis**: wors- (bad) + -e (comparative marker) → worse (irregular comparative of "badly")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "wiers" (comparative of "yfele") → Middle English "worse" (adverb, 12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's doing way worse than last semester.
   - Things turned out worse than expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Control groups performed statistically worse than intervention cohorts.
   - Economic indicators declined worse than forecasted trajectories.

1. **Adverb**: further (type: degree/distance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a greater degree, extent, or distance when compared to something else
   - *(Sense 2)* Additionally or moreover in abstract contexts
   
   **Synonyms**: more, additionally, to a greater extent, moreover, beyond
   
   **Antonyms**: less, to a lesser extent, nearer, closer
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing additional degree or abstract distance; formal contexts emphasizing continuation or extension; metaphorical advancement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to physical distance only (though acceptable, "farther" is traditionally preferred)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - further than expected
   - go further than
   - look further than
   - extend further than
   - develop further than
   
   **Root Analysis**: forth (forward, onward) + -er (comparative suffix) → further
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "furðor" (comparative of "forth") → Middle English "further" (12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's not take this further than necessary.
   - He went way further than I would've.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Analysis extended further than initial scope parameters.
   - Implications reach further than preliminary assessments indicated.

1. **Adverb**: oftener/more often (type: frequency)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a greater frequency or more regularly when compared to something else
   - Happening with increased regularity or repetition
   
   **Synonyms**: more frequently, more regularly, more commonly, more repeatedly
   
   **Antonyms**: less often, less frequently, more rarely, more seldom
   
   **When to Use**: Comparing frequency of occurrence; discussing regularity or repetition of events or actions (Note: "more often" is strongly preferred over "oftener" in modern English)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Oftener" sounds archaic in contemporary usage; always prefer "more often than"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - more often than not
   - more often than usual
   - happen more often than
   - occur more often than
   - visit more often than
   
   **Root Analysis**: oft (frequently, Old English) + -en (adverb suffix) + -er (comparative)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "oft" → Middle English "often" → "more often" (periphrastic comparative preferred, 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I go there way more often than you do.
   - She calls more often than she used to.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Anomalies occurred more often than statistical predictions suggested.
   - Participants reported symptoms more often than control populations.
