1. **Noun**: absence (U/C, pl: absences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being away from a place or person; the fact of not being present
   - *(Sense 2)* The lack or non-existence of something; the state of something not existing or being available
   
   **Synonyms**: non-attendance, lack, want, deficiency, unavailability
   
   **Antonyms**: presence, attendance, existence, availability
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe both physical non-presence and the lack of abstract qualities; appropriate in all registers; very common in formal, academic, and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse "absence" (noun) with "absent" (adjective); avoid redundancy like "complete absence of nothing"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the absence of
   - conspicuous by one's absence
   - absence of evidence
   - leave of absence
   - absence makes the heart grow fonder
   
   **Root Analysis**: ab- (away from) + -sence (state of being) → state of being away
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "absentia" (being away) from "abesse" (to be away) → Old French "absence" → Middle English "absence"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your absence was really noticed at the party last night.
   - There's a total absence of decent coffee shops in this neighborhood.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The prolonged absence of regulatory oversight contributed to systemic failures.
   - In the absence of compelling evidence, the hypothesis cannot be validated.

2. **Noun**: lack (U, occasionally C in pl: lacks)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of not having something or not having enough of something; absence or shortage
   
   **Synonyms**: shortage, deficiency, want, scarcity, insufficiency, dearth
   
   **Antonyms**: abundance, plenty, sufficiency, wealth, surplus
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize insufficiency or complete absence of something needed; common in all registers; typically followed by "of"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Usually uncountable - avoid "a lack" in some contexts where "no" or "without" is clearer; can sound negative or critical
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lack of evidence
   - lack of confidence
   - lack of understanding
   - for lack of
   - no lack of
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle English "lak" from Middle Dutch "laken" (to be wanting)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle Dutch "laken" (to be wanting, deficient) → Middle English "lak" → Modern English "lack"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The project failed mainly due to lack of funding.
   - There's definitely no lack of enthusiasm in this group!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study revealed a significant lack of diversity in leadership positions.
   - Institutional failures can be attributed to a lack of adequate oversight mechanisms.

3. **Noun**: deprivation (U/C, pl: deprivations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of not having something, especially something necessary or desirable; the action of taking something away
   - *(Sense 2)* Severe disadvantage or hardship, especially in terms of basic necessities
   
   **Synonyms**: loss, denial, want, hardship, dispossession, need
   
   **Antonyms**: possession, abundance, provision, fulfillment, satisfaction
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal contexts to describe serious lack or loss; often used in social science, psychology, and policy discussions; implies negative consequences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for everyday casual speech; carries strong negative connotations of suffering or injustice
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sleep deprivation
   - sensory deprivation
   - economic deprivation
   - social deprivation
   - suffer deprivation
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, from) + privation (lack) → taking away leading to lack
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "deprivare" (to deprive) → Medieval Latin "deprivatio" → Old French "deprivation" → English "deprivation"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm running on serious sleep deprivation right now.
   - Living in that area means dealing with a lot of deprivation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Childhood deprivation has been linked to numerous adverse developmental outcomes.
   - Economic deprivation remains a persistent challenge in post-industrial communities.

4. **Noun**: deficiency (C/U, pl: deficiencies)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A lack or shortage of something necessary or required; an inadequate amount or quality
   - *(Sense 2)* A failing or shortcoming; something that is lacking or inadequate
   
   **Synonyms**: shortage, insufficiency, inadequacy, lack, deficit, shortfall
   
   **Antonyms**: sufficiency, adequacy, surplus, abundance, excess
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, technical, or medical contexts; often refers to measurable shortages; implies failure to meet a standard
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound clinical or technical in casual conversation; may sound overly critical when describing people
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vitamin deficiency
   - nutritional deficiency
   - immune deficiency
   - deficiency in skills
   - address deficiencies
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (away, lacking) + fic (make) + -ency (state) → state of lacking making/doing
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "deficere" (to fail, be lacking) → Latin "deficientia" → English "deficiency"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The doctor said I have an iron deficiency.
   - There are some obvious deficiencies in the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audit identified several critical deficiencies in internal control procedures.
   - Vitamin D deficiency is increasingly prevalent in urban populations.

5. **Noun**: shortage (C, pl: shortages)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A state or situation in which there is not enough of something that is needed; an insufficient supply
   
   **Synonyms**: deficit, lack, scarcity, insufficiency, shortfall, want
   
   **Antonyms**: surplus, abundance, excess, plenty, glut
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe insufficient quantities of tangible resources; common in economic, business, and everyday contexts; emphasizes supply issues
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Typically used for measurable/tangible things - less common for abstract qualities (use "lack" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - water shortage
   - food shortage
   - staff shortage
   - housing shortage
   - shortage of supplies
   
   **Root Analysis**: short (not reaching required length/amount) + -age (collective noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: "short" (Old English "sceort") + "-age" (collective suffix) → Modern English "shortage" (first recorded 1868)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a major staff shortage at the hospital right now.
   - We're facing a water shortage this summer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The semiconductor shortage has significantly disrupted global supply chains.
   - Affordable housing shortages continue to exacerbate urban inequality.

6. **Noun**: void (C, pl: voids)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An empty space or gap; an absence of something
   - *(Sense 2)* A feeling of emptiness, loss, or lack of purpose
   
   **Synonyms**: emptiness, gap, vacuum, blank, hollowness, nothingness
   
   **Antonyms**: fullness, presence, substance, content, matter
   
   **When to Use**: Use in both literal (physical space) and figurative (emotional/conceptual) contexts; can be technical or poetic depending on context
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Legal meaning of "void" (invalid) is different; context must clarify whether physical or emotional void is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fill the void
   - void in one's life
   - legal void
   - null and void
   - void of space
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old French "voide" (empty) from Latin "vocivus" (vacant)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "vocivus" (vacant) → Old French "voide/vuit" (empty) → Middle English "voide" → Modern English "void"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After she left, there was this huge void in my life.
   - The explosion left a void in the building's wall.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The resignation created a significant leadership void within the organization.
   - Researchers identified voids in the current theoretical framework.
