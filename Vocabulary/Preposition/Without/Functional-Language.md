1. **Functional Expression**: Can't [do something] without [doing something else] (function: expressing dependency/habit)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express that one action or thing is always accompanied by or dependent on another; indicates an inseparable connection or habit
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: always [do] with, must [do] together, need to [do] as well, have to [do] alongside
   
   **Antonyms**: can [do] independently, [do] separately, [do] alone, [do] without combining
   
   **When to Use**: Use to express habits, dependencies, or inseparable actions; appropriate in casual and neutral contexts; emphasizes the connection between two actions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound like criticism if describing someone else's habits negatively; may reveal personal quirks - use with self-awareness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - cannot [do] without [doing]
   - can never [do] without [doing]
   - unable to [do] without [doing]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Neutral politeness; states fact about dependency or habit; can be humorous when describing quirky habits; face-neutral
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I'm the same way!" (agreement)
   - "Really? I can." (disagreement/surprise)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't watch a movie without eating popcorn - it's just not the same.
   - She can't tell a story without getting all the details mixed up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - One cannot implement effective policy without consulting relevant stakeholders.
   - Researchers cannot draw valid conclusions without controlling for confounding variables.

2. **Functional Expression**: Without wanting to [verb], ... (function: hedging/softening criticism)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to soften criticism, disagreement, or unwelcome information; introduces a statement while distancing oneself from appearing too critical or forward
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I don't mean to [verb], but..., not to [verb], but..., I hesitate to [verb], but...
   
   **Antonyms**: I want to [verb], I intend to [verb], let me be direct
   
   **When to Use**: Use when offering criticism, disagreement, or unwelcome information while maintaining politeness; appropriate in professional and polite contexts; face-saving strategy
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can seem disingenuous if overused; in very casual settings among close friends, directness may be more appreciated
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - without wishing to [verb]
   - without meaning to [verb]
   - without intending to [verb]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness strategy; minimizes face threat; shows awareness of potential offense; allows speaker to proceed with criticism while claiming non-hostile intent
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "No, please, go ahead" (permission to continue)
   - "I appreciate your honesty" (accepting the hedge)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Without wanting to be mean, that outfit really doesn't suit you.
   - Without wanting to complain, the service here has been pretty slow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Without wanting to appear critical, the methodology contains several significant limitations.
   - Without wanting to overstep, I believe this approach may present unforeseen challenges.

3. **Functional Expression**: I can't live without [X] (function: expressing strong preference/need)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express that something is absolutely essential to one's happiness, functioning, or wellbeing; hyperbolic expression of strong attachment or dependency
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct (though often hyperbolic)
   
   **Synonyms**: I need [X] in my life, [X] is essential to me, I must have [X], I couldn't manage without [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I can do without [X], I don't need [X], [X] is dispensable, I could give up [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Use informally to express strong preferences; common in casual conversation; often hyperbolic/exaggerated; can refer to people, things, or activities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too casual for formal contexts; literal interpretation would be inappropriate (not truly a matter of survival); can sound clingy if used about people
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - couldn't live without [X]
   - can't imagine life without [X]
   - don't know what I'd do without [X]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness when expressed about people (builds rapport, shows affection); casual and affectionate tone; hyperbole is understood and accepted; can express gratitude when said to someone directly
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Aww, that's sweet!" (accepting affection)
   - "Me too!" (reciprocating or agreeing)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I seriously can't live without my morning coffee.
   - You've been such amazing support - can't imagine what I'd do without you!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts. Could rephrase as*: "This resource is indispensable to our operations."

4. **Functional Expression**: Not without [X] (function: refusing/setting conditions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to refuse a request or proposal unless a specific condition is met; sets a prerequisite before agreeing
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: moderately direct
   
   **Synonyms**: only if [X], provided that [X], on condition that [X], unless [X] happens
   
   **Antonyms**: unconditionally, regardless, anyway, [X] or not
   
   **When to Use**: Use to set clear boundaries or conditions; appropriate when negotiating or establishing terms; shows firmness while remaining polite
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound confrontational if tone isn't managed; in situations requiring immediate yes/no, conditional response may frustrate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - not without first [doing X]
   - not without [X] in place
   - not without proper [X]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Assertion of boundaries; face-threatening to requester but necessary for self-protection; firmness balanced with politeness; common in negotiations and boundary-setting
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Fair enough" (accepting condition)
   - "Can we work something out?" (negotiating)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Can I borrow your car?" "Not without insurance, you can't!"
   - I'm not going to that party, not without you coming too.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board will not approve this initiative, not without a comprehensive risk assessment.
   - We cannot proceed with implementation, not without stakeholder consultation.

5. **Functional Expression**: Without [X], we're going to have problems (function: warning/expressing concern)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to warn that lack of something will lead to negative consequences; emphasizes necessity and potential problems
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: if we don't have [X], there'll be trouble, we need [X] or there'll be issues, [X] is crucial or we'll face problems
   
   **Antonyms**: we'll be fine without [X], [X] isn't necessary, we can manage without [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize critical needs and warn of consequences; appropriate in planning, problem-solving, and risk management contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound alarmist if overused; ensure the warning is proportionate to actual risk; may create anxiety unnecessarily
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - without [X], we're in trouble
   - without [X], this won't work
   - without [X], we can't succeed
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Assertive warning; emphasizes consequences to motivate action; can be face-threatening if directed at person responsible for [X]; useful in planning and risk mitigation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "You're right, let's make sure we have it" (agreement)
   - "I'll take care of it" (accepting responsibility)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Without more volunteers, we're going to have problems organizing this event.
   - Without a clear plan, we're going to have serious problems finishing on time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Without adequate funding, the organization will face significant operational challenges.
   - Without regulatory compliance, the company risks substantial legal consequences.

6. **Functional Expression**: It goes without saying that... (function: stating the obvious/emphasizing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce something that should be obvious or understood; emphasizes shared knowledge or common sense; ironically often used to say something that does need saying
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: moderately direct
   
   **Synonyms**: obviously, clearly, of course, needless to say, naturally, it's obvious that
   
   **Antonyms**: surprisingly, unexpectedly, contrary to expectations, you might not know
   
   **When to Use**: Use to emphasize points assumed to be shared knowledge; appropriate in all registers; signals that something is or should be obvious
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can seem condescending if listener genuinely doesn't know; paradoxically, often introduces information that does need explicit statement
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - goes without saying
   - needless to say
   - obviously
   - clearly
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness (assumes shared understanding); can be face-threatening if listener doesn't share the "obvious" knowledge; often softens direct statements; creates in-group feeling through shared assumptions
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Of course" (confirming shared understanding)
   - "Actually, I didn't know that" (revealing gap)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It goes without saying that we'll help you move - that's what friends are for.
   - It goes without saying that the party's at my place, as usual.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - It goes without saying that all research must adhere to ethical guidelines.
   - It goes without saying that confidentiality is paramount in these discussions.

7. **Functional Expression**: Don't leave without [X] / Make sure you don't [go/leave] without [X] (function: reminding/instructing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to remind someone to take something or do something before leaving; expresses concern for their needs or wellbeing
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but caring
   
   **Synonyms**: remember to take [X], don't forget [X], make sure you have [X], be sure to [bring/do] [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: leave [X] behind, forget [X], you don't need [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Use when caring for others or ensuring they have what they need; common in parental, hosting, or caretaking contexts; shows thoughtfulness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound parental or patronizing with adults; may imply the person is forgetful; gauge relationship before using
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - don't go without [X]
   - make sure to take [X] before you leave
   - you'll need [X], don't forget it
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive politeness (shows care); can be face-threatening if patronizing; common in host-guest interactions; signals concern for other's wellbeing or success
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Thanks for reminding me!" (gratitude)
   - "I won't, I've got it" (reassurance)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't leave without your jacket - it's cold outside!
   - Make sure you don't go without saying goodbye to grandma.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please ensure you don't depart without collecting your attendance certificate.
   - Visitors should not leave the facility without returning their security badges.

8. **Functional Expression**: I wouldn't do [X] without [Y] (function: advising/recommending)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to give advice about necessary prerequisites or accompaniments; suggests that doing something without a particular element would be unwise
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: moderately indirect (softened by conditional "wouldn't")
   
   **Synonyms**: you should have [Y] if you're going to do [X], it's best to [do Y] when [doing X], make sure you have [Y] before [doing X]
   
   **Antonyms**: you can do [X] without [Y], [Y] isn't necessary for [X], go ahead without [Y]
   
   **When to Use**: Use when offering advice or sharing lessons from experience; appropriate in mentoring, advising contexts; softer than direct commands
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply the person lacks knowledge; unsolicited advice may be unwelcome; gauge if advice is wanted
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I would never [do X] without [Y]
   - you shouldn't [do X] without [Y]
   - it's not advisable to [do X] without [Y]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative politeness (softened advice); shares personal practice as model rather than commanding; face-saving by using "I" statements; implies consequences without threatening
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Good point, thanks" (accepting advice)
   - "I'll keep that in mind" (acknowledging)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I wouldn't go hiking without telling someone where you're going.
   - I wouldn't buy a used car without getting it checked by a mechanic first.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We would not recommend implementing this system without conducting a pilot study.
   - One should not undertake this procedure without appropriate training and certification.
