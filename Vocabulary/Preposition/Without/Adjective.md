1. **Adjective**: lacking
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not having something that is needed or expected; deficient in a particular quality, feature, or element (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: missing, deficient, wanting, devoid of, absent
   
   **Antonyms**: having, possessing, abundant, equipped, complete
   
   **When to Use**: Use "lacking" when describing an absence of a necessary or desirable quality; appropriate in formal and neutral contexts; often followed by "in" + noun
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech where simpler terms like "missing" might be better; can sound critical or negative, so use carefully when describing people
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lacking in confidence
   - lacking motivation
   - sorely lacking
   - sadly lacking
   - lacking substance
   
   **Root Analysis**: lack (Middle English "lakken" = to be wanting) + -ing (present participle/adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "lakken" from Middle Dutch "laken" (to lack, be wanting) → Modern English "lacking"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The movie was okay, but it was kind of lacking in action scenes.
   - I felt like my presentation was lacking something important.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal was found to be lacking in methodological rigor and empirical support.
   - Many educational institutions remain lacking in adequate technological infrastructure.

2. **Adjective**: absent
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not present in a place or situation; not existing or not included (non-gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - *(Sense 2)* Showing that someone is not paying attention or not thinking about what is happening (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: missing, not present, away, lacking, nonexistent
   
   **Antonyms**: present, existing, available, attending
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe physical non-presence or the lack of a quality/feature; common in formal and neutral registers; often followed by "from"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse the physical absence meaning with the inattentive meaning; avoid redundancy (e.g., "completely absent" is redundant as absent is non-gradable in the first sense)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - absent from school
   - conspicuously absent
   - notably absent
   - absent without leave
   - absent-minded
   
   **Root Analysis**: ab- (away from) + sent (from Latin "esse" = to be) → away from being
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "absens" (being away) from "abesse" (to be away) → Old French "absent" → Middle English "absent"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's been absent from class a lot lately.
   - He had this totally absent look on his face during the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Critical thinking skills were notably absent from the student submissions.
   - The defendant was absent from the proceedings without providing adequate justification.

3. **Adjective**: bare
   **Meaning**: 
   - Without the usual covering or decoration; not covered by clothing, plants, or other things (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - *(Sense 2)* Including only the most basic or necessary things; minimal (gradable, attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: naked, uncovered, unadorned, plain, minimal, basic
   
   **Antonyms**: covered, clothed, decorated, adorned, elaborate
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe absence of covering or decoration; to emphasize minimalism or essentials; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be careful with literal "bare" (naked) in formal contexts; consider appropriateness when describing people
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bare essentials
   - bare minimum
   - bare bones
   - bare necessities
   - lay bare
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "bær" (uncovered, naked)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "bær" → Middle English "bare" → Modern English "bare"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The apartment was completely bare when we moved in - not even curtains.
   - I'm traveling with just the bare essentials this time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report contained only the bare minimum of required information.
   - The room's bare walls and sparse furnishings reflected a minimalist aesthetic.

4. **Adjective**: devoid
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely lacking or free from something (non-gradable, predicative only, followed by "of")
   
   **Synonyms**: lacking, without, empty of, free from, bereft of
   
   **Antonyms**: full of, replete with, abundant in, rich in
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and academic writing to emphasize complete absence; always followed by "of"; creates emphatic negative statements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; predicative only (cannot say "a devoid room," must say "a room devoid of...")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - devoid of meaning
   - devoid of emotion
   - devoid of substance
   - devoid of merit
   - completely devoid
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (completely, utterly) + void (empty) → completely empty
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "desvoidier" (to empty out) → Middle English "devoiden" → Modern English "devoid"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His apology was totally devoid of any real feeling.
   - That movie was completely devoid of plot.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant's testimony was devoid of credible evidence.
   - Contemporary discourse on this topic is often devoid of nuanced understanding.

5. **Adjective**: empty
   **Meaning**: 
   - Containing no people, things, or substance; not filled (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - *(Sense 2)* Having no meaning, value, or purpose; hollow or meaningless (gradable, attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: vacant, hollow, void, unfilled, bare, devoid
   
   **Antonyms**: full, filled, occupied, crowded, meaningful
   
   **When to Use**: Use to describe physical emptiness or lack of content/meaning; appropriate in all registers; very common and versatile
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound harsh when describing emotional states or relationships; consider softer alternatives in sensitive contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - empty promises
   - empty words
   - empty handed
   - empty nest
   - running on empty
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "æmetig" (at leisure, not occupied)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "æmetig" (unoccupied) → Middle English "emti" → Modern English "empty"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The fridge is completely empty - we need to go shopping.
   - His promises always end up being empty words.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The auditorium remained conspicuously empty despite extensive promotional efforts.
   - Critics argued that the policy was empty rhetoric devoid of substantive reform.

6. **Adjective**: bereft
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely lacking something, especially a quality or ability that you used to have (non-gradable, predicative, followed by "of")
   - *(Sense 2)* Sad and lonely because you have lost something or someone (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: deprived of, devoid of, lacking, stripped of, without
   
   **Antonyms**: blessed with, endowed with, possessing, having
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and literary contexts; emphasizes loss or deprivation; often followed by "of"; can convey emotional tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for everyday casual speech; can sound archaic or literary in some contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bereft of hope
   - bereft of ideas
   - bereft of meaning
   - left bereft
   - bereft of comfort
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (intensive prefix) + reave (to rob, deprive) + -t (past participle) → robbed or deprived of
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "berēafian" (to deprive, rob) → Middle English "bireven" → Modern English past participle "bereft"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After losing his job, he felt completely bereft of purpose.
   - I was totally bereft when my best friend moved away.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The text is bereft of the analytical depth expected in scholarly discourse.
   - Communities bereft of economic opportunities face increasing social challenges.
