1. **Verb**: overlook
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To fail to notice or consider something; to miss or disregard
   - (transitive) To have a view of something from above; to look down upon
   - (transitive) To deliberately ignore or forgive a fault or offense
   
   **Synonyms**: miss, disregard, ignore, neglect, fail to notice
   
   **Antonyms**: notice, observe, detect, spot, acknowledge
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts when discussing mistakes or omissions; architectural/real estate contexts for views; interpersonal contexts for forgiveness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "look over" (to review); avoid in contexts requiring precision about intentional vs accidental omission
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overlook a detail
   - overlook an error
   - overlook the ocean
   - easy to overlook
   - cannot overlook
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (above, excessive) + look (to see)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English overloken, from over + loken (to look); original sense "to look at from above" evolved to "fail to see" by 1600s
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I completely overlooked that typoâ€”sorry about that!
   - Our hotel room overlooks the beach, which is amazing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audit revealed several critical discrepancies that management had overlooked.
   - The penthouse suite overlooks the entire metropolitan area.

1. **Verb**: overcome
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To succeed in dealing with or controlling a problem or difficulty
   - (transitive) To defeat or overpower someone or something
   - (intransitive/passive) To be affected strongly by an emotion or physical effect
   
   **Synonyms**: conquer, defeat, surmount, triumph over, prevail over
   
   **Antonyms**: surrender to, succumb to, yield to, fail against
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing about challenges, motivational contexts, medical contexts for symptoms, emotional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid trivializing serious challenges; don't use for ongoing struggles where resolution isn't achieved
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overcome obstacles
   - overcome challenges
   - overcome adversity
   - overcome by emotion
   - overcome with grief
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (completely, thoroughly) + come (to approach, achieve)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ofercuman (to reach, overtake), from ofer + cuman; sense of "conquer" developed in Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She finally overcame her fear of public speaking last year.
   - I was completely overcome with emotion at the wedding.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has successfully overcome significant operational challenges.
   - Participants demonstrated remarkable capacity to overcome psychological barriers.

1. **Verb**: overtake
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To catch up with and pass while traveling in the same direction
   - (transitive) To become greater or more successful than something else
   - (transitive) To happen to someone suddenly or unexpectedly
   
   **Synonyms**: pass, surpass, outstrip, outpace, catch up with
   
   **Antonyms**: fall behind, lag behind, trail
   
   **When to Use**: Traffic/driving contexts, business contexts for market competition, sudden events affecting people
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In American English, "pass" is more common for vehicles; avoid in contexts where gradual change is emphasized
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overtake a vehicle
   - overtake competitors
   - overtake the market leader
   - be overtaken by events
   - overtake on the left
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (beyond, past) + take (to catch, seize)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English; from over + taken, originally meaning "to catch up with"; driving sense emerged with automobiles
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That car just overtook us going way too fast!
   - China has overtaken the US in manufacturing output.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company was overtaken by competitors who adapted more rapidly to market conditions.
   - Economic reforms were overtaken by unforeseen political developments.

1. **Verb**: overestimate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To estimate something to be larger, better, or more important than it really is
   - (transitive) To form too high an opinion of someone's abilities or qualities
   
   **Synonyms**: overvalue, overrate, exaggerate, inflate
   
   **Antonyms**: underestimate, undervalue, underrate
   
   **When to Use**: Academic research, business planning, risk assessment, critical analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing intentional exaggeration (use "exaggerate"); don't use for subjective preferences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overestimate the cost
   - overestimate one's abilities
   - easy to overestimate
   - significantly overestimate
   - tend to overestimate
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (excessively) + estimate (to calculate approximately)
   
   **Etymology**: Late 18th century; from over- + estimate, formed on pattern of French surestimer
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think you're overestimating how much time this will take.
   - Don't overestimate your ability to stay awake through a boring movie!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Analysts warn against overestimating the short-term impact of these policy changes.
   - Previous studies may have overestimated the prevalence of this condition.

1. **Verb**: overthrow
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To remove forcibly from power, especially a government or leader
   - (transitive) To throw something too far or beyond its target
   
   **Synonyms**: topple, depose, oust, remove, bring down
   
   **Antonyms**: establish, install, maintain, support
   
   **When to Use**: Political contexts about regime change, historical analysis, sports contexts (baseball)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for peaceful transitions of power; don't use casually for minor disagreements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overthrow the government
   - overthrow a regime
   - attempt to overthrow
   - military overthrow
   - violent overthrow
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (down, completely) + throw (to cast, hurl)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English; from over + throwen; political sense dates from 14th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The rebels are trying to overthrow the dictator.
   - The pitcher made an overthrow to first base.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The coup attempt sought to overthrow the democratically elected government.
   - Revolutionary forces successfully overthrew the monarchy in 1789.

1. **Verb**: overdo
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To do, use, or carry something to excess
   - (transitive) To cook food for too long
   - (transitive) To exaggerate in one's actions or expressions
   
   **Synonyms**: overwork, exaggerate, overplay, overuse, go overboard
   
   **Antonyms**: underdo, understate, moderate, restrain
   
   **When to Use**: Health advice, cooking instructions, performance feedback, self-care contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in formal academic writing; don't use when specificity about excess type is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overdo it
   - overdo things
   - easy to overdo
   - don't overdo the exercise
   - overdo the compliments
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (excessively) + do (to perform)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English oferdon (to do excessively); sense of cooking too much from 17th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Be careful not to overdo it at the gym on your first day back.
   - I think I overdid the salt in this soup.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients are advised not to overdo physical activity during the recovery period.
   - The actor's performance was criticized for overdoing the emotional scenes.

1. **Verb**: oversee
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To supervise or watch over a person, activity, or process to ensure proper execution
   - (transitive) To be in charge of or have responsibility for something
   
   **Synonyms**: supervise, manage, direct, monitor, administer
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, ignore, abandon
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts about management, project descriptions, job responsibilities, organizational structures
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "overlook"; avoid when "manage" would be more direct
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - oversee operations
   - oversee the project
   - directly oversee
   - oversee implementation
   - oversee a team
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (from above) + see (to observe)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English oferseon (to look at from above, survey); managerial sense developed in Middle English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My manager oversees all the marketing projects for our division.
   - Can you oversee things while I'm away next week?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee oversees compliance with regulatory standards across all departments.
   - Dr. Johnson will oversee the implementation of the new curriculum.

1. **Verb**: overwhelm
   **Meaning**: 
   - (transitive) To overcome completely in mind or feeling; to overpower emotionally
   - (transitive) To defeat completely through superior force or numbers
   - (transitive) To give too much of something; to bury or drown beneath a huge mass
   
   **Synonyms**: overpower, overcome, inundate, swamp, devastate
   
   **Antonyms**: underwhelm, calm, soothe, manage
   
   **When to Use**: Emotional contexts, descriptions of excessive demands, military/competitive contexts, natural disaster descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overusing in professional contexts; don't trivialize with minor inconveniences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - feel overwhelmed
   - completely overwhelm
   - overwhelm with emotion
   - overwhelmed by requests
   - overwhelming majority
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (completely) + whelm (to overturn, engulf)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English; from over + whelmen (to overturn); modern sense of emotional overpowering from 17th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm feeling totally overwhelmed with all this work right now.
   - The kids overwhelmed us with questions about the trip.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students reported feeling overwhelmed by the volume of coursework assigned.
   - The emergency services were overwhelmed by the scale of the disaster.
