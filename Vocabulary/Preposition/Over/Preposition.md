1. **Preposition**: over (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Above and covering or spanning from one side to another; higher than something else
   - *(Temporal)* Throughout the duration of; during
   - *(Abstract)* More than in number, amount, or degree; concerning or about something
   
   **Synonyms**: above (spatial), across (movement), throughout (temporal), concerning (abstract), more than (quantity)
   
   **Antonyms**: under, below, beneath
   
   **When to Use**: To express position above something, movement across space, duration of time, quantities exceeding a limit, or control/authority relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "above" (which doesn't imply covering/spanning); avoid using for precise vertical distance measurements where "above" is clearer; be careful with "over" vs "more than" in formal writing (style guides vary)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: over the bridge, over time, over budget, over the phone
   - **Verb + prep**: get over, go over, take over, think over, look over
   - **Adjective + prep**: worried over, concerned over (less common, often replaced by "about")
   - **Noun + prep**: control over, power over, advantage over, victory over
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "ofer" (above, beyond)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "ofer" → Middle English → Modern English; cognate with German "über" and Dutch "over"; from Proto-Germanic *uberi
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's talk about this over coffee tomorrow morning.
   - I can't believe the party went on all night—it's finally over now!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research was conducted over a period of five years across multiple institutions.
   - Management maintains strict control over budgetary expenditures exceeding $10,000.

1. **Preposition**: all over (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Covering every part of a surface or area; throughout a place
   - *(Abstract)* Completely finished or ended
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, everywhere in, across the entirety of, completely covering
   
   **Antonyms**: nowhere in, absent from
   
   **When to Use**: To emphasize complete coverage of an area or thorough completion of something
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal writing where "throughout" or "across" would be more precise; don't use when partial coverage is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: all over the world, all over town, all over the place
   - **Verb + prep**: spread all over, scattered all over, known all over
   - **Adjective + prep**: famous all over, recognized all over
   - **Noun + prep**: fame all over (rare construction)
   
   **Root Analysis**: "all" (entire) + "over" (covering/throughout)
   
   **Etymology**: Combination phrase from Old English components; "all" from "eall" + "over" from "ofer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's mud all over my shoes from walking in the park.
   - That band is famous all over the country now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The disease has been documented all over the African continent.
   - This phenomenon has been observed all over the developed world.

1. **Preposition**: over to (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving or directing towards; transferring to another location or person
   - *(Abstract)* Transferring responsibility, control, or attention to someone else
   
   **Synonyms**: across to, toward, in the direction of, transferred to
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, back from
   
   **When to Use**: When indicating movement toward a destination or transfer of control/responsibility
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when simple "to" suffices; avoid redundancy in formal writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: over to the other side, over to you, over to headquarters
   - **Verb + prep**: go over to, come over to, hand over to, pass over to, turn over to
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare construction)
   - **Noun + prep**: transfer over to, move over to
   
   **Root Analysis**: "over" (across) + "to" (directional)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound preposition combining Old English "ofer" + "to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm heading over to Mike's place later if you want to join.
   - Let me hand this over to Sarah—she knows more about it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The case was transferred over to the appellate court for review.
   - Operational control will transition over to the regional office next quarter.
