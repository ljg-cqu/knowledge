1. **Noun**: overview (C, pl: overviews)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A general review or summary of a subject, situation, or topic
   - A broad view or perspective that covers the main points without excessive detail
   
   **Synonyms**: summary, survey, outline, synopsis, précis
   
   **Antonyms**: detailed analysis, in-depth examination, minutiae
   
   **When to Use**: Academic introductions, business presentations, executive summaries, introductory contexts where broad understanding is the goal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When comprehensive detail is required; avoid when "summary" is more straightforward
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - brief overview
   - provide an overview
   - general overview
   - comprehensive overview
   - overview of the situation
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (comprehensive) + view (perspective, survey)
   
   **Etymology**: Mid-19th century American English; from over + view, originally meaning "inspection or review"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you give me a quick overview of what happened at the meeting?
   - I just need a basic overview, not all the technical details.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This chapter provides an overview of the theoretical framework underpinning the research.
   - The report begins with an overview of current market conditions.

1. **Noun**: overload (C/U, pl: overloads)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An excessive amount of something, especially work or information
   - (Technical) An excessive load of electric current, weight, or demands on a system
   - The act of loading something beyond its capacity
   
   **Synonyms**: excess, surplus, glut, overwhelm, surfeit
   
   **Antonyms**: shortage, deficit, underload, insufficiency
   
   **When to Use**: Describing excessive demands on systems or people, technical contexts about capacity, information management discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid trivializing genuine stress; don't use when "excess" is clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - information overload
   - work overload
   - sensory overload
   - system overload
   - suffer from overload
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (excessive) + load (burden, charge)
   
   **Etymology**: Mid-16th century; from over- + load; psychological sense emerged in late 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm experiencing total information overload from social media lately.
   - The work overload this month has been absolutely crazy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Cognitive overload can significantly impair decision-making processes.
   - The electrical overload triggered the circuit breaker safety mechanism.

1. **Noun**: overlap (C, pl: overlaps)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An area, period, or aspect that is shared or common between two or more things
   - The extent to which something covers or coincides with something else
   - (Verb form also common) The state or fact of occupying the same area or period
   
   **Synonyms**: intersection, convergence, commonality, shared area, coincidence
   
   **Antonyms**: gap, separation, distinction, divergence
   
   **When to Use**: Describing shared characteristics, scheduling conflicts, academic discourse about shared domains, data analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When complete identity is meant (not just partial); avoid when "similarity" is more accurate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - significant overlap
   - considerable overlap
   - overlap between
   - areas of overlap
   - minimize overlap
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (across, covering) + lap (to fold, extend)
   
   **Etymology**: Late 18th century; from over + lap; abstract sense developed in 19th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's a lot of overlap between what we're both working on.
   - Our vacation plans have a two-day overlap with theirs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study identified significant overlap in the genetic profiles of both populations.
   - There is considerable overlap between the responsibilities of these two departments.

1. **Noun**: takeover (C, pl: takeovers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of taking control of a company by buying most of its shares
   - The act of taking control or possession of something, especially by force or stealth
   - The assumption of control or responsibility
   
   **Synonyms**: acquisition, buyout, seizure, coup, appropriation
   
   **Antonyms**: divestiture, relinquishment, sale, surrender
   
   **When to Use**: Business mergers and acquisitions, political coups, discussions of control transfer, corporate finance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Peaceful transitions (use "transition" or "handover"); avoid for temporary coverage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hostile takeover
   - corporate takeover
   - military takeover
   - takeover bid
   - takeover attempt
   
   **Root Analysis**: take (to seize, grasp) + over (control, possession)
   
   **Etymology**: Early 20th century; from phrasal verb "take over"; business sense prominent from 1950s
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you hear about the takeover of that tech company?
   - There was almost a takeover of the student council by the opposition.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hostile takeover was valued at $2.3 billion and faced regulatory scrutiny.
   - A military takeover resulted in the suspension of constitutional governance.

1. **Noun**: handover (C, pl: handovers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of giving control or responsibility to someone else
   - The transfer of power, control, or possession from one person or group to another
   - (Medical) The transfer of care responsibility between healthcare providers
   
   **Synonyms**: transfer, transition, passing over, conveyance, delegation
   
   **Antonyms**: retention, assumption, takeover, seizure
   
   **When to Use**: Political transitions, project management, medical contexts, custody/possession transfers, colonial history
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Hostile or forced transfers (use "takeover"); avoid when "transfer" is clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - smooth handover
   - handover process
   - power handover
   - handover ceremony
   - handover period
   
   **Root Analysis**: hand (to give, pass) + over (to another)
   
   **Etymology**: Mid-20th century; from phrasal verb "hand over"; formalized in political/administrative contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The handover to the new manager went really smoothly.
   - We need a proper handover before you leave for vacation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The handover of sovereignty occurred at midnight on July 1st.
   - Effective patient handover between shifts is critical for continuity of care.

1. **Noun**: hangover (C, pl: hangovers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A severe headache or other after-effects caused by drinking too much alcohol
   - Something remaining from a previous period or situation; a survival or remnant
   
   **Synonyms**: after-effects, morning after, remnant, leftover, survival
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms for literal sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Informal discussions about alcohol effects, metaphorical use for persistent effects of past events/policies
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal or professional writing (literal sense); avoid trivializing serious substance issues
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - terrible hangover
   - bad hangover
   - hangover from
   - cure a hangover
   - hangover effects
   
   **Root Analysis**: hang (to remain, persist) + over (beyond a period)
   
   **Etymology**: Early 19th century; originally "survival, unfinished business"; alcohol sense from early 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have such a terrible hangover from last night's party.
   - This bureaucratic process is a hangover from the old system.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The current economic challenges represent a hangover from previous policy failures.
   - These institutional practices are hangovers from the colonial administration.

1. **Noun**: leftover (C, pl: leftovers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something, especially food, remaining after the rest has been used or consumed
   - A person or thing remaining from an earlier time; a remnant or survival
   
   **Synonyms**: remainder, remnant, residue, surplus, scraps
   
   **Antonyms**: fresh food, new items, original
   
   **When to Use**: Domestic contexts about food, discussions of remnants or remains, resource management
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal contexts prefer "remainder" or "surplus"; avoid for people (can be offensive)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - eat leftovers
   - leftover food
   - leftovers from
   - use up leftovers
   - cultural leftovers
   
   **Root Analysis**: left (remaining) + over (beyond use/need)
   
   **Etymology**: Mid-19th century; from phrase "left over"; primarily food sense in common usage
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm just having leftovers from yesterday's dinner.
   - These customs are leftovers from an earlier generation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The budget was allocated to projects, with leftover funds returned to reserves.
   - These architectural features are leftovers from the building's original Victorian design.

1. **Noun**: overhaul (C, pl: overhauls)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thorough examination and repair or improvement of something
   - A comprehensive reform or reorganization of a system, institution, or process
   
   **Synonyms**: renovation, reform, reorganization, revamp, refurbishment
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, deterioration, maintenance (minor)
   
   **When to Use**: Mechanical/technical repairs, systemic reforms, comprehensive improvement projects, organizational change
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Minor adjustments or routine maintenance; avoid when "repair" or "update" is more precise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - complete overhaul
   - major overhaul
   - overhaul of the system
   - need an overhaul
   - undergo an overhaul
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (thoroughly) + haul (to examine, inspect)
   
   **Etymology**: Early 16th century nautical term; from over + haul (examine rigging); expanded to general use
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My car needs a complete overhaul—it's making weird noises.
   - The website got a major overhaul last month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The education system requires a comprehensive overhaul to address current deficiencies.
   - The engine underwent a complete overhaul following the failure analysis.
