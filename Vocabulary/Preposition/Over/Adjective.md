1. **Adjective**: overall (non-gradable, attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Taking everything into account; general rather than specific
   - Including or covering everything; total and complete
   
   **Synonyms**: general, total, comprehensive, all-inclusive, aggregate
   
   **Antonyms**: specific, particular, individual, partial
   
   **When to Use**: Summarizing conclusions, describing comprehensive views, business reporting, academic analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specific details are needed; avoid when precision about particular aspects is required
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overall performance
   - overall impression
   - overall picture
   - overall strategy
   - overall effect
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (complete, entire) + all (every part)
   
   **Etymology**: Late 18th century from over + all, originally meaning "covering all"; adjectival use emerged in business contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The overall vibe of the party was really relaxed and fun.
   - What's your overall impression of the new restaurant?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The overall results demonstrate a significant improvement in student outcomes.
   - Our overall strategy focuses on sustainable long-term growth.

1. **Adjective**: overdue (non-gradable, typically predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not having arrived, happened, or been done by the expected time; late
   - (Of a payment) Not having been made by the required date
   - (Of recognition or action) Having been needed or expected for a long time
   
   **Synonyms**: late, delayed, belated, tardy, unpaid
   
   **Antonyms**: early, prompt, timely, on-time, punctual
   
   **When to Use**: Library/billing contexts, project management, social commentary about delayed action
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for people (use "late"); don't use when specific delay time matters more than the fact of delay
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overdue payment
   - overdue library book
   - long overdue
   - overdue for change
   - overdue recognition
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (past, beyond) + due (expected time)
   
   **Etymology**: Mid-17th century; originally commercial term for debts past their payment date
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That library book is three weeks overdue—I'll get a huge fine!
   - We're long overdue for a catch-up coffee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The infrastructure investment is long overdue and critically necessary.
   - Payment is now 30 days overdue and subject to penalty fees.

1. **Adjective**: overnight (non-gradable, attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Done, made, or happening in or during the night
   - Sudden or very quick, especially in achieving success
   - For use or travel during a single night
   
   **Synonyms**: nocturnal, nighttime, sudden, instant, immediate
   
   **Antonyms**: gradual, daytime, long-term, prolonged
   
   **When to Use**: Travel contexts, business shipping, describing rapid success/change, accommodation descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing genuinely instantaneous action; avoid when overnight duration isn't literal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overnight success
   - overnight delivery
   - overnight stay
   - overnight sensation
   - overnight bag
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (during, through) + night
   
   **Etymology**: Old English; compound of "over" + "night"; figurative sense of "sudden" from 1800s
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She became an overnight sensation after that viral video.
   - I just need an overnight bag for this quick trip.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company's transition to remote work was accomplished virtually overnight.
   - Overnight shipping is available for an additional fee.

1. **Adjective**: overbearing (gradable, typically attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Unpleasantly overpowering in manner or attitude; domineering and arrogant
   - Overwhelming in effect or amount
   
   **Synonyms**: domineering, autocratic, bossy, imperious, tyrannical
   
   **Antonyms**: modest, humble, submissive, unassuming, gentle
   
   **When to Use**: Describing negative personality traits, critical feedback about management style, literary character analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in direct professional feedback without context; not for assertive behavior that's appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overbearing manner
   - overbearing personality
   - overbearing mother
   - overbearing boss
   - increasingly overbearing
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (excessively) + bearing (manner, behavior)
   
   **Etymology**: 16th century; from over + bearing (present participle of bear); originally "overpowering," negative sense developed later
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His overbearing attitude really puts people off at parties.
   - My uncle can be a bit overbearing when giving advice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manager's overbearing leadership style contributed to high staff turnover.
   - Critics described the intervention as overbearing and unnecessary.

1. **Adjective**: overwhelming (gradable, attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very great in amount, effect, or force; overpowering
   - (Of emotion) Very strong and difficult to manage or resist
   - (Of victory or majority) Complete and decisive
   
   **Synonyms**: overpowering, immense, enormous, devastating, crushing
   
   **Antonyms**: underwhelming, manageable, slight, minimal, negligible
   
   **When to Use**: Describing intense emotions, large quantities/majorities, powerful effects, exceptional responses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overusing for minor situations; don't use when "significant" would be more measured
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overwhelming majority
   - overwhelming evidence
   - overwhelming response
   - overwhelming emotion
   - feel overwhelming
   
   **Root Analysis**: overwhelm (to overpower) + -ing (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From overwhelm + -ing; emotional sense prominent since 18th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The response to our fundraiser was absolutely overwhelming!
   - I find the whole college application process overwhelming.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The evidence supporting this conclusion is overwhelming and unequivocal.
   - Survey participants expressed overwhelming support for the proposed changes.

1. **Adjective**: overqualified (non-gradable, predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having more qualifications, skills, or experience than a job or position requires
   - Too experienced or educated for a particular role
   
   **Synonyms**: overskilled, too experienced, overeducated (for position)
   
   **Antonyms**: underqualified, inexperienced, unqualified
   
   **When to Use**: Job application contexts, hiring discussions, career counseling, workforce analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid as a euphemism for age discrimination; don't use judgmentally about career choices
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overqualified candidate
   - overqualified for the position
   - considered overqualified
   - risk of being overqualified
   - worried about being overqualified
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (excessively) + qualified (having credentials)
   
   **Etymology**: Mid-20th century; from over- + qualified; emerged with modern human resources practices
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They said I was overqualified for the internship position.
   - I'm worried I'll seem overqualified if I apply for that entry-level role.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Candidates may be rejected if deemed overqualified due to retention concerns.
   - The applicant's extensive experience rendered them overqualified for the junior position.

1. **Adjective**: overrated (non-gradable, predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Rated or valued more highly than deserved; not as good or important as people believe
   
   **Synonyms**: overhyped, overvalued, overestimated, exaggerated
   
   **Antonyms**: underrated, undervalued, underappreciated
   
   **When to Use**: Critical reviews, personal opinions about popular things, debating cultural phenomena
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in objective analysis; don't use when personal taste differs from quality
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - highly overrated
   - completely overrated
   - most overrated
   - widely considered overrated
   - overrated reputation
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (excessively) + rated (evaluated)
   
   **Etymology**: Early 19th century; from over- + rate + -ed; critical/cultural sense expanded in 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That movie is totally overrated—I don't get what the hype is about.
   - Everyone says this restaurant is amazing, but I think it's overrated.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Some scholars argue that the author's influence has been considerably overrated.
   - The technology's practical applications may prove more limited than its currently overrated reputation suggests.

1. **Adjective**: overhead (non-gradable, attributive and predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Located, situated, or operating above the level of one's head
   - (Business) Relating to the general costs of running a business, not directly attributable to specific products
   
   **Synonyms**: above, upper, elevated, general expenses (business sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: underground, below, ground-level, direct costs (business sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Aviation contexts, storage descriptions, business accounting, infrastructure descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "overheard" (heard by accident); business sense requires specific accounting context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overhead compartment
   - overhead costs
   - overhead projector
   - overhead lighting
   - overhead expenses
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (above) + head
   
   **Etymology**: Early 19th century; from over + head; business accounting sense from early 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Put your bag in the overhead compartment on the plane.
   - A plane flew overhead while we were having our picnic.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Reducing overhead costs remains a primary objective for operational efficiency.
   - The overhead lighting system was upgraded to LED technology.
