1. **Expression**: get over (type: phrasal verb/idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To recover from or overcome an illness, disappointment, or shock
   - To communicate something clearly; to make something understood
   - To complete or finish dealing with an unpleasant task
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: recover from, overcome, move past, move on from, recuperate
   
   **Antonyms**: succumb to, dwell on, fixate on, remain affected by
   
   **When to Use**: Informal conversations about recovery from setbacks, discussing emotional healing, communication contexts, everyday problem-solving
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May sound dismissive of serious trauma; avoid in very formal writing; don't use when "recover" is more appropriate and sensitive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get over it (often sounds dismissive)
   - getting over (progressive form)
   - can't get over (expressing amazement)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Get" (achieve a state) + "over" (past a barrier) creates metaphor of passing beyond an obstacle; highly idiomatic combination
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "getan" (obtain) + "ofer" (beyond)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It took me weeks to get over that flu.
   - You need to get over your fear of public speaking.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients typically require several months to fully recover from such procedures. *(Use "recover" instead)*
   - Organizations must develop resilience to overcome operational setbacks. *(Use "overcome" instead)*

1. **Expression**: all over again (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Once more from the beginning; repeating the entire process or experience
   - Starting from scratch after completion or interruption
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: once more, from the start, from scratch, anew, a second time
   
   **Antonyms**: continue from where stopped, pick up where left off
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing frustration about repetition, describing complete restart situations, narrative contexts about recurring experiences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When continuation (not restart) is meant; formal writing may prefer "repeatedly" or "anew"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - start all over again
   - do it all over again
   - happen all over again
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For fixed phrase*: "All over" (completely finished) + "again" (repeated action) emphasizes total restart; idiomatic intensifier
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English components: eall + ofer + āgēn
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My computer crashed and I have to write the whole paper all over again!
   - If I could do it all over again, I'd make different choices.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The experiment was compromised and required repetition from the initial phase. *(Use more formal phrasing)*
   - Historical cycles suggest the pattern may recur. *(Avoid colloquial expression)*

1. **Expression**: take over (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To assume control, responsibility, or ownership of something
   - To begin to do something that someone else was doing
   - To become more powerful or dominant, especially gradually
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: assume control, take charge, take command, assume responsibility, seize control
   
   **Antonyms**: relinquish control, hand over, surrender, step down
   
   **When to Use**: Business contexts (acquisitions), responsibility transfers, leadership changes, discussions of dominance or replacement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Peaceful collaborative work (use "share"); temporary help (use "cover for")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - take over from (specifying predecessor)
   - take over as (specifying role)
   - completely take over
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Take" (seize, grasp) + "over" (control, possession) creates meaning of assumption of control; particle changes meaning significantly
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old Norse "taka" + Old English "ofer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you take over for me while I grab lunch?
   - Social media has completely taken over our lives.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new CEO will officially assume leadership on January 1st. *(More formal than "take over")*
   - The acquiring firm will take over operations following regulatory approval.

1. **Expression**: over the moon (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Extremely happy, pleased, or delighted
   - Overjoyed about something
   - Literal meaning vs. Figurative meaning: No literal meaning; purely idiomatic expression of extreme happiness
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiom)
   
   **Synonyms**: overjoyed, thrilled, ecstatic, delighted, elated
   
   **Antonyms**: devastated, heartbroken, disappointed, miserable
   
   **When to Use**: Informal conversations expressing extreme happiness, personal good news, informal writing; primarily British English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal writing, professional communications, situations requiring measured emotional expression, American English formal contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - absolutely over the moon
   - over the moon about/with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: From nursery rhyme "Hey Diddle Diddle" (cow jumped over the moon); hyperbolic expression of impossible height representing extreme joy
   
   **Root Analysis**: Idiomatic; from nursery rhyme imagery
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm absolutely over the moon about getting that job offer!
   - She was over the moon when she heard the news.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts—use "delighted" or "extremely pleased" instead*

1. **Expression**: hand over (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give something to someone, especially when required or requested
   - To transfer control, power, or responsibility to another person
   - To surrender or relinquish possession
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: give up, transfer, relinquish, surrender, pass over
   
   **Antonyms**: retain, keep, withhold, hold onto
   
   **When to Use**: Legal contexts (transferring property), business transitions, physical transfers of objects, power transitions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual sharing (use "give"); voluntary gifts (use "present" or "give")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - hand over to (specifying recipient)
   - hand something over
   - be handed over
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Hand" (to give by hand) + "over" (to another's possession) emphasizes physical or metaphorical transfer across boundary
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "hand" + "ofer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The robber told them to hand over their wallets.
   - Just hand over the remote—it's my turn to choose.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant was ordered to hand over all relevant documents.
   - Authority will be formally handed over during the transition ceremony.

1. **Expression**: think over (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To consider something carefully before making a decision
   - To deliberate about options or possibilities
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: consider carefully, deliberate on, ponder, reflect on, contemplate
   
   **Antonyms**: decide immediately, act impulsively, dismiss without thought
   
   **When to Use**: Decision-making contexts, negotiations, offering time for reflection, advice-giving situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When immediate decision is appropriate; formal writing may prefer "consider" or "deliberate"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - think it over
   - think something over carefully
   - take time to think over
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Think" (consider) + "over" (thoroughly, covering all aspects) suggests comprehensive consideration
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "þencan" + "ofer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need some time to think over your offer.
   - Think it over and let me know what you decide.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We encourage you to consider the proposal carefully before responding. *(More formal)*
   - Please take time to think over the implications of this decision.

1. **Expression**: over and done with (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely finished and no longer requiring attention
   - Concluded definitively with no possibility of continuation
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiom)
   
   **Synonyms**: finished, completed, concluded, ended, finalized
   
   **Antonyms**: ongoing, continuing, unfinished, pending
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing relief about completion, emphasizing finality, informal contexts about unpleasant tasks
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal writing prefers "completed" or "concluded"; avoid when task might recur
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - over and done
   - get it over and done with
   - finally over and done with
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idiom*: Redundant construction for emphasis; "over" (finished) + "done" (completed) + "with" (past tense marker) strengthens finality
   
   **Root Analysis**: Emphatic combination of completion words
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thank goodness that exam is over and done with!
   - Let's just get this meeting over and done with so we can go home.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Once the legal proceedings are concluded... *(Use "concluded" instead)*
   - The transition has been completed. *(Use "completed" instead)*

1. **Expression**: look over (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To examine or inspect something, usually quickly or casually
   - To review documents, plans, or written work
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: examine, review, inspect, check, peruse
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, skip, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: Requesting review of documents, quick inspections, preliminary examinations, editing contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Detailed analysis (use "examine thoroughly"); missing things (that's "overlook")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - look something over
   - quickly look over
   - look over carefully
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Look" (examine visually) + "over" (covering the surface) suggests surveying or scanning
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "lōcian" + "ofer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you look over my essay before I submit it?
   - I'll look over the contract tonight and get back to you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please review the attached documents at your earliest convenience. *(More formal)*
   - The committee will examine the proposal during next week's session. *(More formal)*

1. **Expression**: start over (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To begin again from the beginning, especially after an unsuccessful attempt
   - To make a fresh start
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal verb); British English often uses "start again"
   
   **Synonyms**: begin again, start afresh, start from scratch, restart, recommence
   
   **Antonyms**: continue, proceed, keep going, persist
   
   **When to Use**: After mistakes or failures, discussing fresh beginnings, motivational contexts, relationship contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When continuing from a checkpoint is possible; British English prefers "start again"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - start over from scratch
   - start all over
   - starting over
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For phrasal verb*: "Start" (begin) + "over" (again, from the beginning) emphasizes complete restart
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "styrtan" + "ofer"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This isn't working—let's just start over.
   - After the divorce, she decided to start over in a new city.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project will be reinitiated following a comprehensive review. *(More formal)*
   - Participants were instructed to recommence the exercise. *(More formal)*
