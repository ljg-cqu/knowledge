1. **Linking Word**: moreover (type: discourse marker/connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a further and more important point; furthermore; in addition
   - *Function*: Adds information that strengthens or supports the previous statement; establishes additive logical relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: furthermore, besides, in addition, additionally, what's more
   
   **Antonyms**: however, conversely, on the other hand, nevertheless
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, academic discourse, argumentation when adding supporting evidence; building cumulative arguments; written more than spoken
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds overly formal); avoid overuse (alternate with other connectors); don't use when contrasting or introducing opposing ideas
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [independent clause]. Moreover, [independent clause]
   - [Position]: sentence-initial (most common), occasionally mid-sentence with commas
   - [Punctuation]: Period before, comma after when sentence-initial; commas on both sides when mid-sentence
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Using in casual speech (sounds stilted), forgetting comma after moreover, using to introduce contrasting information
   
   **Root Analysis**: more (additional quantity) + over (beyond)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English; from "more" + "over" (in sense of "beyond that"); formalized in academic register
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *Rarely used in casual speech; sounds overly formal*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data is incomplete. Moreover, the methodology employed raises significant concerns.
   - These policies are ineffective. Moreover, they impose unnecessary costs on taxpayers.

1. **Linking Word**: over (type: subordinating conjunction - rare)
   **Meaning**: 
   - While; during; in the course of (archaic or regional usage)
   - *Function*: Introduces temporal clause indicating simultaneous or overlapping action; very limited modern usage
   
   **Synonyms**: while, during, as, whilst (British)
   
   **Antonyms**: before, after
   
   **When to Use**: Very limited; primarily in fixed expressions like "over dinner" or regional dialects; mostly prepositional rather than conjunctive
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Modern standard English prefers "while" as conjunction; "over" typically functions as preposition in temporal contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [action] + over + [noun phrase] (prepositional, not conjunctive)
   - [Position]: Rarely used as true conjunction in modern English
   - [Punctuation]: Not typically used as conjunction requiring special punctuation
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Confusing prepositional "over" with conjunctive use; overgeneralizing from fixed phrases
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "ofer"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "ofer" had conjunctive use; largely replaced by "while" in modern English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We can talk about it over lunch. *(prepositional, not conjunctive)*
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The matter was discussed during the course of dinner. *(Use "during" or "while" instead)*

   - Used informally to indicate that something is entirely characteristic or typical of someone
   - Completely finished
   - *Function*: Provides emphasis about completion or characteristic nature; primarily informal discourse
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, entirely, typically, characteristically, totally finished
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, incompletely, uncharacteristically
   
   **When to Use**: Informal discourse emphasizing completion or typical behavior; conversational contexts; storytelling
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal writing, academic discourse, professional communication; when precision is required

   - [Punctuation]: Standard sentence punctuation; often preceded by "that's" or "it's"
