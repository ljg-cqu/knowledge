1. **Functional Expression**: get over [something] (function: expressing recovery/acceptance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To recover from an illness, disappointment, or difficult experience
   - To accept and move past something unpleasant
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: recover from, move past, come to terms with, accept, move on from
   
   **Antonyms**: dwell on, fixate on, remain affected by, struggle with
   
   **When to Use**: Informal advice-giving, discussing personal recovery, encouraging resilience, counseling contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound dismissive of serious trauma; avoid with recently bereaved; formal medical contexts prefer "recover"; don't use imperatively ("get over it!") without sensitivity
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You'll get over it (reassurance)
   - It takes time to get over [X] (acknowledgment of process)
   - I can't get over [X] (expressing amazement)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Imperative form "Get over it!" often perceived as dismissive and unsympathetic; softer forms like "you'll get over it" or "it takes time to get over it" show more empathy
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I know, I just need time."
   - "I'm trying to."
   - "It's harder than you think."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't worry, you'll get over this breakup eventually.
   - I can't get over how amazing that concert was!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients typically recover from such procedures within six weeks. *(Use "recover" instead)*

1. **Functional Expression**: Can we go over [something]? (function: requesting review/clarification)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To request examination, review, or discussion of material or topics
   - To ask for clarification or explanation
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite request)
   
   **Synonyms**: Can we review [X]?, Could we discuss [X]?, Can we look at [X]?, Let's examine [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's skip [X], We don't need to discuss [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Classroom settings, meetings, training sessions, collaborative work; when seeking clarification or thoroughness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When immediate understanding is possible without review; very formal contexts might prefer "review" over "go over"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could we go over [X] again? (repetition request)
   - Let me go over [X] with you (offering explanation)
   - Should we go over [X] before...? (preemptive review)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Modal "can" makes request softer than imperative; "could" is more formal/polite; common in educational and professional contexts
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Sure, let's start with..."
   - "Of course, what part specifically?"
   - "We covered that earlier, but yes..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we go over the homework one more time? I'm still confused.
   - Let's go over the plan before we present it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could we review the quarterly results during this meeting?
   - I'd like to examine the proposal in detail before proceeding.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's talk over [something] (function: suggesting discussion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suggest discussing or negotiating something thoroughly
   - To propose collaborative problem-solving
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (suggestion)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's discuss [X], Why don't we talk about [X], We should discuss [X], Let's chat about [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's decide now, No need to discuss, We're done talking about this
   
   **When to Use**: Proposing collaborative discussion, conflict resolution, decision-making contexts, informal negotiation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When immediate decision is needed; very formal contexts prefer "discuss" or "deliberate"; when discussion is mandatory rather than optional
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We need to talk this over (stronger necessity)
   - Let's talk it over coffee (social context)
   - Why don't we talk this over? (gentle suggestion)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Let's" creates inclusive, collaborative tone; "talk over" sounds less formal than "discuss"; implies thorough consideration
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Good idea, when works for you?"
   - "Sure, what's on your mind?"
   - "I think we should, yes."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's talk over your concerns about the project tomorrow.
   - We should talk this over before making any big decisions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I propose we discuss this matter during Thursday's committee meeting.
   - We should deliberate on these options before proceeding.

1. **Functional Expression**: [Something] is over (function: announcing completion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To announce or declare that something has finished or ended
   - To state that an event, period, or situation has concluded
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: [X] has ended, [X] is finished, [X] is complete, [X] has concluded
   
   **Antonyms**: [X] is ongoing, [X] continues, [X] isn't finished
   
   **When to Use**: Announcements of endings, relief expressions, status updates, relationship endings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When ongoing consequences remain; formal contexts often prefer "concluded" or "completed"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It's all over (emphatic completion)
   - It's finally over (relief)
   - When will this be over? (questioning duration)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Can express relief, finality, or sometimes resignation; tone matters significantly; "it's over" in relationship contexts = breakup announcement
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Thank goodness!"
   - "Finally!"
   - "What happens now?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The exam is over—let's celebrate!
   - I'm sorry, but this relationship is over.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The fiscal year has concluded, and results are being compiled.
   - The trial has ended, and the jury is deliberating.

1. **Functional Expression**: Hand over [something] (function: demanding/requesting transfer)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To demand or request that someone give something to you
   - To order the transfer of possession or control
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct (can be confrontational)
   
   **Synonyms**: Give me [X], Transfer [X], Turn over [X], Relinquish [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: Keep [X], Retain [X], Hold onto [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Authority contexts, legal demands, robbery scenarios (imperative), business transitions (softer forms)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Polite requests (too demanding); collaborative contexts (sounds authoritarian); when "please give me" is more appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you hand over [X]? (polite request)
   - Hand it over (imperative, demanding)
   - We need you to hand over [X] (formal demand)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Bare imperative "Hand over X" sounds threatening/demanding; modal softeners ("could you hand over") make it more polite; official contexts use full form
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Here you go" (compliance)
   - "I don't have it" (denial)
   - "Do I have to?" (resistance)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hand over the remote—it's my turn to choose!
   - The robber demanded they hand over their valuables.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please transfer all relevant documents to the legal department.
   - Defendants are ordered to relinquish custody of the materials.

1. **Functional Expression**: think [something] over (function: suggesting deliberation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suggest or request that someone consider something carefully before deciding
   - To offer time for reflection
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (respectful of autonomy)
   
   **Synonyms**: consider it carefully, deliberate on it, take time to decide, reflect on it
   
   **Antonyms**: decide now, don't hesitate, act immediately
   
   **When to Use**: Decision-making contexts, negotiations, giving advice, offering choices, respecting autonomy
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Urgent decisions; when immediate response is necessary; can sound like stalling tactic
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Why don't you think it over? (gentle suggestion)
   - I need time to think this over (requesting time)
   - Think it over and let me know (giving autonomy)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows respect for decision-making process; reduces pressure; implies decision is significant; "take your time to think it over" maximizes politeness
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "I will, thanks for understanding"
   - "How long do I have?"
   - "I don't need to think about it"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's a big decision—you should think it over before committing.
   - Think over my offer and get back to me when you're ready.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please consider our proposal carefully and respond at your convenience.
   - We encourage you to deliberate on this matter before providing a response.

1. **Functional Expression**: over to you (function: transferring turn/responsibility)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To transfer the floor, responsibility, or turn to speak to another person
   - To hand off control of a situation or conversation
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: your turn, I'll hand over to you, you're up, the floor is yours
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll continue, back to me, I'm not finished
   
   **When to Use**: Meetings, presentations, broadcasting, collaborative work, turn-taking in formal discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal contexts might prefer "I'll now hand over to..."; written communication (it's primarily spoken)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'll hand over to [name]
   - Over to [name] now
   - And with that, over to you
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Common in broadcast media ("over to our correspondent"); signals clear transition; shows organization and structure; acknowledges collaborative nature
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - "Thanks, [name]..." (acceptance)
   - "Thank you for that..." (acknowledging previous speaker)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've explained the basics, so now over to Sarah for the details.
   - That's all from me—over to you!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'll now hand over to Dr. Johnson, who will discuss the methodology.
   - And with that summary complete, I transfer the floor to my colleague.

1. **Functional Expression**: over and out (function: ending communication)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Traditional radio communication phrase meaning "I have finished transmitting and do not expect a response"
   - Used informally to signal definitive ending of conversation
   - *Formality*: informal (when used outside radio communication)
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: goodbye (in radio), I'm done, that's all, signing off
   
   **Antonyms**: go ahead (radio), continue, I'm listening
   
   **When to Use**: Radio communication (original context), humorous conversation endings, emphasizing finality informally
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Professional contexts (sounds joking or unprofessional); actual radio procedure (technically incorrect—should be "out" alone); when conversation might continue
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Roger that, over and out (military-style closure)
   - Signing off, over and out (broadcast style)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Technically incorrect radio procedure (should use "over" OR "out," not both); colloquially used for humorous emphatic endings; signals no response expected
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Usually no response expected]
   - [May laugh if used humorously]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Okay, I'm going to bed now—over and out!
   - Message delivered, over and out! [hangs up humorously]
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Not appropriate for formal contexts; use proper closings instead*
