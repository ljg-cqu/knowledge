1. **Noun**: topic (C, pl: topics)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A subject of conversation, discussion, or writing; a matter dealt with in a text or speech
   
   **Synonyms**: subject, theme, matter, issue, question
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing what something is about; academic, professional, and casual contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to physical objects rather than subjects of discussion
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - on topic / off topic
   - change the topic
   - hot topic
   - topic of conversation
   - discuss a topic
   
   **Root Analysis**: top (place) + -ic (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek topos (place) → Latin topica (matters concerning commonplaces) → English topic (subject matter)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we change the topic? This is getting boring.
   - Climate change is a hot topic right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The topic of this symposium is renewable energy innovation.
   - Researchers must select a clearly defined topic for their dissertation.

1. **Noun**: subject (C, pl: subjects)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A matter, concern, or theme that is being discussed, described, or dealt with
   - An area of knowledge studied in school or university
   
   **Synonyms**: topic, theme, matter, issue, field
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts; when discussing what something is about
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid confusion with "subject" as grammatical term or person under authority
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - change the subject
   - on the subject of
   - sensitive subject
   - subject matter
   - favorite subject
   
   **Root Analysis**: sub- (under) + -ject (throw)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin subjectum (something placed under) → Old French → English subject (matter under consideration)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's not talk about that subject anymore.
   - Math was never my best subject.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The subject of genetic engineering raises important ethical questions.
   - This research focuses on subjects related to cognitive development.

1. **Noun**: concern (C/U, pl: concerns)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A matter that is important to someone; something that causes worry or interest
   - *(U)* A feeling of worry or anxiety about something
   
   **Synonyms**: worry, issue, matter, interest, anxiety
   
   **Antonyms**: indifference, unconcern
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing what something is about in terms of relevance or worry; formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to businesses (though "concern" can mean company in formal contexts)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cause for concern
   - express concern about
   - growing concern
   - primary concern
   - of concern
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (with) + -cern (separate, sift)
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin concernere (to relate to) → Old French → English concern (matter of importance)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My main concern is about the cost.
   - That's not really my concern.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report addresses concerns about data security.
   - Environmental degradation is a concern about which we must take action.

1. **Noun**: information (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Facts or knowledge provided or learned about something or someone
   
   **Synonyms**: data, facts, details, knowledge, intelligence
   
   **Antonyms**: ignorance, misinformation
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing what you know about something; all contexts from casual to formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Note: uncountable in English (cannot say "an information")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - information about
   - gather information
   - provide information
   - lack of information
   - piece of information
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + form (shape) + -ation (state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin informatio (conception, idea) → Old French → English information (knowledge communicated)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you have any information about the concert times?
   - I found some useful information about that company online.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The survey collected information about consumer preferences.
   - Additional information about the program is available on our website.

1. **Noun**: knowledge (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Information, understanding, or skill acquired through experience or education; awareness or familiarity gained by experience
   
   **Synonyms**: understanding, awareness, familiarity, expertise, wisdom
   
   **Antonyms**: ignorance, unawareness
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing what someone knows about something; formal and academic contexts primarily
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Note: uncountable in English
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - knowledge about / of
   - common knowledge
   - to the best of my knowledge
   - lack of knowledge
   - expand one's knowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: know (perceive) + -ledge (condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English cnawan (to know) → Middle English knowleche → English knowledge
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't have much knowledge about cars.
   - His knowledge about history is impressive.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research contributes to our knowledge about neural plasticity.
   - Participants demonstrated limited knowledge about risk factors.

1. **Noun**: details (C, pl: details)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Individual facts or items of information about something; specific pieces of information
   
   **Synonyms**: particulars, specifics, facts, information, points
   
   **Antonyms**: overview, generalities, summary
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing specific information about something; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When general overview is more appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - details about
   - go into details
   - provide details
   - further details
   - in detail
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down, from) + -tail (cut)
   
   **Etymology**: French détail (small piece) → English detail (particular item of information)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you give me more details about the party?
   - I need to know all the details about what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report provides comprehensive details about the implementation process.
   - Please submit details about your qualifications and experience.

1. **Noun**: facts (C, pl: facts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Things that are known or proved to be true; information used as evidence
   
   **Synonyms**: realities, truths, data, information, evidence
   
   **Antonyms**: fiction, falsehood, lies, myths
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing true information about something; emphasizing accuracy
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When dealing with opinions or interpretations
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - facts about
   - face the facts
   - hard facts
   - matter of fact
   - get the facts straight
   
   **Root Analysis**: fact (something done) from Latin factum
   
   **Etymology**: Latin factum (thing done, deed) → English fact (what is true)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I want to know the facts about what really happened.
   - These are just facts about history, not opinions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The article presents facts about climate change supported by research.
   - Participants were asked to recall facts about the stimulus materials.

1. **Noun**: question (C, pl: questions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A matter requiring resolution or discussion; a problem; a sentence or phrase used to find out information
   
   **Synonyms**: query, inquiry, issue, matter, problem
   
   **Antonyms**: answer, reply, response
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing inquiries or issues about something; all contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When statement form is more appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - question about
   - ask a question
   - raise a question
   - call into question
   - out of the question
   
   **Root Analysis**: quest (seek) + -ion (action)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin quaestio (seeking, inquiry) → Old French → English question
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a question about your comment.
   - Can I ask you a question about the assignment?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research addresses critical questions about sustainable development.
   - Participants were asked questions about their dietary habits.

1. **Noun**: discussion (C/U, pl: discussions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action or process of talking about something to reach a decision or exchange ideas
   
   **Synonyms**: conversation, dialogue, debate, talk, discourse
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, monologue
   
   **When to Use**: Describing conversations or exchanges about topics; formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For one-sided communication (use "lecture" or "speech")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - discussion about
   - under discussion
   - open for discussion
   - panel discussion
   - lengthy discussion
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart) + -cuss (shake) + -ion (action)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin discussio (examination) → Old French → English discussion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had a long discussion about where to go on vacation.
   - Let's have a discussion about this later.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The panel held a discussion about emerging technologies.
   - Further discussion about policy implementation is warranted.

1. **Noun**: argument (C, pl: arguments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An exchange of diverging views, typically heated; a reason or set of reasons given to support a position
   
   **Synonyms**: dispute, disagreement, debate, quarrel, reasoning
   
   **Antonyms**: agreement, harmony, accord
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing disagreements or reasoning about topics; formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "discussion" is more neutral and appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - argument about
   - get into an argument
   - strong argument
   - heated argument
   - settle an argument
   
   **Root Analysis**: argu (make clear) + -ment (result)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin argumentum (evidence, proof) → Old French → English argument
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They had an argument about money.
   - Let's not get into an argument about this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The paper presents a compelling argument about market efficiency.
   - Scholars debate arguments about the origins of language.
