1. **Original Expression**: I want to talk about your performance  
   **Context**: Manager giving feedback to employee
   
   **Core Intent**: To initiate a discussion concerning someone's work performance
   
   **Casual**: Let's chat about how things are going  
   *Characteristics*: Friendly verb "chat," general phrase "how things are going," relaxed tone  
   *Use when*: Close working relationship, friendly team culture, informal check-in
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'd like to discuss your performance  
   *Characteristics*: Professional structure "I'd like to," neutral verb "discuss," clear topic reference  
   *Use when*: Standard manager-employee relationship, regular review meetings, professional setting
   
   **Formal**: I would like to schedule a meeting to review your performance  
   *Characteristics*: Full modal form "would like," procedural language "schedule a meeting," formal verb "review"  
   *Use when*: Official performance reviews, HR-involved meetings, documented discussions
   
   **Humorous**: So, let's talk about your superhero efforts at work  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor "superhero efforts," casual opener "so"  
   *Use when*: Positive feedback context, rapport-building, creative workplace culture  
   *Avoid when*: Negative feedback, serious performance issues, formal review processes
   
   **Lively**: I'm excited to talk about all the great work you've been doing!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic tone, exclamation mark, positive framing "great work"  
   *Use when*: Positive performance discussion, motivational context, celebrating achievements
   
   **Empathetic**: I'd like us to talk about your performance, and I want you to know this is a supportive conversation  
   *Characteristics*: Inclusive pronoun "us," reassurance "supportive conversation," considerate framing  
   *Use when*: Sensitive discussions, addressing difficulties, building trust
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher formality for hierarchical organizations; casual for flat structures
   - *Social Distance*: Formal for new relationships; can be casual with long-term colleague
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Formal for serious issues/documentation; casual for routine check-ins
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect high formality in workplace; others prefer informality
   - *Medium*: Email/written tends toward formal; face-to-face allows casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using humorous register for negative feedback - inappropriate and unprofessional
   - Too casual for documented performance issues - lacks proper gravitas
   - Overly formal for routine positive feedback - creates unnecessary anxiety
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Anglo-American workplaces often accept casual register; Asian and European cultures may expect semi-formal minimum
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Annual formal review → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (documentation required, official process)
   - **Situation 2**: Weekly one-on-one with close colleague → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual (established rapport, informal check-in)
   - **Situation 3**: First review with new employee → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal (professional but not intimidating)

2. **Original Expression**: What do you think about this idea?  
   **Context**: Asking for colleague's opinion on a proposal
   
   **Core Intent**: To solicit someone's opinion or perspective on a suggested idea
   
   **Casual**: What do you reckon?  
   *Characteristics*: Colloquial verb "reckon," implied context, brief  
   *Use when*: Friends, close colleagues, informal brainstorming, relaxed environment
   
   **Semi-Formal**: What are your thoughts on this idea?  
   *Characteristics*: Professional phrasing "your thoughts," explicit topic reference, neutral  
   *Use when*: Standard workplace, team meetings, professional peers, typical collaboration
   
   **Formal**: I would appreciate your assessment of this proposal  
   *Characteristics*: Elevated vocabulary "assessment," "proposal," deferential "I would appreciate"  
   *Use when*: Senior stakeholders, formal presentations, written proposals, hierarchical settings
   
   **Humorous**: So, brilliant idea or total disaster?  
   *Characteristics*: Playful binary choice, self-deprecating humor, casual opener  
   *Use when*: Creative teams, positive rapport, brainstorming sessions  
   *Avoid when*: Serious proposals, stakeholder presentations, documented decision-making
   
   **Lively**: I'm really curious to hear what you think!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic "really curious," exclamation mark, engaging tone  
   *Use when*: Collaborative environments, valuing input, building engagement
   
   **Empathetic**: I really value your perspective on this, so please share your honest thoughts  
   *Characteristics*: Validation "I really value," invitation to honesty, reassurance  
   *Use when*: Encouraging hesitant speakers, diverse opinions needed, psychological safety important
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Formal when asking superiors; casual when asking peers
   - *Social Distance*: Semi-formal for new relationships; casual develops with familiarity
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Formal for major decisions; casual for minor choices
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct question acceptable in Anglo cultures; may need softening in Asian contexts
   - *Medium*: Written communication tends more formal; spoken allows casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Too casual when asking executives - shows lack of respect
   - Overly formal with peers - creates unnecessary distance
   - Humorous register when stakes are high - trivializes important decisions
   
   **Cultural Notes**: British English often uses "reckon"; American English prefers "think"; some cultures require more deferential language
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Presenting to board of directors → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (high power distance, serious context)
   - **Situation 2**: Brainstorming with creative team → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Humorous (collaborative, creative environment)
   - **Situation 3**: Seeking input from diverse team → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic (encouraging all voices)
