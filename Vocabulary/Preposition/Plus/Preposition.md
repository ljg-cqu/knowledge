1. **Preposition**: plus (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Mathematical)* used to indicate addition or the combining of two or more quantities (e.g., 5 plus 3 equals 8)
   - *(Abstract)* used to indicate something additional or extra beyond what has been mentioned (e.g., the benefits plus the salary make it attractive)
   - *(Informal - Linking)* used as a connector to add information, similar to "and" or "additionally" (e.g., it's expensive, plus it's hard to maintain)
   
   **Synonyms**: and (in adding context), in addition to (formal), combined with (formal), along with (neutral), together with (neutral)
   
   **Antonyms**: minus, without, less, excluding, except for
   
   **When to Use**: Use when combining quantities in mathematics; when indicating additional elements or benefits; in informal speech to add supporting information or reasons; when listing multiple positive attributes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing as a sentence connector (use "moreover" or "furthermore" instead); don't use when subtraction or exclusion is meant; avoid overuse as a conjunction in professional documents
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: plus tax, plus shipping, plus benefits, plus expenses
   - **Verb + prep**: calculate plus, add plus, include plus
   - **Adjective + prep**: valued at X plus Y
   - **Noun + prep**: cost plus, price plus, salary plus
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "plus" meaning "more"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → Old French → Middle English; entered English directly from Latin "plus" (meaning "more"), originally used only in mathematical contexts, later expanded to general additive uses
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The job pays $50K plus bonuses, which is pretty good.
   - I don't want to go—it's too far, plus I'm really tired tonight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The total investment amounts to $2 million plus associated overhead costs.
   - The program offers extensive training plus ongoing professional development opportunities.

2. **Preposition**: plus (type: simple - adjective-like usage)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Mathematical notation)* indicating a positive value or quantity above zero (e.g., plus 10 degrees)
   - *(Grading/Rating)* indicating a slightly higher grade than the letter alone (e.g., B plus, grade of 85 plus)
   
   **Synonyms**: positive (in mathematics), above (in temperature contexts), higher than (in grading)
   
   **Antonyms**: minus, negative, below
   
   **When to Use**: Use in mathematical contexts to denote positive numbers; in grading systems to indicate incremental improvement; in temperature readings above zero; in rating systems to show slight advantage
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use in contexts where precision requires exact numerical values; avoid in formal scientific writing where "positive" is preferred
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: plus five, plus ten degrees, plus or minus
   - **Adjective + prep**: temperature is plus, grade of B plus
   - **Noun + prep**: plus sign, plus value, plus rating
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Latin "plus" (more)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → mathematical notation; adopted from Latin mathematical treatises in the 15th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's plus 5 degrees outside—finally above freezing!
   - I got a B plus on my essay, so I'm pretty happy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The experimental results showed a deviation of plus 2.5 percent.
   - Students achieving grades of A minus or A plus qualify for the honor roll.

3. **Preposition**: in addition to / plus (type: phrasal comparison)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* used to introduce extra information or elements that are added to what has already been stated
   
   **Synonyms**: besides, as well as, on top of, along with, together with
   
   **Antonyms**: except for, minus, without, excluding, apart from
   
   **When to Use**: When formally adding supplementary information to a statement; when emphasizing that something is extra or bonus; in business contexts listing benefits or features
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using informal "plus" in highly formal academic or legal writing; don't use when the relationship is not truly additive
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in addition to X plus Y, X plus the fact that Y
   - **Verb + prep**: comes with X plus Y, includes X plus Y
   - **Noun + prep**: benefits plus advantages, features plus extras
   
   **Root Analysis**: "in addition to" = in (preposition) + addition (noun from Latin addere "to add") + to (preposition); "plus" from Latin "plus" (more)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin addere → addition (14th century) → "in addition to" (16th century); "plus" borrowed directly from Latin in 15th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You get free delivery plus a discount if you order today.
   - She's smart, funny, plus she's really good at sports.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In addition to the base salary, employees receive comprehensive health insurance plus retirement benefits.
   - The research demonstrates significant findings in cognitive function, plus notable improvements in memory retention.
