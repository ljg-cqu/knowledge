1. **Original Expression**: Plus, it's really expensive.  
   **Context**: Adding a supporting reason in casual conversation
   
   **Core Intent**: To introduce an additional reason or piece of information that supports the previous statement
   
   **Casual**: Plus, it's super expensive.  
   *Characteristics*: Uses "plus" as informal connector; intensifier "super"; contraction-friendly tone  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, family, close colleagues; informal emails; text messages
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Additionally, the cost is quite high.  
   *Characteristics*: Formal connector "additionally"; neutral adjective "high"; complete sentence structure  
   *Use when*: Professional emails; workplace discussions; business meetings; polite but not academic contexts
   
   **Formal**: Furthermore, the associated expenses are substantial.  
   *Characteristics*: Formal connector "furthermore"; sophisticated vocabulary "substantial"; precise noun "expenses"  
   *Use when*: Academic writing; formal reports; professional presentations; official documentation
   
   **Humorous**: Plus, it costs about as much as a small island!  
   *Characteristics*: Exaggeration for comic effect; hyperbolic comparison  
   *Use when*: Informal settings where humor is appropriate; building rapport; casual complaints  
   *Avoid when*: Professional settings; when discussing someone's financial difficulties; formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: And get this—it's ridiculously expensive!  
   *Characteristics*: Attention-grabbing phrase "get this"; emphatic adverb "ridiculously"; exclamation mark  
   *Use when*: Storytelling; sharing surprising information; enthusiastic conversation
   
   **Empathetic**: I know it's disappointing, especially since the price is so high.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of feelings "I know it's disappointing"; softening "especially since"  
   *Use when*: Comforting someone; acknowledging frustration; sensitive discussions
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status contexts require "furthermore" or "additionally"; equals allow "plus"
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers and new acquaintances prefer "additionally"; close relationships allow "plus"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious matters require formal connectors; casual topics allow informal "plus"
   - *Cultural Context*: Academic and professional cultures expect formal connectors
   - *Medium*: Written professional documents require formal; spoken casual allows "plus"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "plus" as connector in academic papers: Sounds too informal and colloquial
   - Starting every sentence with different formal connectors: Creates repetitive, stilted writing
   - Using "furthermore" in casual conversation: Sounds pretentious and overly formal
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In American English, "plus" as a connector is very common in casual speech. In formal writing contexts globally, it's considered too informal. British English may prefer "moreover" or "what's more" in semi-formal contexts.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Text message to friend about not going to a concert → **Appropriate Variant**: "Plus, it's super expensive!" (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Email to manager about project feasibility → **Appropriate Variant**: "Additionally, the associated costs exceed our budget." (Semi-Formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Academic research paper discussing limitations → **Appropriate Variant**: "Furthermore, the financial constraints restrict sample size." (Formal)

2. **Original Expression**: The package includes accommodation plus meals.  
   **Context**: Describing what is included in a package or offer
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that additional items are included together with the main item
   
   **Casual**: The package comes with a room and food.  
   *Characteristics*: Informal verb "comes with"; simple nouns "room" and "food"; coordinating conjunction "and"  
   *Use when*: Casual inquiries; talking with friends; informal customer service
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The package includes accommodation and meals.  
   *Characteristics*: Clear verb "includes"; standard terms "accommodation" and "meals"; professional but accessible  
   *Use when*: Standard business communications; travel websites; customer-facing materials
   
   **Formal**: The comprehensive package encompasses lodging in addition to full board.  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "encompasses"; technical terms "lodging" and "full board"; formal connector  
   *Use when*: Luxury service descriptions; legal contracts; formal proposals
   
   **Humorous**: You get a place to crash plus all the food you can eat!  
   *Characteristics*: Slang "place to crash"; exaggeration "all the food you can eat"; enthusiastic tone  
   *Use when*: Marketing to young audiences; casual promotional materials; friendly conversations  
   *Avoid when*: Professional contexts; formal services; luxury brands
   
   **Lively**: Everything's included—your room AND all your meals!  
   *Characteristics*: Emphasis with caps "AND"; inclusive "everything"; exclamation for excitement  
   *Use when*: Enthusiastic sales pitch; promotional materials; engaging presentations
   
   **Empathetic**: You won't have to worry about anything—accommodation and meals are both taken care of.  
   *Characteristics*: Reassurance "won't have to worry"; softening "both taken care of"  
   *Use when*: Supporting anxious customers; providing reassurance; customer service interactions
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "Plus" works well in peer-to-peer business contexts; formal terms needed for luxury services
   - *Social Distance*: "Plus" appropriate in standard business; formal terms for first-time luxury clients
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Significant purchases require clear, formal language; casual purchases allow informal
   - *Cultural Context*: Tourism industry commonly uses "plus" or "and" globally
   - *Medium*: Contracts require formal language; brochures can use semi-formal "plus"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using overly casual language in high-end service descriptions: Undermines perceived value
   - Using "plus" ambiguously without clarifying what's included: Creates confusion about package contents
   - Over-formalizing in customer-facing communications: Creates distance and reduces accessibility
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Plus" in package descriptions is universally understood and accepted across English-speaking markets. However, luxury services typically avoid it in favor of more sophisticated language.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Budget travel website describing hostel package → **Appropriate Variant**: "The package comes with a bed and breakfast." (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Hotel booking platform standard listing → **Appropriate Variant**: "The package includes accommodation and meals." (Semi-Formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Five-star resort contract → **Appropriate Variant**: "The comprehensive package encompasses luxury accommodations in addition to gourmet dining." (Formal)

3. **Original Expression**: 5 plus 3 equals 8.  
   **Context**: Mathematical expression or calculation
   
   **Core Intent**: To express the addition of two numbers
   
   **Casual**: 5 plus 3 is 8. / Five and three makes eight.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple verb "is" or "makes"; numbers can be written as words or digits; relaxed structure  
   *Use when*: Everyday calculations; teaching young children; casual conversation
   
   **Semi-Formal**: 5 plus 3 equals 8.  
   *Characteristics*: Standard mathematical verb "equals"; digits for numbers; clear structure  
   *Use when*: Standard educational contexts; textbooks; general mathematical communication
   
   **Formal**: The sum of five and three yields eight. / Five added to three results in eight.  
   *Characteristics*: Formal vocabulary "sum," "yields," "results in"; numbers written as words in formal texts  
   *Use when*: Academic papers; formal mathematical proofs; published research
   
   **Humorous**: If you stick 5 and 3 together, you get 8!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful language "stick together"; informal structure  
   *Use when*: Making math fun for kids; casual tutoring; lighthearted explanations  
   *Avoid when*: Formal education; testing situations; serious mathematical contexts
   
   **Lively**: So, 5 plus 3? That gives you 8!  
   *Characteristics*: Interactive question format; conversational "that gives you"; exclamation point  
   *Use when*: Engaging teaching; enthusiastic explanations; interactive learning
   
   **Empathetic**: Don't worry—it's simple. When you add 5 and 3, you get 8.  
   *Characteristics*: Reassurance "don't worry"; explanation "when you add"; encouraging tone  
   *Use when*: Tutoring struggling students; patient explanation; supportive teaching
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Mathematical notation is largely register-neutral; context determines formality
   - *Social Distance*: "Plus" and "equals" are universal; formal writing may use "the sum of"
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Tests and formal proofs require standard notation; casual learning allows variation
   - *Cultural Context*: Mathematical language is highly standardized globally
   - *Medium*: Written mathematics follows conventions; spoken allows more variation
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using overly casual language in mathematics assessments: May be marked incorrect for lack of precision
   - Mixing notation styles inconsistently: Creates confusion in formal mathematical writing
   - Over-formalizing in elementary education: Can intimidate young learners
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Mathematical notation is remarkably consistent across cultures. "Plus" and "equals" are universal in English mathematical contexts, though formal academic writing may prefer "the sum of" or "added to."
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Helping a 6-year-old with homework → **Appropriate Variant**: "Five and three makes eight!" (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Middle school math textbook → **Appropriate Variant**: "5 plus 3 equals 8." (Semi-Formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Published mathematics research paper → **Appropriate Variant**: "The sum of five and three yields eight." (Formal)

4. **Original Expression**: That's a real plus.  
   **Context**: Identifying something as an advantage or positive feature
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that something is a beneficial or advantageous feature
   
   **Casual**: That's awesome! / That's a big plus!  
   *Characteristics*: Informal intensifiers "awesome," "big"; exclamatory tone  
   *Use when*: Enthusiastic conversations; casual evaluations; friendly discussions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: That's definitely an advantage. / That's a significant benefit.  
   *Characteristics*: Clear nouns "advantage," "benefit"; moderate intensifiers "definitely," "significant"  
   *Use when*: Professional discussions; job interviews; business evaluations
   
   **Formal**: This constitutes a substantial advantage. / This represents a considerable benefit.  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verbs "constitutes," "represents"; sophisticated adjectives "substantial," "considerable"  
   *Use when*: Formal assessments; academic evaluations; executive reports
   
   **Humorous**: Now that's what I call a sweet deal! / That's the cherry on top!  
   *Characteristics*: Idiomatic expressions; playful metaphors; enthusiastic tone  
   *Use when*: Casual conversations; building rapport; informal negotiations  
   *Avoid when*: Serious business negotiations; formal evaluations; professional assessments
   
   **Lively**: That's absolutely fantastic! / What a bonus!  
   *Characteristics*: Strong positive intensifier "absolutely fantastic"; exclamatory "what a"; high energy  
   *Use when*: Expressing enthusiasm; celebrating positive features; engaging discussions
   
   **Empathetic**: That must be really helpful for you. / I can see how that would be beneficial.  
   *Characteristics*: Person-focused "for you"; acknowledging impact "I can see"; validating language  
   *Use when*: Supportive conversations; acknowledging others' needs; empathetic discussions
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "Plus" acceptable between peers; formal terms needed for superior evaluations
   - *Social Distance*: "Plus" works for colleagues; formal terms for professional strangers
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes decisions require formal language; casual discussions allow informal
   - *Cultural Context*: Business contexts vary—startups accept casual, corporations prefer formal
   - *Medium*: Spoken allows "plus"; written reports prefer "advantage" or "benefit"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "that's a plus" in formal written evaluations: Too casual for professional documents
   - Overusing superlatives in professional contexts: Reduces credibility and appears unprofessional
   - Being too formal in team discussions: Creates unnecessary distance in collaborative environments
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Plus" as a noun meaning "advantage" is common in American English business contexts. British English may prefer "advantage" or "benefit" even in semi-formal settings.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Discussing apartment features with roommate → **Appropriate Variant**: "That's a big plus!" (Casual)
   - **Situation 2**: Performance review with manager → **Appropriate Variant**: "That's definitely an advantage in your skill set." (Semi-Formal)
   - **Situation 3**: Formal business proposal evaluation → **Appropriate Variant**: "This feature constitutes a substantial competitive advantage." (Formal)
