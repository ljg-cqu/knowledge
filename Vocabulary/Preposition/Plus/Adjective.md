1. **Adjective**: additional
   **Meaning**: 
   - added to what already exists or is already present; extra or supplementary (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: extra, supplementary, further, added, bonus
   
   **Antonyms**: fewer, reduced, less, subtracted
   
   **When to Use**: When describing something that is added to an existing amount or quantity; in business contexts describing extra features or benefits; in formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing something that is integral or essential (not truly extra); don't use when "necessary" or "required" is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - additional cost
   - additional benefits
   - additional information
   - additional charge
   - at no additional cost
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + -dition (from Latin dare "to give") + -al (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin additionalis → Old French → Middle English (14th century); from "addition" meaning something added
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can get two additional toppings for free.
   - I need some additional time to finish this project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract stipulates that additional services will be charged separately.
   - Participants may request additional materials for the research study.

2. **Adjective**: positive
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Mathematics) greater than zero; having a value above zero (non-gradable)
   - (General) characterized by the presence rather than absence of features; constructive or optimistic (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: favorable, constructive, affirmative, optimistic, beneficial
   
   **Antonyms**: negative, unfavorable, pessimistic, adverse
   
   **When to Use**: In mathematical contexts to describe numbers greater than zero; when describing constructive attitudes or outcomes; in test results indicating presence of something; when emphasizing beneficial aspects
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use in mathematical contexts when referring to zero itself; avoid when neutrality is more accurate than optimism
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - positive number
   - positive attitude
   - positive outcome
   - positive feedback
   - positive correlation
   
   **Root Analysis**: posit- (from Latin ponere "to place, put") + -ive (adjective suffix meaning "having the quality of")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin positivus → Old French positif → Middle English (13th century); originally meaning "formally laid down," later developed mathematical meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stay positive—things will work out!
   - The test came back positive, so we need treatment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The experimental data revealed a positive correlation between variables.
   - Stakeholders provided predominantly positive feedback regarding the initiative.

3. **Adjective**: extra
   **Meaning**: 
   - added to an existing or usual amount; more than is usual, expected, or necessary (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: additional, surplus, spare, supplementary, bonus
   
   **Antonyms**: less, insufficient, reduced, standard
   
   **When to Use**: In informal and semi-formal contexts when describing something beyond the standard or expected amount; in retail contexts for additional charges; when emphasizing surplus or bonus items
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing (prefer "additional"); don't use when describing essential or standard components
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - extra charge
   - extra credit
   - extra mile
   - extra time
   - extra effort
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin extra meaning "outside, beyond"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin extra → Middle English (14th century); originally meant "outside" or "beyond," evolved to mean "more than usual"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I get extra cheese on my burger?
   - I brought extra snacks just in case.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees who demonstrate extra initiative will be considered for promotion.
   - The budget includes provisions for extra expenditures during peak season.

4. **Adjective**: supplementary
   **Meaning**: 
   - completing or enhancing something; added to supply what is lacking (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: additional, complementary, auxiliary, ancillary, supporting
   
   **Antonyms**: primary, main, essential, core
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and academic contexts when describing materials or information that complements main content; in educational settings for extra assignments; in technical documentation for additional resources
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (too formal); don't use when describing primary or essential components
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supplementary materials
   - supplementary information
   - supplementary benefits
   - supplementary evidence
   - supplementary reading
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (under, from below) + -ple (from Latin plere "to fill") + -ment (noun suffix) + -ary (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplementum → Late Latin supplementarius → English (17th century); from "supplement" meaning something that completes or enhances
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The teacher gave us some supplementary practice problems.
   - I take supplementary vitamins every morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The appendix contains supplementary data referenced in the main text.
   - Supplementary documentation must be submitted with the application.

5. **Adjective**: combined
   **Meaning**: 
   - brought together to form a single entity or amount; merged or united (non-gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: joint, merged, collective, unified, aggregated
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, individual, divided, distinct
   
   **When to Use**: When describing things that have been brought together; in statistics or mathematics when referring to totals; in business contexts for merged entities or joint efforts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when elements remain separate or independent; don't use when individual contributions need emphasis
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - combined total
   - combined effort
   - combined forces
   - combined income
   - combined experience
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + -bine (from Latin bini "two by two") + -ed (past participle suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin combinare → Old French combiner → Middle English (15th century); from "combine" meaning to bring together
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our combined savings are enough for a down payment.
   - The team's combined experience is over 50 years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The combined revenue of both divisions exceeded projections.
   - The study examined the combined effects of multiple variables.
