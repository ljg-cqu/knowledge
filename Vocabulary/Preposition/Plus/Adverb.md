1. **Adverb**: additionally (type: sentence adverb / linking adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - used to introduce extra information or facts; furthermore; moreover (usually front position)
   
   **Synonyms**: furthermore, moreover, in addition, besides, also
   
   **Antonyms**: however, conversely, alternatively, instead
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal writing to add information; when building arguments with multiple supporting points; in academic or professional contexts; typically at the beginning of a sentence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse which makes writing repetitive; don't use in very casual conversation (sounds formal); avoid when the information is contrasting rather than additive
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - Additionally, it should be noted
   - Additionally, the study found
   - Additionally, participants reported
   
   **Root Analysis**: addition (noun from Latin addere "to add") + -al (adjective suffix) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin additionalis → additionaly → additionally (17th century); formed from "additional" by adding adverb suffix
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I don't want to go. Additionally, I'm really tired.
   - The phone is expensive, and additionally, it breaks easily.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Additionally, the research demonstrates significant implications for clinical practice.
   - The proposal requires board approval. Additionally, stakeholder consultation is mandatory.

2. **Adverb**: furthermore (type: sentence adverb / linking adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - in addition to what has been said; moreover; used to introduce a further point (front position)
   
   **Synonyms**: moreover, additionally, besides, what's more, in addition
   
   **Antonyms**: however, nevertheless, conversely, on the other hand
   
   **When to Use**: In formal writing and speeches to add supporting information; when building logical arguments; in academic and professional contexts; typically sentence-initial position
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (too formal); don't use when introducing contrasting ideas; avoid overuse in a single paragraph
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - Furthermore, it is important to note
   - Furthermore, evidence suggests
   - Furthermore, research indicates
   
   **Root Analysis**: further (comparative of "far" meaning "more distant" or "additional") + more (intensifier)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "further" + "more" → "furthermore" (14th century); combination emphasizing additional information
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't afford it. Furthermore, I don't even like the color.
   - The weather is bad, and furthermore, the roads are closed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Furthermore, the data corroborate previous findings in the literature.
   - The methodology exhibits several limitations. Furthermore, the sample size restricts generalizability.

3. **Adverb**: moreover (type: sentence adverb / linking adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - used to add information that supports or reinforces a previous statement; in addition; besides (front position)
   
   **Synonyms**: furthermore, additionally, besides, what's more, in addition
   
   **Antonyms**: however, nevertheless, nonetheless, conversely
   
   **When to Use**: In formal writing to strengthen an argument by adding further evidence; in academic discourse; in professional presentations; typically at sentence beginning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in everyday casual speech (too formal); don't use for contrasting information; avoid in personal emails or text messages
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - Moreover, this approach
   - Moreover, the findings
   - Moreover, evidence demonstrates
   
   **Root Analysis**: more (comparative meaning "additional") + over (suggesting "beyond" or "in addition to")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English more + over → moreover (13th century); literally meaning "more beyond" or "more in addition"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's too expensive. Moreover, the quality isn't great.
   - I'm not interested, and moreover, I don't have time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Moreover, the statistical analysis reveals significant correlations among variables.
   - The intervention proved cost-effective. Moreover, patient satisfaction ratings increased substantially.

4. **Adverb**: also (type: linking adverb / degree adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - in addition; too; as well (mid or end position, occasionally front position)
   
   **Synonyms**: too, as well, additionally, furthermore, in addition
   
   **Antonyms**: only, exclusively, merely, solely
   
   **When to Use**: In all registers from casual to formal; when adding information that is equally important; can be placed in various sentence positions for different emphasis; very versatile connector
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse which weakens impact; be careful with position—mid-position is most common; front position can sound formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - We also need
   - It is also important
   - They also mentioned
   - Also included
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (Old English eall) + so (Old English swa meaning "in this way")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ealswā → also (before 12th century); literally "all so" meaning "in just the same way"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like pizza, and I also love pasta.
   - She's smart, and she's also really funny.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study also examined secondary outcomes across demographic subgroups.
   - This approach also addresses previously identified methodological limitations.

5. **Adverb**: too (type: degree adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - in addition; also; as well (typically end position, occasionally mid position)
   
   **Synonyms**: also, as well, additionally, likewise, besides
   
   **Antonyms**: only, merely, exclusively, alone
   
   **When to Use**: In all registers but especially casual speech; at the end of sentences or clauses; when adding information informally; in positive statements and questions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not typically used in very formal academic writing (prefer "also" or "additionally"); doesn't work well in front position; sounds awkward in negative contexts (use "either" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - me too
   - that too
   - included too
   - applies too
   
   **Root Analysis**: Intensified form of "to" (Old English tō meaning "in the direction of")
   
   **Etymology**: Old English tō → too (16th century); developed from the preposition "to" meaning "in addition to" or "moreover"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I want pizza too!
   - She's coming to the party, and John is coming too.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These benefits apply to part-time employees too.
   - The policy affects international students too.
