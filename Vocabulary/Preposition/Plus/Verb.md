1. **Verb**: add
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) to put something together with something else to increase the size, number, or amount
   - (T/mathematics) to calculate the total of two or more numbers
   - (I) to say something more; to make an additional remark
   
   **Synonyms**: include, append, attach, incorporate, supplement
   
   **Antonyms**: subtract, remove, delete, take away, deduct
   
   **When to Use**: In mathematical contexts for addition operations; when describing the inclusion of additional elements; in instructions for combining ingredients or components; in formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing removal or reduction; don't use when subtraction is meant; avoid when elements remain separate (use "combine" with caution)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - add to the list
   - add up the numbers
   - add value
   - add insult to injury
   - add fuel to the fire
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin addere: ad- (to) + -dere (put, place)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin addere → Old French ajouter → Middle English (14th century); from Latin "addere" meaning "to give to, join to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you add milk to my shopping list?
   - If you add 5 plus 3, you get 8.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research adds significant value to the existing body of knowledge.
   - Participants were instructed to add their responses to the online survey.

2. **Verb**: include
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) to contain something as a part of the whole; to make something part of a larger group or set
   - (T) to allow someone or something to be part of a group or activity
   
   **Synonyms**: incorporate, encompass, comprise, contain, embrace
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, leave out, eliminate, reject
   
   **When to Use**: When describing what is contained within something; when specifying components of a package or offer; in formal and business contexts; when emphasizing that something is part of a whole
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when elements are separate or optional; don't use when describing exclusion; avoid when listing complete contents (use "comprise" or "consist of")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - include in the price
   - included in the package
   - include everyone
   - not included
   - tax included
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + -clude (from Latin claudere "to close, shut")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin includere → Old French → Middle English (15th century); literally "to shut in" or "to enclose"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Does the ticket price include food?
   - Don't forget to include Sarah in the group chat.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The comprehensive package includes accommodation, meals, and transportation.
   - The analysis included data from over 500 participants across multiple sites.

3. **Verb**: combine
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T/I) to join or mix together to form a single unit or substance
   - (T) to bring together different qualities, features, or things
   
   **Synonyms**: merge, unite, blend, mix, fuse
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, divide, split, isolate, disconnect
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the merging of separate elements into one; in cooking for mixing ingredients; when describing the integration of qualities or features; in formal and technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when elements remain distinct or separate; don't use when describing simple addition without integration
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - combine forces
   - combine ingredients
   - combined with
   - combine efforts
   - combine business with pleasure
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + -bine (from Latin bini "two by two")
   
   **Etymology**: Late Latin combinare → Old French combiner → Middle English (15th century); from "combinare" meaning "to unite"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's combine our money and buy something together.
   - Combine the flour and sugar in a bowl.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology combines quantitative and qualitative approaches effectively.
   - Organizations must combine resources to address this systemic challenge.

4. **Verb**: supplement
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) to add something to something else in order to improve or complete it
   - (T) to add to something to make it larger or better
   
   **Synonyms**: complement, augment, enhance, add to, reinforce
   
   **Antonyms**: reduce, diminish, decrease, deplete, weaken
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when describing enhancement or completion; when referring to additional income or resources; in academic contexts for supplementary materials; typically more formal than "add"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (too formal); don't use when describing primary components; avoid when reduction is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supplement income
   - supplement the diet
   - supplement with
   - supplement existing
   - supplement the information
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (under, from below) + -ple (from Latin plere "to fill") + -ment (noun → verb conversion)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplementum → supplement (noun) → supplement (verb) (17th century); from "supplere" meaning "to fill up"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I supplement my income by doing freelance work.
   - You should supplement your diet with more vegetables.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The curriculum is supplemented with practical training and internships.
   - Participants supplemented traditional methods with innovative digital approaches.

5. **Verb**: total
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T) to calculate the sum of; to add up to a particular amount
   - (T) to amount to a specified figure when added together
   
   **Synonyms**: add up to, amount to, come to, sum, aggregate
   
   **Antonyms**: subtract from, reduce, decrease, diminish
   
   **When to Use**: In accounting and financial contexts for calculating sums; when describing final amounts; in formal and business contexts; often used with "up"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing partial amounts; don't use when individual components need emphasis; avoid in contexts where "total" as an adjective creates confusion
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - total up
   - expenses totaled
   - sales total
   - totaling more than
   - total the figures
   
   **Root Analysis**: From total (adjective/noun from Latin totalis "whole")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin totalis → total (noun/adj) → total (verb) (17th century); verb form developed from the noun/adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you total up the bill for me?
   - Our expenses totaled about $500 this month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The combined expenditures totaled approximately three million dollars.
   - Responses from all participants totaled 847 individual data points.

6. **Verb**: accumulate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (T/I) to gradually increase in number or amount over time
   - (T) to collect or gather things over a period of time
   
   **Synonyms**: gather, collect, amass, build up, aggregate
   
   **Antonyms**: dissipate, disperse, scatter, diminish, deplete
   
   **When to Use**: When describing gradual increase over time; in financial contexts for interest or wealth building; in scientific contexts for data collection; in formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing instant or sudden addition; don't use for one-time additions; avoid in casual contexts where simpler words work better
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accumulate wealth
   - accumulate evidence
   - accumulate over time
   - debts accumulate
   - accumulate interest
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to, toward) + -cumul (from Latin cumulus "heap") + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin accumulare → accumulate (16th century); from "cumulare" meaning "to heap up"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've accumulated way too much stuff over the years.
   - Dust really accumulates if you don't clean regularly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investment account accumulates interest at a rate of 3.5 percent annually.
   - Evidence has accumulated demonstrating the efficacy of this intervention.
