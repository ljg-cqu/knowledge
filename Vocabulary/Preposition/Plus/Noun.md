1. **Noun**: addition (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (U) the action or process of adding something to something else
   - (C) a person or thing added or joined to another, typically to improve or enhance it
   - (U/mathematics) the process of calculating the total of two or more numbers or amounts
   
   **Synonyms**: supplement, addendum, extension, extra, inclusion
   
   **Antonyms**: subtraction, removal, deletion, reduction, omission
   
   **When to Use**: In mathematical contexts for the operation of adding; when referring to something added to improve or expand; in construction when describing extensions to buildings; in formal and technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing essential components (not truly additional); don't use when subtraction or removal is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in addition to
   - latest addition
   - welcome addition
   - addition to the family
   - simple addition
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + -dition (from Latin dare "to give, put")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin additionem → Old French adition → Middle English (14th century); from Latin "addere" meaning "to add to"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our new puppy is a great addition to the family.
   - Can you help me with this addition problem?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The addition of new facilities enhanced the institution's capacity significantly.
   - This research represents a valuable addition to the existing literature.

2. **Noun**: supplement (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - something added to complete or enhance something else
   - an additional section of a newspaper or magazine
   - a substance taken to remedy dietary deficiencies
   
   **Synonyms**: addition, extra, complement, appendix, addendum
   
   **Antonyms**: reduction, subtraction, main component, core element
   
   **When to Use**: When referring to something that completes or improves; in health contexts for vitamins/minerals; in publishing for additional sections; in formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for primary or essential components; avoid when describing replacements rather than additions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - dietary supplement
   - food supplement
   - supplement to income
   - Sunday supplement
   - vitamin supplement
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (under, from below) + -ple (from Latin plere "to fill") + -ment (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplementum → Old French → Middle English (14th century); from "supplere" meaning "to fill up"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I take a vitamin D supplement every day.
   - The weekend paper comes with a travel supplement.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants received a comprehensive nutritional supplement throughout the study.
   - The document serves as a supplement to the main contract.

3. **Noun**: bonus (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - something good that is more than what was expected or usual
   - an extra payment given to an employee in addition to their regular salary
   
   **Synonyms**: extra, benefit, addition, perk, premium
   
   **Antonyms**: penalty, deduction, reduction, disadvantage
   
   **When to Use**: In employment contexts for extra payments; when describing unexpected benefits; in informal and business contexts for positive extras
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing; don't use when describing standard or expected components
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - year-end bonus
   - signing bonus
   - performance bonus
   - bonus points
   - added bonus
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin bonus meaning "good"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin bonus → English (18th century); originally meant "good," later specialized to mean "extra payment" or "additional benefit"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Getting a window seat was an added bonus!
   - My company gives out Christmas bonuses every year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees meeting performance targets receive a substantial year-end bonus.
   - The policy provides comprehensive coverage, with life insurance as an additional bonus.

4. **Noun**: benefit (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (C) an advantage or profit gained from something
   - (C) a payment made by the state or an insurance company
   - (U) practical help or advantage
   
   **Synonyms**: advantage, profit, gain, plus, perk
   
   **Antonyms**: disadvantage, drawback, cost, detriment, harm
   
   **When to Use**: In employment contexts for additional perks; when discussing advantages; in government contexts for welfare payments; in formal and informal settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing disadvantages or costs; don't use when describing problems or issues
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - health benefits
   - employment benefits
   - benefit of the doubt
   - mutual benefit
   - fringe benefits
   
   **Root Analysis**: bene- (well, good) + -fit (from Latin facere "to do, make")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin benefactum → Old French bienfait → Middle English benefit (14th century); literally "good deed"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The job doesn't pay much, but the benefits are amazing.
   - Regular exercise has lots of health benefits.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The program provides substantial benefits to participating organizations.
   - Employees receive comprehensive benefits including health insurance and retirement plans.

5. **Noun**: total (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - the whole number or amount obtained by adding together several numbers or amounts
   - the final amount after everything has been counted or calculated
   
   **Synonyms**: sum, aggregate, whole, entirety, grand total
   
   **Antonyms**: portion, part, fraction, component, segment
   
   **When to Use**: In mathematical contexts for sums; in accounting and finance for final amounts; when referring to complete quantities; in formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to partial amounts or components; don't use when discussing individual items rather than combined amounts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - grand total
   - total amount
   - running total
   - total cost
   - in total
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin totalis from totus meaning "whole, entire"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin totalis → Old French total → Middle English (14th century); from "totus" meaning "whole"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the total for dinner? I'll split it with you.
   - In total, we raised over $5,000 for charity.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The total expenditure exceeded the allocated budget by fifteen percent.
   - Participants completed a total of 240 trials across four experimental conditions.

6. **Noun**: sum (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - a particular amount of money
   - the total obtained by adding together numbers or amounts
   - (mathematics) an arithmetic problem involving addition
   
   **Synonyms**: total, amount, aggregate, whole, quantity
   
   **Antonyms**: part, portion, fraction, component
   
   **When to Use**: In financial contexts for amounts of money; in mathematical contexts for addition problems; in formal writing for totals; in the phrase "in sum" meaning "in summary"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation when simpler words like "total" or "amount" work better; don't use when referring to individual components
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sum of money
   - large sum
   - in sum
   - sum total
   - tidy sum
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin summa meaning "highest, top, sum" from summus "highest"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin summa → Old French somme → Middle English sum (13th century); originally meant "highest point," later "total amount"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's a large sum of money to spend on a car!
   - Can you check my math? I'm not sure the sum is right.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investment required a substantial sum exceeding two million dollars.
   - In sum, the evidence supports the proposed hypothesis conclusively.
