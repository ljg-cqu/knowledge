1. **Functional Expression**: Plus, [additional reason/information] (function: adding supporting information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce an additional reason, fact, or piece of information that supports or reinforces the previous statement
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Also, Additionally, Furthermore, Moreover, And also, Besides
   
   **Antonyms**: However, But, On the other hand, Conversely (contrast markers)
   
   **When to Use**: In casual conversations to add supporting reasons; when building an argument informally; in everyday problem-solving discussions; when listing multiple considerations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal academic writing (use "furthermore" or "moreover"); in very formal presentations; in legal documents; when the additional information contrasts rather than supports
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Also, [information]
   - And, [information]
   - Besides, [information]
   - Additionally, [information] (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Plus" as an information-adding device is indirect and collaborative; it builds agreement by accumulating reasons; shows solidarity with interlocutor; face-saving by providing multiple justifications
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Accepting] "Yeah, that's true." / "Good point." / "You're right."
   - [Adding more] "And on top of that..." / "Plus..." / "Also..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "I don't want to go to the party." B: "Why not?" A: "I'm tired. Plus, I have work tomorrow."
   - "Let's order pizza—it's quick, plus everyone likes it."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The project timeline is unrealistic. Additionally, resource allocation remains insufficient." *(Note: "Plus" would be too informal here)*
   - "Market conditions are unfavorable. Furthermore, competitive pressures have intensified." *(Formal alternative to "plus")*

2. **Functional Expression**: [Price/Amount] plus [additional cost] (function: specifying additional charges)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that an additional amount (tax, shipping, fees) must be added to the stated price
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: [Price] excluding [cost], [Price] not including [cost], [Price] with additional [cost], [Price] + [cost]
   
   **Antonyms**: [Price] including [cost], [Price] with [cost] included, All-inclusive [price], Tax-included [price]
   
   **When to Use**: In retail and commercial contexts; when quoting prices; in transparent pricing communications; in contracts specifying additional costs; in e-commerce and service industries
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When offering all-inclusive pricing; when additional costs are negligible or built-in; in contexts where hidden fees would be problematic
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - plus tax
   - plus shipping and handling
   - plus applicable fees
   - excluding [cost]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Transparent pricing strategy; legally required in many jurisdictions to specify additional costs; manages customer expectations; reduces negative face threat by being upfront about total costs
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Clarifying] "So what's the total with tax?" / "How much is shipping?"
   - [Accepting] "Okay, that's fine." / "Got it."
   - [Negotiating] "Can you include shipping?" / "Is there any way to waive that fee?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "How much is the phone?" B: "It's $500 plus tax."
   - "The shirt is $25 plus shipping—still a good deal!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The consultation fee is $200 per hour plus applicable state taxes."
   - "Annual membership costs $1,000 plus a one-time enrollment fee of $150."

3. **Functional Expression**: That's a (real) plus. (function: expressing positive evaluation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that something is an advantage, benefit, or positive feature
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: That's an advantage, That's a benefit, That's a good thing, That's positive, That's helpful
   
   **Antonyms**: That's a drawback, That's a disadvantage, That's a problem, That's a minus, That's a downside
   
   **When to Use**: In evaluations and assessments; when discussing features or benefits; in job interviews discussing skills; in decision-making conversations; in informal to semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal written evaluations (use "advantage" or "benefit"); in contexts requiring detailed analysis rather than simple positive/negative assessment
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - That's a big plus.
   - That's definitely a plus.
   - That's a major plus.
   - That's a huge advantage. (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive face strategy—affirms value and highlights benefits; collaborative evaluation stance; can soften negative evaluations by acknowledging positive aspects; builds rapport through shared positive assessment
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Agreeing] "Definitely!" / "Absolutely." / "For sure."
   - [Expanding] "Yeah, and..." / "Plus..." / "It also means..."
   - [Qualifying] "Although..." / "But..." / "On the other hand..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "The apartment has a balcony." B: "Oh, that's a real plus!"
   - "You speak Spanish? That's a huge plus for this job."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Your experience in project management constitutes a significant advantage for this role."
   - "The system's compatibility with existing infrastructure represents a considerable benefit."

4. **Functional Expression**: On the plus side, [positive aspect] (function: presenting positive perspective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a positive aspect or advantage of a situation, especially when balancing negative aspects
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (softens by acknowledging positives)
   
   **Synonyms**: On the positive side, The good news is, Looking at the bright side, The advantage is, Positively speaking
   
   **Antonyms**: On the downside, On the negative side, The problem is, Unfortunately, The disadvantage is
   
   **When to Use**: When balancing positive and negative aspects; in problem-solving discussions; when trying to maintain optimism; in presentations showing balanced view; after discussing problems or challenges
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In purely positive contexts (unnecessary); when negatives far outweigh positives (may sound dismissive); in very formal academic writing (use "conversely" or "by contrast")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - On the positive side
   - Looking on the bright side
   - The silver lining is
   - Positively
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Face-saving strategy that acknowledges problems while maintaining optimism; collaborative problem-solving stance; balances criticism with recognition of benefits; manages morale by highlighting positives
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Agreeing] "True." / "That's right." / "Good point."
   - [Balancing] "But on the downside..." / "The problem is..." / "However..."
   - [Building on positive] "And also..." / "Plus..." / "Not to mention..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The commute is long, but on the plus side, the pay is great."
   - A: "This apartment is tiny." B: "True, but on the plus side, the rent is cheap!"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Implementation costs are substantial. However, positively speaking, long-term savings will be significant."
   - "The timeline presents challenges. Conversely, resource allocation has improved considerably."

5. **Functional Expression**: [Number] plus [Number] equals/is [Total] (function: performing or explaining calculation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to express addition in mathematics or to explain simple calculations
   - *Formality*: neutral (mathematical language)
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: [Number] and [Number] makes [Total], [Number] added to [Number] gives [Total], The sum of [Number] and [Number] is [Total]
   
   **Antonyms**: [Number] minus [Number] equals [Total], [Number] less [Number] is [Total], [Number] subtract [Number] leaves [Total]
   
   **When to Use**: In mathematical contexts; when teaching or explaining calculations; in technical discussions involving quantities; in everyday arithmetic; universal across formality levels
   
   **When NOT to Use**: N/A—standard mathematical expression appropriate in all contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - [Number] plus [Number] is [Total] (more casual)
   - [Number] and [Number] equals [Total]
   - [Number] add [Number] gives you [Total]
   - The sum of [Number] and [Number] equals [Total] (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Mathematically neutral language; universal understanding; no face threat; collaborative problem-solving in educational contexts; precision valued over indirectness
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Confirming] "Yes, that's correct." / "Right." / "Exactly."
   - [Questioning] "Wait, is that right?" / "Let me check." / "Are you sure?"
   - [Expanding] "So then..." / "Which means..." / "Therefore..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "So, 10 plus 15 is 25, right?"
   - A: "How much do I owe you?" B: "Well, $50 plus $20 is $70 total."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The sum of the initial investment plus accrued interest equals $125,000."
   - "When variable A plus variable B exceeds the threshold, the system activates."

6. **Functional Expression**: What are the pluses and minuses? / What are the pros and cons? (function: requesting balanced evaluation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to request a balanced assessment of advantages and disadvantages
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but collaborative
   
   **Synonyms**: What are the advantages and disadvantages?, What are the pros and cons?, What's good and bad about it?, What are the benefits and drawbacks?
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A (this is a request for balanced view, no opposite)
   
   **When to Use**: When seeking comprehensive evaluation; in decision-making discussions; during brainstorming sessions; in problem analysis; when comparing options
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When only positive or only negative feedback is sought; in contexts where balanced view isn't needed; in very formal academic contexts (use "advantages and disadvantages")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What are the pros and cons?
   - What are the advantages and disadvantages?
   - What's the upside and downside?
   - What are the positives and negatives?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Collaborative information-seeking; shows desire for balanced, objective assessment; face-neutral request; encourages comprehensive thinking; common in decision-making and problem-solving contexts
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Providing structure] "Well, on the plus side... but on the minus side..."
   - [Starting with positive] "The advantages are... The disadvantages are..."
   - [Balanced list] "Pros: ... Cons: ..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Should I take the job?" B: "What are the pluses and minuses?"
   - "Let's list the pros and cons before we decide."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Could you outline the advantages and disadvantages of each approach?"
   - "Please provide a comprehensive analysis of the benefits and drawbacks of this proposal."

7. **Functional Expression**: Plus, there's the fact that [important consideration] (function: emphasizing additional important point)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce an additional significant fact or consideration that strengthens the argument
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but layered
   
   **Synonyms**: Additionally, there's [fact], Also, [fact], Moreover, [fact], Furthermore, [fact], Not to mention [fact]
   
   **Antonyms**: However, [contrary fact], But, [contrary fact], Despite [fact]
   
   **When to Use**: When building a persuasive argument; when adding a particularly important consideration; in discussions where multiple factors matter; in informal to semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal academic writing (restructure or use formal connectors); when the additional point contradicts rather than supports; when already overwhelming with information
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Plus, don't forget that [fact]
   - And there's also the fact that [fact]
   - Not to mention [fact]
   - Additionally, [fact] (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Emphasizes importance of additional consideration; builds persuasive case by accumulation; collaborative argumentation; can signal that this point is particularly significant; face-saving by providing multiple justifications
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Agreeing] "That's true." / "You're right." / "Good point."
   - [Considering] "Hmm, I hadn't thought of that." / "That's worth considering."
   - [Countering] "But..." / "However..." / "On the other hand..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I don't think we should go. Plus, there's the fact that it's supposed to rain all day."
   - "The car is expensive, plus there's the fact that insurance will be high too."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The timeline is compressed. Moreover, resource constraints further complicate implementation."
   - "Initial costs are substantial. Additionally, one must consider ongoing maintenance expenses."
