1. **Original Expression**: We’re very close to the deadline.  
   **Context**: Team status update about project timing
   
   **Core Intent**: Warn the team that the deadline is near and signal that focused action is needed.
   
   **Casual**: The deadline’s super close—we really need to get a move on.  
   *Characteristics*: Contraction ("deadline’s"), intensifier ("super"), colloquial "get a move on", energetic tone  
   *Use when*: Talking with close colleagues or friends in a relaxed environment; informal team chats or group messages.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The deadline is very close, so we need to focus on completing the remaining tasks.  
   *Characteristics*: Standard grammar, neutral vocabulary ("very close", "focus"), clear call to action, professional tone  
   *Use when*: Internal emails, stand-up meetings, status reports to peers and immediate managers.
   
   **Formal**: As the deadline draws close, it is essential that all outstanding tasks be finalized promptly.  
   *Characteristics*: More formal verb choice ("draws close"), impersonal construction ("it is essential"), modal-like necessity, no contractions  
   *Use when*: Reporting to senior management, formal written updates, project documentation.
   
   **Humorous**: The deadline is creeping up way too close for comfort—time to switch to turbo mode.  
   *Characteristics*: Personification ("creeping up"), idiom ("too close for comfort"), playful metaphor ("turbo mode")  
   *Use when*: Lightening the mood with a familiar team, post-lunch pep talks, internal chat channels.  
   *Avoid when*: Communicating with clients, in high-stress crisis situations, or with very senior stakeholders.
   
   **Lively**: The deadline is really close—let’s lock in, power through, and finish strong.  
   *Characteristics*: High-energy verbs ("lock in", "power through"), motivational framing ("finish strong"), inclusive "let’s"  
   *Use when*: Rallying a team during the final push, stand-ups, short verbal updates.
   
   **Empathetic**: I know the deadline is very close and the pressure is high—let’s support each other so this last stretch feels manageable.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges feelings ("pressure is high"), inclusive language ("let’s"), focus on support and manageability  
   *Use when*: Team is tired or stressed, one-on-ones with team members, debriefs near the end of a demanding phase.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**: 
   - *Power Distance*: Use Semi-Formal or Formal with higher-level stakeholders; Casual/Lively with peers.
   - *Social Distance*: Casual is suitable for close colleagues; Semi-Formal for mixed groups or new team members.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Use Formal/Empathetic when the risk of missing the deadline is serious.
   - *Cultural Context*: Hierarchical or risk-averse cultures favor Semi-Formal/ Formal; flat or startup cultures accept more Casual/Lively.
   - *Medium*: Email and written status reports lean Semi-Formal or Formal; spoken stand-ups can be Casual, Lively, or Empathetic.
   
   **Common Mistakes**: 
   - Using overly humorous language when stakeholders are worried (seems out of touch).
   - Being too casual with senior leaders (undermines credibility).
   - Warning that the deadline is close without giving any sense of next steps.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some cultures, mentioning pressure openly (Empathetic variant) is appreciated; in others, the focus is expected to remain on solutions rather than emotions.
   
   **Context Examples**: 
   - **Situation 1**: Daily team stand-up → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively or Semi-Formal (motivating but focused).  
   - **Situation 2**: Email to senior sponsor → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal (signals seriousness and professionalism).  
   - **Situation 3**: Check-in with a stressed teammate → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic (acknowledges pressure and offers support).

1. **Original Expression**: This number is very close to the limit.  
   **Context**: Technical or risk report about a quantitative threshold
   
   **Core Intent**: Explain that a measured value is near a critical limit and could cross it soon.
   
   **Casual**: This number’s pretty close to the limit—we don’t have much wiggle room left.  
   *Characteristics*: Contraction ("number’s"), informal "pretty", idiom ("wiggle room"), relaxed but clear warning  
   *Use when*: Informal discussions within a technical team, whiteboard sessions, internal chats.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: This value is very close to the specified limit, so we should be cautious about further increases.  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral vocabulary ("value", "specified limit"), polite modal ("should"), explicit caution  
   *Use when*: Internal reports, stakeholder presentations, cross-functional meetings.
   
   **Formal**: The measured value lies close to the prescribed limit, indicating that any additional increase may lead to non-compliance.  
   *Characteristics*: Formal lexical choices ("lies close to", "prescribed", "non-compliance"), impersonal, precise  
   *Use when*: Regulatory reports, risk registers, formal documentation.
   
   **Humorous**: This number is flirting a little too close to the limit—we don’t want it crossing that line.  
   *Characteristics*: Metaphor ("flirting" with the limit), playful tone, mild personification  
   *Use when*: Internal team updates where relationships are comfortable and humor is acceptable.  
   *Avoid when*: Speaking to regulators, auditors, or risk-averse management.
   
   **Lively**: We’re really close to the limit here—one more step and we’re over the line.  
   *Characteristics*: Strong visual metaphor ("over the line"), dynamic framing, heightened sense of urgency  
   *Use when*: Rallies a team to correct course quickly, especially in spoken updates.
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand it’s difficult to hold this number down, but we’re already very close to the limit, so let’s explore options to reduce the pressure.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges difficulty, balances caution with support, collaborative tone  
   *Use when*: Discussing challenging constraints with teams or individuals responsible for the metric.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**: 
   - *Power Distance*: Formal for external authorities; Semi-Formal for internal management; Casual only with peers.
   - *Social Distance*: Use more neutral language when you don’t know the audience well.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: If crossing the limit has serious consequences, prefer Semi-Formal or Formal.
   - *Cultural Context*: Risk-sensitive cultures expect explicit, formal warnings; others may accept lighter framing internally.
   - *Medium*: Written risk logs require Semi-Formal/Formal; quick spoken warnings can be Casual/Lively.
   
   **Common Mistakes**: 
   - Downplaying serious risk with jokes.
   - Being vague about how close the number is ("kind of close" without context).
   - Using technical language with non-technical audiences without explanation.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some contexts, people expect numeric detail alongside "very close" (e.g., percentage of limit); in others, qualitative descriptions are acceptable.
   
   **Context Examples**: 
   - **Situation 1**: Slide in a board presentation → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal or Formal.  
   - **Situation 2**: Quick comment between engineers → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual or Lively.  
   - **Situation 3**: Coaching a team managing the metric → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic.

1. **Original Expression**: You’re very close to the right answer.  
   **Context**: Teacher or coach giving feedback during problem-solving
   
   **Core Intent**: Encourage someone by telling them their answer is nearly correct while prompting them to keep working.
   
   **Casual**: You’re super close—just tweak this part and you’ve got it.  
   *Characteristics*: Intensifier ("super"), casual verb ("tweak"), direct praise ("you’ve got it"), upbeat tone  
   *Use when*: Informal tutoring, peer help, relaxed classroom settings.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: You’re very close to the correct answer; review this step and you should get there.  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral vocabulary, specific guidance ("review this step"), supportive modal ("should"), clear next action  
   *Use when*: Classroom instruction, coaching sessions, written feedback to students.
   
   **Formal**: Your response is close to the correct solution and requires only minor adjustment to be fully accurate.  
   *Characteristics*: Formal nouns ("response", "solution"), impersonal tone, emphasis on "minor adjustment"  
   *Use when*: Written academic feedback, formal assessment comments.
   
   **Humorous**: You’re so close you can practically shake hands with the right answer—nudge it just a bit.  
   *Characteristics*: Playful exaggeration, vivid metaphor, light humor  
   *Use when*: Encouraging learners who respond well to humor and are not under high-stress assessment conditions.  
   *Avoid when*: In high-stakes exams or with learners who dislike playful feedback.
   
   **Lively**: That’s really close! One tiny change and you’ll nail it.  
   *Characteristics*: High-energy praise ("really close", "nail it"), "tiny change" lowers anxiety, future success framed as certain  
   *Use when*: Boosting confidence in interactive learning sessions.
   
   **Empathetic**: I can see you’ve put real thought into this—you’re very close, and with a small adjustment here, it will be completely correct.  
   *Characteristics*: Validates effort, gentle guidance, emphasis on small remaining gap  
   *Use when*: Supporting anxious or discouraged learners, one-on-one coaching.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**: 
   - *Power Distance*: Semi-Formal or Empathetic works well from teacher to student; Casual is suitable among peers.
   - *Social Distance*: Use more neutral language for unfamiliar learners; warmer tones for students you know well.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes contexts (exams, grading) favor Semi-Formal or Formal.
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more direct correction and less overt praise; others value strong encouragement.
   - *Medium*: Written feedback to keep → Semi-Formal or Formal; spoken in-class comments → Casual, Lively, or Empathetic.
   
   **Common Mistakes**: 
   - Saying "very close" when the answer is actually far off (confuses learners).
   - Using only praise without any indication of what needs to change.
   - Being overly formal in low-stakes, interactive activities (can feel cold or distant).
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some educational contexts, direct praise may feel unusual; framing it as "on the right track" can be more acceptable.
   
   **Context Examples**: 
   - **Situation 1**: In-class problem-solving → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively or Casual.  
   - **Situation 2**: Written feedback on an assignment → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal or Formal.  
   - **Situation 3**: One-on-one with a struggling student → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic.
