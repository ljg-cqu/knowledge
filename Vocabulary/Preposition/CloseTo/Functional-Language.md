1. **Functional Expression**: We’re very close to [goal/deadline] (function: status update/encouragement)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Communicates that a goal or deadline is almost reached and encourages continued effort.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (states progress rather than commanding action)
   
   **Synonyms**: We’re almost there, We’re nearly finished, We’re close to hitting [target], We’re on the verge of [goal]
   
   **Antonyms**: We’ve barely started, We’re nowhere near [goal], We still have a long way to go
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Motivating a team as they approach completion.
   - Updating stakeholders when work is nearly done.
   - Reassuring learners that their effort is paying off.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - When the goal is still far away (sounds dishonest and undermines trust).
   - When precise status is required (give numbers or specific milestones instead of a vague "very close").
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We’re so close to [goal].
   - We’re really close to finishing.
   - We’re very close to being ready.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Positive politeness: recognizes shared effort and builds solidarity.
   - Can soften pressure by focusing on progress instead of commands.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Great, let’s push through the last bit.
   - How close are we exactly? / What’s still missing?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re very close to wrapping this up—just two more slides.
   - We’re so close to our fundraising target; one more event should do it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We are very close to meeting the quarterly performance indicators.
   - The project is very close to completion, pending final sign-off from stakeholders.

1. **Functional Expression**: We’re a bit too close to [limit] for comfort (function: warning/raising concern)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely warns that a value or situation is very near a risk threshold and may soon become unacceptable.
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (frames concern as shared feeling rather than accusation)
   
   **Synonyms**: We’re dangerously close to [limit], We’re cutting it too close, We’re almost at the limit, This is very close to being a problem
   
   **Antonyms**: We’re safely within the limit, We’re well below the threshold, We’re nowhere near the maximum
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Flagging risk about time, budget, capacity, or safety limits.
   - Raising an issue gently in meetings or team discussions.
   - Suggesting that extra caution or margin is needed.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - In urgent emergencies where direct language is required ("We are over the limit" / "This is unsafe").
   - When you have not checked the actual figures—avoid vague alarmism.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We’re a little too close to [limit] for my liking.
   - We’re getting uncomfortably close to [limit].
   - This is too close to the edge for comfort.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Uses a shared, emotional frame ("for comfort") to reduce blame and invite joint problem-solving.
   - Works as a soft form of disagreement with current risk level.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - You’re right, let’s add more margin.
   - What can we adjust to move away from the limit?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re a bit too close to the deadline for comfort—I think we need to focus.
   - Those temperatures are getting too close to the red zone for comfort.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Current workload levels are uncomfortably close to the team’s sustainable capacity.
   - Our error rate is a bit too close to the contractual threshold for comfort and should be reduced.

1. **Functional Expression**: I’m not even close to [ready/finished] (function: managing expectations/refusing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Clearly states that something is far from completion, pushing back against unrealistic expectations.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I’m nowhere near done, I’m far from ready, I’ve barely started, I still have a long way to go
   
   **Antonyms**: I’m almost done, I’m very close to ready, I’m just about finished
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - When someone assumes you are nearly ready but you are not.
   - When you need to request more time or renegotiate a deadline.
   - In honest status updates where progress is still low.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - With senior stakeholders if you should have made more progress and need to show accountability and a plan instead.
   - When a more precise estimate ("about 30% done") would be more helpful.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I’m nowhere close to being done.
   - I’m not even close to ready.
   - We’re not close to the finish line yet.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Asserts boundaries and helps prevent unrealistic expectations.
   - Can sound defensive if overused without proposing next steps.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, what do you still need? / How much more time do you need?
   - Thanks for being honest—let’s adjust the plan.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you send it today? — Honestly, I’m not even close to finished.
   - I’m not even close to ready for that exam yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project is not yet close to completion; several key stages remain outstanding.
   - We are not close to meeting the specified benchmarks and must revise the schedule.

1. **Functional Expression**: You’re very close—keep going (function: feedback/encouragement)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Provides supportive feedback that someone’s answer or attempt is nearly correct and encourages further effort.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but positive
   
   **Synonyms**: You’re almost there, You’re very close to the right answer, You’re on the right track, That’s very close
   
   **Antonyms**: You’re way off, That’s not close, You’re going in the wrong direction
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Teaching, tutoring, and coaching situations.
   - Practice sessions, brainstorming, and problem-solving discussions.
   - Any context where you want to motivate someone who is nearly correct.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - When the attempt is actually far from correct (misleads the learner).
   - When the person is very frustrated and needs a clear answer instead of more attempts.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You’re really close—just check this part again.
   - That’s very close; think about [hint].
   - You’re close to the idea—try rephrasing it.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Combines correction with encouragement, protecting the learner’s confidence.
   - Often paired with a small hint to guide the next step.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, let me try again.
   - What am I missing? / Can you give me a hint?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You’re very close—just move that minus sign to the other side.
   - You’re close to the right word; think of a synonym for "near".
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Your interpretation is very close to the intended meaning; a minor adjustment will make it precise.
   - The proposed solution is close to optimal; a small parameter change should improve it further.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let’s stay close to [plan/limit/budget] (function: soft directive/constraint reminder)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Gently reminds a group to remain near an agreed plan, time, or limit without explicitly ordering them.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect directive
   
   **Synonyms**: Let’s stick close to [plan], Let’s keep near [budget], We should stay close to [schedule], Let’s avoid straying too far from [scope]
   
   **Antonyms**: Let’s move away from [plan], We can go well beyond [budget], We don’t need to follow [schedule] closely
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Chairing meetings where time or agenda is limited.
   - Managing projects with strict budgets or scope.
   - Encouraging discipline without sounding authoritarian.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - In crises where firm instructions are needed.
   - When creative divergence is intentionally encouraged (brainstorming with no constraints).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let’s try to stay close to the original plan.
   - Let’s keep as close to the budget as we can.
   - Let’s stay close to the agreed scope.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Uses inclusive "let’s" to reduce power distance and invite cooperation.
   - Frames constraint as a shared commitment rather than a top-down order.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreed, we’ll keep to the plan.
   - If we stay that close to the budget, we may need to cut [X]; is that acceptable?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let’s stay close to the schedule, or this meeting will drag on forever.
   - Can we stay close to the script this time and not improvise too much?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Let us stay close to the approved budget while evaluating additional requests.
   - During implementation, we should remain close to the documented specification to minimize risk.
