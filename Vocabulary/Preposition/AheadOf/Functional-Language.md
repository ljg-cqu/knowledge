1. **Functional Expression**: Look/Think ahead (function: advising/encouraging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Advise or encourage someone to plan for or consider future events rather than focusing only on the present
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Plan for the future, Consider what's coming, Prepare for what's next, Think forward
   
   **Antonyms**: Look back, Dwell on the past, Focus on now only, Live in the moment
   
   **When to Use**: Mentoring contexts, strategic planning discussions, encouraging proactive thinking, career guidance, life advice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When reflection on past is needed; when present-moment focus is more appropriate; can sound dismissive of current concerns
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Look ahead to the future
   - Think ahead about what might happen
   - Plan ahead for potential challenges
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies current focus may be too short-term; can be face-threatening if suggests person isn't already planning; soften with "we" for inclusivity
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "You're right, I should start planning for that."
   - Defensive: "I am thinking ahead, but I also need to deal with today."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "I'm just focused on getting through this semester." B: "I get that, but you should also think ahead about internships for next summer."
   - "Look ahead—where do you want to be in five years?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Strategic leaders must think ahead to anticipate market disruptions before they occur."
   - "We advise clients to look ahead and consider long-term implications of current decisions."

1. **Functional Expression**: Get ahead of [problem/situation] (function: suggesting action)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggest taking proactive action to address something before it becomes more serious or difficult
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Address this proactively, Deal with this now, Take preemptive action, Tackle this early
   
   **Antonyms**: Wait and see, React when it happens, Deal with it later, Let it play out
   
   **When to Use**: Problem-solving contexts, risk management, project management, preventing escalation, urging prompt action
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When waiting for more information is wiser; when action would be premature; avoid when you lack authority to direct action
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - We need to get ahead of this issue
   - Let's get ahead of potential problems
   - Try to get ahead of the situation before it worsens
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies current pace is insufficient; creates urgency; can be face-threatening if suggests others are being reactive; often paired with rationale to justify urgency
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Absolutely, what should we do first?"
   - Resistance: "I'm not sure we need to act this quickly."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Some customers are starting to complain about delivery times." B: "We need to get ahead of this before it becomes a PR nightmare."
   - "Let's get ahead of the holiday rush by ordering supplies now."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The committee recommends taking immediate action to get ahead of regulatory changes anticipated next quarter."
   - "Organizations must get ahead of cybersecurity threats through proactive infrastructure investment."

1. **Functional Expression**: Stay ahead of [competition/curve/game] (function: advising/encouraging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Advise maintaining a leading or advantageous position through continued effort and innovation
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (advice framed as wisdom)
   
   **Synonyms**: Maintain your advantage, Keep your competitive edge, Keep innovating, Don't fall behind
   
   **Antonyms**: Fall behind, Lose ground, Rest on your laurels, Get complacent
   
   **When to Use**: Business strategy, career advice, competitive contexts, encouraging continuous improvement, motivating ongoing effort
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When person/organization is already struggling (sounds insensitive); when rest and recovery are needed more than push forward
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Stay ahead of the competition
   - Stay ahead of the curve
   - Stay ahead of the game
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies constant vigilance needed; can create pressure; positive politeness through shared goal framing; motivational intent
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Definitely, we can't get complacent."
   - Exhaustion: "We're already working so hard though."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Our sales are good this quarter." B: "That's great, but we need to keep innovating to stay ahead of the competition."
   - "If you want to stay ahead of the curve in tech, you need to keep learning new skills."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "To stay ahead of the curve, organizations must invest continuously in research and development."
   - "The firm's strategy focuses on staying ahead of regulatory changes through proactive compliance monitoring."

1. **Functional Expression**: Plan ahead (function: advising/recommending)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Recommend making arrangements or preparations before they're urgently needed
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (advice not command)
   
   **Synonyms**: Prepare in advance, Make arrangements early, Organize beforehand, Think ahead and prepare
   
   **Antonyms**: Wing it, Play it by ear, Deal with things as they come, Improvise
   
   **When to Use**: Travel planning, event organization, project management, financial advice, general life guidance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When spontaneity is valued; when over-planning causes anxiety; when flexibility is more important than preparation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You should plan ahead for [specific event]
   - It's important to plan ahead
   - Planning ahead will save you trouble later
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Mild face-threat implies recipient may not be planning sufficiently; often framed as general wisdom rather than personal criticism; positive politeness through concern framing
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "You're right, I should start planning now."
   - Resistance: "I prefer to be flexible and see what happens."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "I'm thinking about going to Europe next summer." B: "You should definitely plan ahead—flights get expensive if you wait."
   - "Plan ahead and bring snacks for the kids on the road trip."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Clients are advised to plan ahead for retirement by establishing diversified investment portfolios early."
   - "Effective project management requires planning ahead to identify and mitigate potential risks."

1. **Functional Expression**: [X] is ahead of schedule/time (function: informing/praising)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Report that something is progressing faster than planned or expected; often implies positive performance
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (factual statement)
   
   **Synonyms**: [X] is early, [X] is progressing faster than expected, [X] is before deadline, [X] is ahead of plan
   
   **Antonyms**: [X] is behind schedule, [X] is delayed, [X] is running late, [X] is overdue
   
   **When to Use**: Progress reports, status updates, performance reviews, project management, celebrating good progress
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When earliness is problematic (premature action); when trying to maintain pressure (announcing early completion may reduce urgency)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - The project is ahead of schedule
   - We're ahead of time on delivery
   - Everything is ahead of the timeline
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Typically carries positive evaluation; often followed by explanation or next steps; can be used to request resources or recognition; sets positive expectations
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Positive: "Excellent work! Keep it up."
   - Cautionary: "Good, but let's make sure quality isn't compromised."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "How's the website redesign going?" B: "Great! We're actually ahead of schedule by about two weeks."
   - "Good news—the contractors are ahead of schedule on the renovation."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The implementation phase is proceeding ahead of schedule, with completion now anticipated in Q2."
   - "I am pleased to report that the research project is ahead of the planned timeline, enabling early dissemination of findings."

1. **Functional Expression**: Let me know ahead of time (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request advance notification or warning before something happens
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite request using "let me know")
   
   **Synonyms**: Please inform me in advance, Give me advance notice, Tell me beforehand, Notify me early
   
   **Antonyms**: Just show up, No need to tell me beforehand, Surprise me, Let me know at the last minute
   
   **When to Use**: Scheduling, event planning, managing expectations, requesting consideration, workplace coordination
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When spontaneity is the point; when advance notice is already standard practice (sounds redundant)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let me know ahead of time if you need anything
   - Please let me know ahead of time if you can't make it
   - I'd appreciate if you could let me know ahead of time
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Softens request through indirect phrasing; "let me" is more polite than "tell me"; implies speaker needs time to prepare; negative politeness (minimizing imposition)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Of course, I'll make sure to give you notice."
   - Hedging: "I'll try, but sometimes things come up last minute."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Can I bring my kids to your party?" B: "Sure, just let me know ahead of time so I can plan for them."
   - "If you're going to be late, just let me know ahead of time, okay?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Participants are requested to notify the coordinator ahead of time if they require special accommodations."
   - "Please inform the committee ahead of time should you need to reschedule your presentation."

1. **Functional Expression**: [Person] is ahead of [their] time (function: praising/evaluating)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Praise someone's ideas or work as too advanced for contemporary understanding or appreciation
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (praise through historical framing)
   
   **Synonyms**: [Person] is visionary, [Person] is innovative, [Person's] ideas are revolutionary, [Person] is forward-thinking
   
   **Antonyms**: [Person] is behind the times, [Person] is old-fashioned, [Person's] ideas are outdated, [Person] is conventional
   
   **When to Use**: Praising innovators, historical assessment, recognizing unappreciated genius, discussing pioneering work
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound patronizing if person's work is being dismissed by others (validates dismissal); when contemporaneous appreciation exists
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Her ideas were ahead of their time
   - He was ahead of his time as a [role]
   - That work was ahead of its time
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often retrospective assessment; can imply current lack of recognition; double-edged (praising but noting lack of acceptance); validates both innovation and non-acceptance
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "Absolutely, people didn't appreciate their brilliance then."
   - Qualification: "Maybe, or maybe they just missed the mark for their audience."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Nobody liked that artist's work back then." B: "Yeah, they were totally ahead of their time."
   - "That inventor was way ahead of their time—the technology didn't exist yet to make it work."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Tesla's innovations were decades ahead of their time, anticipating wireless communication technologies."
   - "The philosopher's theories were ahead of their time, gaining recognition only in subsequent intellectual movements."

1. **Functional Expression**: To get/move ahead (function: expressing goals)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Express desire or plan to advance in position, career, or competitive standing
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To advance, To progress, To improve one's position, To climb the ladder, To succeed
   
   **Antonyms**: To fall behind, To stagnate, To stay in place, To lose ground
   
   **When to Use**: Career discussions, competitive contexts, expressing ambition, motivational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly ambitious or selfish in collaborative contexts; some cultures view explicit advancement talk as inappropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I want to get ahead in my career
   - How can I move ahead?
   - He's always trying to get ahead
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Can carry positive (ambitious) or negative (ruthlessly competitive) connotations depending on context; individualistic orientation; may threaten face in collectivist cultures
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Supportive: "What do you think you need to do to get there?"
   - Cautionary: "Just make sure you don't step on people along the way."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Why are you taking all these extra courses?" B: "I'm trying to get ahead in my field."
   - "If you want to move ahead, you need to take on more responsibility."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Professional development opportunities enable employees to advance in their careers."
   - "The mentorship program assists junior staff in developing skills necessary to progress within the organization."

1. **Functional Expression**: Go ahead (function: giving permission)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Grant permission for someone to proceed with an action; authorize or encourage continuation
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Please proceed, Go right ahead, Feel free, You may, Please do
   
   **Antonyms**: Wait, Hold on, Stop, Don't, You may not
   
   **When to Use**: Granting permission, encouraging action, responding to requests, facilitating turn-taking
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal contexts where "please proceed" is more appropriate; when explicit authorization language is needed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Go ahead and [action]
   - Go right ahead
   - Sure, go ahead
   - Yes, go ahead
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Very common; can express enthusiasm (with stress) or neutrality; often paired with additional encouragement or information; positive politeness strategy
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acceptance: "Thank you!" [proceeds with action]
   - Confirmation: "Are you sure? I don't want to interrupt."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Can I use your phone?" B: "Yeah, go ahead."
   - A: "Should I start the presentation?" B: "Go ahead, everyone's here."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A: "May I present the quarterly results now?" B: "Yes, please go ahead."
   - "If there are no further questions, we may go ahead with the vote."

1. **Functional Expression**: If we plan ahead... / If we think ahead... (function: conditional suggestion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Present proactive planning as conditional for achieving positive outcomes; suggest benefits of advance preparation
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (conditional framing softens directive)
   
   **Synonyms**: If we prepare in advance..., If we anticipate..., If we look forward..., If we're proactive...
   
   **Antonyms**: If we wait until the last minute..., If we react after the fact..., If we don't prepare...
   
   **When to Use**: Persuading others to plan, presenting benefits of preparation, strategic discussions, encouraging foresight
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When time for planning has passed; when spontaneity is valued; when over-planning causes paralysis
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - If we plan ahead, we can avoid problems
   - If we think ahead about this, we'll be better prepared
   - Had we planned ahead, this wouldn't have happened
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conditional framing reduces face-threat; implies causal relationship between planning and positive outcomes; can be used retrospectively to critique lack of planning; persuasive function
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: "You're absolutely right, let's start planning now."
   - Resistance: "But we can't predict everything that might happen."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "If we plan ahead and book now, we can save like 40% on flights."
   - "If we'd thought ahead about parking, we wouldn't be circling the block right now."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "If we plan ahead strategically, we can position the organization to capitalize on emerging opportunities."
   - "Had stakeholders thought ahead about implementation challenges, many current obstacles could have been anticipated."
