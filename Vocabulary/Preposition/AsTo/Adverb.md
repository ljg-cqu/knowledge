1. **Adverb**: still (type: time/continuative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Continuing to happen or be the case; up to and including the present or the time mentioned; even now or then (mid-position or front-position)
   
   **Synonyms**: yet, even now, up to now, as yet, to this day
   
   **Antonyms**: no longer, not anymore, already (in some contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: To emphasize that a state or situation continues, especially when expressing ongoing uncertainty; commonly appears with "uncertain as to" or "unclear as to"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When a situation has definitively ended; avoid in contexts requiring "already" or "yet" in negative sentences
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - still uncertain
   - still unclear
   - still debating
   - still remain
   - still questioning
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English stille (motionless, fixed)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English stille (stationary) → Middle English (continuing in time) → modern temporal meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm still not sure as to what we should do.
   - They're still arguing as to who's right.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is still deliberating as to the appropriate course of action.
   - Researchers remain still uncertain as to the underlying mechanism.

1. **Adverb**: particularly (type: degree/emphasis)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a higher degree than is usual or average; especially; in particular; used to single out one person or thing over others (mid-position or front-position)
   
   **Synonyms**: especially, notably, specifically, in particular, above all
   
   **Antonyms**: generally, broadly, ordinarily
   
   **When to Use**: To emphasize specific aspects when expressing questions or uncertainties; adds precision to formal discourse; often appears before "as to" constructions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When making general statements; avoid overuse which can weaken emphasis
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - particularly uncertain
   - particularly unclear
   - particularly concerned
   - particularly interested
   - particularly important
   
   **Root Analysis**: particular + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin particularis (of a part) → particular → particularly (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm particularly confused as to why she left so early.
   - Nobody seems particularly sure as to what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings are particularly ambiguous as to the direction of causation.
   - Stakeholders are particularly concerned as to the timeline for implementation.

1. **Adverb**: completely (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In every way or as much as possible; totally; entirely; to the fullest degree (mid-position, can modify adjectives or verbs)
   
   **Synonyms**: totally, entirely, wholly, fully, utterly
   
   **Antonyms**: partly, partially, somewhat, incompletely
   
   **When to Use**: To emphasize the total or absolute nature of a state, especially uncertainty or lack of clarity; intensifies adjectives like "unclear" or "uncertain"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When partial degrees exist; avoid overuse which can sound hyperbolic
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - completely uncertain
   - completely unclear
   - completely confused
   - completely unsure
   - completely unaware
   
   **Root Analysis**: complete + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin completus (filled up) → complete → completely (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm completely lost as to what you're talking about.
   - We're completely in the dark as to when this will happen.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulatory framework is completely silent as to the enforcement mechanisms.
   - Participants were completely unaware as to the true purpose of the study.

   **Synonyms**: presently, now, at present, at the moment, right now
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, formerly, in the past, then
   
   **When to Use**: To specify that a state of uncertainty or questioning exists at the present moment; common in formal reports and updates
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to past or future states; avoid in timeless statements
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - currently uncertain
   - currently unclear
   - currently debating
   - currently under review
   - currently investigating
   
   **Root Analysis**: current + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin currens (running, flowing) → current → currently (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're currently not sure as to when the package will arrive.
   - I'm currently debating as to whether I should apply.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution is currently evaluating as to whether the program should continue.

1. **Adverb**: increasingly (type: degree/manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - More and more; to a progressively greater degree; in a way that becomes greater in amount, extent, or intensity over time (typically mid-position or modifying adjective)
   
   **Synonyms**: progressively, more and more, ever more, gradually more
   
   **Antonyms**: decreasingly, less and less, diminishingly
   
   **When to Use**: To indicate growing doubt, confusion, or questioning over time; emphasizes development of a state; common in analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing sudden changes; when no progression is involved
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - increasingly uncertain
   - increasingly unclear
   - increasingly skeptical
   - increasingly concerned
   - increasingly difficult
   
   **Root Analysis**: increase + -ing + -ly
   
   **Etymology**: Latin increscere (to grow) → increase → increasingly (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm getting increasingly confused as to what the plan actually is.
   - People are increasingly skeptical as to whether this will work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Scholars are increasingly divided as to the interpretation of these findings.
   - Investors have become increasingly uncertain as to the company's long-term viability.

1. **Adverb**: wholly (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Entirely; fully; completely; to the full or entire extent (mid-position or modifying adjective)
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, entirely, totally, fully, altogether
   
   **Antonyms**: partly, partially, incompletely, somewhat
   
   **When to Use**: In formal writing to emphasize completeness; often appears in negative constructions with "unclear" or "uncertain"; more formal than "completely"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where "completely" or "totally" is more natural; when partial states exist
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - not wholly clear
   - wholly inadequate
   - wholly dependent
   - wholly unacceptable
   - wholly unexpected
   
   **Root Analysis**: whole + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English hal (whole, healthy) → whole → wholly (Middle English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not wholly convinced as to the truth of that story.
   - The situation isn't wholly clear yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The evidence is not wholly conclusive as to the direction of causality.
   - The legal framework remains wholly ambiguous as to the scope of liability.

1. **Adverb**: precisely (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In exact terms or detail; accurately; exactly; used to emphasize accuracy or to ask for exact information (mid-position or front-position)
   
   **Synonyms**: exactly, accurately, specifically, just, right
   
   **Antonyms**: vaguely, approximately, roughly, loosely
   
   **When to Use**: When requesting or providing exact information; often appears in questions with "as to" seeking specific details; signals demand for precision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When approximation is acceptable or preferred; in casual contexts where it might sound pedantic
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - precisely what
   - precisely how
   - precisely when
   - more precisely
   - not precisely
   
   **Root Analysis**: precise + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praecisus (cut short) → precise → precisely (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to know precisely as to when you'll arrive.
   - Can you tell me precisely what you mean?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The statute does not specify precisely as to which entities are subject to regulation.
   - Researchers must determine precisely as to how the intervention produces its effects.

1. **Adverb**: apparently (type: sentence/evidential)
   **Meaning**: 
   - According to what appears or has been reported; seemingly; used to indicate that something appears to be true based on evidence but may not be certain (typically front-position or mid-position)
   
   **Synonyms**: seemingly, evidently, ostensibly, reportedly, supposedly
   
   **Antonyms**: certainly, definitely, clearly, actually
   
   **When to Use**: To express tentative conclusions or report information with some uncertainty; hedges claims; common in academic and journalistic writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When absolute certainty is claimed; when evidence is conclusive
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - apparently unclear
   - apparently not
   - apparently confused
   - quite apparently
   - most apparently
   
   **Root Analysis**: apparent + -ly (manner suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin apparere (to appear) → apparent → apparently (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Apparently there's some confusion as to what time we're meeting.
   - She's apparently uncertain as to whether she'll come.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data are apparently inconsistent as to the temporal sequence of events.
   - Officials are apparently divided as to the appropriate regulatory response.
