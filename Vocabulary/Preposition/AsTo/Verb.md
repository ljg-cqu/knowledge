1. **Verb**: inquire (also enquire in British English)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To ask for information from someone; to make an official investigation into something (transitive and intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: ask, query, investigate, question, probe
   
   **Antonyms**: answer, respond, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: Formal verb frequently used with "as to" when asking for information or conducting investigations; "inquire as to" is a formal collocation common in business, legal, and academic contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where "ask about" is more natural; avoid when simple "ask" suffices
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inquire as to whether/how/why
   - inquire about
   - inquire into
   - inquire of someone
   - fail to inquire
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + quaer (seek, ask)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inquirere (to seek for, search after) → Old French enquerre → English inquire (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I called to inquire as to when you'd be arriving.
   - Did anyone inquire as to the price?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee inquired as to the methodology employed in the research.
   - Authorities have yet to inquire as to the circumstances surrounding the incident.

1. **Verb**: debate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To argue about or discuss a subject, especially in a formal manner; to consider a question or issue carefully before making a decision (transitive and intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: discuss, argue, dispute, deliberate, consider
   
   **Antonyms**: agree, accept, concur
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "as to" to introduce the subject of discussion or disagreement; "debate as to" is formal and common in academic and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing violent arguments; in casual contexts where "argue about" or "discuss" is clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - debate as to whether/how
   - debate the issue
   - hotly debated
   - continue to debate
   - openly debate
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (down) + batt (beat)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French debatre (to fight, contend) → debate (13th century), originally meaning "to fight"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're still debating as to where we should eat.
   - They debated as to which movie to watch.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Scholars continue to debate as to the interpretation of these historical events.
   - The committee debated extensively as to the appropriate allocation of resources.

1. **Verb**: wonder
   **Meaning**: 
   - To think about something and try to decide what is true, what will happen, what you should do; to feel curious about something; to be surprised (transitive and intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: question, ponder, speculate, contemplate, muse
   
   **Antonyms**: know, understand, be certain
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "as to" to express curiosity or uncertainty about something; "wonder as to" is somewhat formal; common in both spoken and written English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When certainty exists; when "wonder" is used in the sense of "marvel at" (different meaning)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wonder as to whether/how/why
   - wonder about
   - wonder if
   - can't help but wonder
   - left wondering
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English wundor (marvelous thing)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wundrian (to be affected with wonder) → wonder (12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I wonder as to why she left so early.
   - We're all wondering as to what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - One might wonder as to the validity of such assumptions.
   - Researchers wonder as to the generalizability of these findings.

1. **Verb**: speculate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To form a theory or conjecture about something without firm evidence; to make guesses about future events or outcomes (intransitive, used with "about," "on," or "as to")
   
   **Synonyms**: conjecture, theorize, hypothesize, guess, surmise
   
   **Antonyms**: know, ascertain, verify, confirm
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "as to" in formal contexts when discussing theories or guesses about uncertain matters; common in journalism, academia, and analytical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When dealing with proven facts; when certainty is being expressed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - speculate as to
   - speculate about
   - refuse to speculate
   - continue to speculate
   - widely speculated
   
   **Root Analysis**: specul (observe, watch) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin speculari (to spy out, observe) → speculate (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - People are speculating as to who the new manager will be.
   - I can only speculate as to what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Analysts continue to speculate as to the long-term economic implications.
   - It would be premature to speculate as to the causes of the phenomenon.

1. **Verb**: determine
   **Meaning**: 
   - To discover the facts or truth about something; to decide or settle something conclusively; to cause something to occur in a particular way or have a particular nature (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: establish, ascertain, decide, conclude, resolve
   
   **Antonyms**: question, doubt, wonder, speculate
   
   **When to Use**: Formal verb used with "as to" when discussing the process of reaching conclusions or making decisions; "determine as to" is very formal, sometimes considered overly formal; common in legal, scientific, and administrative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple "decide" or "find out" would be clearer; some style guides discourage "determine as to" as redundant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - determine as to whether/how
   - determine the cause
   - difficult to determine
   - yet to be determined
   - help determine
   
   **Root Analysis**: de- (completely) + termin (boundary, end)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin determinare (to set bounds to) → Old French determiner → English determine (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to determine as to whether this is worth the cost.
   - It's hard to determine as to what really happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court must determine as to whether the evidence is admissible.
   - The investigation aims to determine as to the root causes of the failure.

1. **Verb**: clarify
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make a statement or situation less confused and more comprehensible; to explain or provide more information about something (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: explain, elucidate, illuminate, clear up, resolve
   
   **Antonyms**: confuse, obscure, complicate, muddle
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "as to" in formal contexts when seeking or providing clearer understanding; common in professional and academic communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the matter is already clear; in very casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - clarify as to
   - need to clarify
   - help clarify
   - fail to clarify
   - attempt to clarify
   
   **Root Analysis**: clar (clear) + -ify (make)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin clarus (clear) → clarify (14th century) from clarificare
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you clarify as to what you mean by that?
   - I need to clarify as to when we're meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The ministry issued a statement to clarify as to the scope of the new regulations.
   - The author clarifies as to the theoretical framework employed in the analysis.

1. **Verb**: question
   **Meaning**: 
   - To ask someone questions about something; to express doubt about something; to challenge the truth or validity of something (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: ask, interrogate, query, doubt, challenge
   
   **Antonyms**: answer, accept, believe, trust
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "as to" when expressing doubt or asking about specific matters; "question as to" is formal; can mean both "ask questions" and "express doubt"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple "ask" would be clearer; when unambiguous support or belief is being expressed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - question as to whether/how
   - question the validity
   - call into question
   - without question
   - open to question
   
   **Root Analysis**: quest (seek) + -ion (noun) → question (verb from noun)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin quaestio → question (noun 13th century) → question (verb 15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm questioning as to whether this is a good idea.
   - She questioned him as to where he'd been.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The audit questioned as to the adequacy of internal controls.
   - Several participants questioned as to the ethical implications of the procedure.

1. **Verb**: disagree
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have or express a different opinion; to fail to agree about something; to be inconsistent or at variance (intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: differ, dispute, dissent, object, contradict
   
   **Antonyms**: agree, concur, consent, accord
   
   **When to Use**: Used with "as to" to specify what the disagreement concerns; "disagree as to" is formal; common in academic and professional discourse
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing physical incompatibility; in contexts requiring stronger opposition language
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disagree as to whether/how
   - disagree with
   - disagree on
   - strongly disagree
   - tend to disagree
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (not, opposite) + agree
   
   **Etymology**: Old French agreer → agree → disagree with negative prefix (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We disagree as to which approach is better.
   - They always disagree as to what movie to watch.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Scholars disagree as to the interpretation of the archaeological evidence.
   - The committee members disagreed as to the appropriate course of action.

1. **Verb**: advise
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give guidance or recommendations to someone about what should be done; to formally inform someone about something (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: counsel, recommend, guide, suggest, inform
   
   **Antonyms**: mislead, misinform, dissuade
   
   **When to Use**: Formal verb used with "as to" when providing guidance or information about specific matters; "advise as to" is very formal; common in legal and business contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where "tell" or "suggest" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advise as to
   - advise against
   - advise on
   - ill-advised
   - strongly advise
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + vis (see)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin advisare (to consider) → Old French aviser → English advise (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you advise as to what I should do?
   - The doctor advised me as to the best treatment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Legal counsel advised the board as to their fiduciary responsibilities.
   - The consultant will advise as to the most appropriate implementation strategy.

1. **Verb**: inform
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give someone facts or information; to tell someone about something formally or officially (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: tell, notify, advise, apprise, brief
   
   **Antonyms**: conceal, withhold, mislead, deceive
   
   **When to Use**: Formal verb used with "as to" when providing information about specific matters; "inform as to" is very formal; common in official communications
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual contexts where "tell" is clearer; when the communication is unofficial
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inform as to
   - inform of
   - inform about
   - keep informed
   - well-informed
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + form (shape)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin informare (to shape, form an idea of) → inform (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Please inform me as to when you'll arrive.
   - They informed us as to the change in schedule.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agency will inform stakeholders as to the timeline for implementation.
   - Participants were informed as to their rights under the research protocol.
