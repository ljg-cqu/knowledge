1. **Functional Expression**: Speak up (function: encouraging expression/increasing volume)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Request to talk louder or make oneself heard
   - *(Sense 2)* Encouragement to express one's opinion or contribute to discussion
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Talk louder, Raise your voice (sense 1), Voice your opinion, Share your thoughts (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: Be quiet, Stay silent, Keep your opinion to yourself
   
   **When to Use**: When someone can't be heard (sense 1); encouraging participation in discussions (sense 2); appropriate in classroom, meeting, or supportive contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound demanding if tone is harsh; "speak up" to authority requires careful, polite framing; in some cultures, speaking up is discouraged
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Please speak up
   - Don't be afraid to speak up
   - Feel free to speak up
   - You should speak up more
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Dual meaning requires context; encouraging participation shows inclusive leadership; requesting volume increase should be polite; can be empowering or demanding depending on delivery
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Compliance: [speaks louder or shares opinion]
   - Resistance: "I don't have anything to add" / "I'm fine, thanks"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Speak up! I can't hear you back here.
   - If you disagree, you should speak up about it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please feel free to speak up if you have questions or concerns.
   - Would you mind speaking up slightly? The acoustics are challenging.

1. **Functional Expression**: Cheer up (function: encouraging/comforting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Directive or encouragement to become happier or less sad; attempt to improve someone's mood
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct (can be perceived as dismissive)
   
   **Synonyms**: Feel better, Don't be sad, Brighten up, Smile
   
   **Antonyms**: Give up, Stay sad, Wallow
   
   **When to Use**: Comforting friends, family, or close colleagues; appropriate when relationship allows emotional support; works best when followed by supportive action
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can invalidate serious emotions; avoid if person has legitimate grief or depression; don't use alone without offering real support; inappropriate in professional contexts; can sound dismissive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Cheer up! (standalone)
   - You need to cheer up
   - Come on, cheer up
   - Try to cheer up
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: High face-threat potential if used insensitively; more effective with young children or minor disappointments; better paired with specific help ("Let's cheer you up—ice cream?"); can trivialize serious emotions
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Positive: "You're right, I'm being silly" / "Thanks, I needed that"
   - Negative: "I can't just cheer up" / "You don't understand"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Cheer up! It's not the end of the world.
   - Let me buy you lunch to help you cheer up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Generally inappropriate for formal contexts; use supportive professional language instead)
   - We hope these support resources will help improve team morale.

1. **Functional Expression**: What's up? (function: greeting/inquiry)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Casual greeting asking how someone is or what they're doing
   - *Formality*: very informal (slang)
   - *Directness*: direct but casual
   
   **Synonyms**: How are you?, What's going on?, How's it going?, Hey
   
   **Antonyms**: (No direct antonym; formal equivalents: How do you do?, Good day)
   
   **When to Use**: Greeting friends, peers, casual acquaintances; very informal social contexts; establishing casual rapport
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Inappropriate for professional contexts, formal settings, authority figures, or cross-cultural contexts where it may not be understood; never in business correspondence
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What's up?
   - Sup? (extremely casual)
   - What's up with you?
   - What's up, man/dude? (gender-specific, very casual)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: American slang greeting; often used without expecting detailed answer—"not much" is common response; establishes casual, friendly tone; age-dependent—younger generations more comfortable; can be answered or returned as greeting
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Casual: "Not much, you?" / "Same old, same old" / "What's up?"
   - Genuine: "Actually, I wanted to talk to you about..."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hey! What's up? Long time no see!
   - What's up? Want to grab lunch?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Not appropriate for formal contexts)
   - Good morning. How are you today?

1. **Functional Expression**: Keep up the good work (function: praising/encouraging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Encouragement to continue performing well; recognition of current positive efforts
   - *Formality*: neutral to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Continue the excellent work, Keep it up, Well done—continue, Maintain this standard
   
   **Antonyms**: Stop slacking, Improve your performance
   
   **When to Use**: Providing positive feedback; encouraging continued effort; appropriate in workplace, educational, or mentoring contexts; shows appreciation and motivates
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound patronizing if overused or insincere; ensure praise is specific and earned; may ring hollow if not accompanied by tangible recognition; be careful not to use only when you want more from someone
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Keep up the great work
   - Keep it up!
   - Keep up the excellent work
   - Continue the good work
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive feedback that motivates continued effort; most effective when specific about what's being praised; builds morale and reinforces desired behaviors; can be used by superiors, peers, or mentors
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acceptance: "Thank you!" / "I appreciate that" / "Will do!"
   - Humble: "Thanks, just doing my best"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Nice job on that presentation! Keep up the good work.
   - You're really improving—keep it up!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Your contributions have been exemplary. Please keep up the excellent work.
   - The committee commends your efforts and encourages you to maintain this standard.

1. **Functional Expression**: It's up to you (function: deferring decision/assigning responsibility)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Statement that the decision, choice, or action is the other person's responsibility
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (appears to give choice but can imply burden)
   
   **Synonyms**: Your choice, Your decision, Your call, You decide
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll decide, Let me choose, I'm taking charge
   
   **When to Use**: Empowering someone to make decisions; deferring to someone's preference; appropriate when genuinely giving choice; respecting autonomy
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be passive-aggressive if used to avoid responsibility; inappropriate when speaker should make decision; may transfer unwanted pressure; can sound unsupportive if person needs guidance
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - That's up to you
   - It's entirely up to you
   - The decision is up to you
   - Whatever you think is best—it's up to you
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Transfers decision authority; can be empowering or abandoning depending on context; most positive when person has expertise or preference; can be evasive if speaker should provide input; tone matters—supportive vs. indifferent
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acceptance: "Okay, I'll think about it" / "Great, I prefer..."
   - Seeking input: "What do you think?" / "I'd like your opinion too"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Where should we eat? It's up to you—I'm flexible.
   - Do you want to go? It's totally up to you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The final decision regarding implementation strategy is up to your department.
   - Whether to proceed is ultimately up to the board of directors.

1. **Functional Expression**: Heads up (function: warning/alerting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Alert or warning to pay attention; advance notice of something coming
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Watch out, Be careful, FYI, Just so you know, Alert
   
   **Antonyms**: (No direct antonym; opposite would be failing to warn)
   
   **When to Use**: Giving friendly warning or advance notice; appropriate among colleagues, friends, or in casual professional contexts; shows consideration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too casual for formal warnings or critical safety alerts; legal or compliance contexts need formal language; may not be understood internationally
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Heads up! (as interjection)
   - Just a heads up
   - Heads up on this
   - Giving you a heads up
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Friendly, considerate warning; establishes speaker as helpful and aware; originally sports term (watch out for ball); now general alert; shows social awareness and helpfulness
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Appreciation: "Thanks for the heads up!" / "Good to know"
   - Acknowledgment: "Got it" / "Noted"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Heads up—the boss is in a bad mood today.
   - Just a heads up: that report is due Friday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please be advised that the deadline has been moved forward.
   - This is to inform you of upcoming schedule changes.

1. **Functional Expression**: Catch up (function: reconnecting/equalizing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Proposal to meet and talk to share recent news and reconnect
   - *(Sense 2)* To reach the same level or position as others who are ahead
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (suggestion rather than directive)
   
   **Synonyms**: Reconnect, Get together, Meet up (sense 1), Reach the same level, Get up to speed (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: Avoid, Stay apart (sense 1), Fall behind (sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Suggesting social meetings with friends or colleagues; discussing progress or learning (sense 2); appropriate in friendly or collegial contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Vague for business meetings—be specific about purpose and time; "catch up" implies informal chat, not formal meeting
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's catch up soon
   - We should catch up
   - Catch up with you
   - Need to catch up on work
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Friendly, low-pressure social suggestion; implies informal conversation over formal meeting; can be vague on timing (sometimes intentionally); work context (sense 2) implies being behind and needing to reach current level
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acceptance: "Yes! Let's find a time" / "I'd love to"
   - Vague agreement: "Sure, let's do that sometime"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's been too long! We should catch up over coffee.
   - I need to catch up on all the episodes I missed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I suggest we schedule time to discuss recent developments. (More specific than "catch up")
   - New employees will receive training to ensure they're current with protocols.

1. **Functional Expression**: Give up (function: surrendering/quitting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Encouragement or admission to stop trying; to surrender or quit an effort
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Quit, Surrender, Stop trying, Abandon effort
   
   **Antonyms**: Keep trying, Persist, Continue, Don't give up
   
   **When to Use**: Admitting defeat; advising someone to stop futile efforts; appropriate in varied contexts but often negative connotation; sometimes used in encouragement ("Don't give up!")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Give up" is usually demotivating; use cautiously; "Don't give up" is much more common as encouragement; can sound defeatist or insensitive
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Don't give up! (encouraging persistence)
   - I give up (admitting defeat)
   - Give up on something/someone
   - Never give up
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Usually used in negative form ("don't give up") as encouragement; positive form ("give up") admits defeat; can be realistic advice when effort is truly futile; powerful when used in motivational contexts (never give up)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - To encouragement: "You're right, I'll keep trying"
   - To suggestion to quit: "Maybe you're right" / "No, I'm not giving up"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't give up! You're so close to solving it.
   - I give up—this puzzle is impossible.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Despite challenges, the team refused to abandon the project.
   - Persistence is essential; one must not surrender in the face of initial setbacks.

1. **Functional Expression**: Bring up (function: introducing topic/mentioning)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To introduce a topic into conversation; to mention or raise an issue for discussion
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (topic introduction rather than demand)
   
   **Synonyms**: Mention, Raise, Introduce (a topic), Broach, Discuss
   
   **Antonyms**: Avoid mentioning, Drop the subject, Let it go
   
   **When to Use**: Introducing topics for discussion; appropriate in meetings, conversations, negotiations; signals new discussion area
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sensitive topics need more careful framing than just "bringing up"; ensure timing is appropriate; power dynamics matter
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I want to bring up something
   - Let me bring up an issue
   - You brought up a good point
   - I hate to bring this up, but...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Neutral topic introduction; often with hedge ("I hate to bring this up") for sensitive topics; shows speaker has agenda item; can be tentative or direct depending on phrasing
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Interest: "Yes, let's discuss that" / "Good point"
   - Deflection: "Can we talk about that later?" / "Is now the best time?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to bring up something that's been bothering me.
   - You brought up a really interesting point earlier.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would like to raise an issue that requires our attention.
   - The committee member brought up concerns regarding budget allocation.

1. **Functional Expression**: Show up (function: arriving/appearing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To arrive or appear, especially at an expected or planned event
   - *(Sense 2)* To become visible or apparent
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Arrive, Appear, Turn up, Attend
   
   **Antonyms**: No-show, Miss, Skip, Be absent
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing attendance at events; talking about appearance or visibility; appropriate in casual to semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Show up" can sound casual for formal invitations—use "attend"; can sound accusatory ("you didn't show up")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Show up on time
   - Didn't show up
   - Show up for someone (support them)
   - Show up late
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Implies expectation of attendance; "didn't show up" often carries criticism; "show up for" (sense: support) is idiomatic meaning; timing matters—"show up late" is negative
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Commitment: "I'll be there" / "I'll show up"
   - Excuse: "Sorry I couldn't show up" / "I got held up"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just show up whenever—we'll be there all afternoon.
   - He didn't even show up for his own party!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All participants are expected to attend the mandatory session.
   - Failure to appear at the scheduled hearing may result in penalties.

1. **Functional Expression**: Look up (function: researching/seeking information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To search for information, typically in a reference source
   - *(Sense 2)* To visit or contact someone
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Search for, Research, Check, Find information about (sense 1), Visit, Contact (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: Ignore, Assume, Guess
   
   **When to Use**: Suggesting research or information-seeking; appropriate in educational, professional, or casual contexts; sense 2 for social visits
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In digital age, often specify "look up online" or specific source; sense 2 (visit) somewhat dated
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Look it up
   - Look up the answer
   - Look someone up (visit/contact)
   - Look up online
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Encourages self-directed learning; can be dismissive if used instead of helping; in teaching contexts, promotes independent research; "look someone up" (visit) is older usage
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Compliance: "Okay, I will" / "Let me check"
   - Requesting help: "Can you help me find it?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just look it up on Google.
   - Look me up if you're ever in town! (visit sense)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please consult the reference materials for additional information.
   - Students should research unfamiliar terms using appropriate academic sources.

1. **Functional Expression**: Open up (function: sharing emotions/becoming accessible)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To begin sharing feelings, thoughts, or personal information; to become less reserved
   - *(Sense 2)* To make accessible or available
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (encouragement rather than demand)
   
   **Synonyms**: Share, Confide, Be honest, Talk about feelings (sense 1), Make available, Provide access (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: Close off, Shut down, Keep to yourself, Bottle up
   
   **When to Use**: Encouraging emotional expression; appropriate in supportive, therapeutic, or close relationship contexts; sense 2 for access/availability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Forcing someone to "open up" can be invasive; respect boundaries; requires trust and appropriate context; professional contexts usually inappropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Open up to someone
   - Open up about something
   - Open up emotionally
   - Open up opportunities (different meaning: create)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Requires trust and safe environment; can't be forced; shows care and interest; respecting "no" is crucial; implies current closed-off state; positive connotation of vulnerability and trust
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Acceptance: "Okay, I've been wanting to talk about..." / "Thanks for listening"
   - Resistance: "I'd rather not" / "I'm not ready"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You seem upset. Do you want to open up about it?
   - It took a while, but he finally opened up about his past.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The counseling program provides a safe space for students to discuss concerns.
   - Access to the archives will be made available to qualified researchers.
