1. **Original Expression**: I'm up for that  
   **Context**: Expressing willingness or interest in participating in a suggested activity
   
   **Core Intent**: To communicate willingness and readiness to participate
   
   **Casual**: I'm up for it! / Sounds good, I'm in  
   *Characteristics*: Colloquial, enthusiastic, brief, contemporary  
   *Use when*: Close friends, peers, informal social planning, relaxed settings
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I'm interested / I'd be happy to participate  
   *Characteristics*: Standard vocabulary, polite, professional-friendly  
   *Use when*: Workplace social events, networking, colleagues, general professional contexts
   
   **Formal**: I would be pleased to participate / I would welcome the opportunity  
   *Characteristics*: Elevated diction, complete sentences, formal register  
   *Use when*: Official invitations, formal events, superiors, written responses
   
   **Humorous**: I'm up for it like a gopher at sunrise!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful comparison, exaggerated enthusiasm, vivid imagery  
   *Use when*: Close friends, casual settings where humor is welcome, building rapport  
   *Avoid when*: Professional contexts, formal situations, unfamiliar people
   
   **Lively**: Absolutely! Count me in! I'm totally up for that!  
   *Characteristics*: Exclamations, intensifiers, energetic vocabulary  
   *Use when*: Showing enthusiasm, social events, when energy and excitement are appropriate
   
   **Empathetic**: That sounds wonderful—I'd love to be part of it  
   *Characteristics*: Warm tone, positive word choice, connection-building  
   *Use when*: Friend's invitation, building relationships, showing genuine interest
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "Up for" with equals; superiors require formal acceptance language
   - *Social Distance*: "Up for" only with friends/close colleagues; acquaintances need more formal language
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Casual plans allow "up for"; important events require formal acceptance
   - *Cultural Context*: "Up for" is informal English; international contexts may prefer direct statements
   - *Medium*: Text messages allow casual; formal invitations require formal responses
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "I'm up for that" in formal email responses—sounds inappropriately casual
   - Using "I'm up for" with new colleagues or clients—relationship not established
   - Over-formalizing casual friend invitations—"I would be pleased" sounds stiff
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Up for" is common informal English expression; conveys readiness and willingness; British English also uses "keen for" or "game for"
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend texts "Coffee tomorrow?" → **Appropriate Variant**: "I'm up for it!" (Casual—friend, text, social)
   - **Situation 2**: Colleague asks if you'd join committee → **Appropriate Variant**: "I'd be happy to participate" (Semi-formal—colleague, professional)
   - **Situation 3**: Formal invitation to board meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: "I would be pleased to attend" (Formal—written, authority)

1. **Original Expression**: What's up?  
   **Context**: Casual greeting asking how someone is doing or what they're doing
   
   **Core Intent**: To greet someone and inquire about their current state or activities
   
   **Casual**: What's up? / Sup? / What's happening?  
   *Characteristics*: Very informal, contracted, conversational  
   *Use when*: Close friends, peers of similar age, very casual settings
   
   **Semi-Formal**: How are you? / How are things going?  
   *Characteristics*: Standard greeting, neutral, universally appropriate  
   *Use when*: Workplace, acquaintances, general professional interactions
   
   **Formal**: How do you do? / Good morning, how are you today?  
   *Characteristics*: Traditional formal greeting, complete sentences, respectful  
   *Use when*: First meetings, formal events, significant age/status difference
   
   **Humorous**: What's the good word? / What's shaking, bacon?  
   *Characteristics*: Playful, rhyming variants, lighthearted  
   *Use when*: Close friends, relaxed atmosphere, establishing playful rapport  
   *Avoid when*: Professional settings, first meetings, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: Hey! What's going on?! / What's new and exciting?  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic, shows genuine interest, animated  
   *Use when*: Greeting friends enthusiastically, social events, catching up
   
   **Empathetic**: How have you been? / How are you holding up?  
   *Characteristics*: Shows genuine concern, allows deeper response, caring  
   *Use when*: Friend going through challenges, checking in on someone's wellbeing
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Never use "What's up?" with superiors; formal greeting required
   - *Social Distance*: "What's up?" only for friends; strangers require formal greetings
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Casual social only; formal events need proper greetings
   - *Cultural Context*: "What's up?" is American slang; international audiences may not understand
   - *Medium*: Text allows very casual; in-person professional requires formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Greeting boss or clients with "What's up?"—unprofessional and disrespectful
   - Using "What's up?" in formal email—inappropriate register
   - Using "How do you do?" with close friends—overly stiff and distant
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "What's up?" became popular American slang in late 20th century; extremely casual; British equivalent might be "Alright?"; not appropriate across many cultures
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Seeing friend at party → **Appropriate Variant**: "Hey! What's up?" (Casual—friend, social setting)
   - **Situation 2**: Greeting colleague in office → **Appropriate Variant**: "Good morning, how are you?" (Semi-formal—workplace)
   - **Situation 3**: Meeting client for first time → **Appropriate Variant**: "Good afternoon, pleased to meet you" (Formal—client, first meeting)

1. **Original Expression**: I'll catch up with you later  
   **Context**: Expressing intention to talk or meet with someone at a future time
   
   **Core Intent**: To defer conversation or meeting to a later time
   
   **Casual**: Catch you later! / Talk later / Let's catch up soon  
   *Characteristics*: Abbreviated, informal, friendly  
   *Use when*: Friends, colleagues you're comfortable with, informal farewells
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Let's reconnect later / I'll follow up with you  
   *Characteristics*: Professional but warm, clear intention, standard business language  
   *Use when*: Workplace conversations, networking, professional relationships
   
   **Formal**: I will contact you at a later time / We shall reconvene subsequently  
   *Characteristics*: Formal vocabulary, complete structure, professional distance  
   *Use when*: Formal business contexts, superiors, official communications
   
   **Humorous**: I'll catch you on the flip side! / Until we meet again in the land of productivity  
   *Characteristics*: Playful expressions, dated slang, humorous tone  
   *Use when*: Close colleagues, casual workplace, building camaraderie  
   *Avoid when*: Client meetings, formal contexts, unfamiliar relationships
   
   **Lively**: Can't wait to catch up! Talk to you soon!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic, shows anticipation, energetic  
   *Use when*: Friends you're excited to talk with, genuine enthusiasm for future conversation
   
   **Empathetic**: I know you're busy—let's find time to catch up when things settle  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges constraints, flexible, considerate  
   *Use when*: Friend or colleague under pressure, showing understanding
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "Catch up" too casual for superiors; use "follow up" or "reconnect"
   - *Social Distance*: "Catch up" implies friendly relationship; neutral "speak later" for acquaintances
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Urgent matters need specific time commitment, not vague "catch up"
   - *Cultural Context*: "Catch up" is informal idiom; clearer direct language for international contexts
   - *Medium*: Professional emails need clearer commitment than "catch up later"
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "catch you later" to boss or clients—too casual
   - "Catch up" when specific follow-up is needed—sounds uncommitted
   - Overly formal "reconvene subsequently" with peers—unnecessarily stiff
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Catch up" is common informal English expression meaning to talk and share recent news; very casual and friendly in connotation
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Ending phone call with friend → **Appropriate Variant**: "Catch you later!" (Casual—friend, phone call)
   - **Situation 2**: Ending meeting with colleague → **Appropriate Variant**: "I'll follow up with you on Monday" (Semi-formal—workplace, specific)
   - **Situation 3**: Formal business correspondence → **Appropriate Variant**: "I will contact you next week to discuss further" (Formal—written, business)

1. **Original Expression**: Can you speak up?  
   **Context**: Requesting someone to talk louder because you can't hear them well
   
   **Core Intent**: To ask someone to increase their voice volume
   
   **Casual**: Speak up! / Can you talk louder? / I can't hear you  
   *Characteristics*: Direct, imperative form acceptable, brief  
   *Use when*: Friends, casual settings, noisy environments
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Could you speak a bit louder, please? / I'm having trouble hearing you  
   *Characteristics*: Polite request form, softening phrases, acknowledges own difficulty  
   *Use when*: Meetings, presentations, professional settings, being considerate
   
   **Formal**: Would you mind speaking up slightly? / I apologize, but I'm having difficulty hearing  
   *Characteristics*: Very polite, takes responsibility ("I'm having difficulty"), formal request  
   *Use when*: Formal presentations, speaking to authority, public settings
   
   **Humorous**: You're not a mime—I need words I can actually hear!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful exaggeration, lighthearted teasing, humorous  
   *Use when*: Close friends, comfortable relationships, lighten mood  
   *Avoid when*: Professional settings, could embarrass speaker, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: Hey! Project that voice! / Give us some volume!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic, theatrical language, encouraging tone  
   *Use when*: Presentations where encouragement is welcome, teaching contexts, supportive atmosphere
   
   **Empathetic**: I really want to hear what you're saying—could you speak a little louder?  
   *Characteristics*: Shows interest, supportive, emphasizes value of speaker's words  
   *Use when*: Encouraging shy speaker, showing genuine interest, supportive contexts
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Requesting superior to speak up requires very polite form
   - *Social Distance*: Friends allow direct imperative; others need polite requests
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Important presentations need polite, supportive request
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures view this request as face-threatening; use carefully
   - *Medium*: In-person requires immediate request; virtual meetings have technical alternative
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Shouting "Speak up!" to authority figure—disrespectful and demanding
   - Not requesting at all in professional settings—missing important information
   - Using humor with nervous presenter—could increase anxiety
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Direct requests to speak louder can be face-threatening in some cultures; American culture generally accepts direct requests; British English might say "Speak up, please"
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend talking in noisy bar → **Appropriate Variant**: "Speak up! I can't hear you!" (Casual—friend, noisy)
   - **Situation 2**: Colleague presenting to team → **Appropriate Variant**: "Could you speak a bit louder? Thanks!" (Semi-formal—workplace, helpful)
   - **Situation 3**: Academic lecture, distinguished professor → **Appropriate Variant**: "Excuse me, would you mind speaking slightly louder?" (Formal—academic, respectful)

   *Characteristics*: Idiomatic, optimistic, conversational  
   *Use when*: Friends, sharing good news, casual updates
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The situation is improving / We're seeing positive developments  
   *Characteristics*: Professional language, factual tone, business-appropriate  
   *Use when*: Workplace updates, professional communications, status reports
   
   **Formal**: We are observing favorable trends / Conditions are showing marked improvement  
   *Characteristics*: Formal vocabulary, measured tone, official language  
   *Use when*: Formal reports, presentations to executives, official announcements
   
   **Humorous**: We're climbing out of the pit of despair! / The tunnel has a light, and it's not a train!  
   *Characteristics*: Dramatic exaggeration, humorous contrast, relief expressed playfully  
   *Use when*: After difficult period with close colleagues, stress relief, building morale  
   *Avoid when*: Situation still serious, stakeholders affected, formal reporting
   
   **Lively**: Yes! We're on the upswing! / Things are really turning around!  
   *Characteristics*: Exclamatory, enthusiastic, energetic vocabulary  
   *Use when*: Celebrating improvements, motivating team, sharing good news enthusiastically
   
   **Empathetic**: I'm so glad things are getting better for you / It's wonderful to see progress  
   *Characteristics*: Personal focus, warm tone, celebrates other's success  
   *Use when*: Friend or colleague recovering from difficulty, showing genuine happiness for others
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "Looking up" too casual for formal reports to executives; use "showing improvement"
   - *Social Distance*: Idiomatic "looking up" fine with colleagues; clients need clearer language
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious business challenges need measured, professional language
   - *Cultural Context*: "Looking up" is idiomatic English; international audiences may need literal language
   - *Medium*: Casual conversation allows idiom; written reports need professional language
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "looking up" in formal financial reports—too casual and imprecise
   - Being overly optimistic when situation is still fragile—manage expectations
   - Using stiff formal language with friends celebrating recovery—sounds distant
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Looking up" is common English idiom meaning "improving"; metaphor of upward direction = positive change; understood in most English-speaking contexts but idiomatic
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend asks how job search is going → **Appropriate Variant**: "Things are looking up! I have an interview!" (Casual—friend, personal)
   - **Situation 2**: Team meeting status update → **Appropriate Variant**: "We're seeing positive developments in customer engagement" (Semi-formal—workplace)
   - **Situation 3**: Quarterly report to board → **Appropriate Variant**: "Market conditions are showing favorable trends" (Formal—executives, official)

1. **Original Expression**: Step up your game  
   **Context**: Encouraging or demanding someone to improve their performance or effort
   
   **Core Intent**: To request or demand better performance or increased effort
   
   **Casual**: Step it up / You gotta do better / Bring your A-game  
   *Characteristics*: Direct, motivational slang, sports metaphors  
   *Use when*: Friends, peers, casual coaching, sports contexts
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We need to elevate our performance / Let's improve our execution  
   *Characteristics*: Professional, inclusive language ("we"), business terminology  
   *Use when*: Team meetings, professional development, manager to team
   
   **Formal**: Enhanced performance is required / Standards must be elevated  
   *Characteristics*: Formal vocabulary, passive constructions, impersonal tone  
   *Use when*: Formal performance reviews, official communications, policy statements
   
   **Humorous**: Time to level up! / Let's go from good to legendary!  
   *Characteristics*: Gaming references, exaggerated target, playful motivation  
   *Use when*: Close team with gaming culture, motivating through humor, casual workplace  
   *Avoid when*: Serious performance issues, formal contexts, could seem dismissive
   
   **Lively**: Let's bring it! Time to shine! / Show them what you've got!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic, motivational, coaching tone, exclamatory  
   *Use when*: Sports contexts, before presentations, pumping up team
   
   **Empathetic**: I believe you can do even better / Let's work together to improve this  
   *Characteristics*: Supportive, collaborative, belief in potential, offers help  
   *Use when*: Coaching, mentoring, when offering support along with expectations
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "Step up" from superior can sound harsh; from peer is motivational
   - *Social Distance*: Friends accept direct challenge; professionals need constructive framing
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Performance issues need specific feedback, not just "step up"
   - *Cultural Context*: Sports metaphors common in American English; translate poorly internationally
   - *Medium*: Never use in written performance review without specific guidance
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - "Step up your game" in formal review without specifics—vague and unhelpful
   - Using harsh tone when employee is trying hard—demotivating
   - Too soft language when serious improvement is actually required—unclear expectations
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Step up your game" is American sports/gaming idiom meaning improve performance; can sound aggressive or dismissive if not delivered with supportive tone; provides no specific guidance
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Friend preparing for competition → **Appropriate Variant**: "Time to step it up! You've got this!" (Casual—friend, motivational)
   - **Situation 2**: Team meeting after missed targets → **Appropriate Variant**: "We need to elevate our performance and focus on key metrics" (Semi-formal—team, specific)
   - **Situation 3**: Formal performance improvement plan → **Appropriate Variant**: "Performance must meet established standards within 90 days" (Formal—official, documented)

1. **Original Expression**: Heads up  
   **Context**: Giving someone advance warning or alerting them to pay attention
   
   **Core Intent**: To warn someone or get their attention about something important
   
   **Casual**: Heads up! / Just so you know / FYI  
   *Characteristics*: Brief, informal warning, conversational  
   *Use when*: Friends, colleagues, quick warnings, casual alerts
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Please be aware / I wanted to alert you / Just a reminder  
   *Characteristics*: Professional, courteous, clear purpose  
   *Use when*: Workplace communications, professional warnings, status updates
   
   **Formal**: Please be advised / I wish to inform you / For your information  
   *Characteristics*: Formal structures, official tone, complete sentences  
   *Use when*: Official notices, formal communications, legal/compliance contexts
   
   **Humorous**: Incoming! / Duck! (metaphorically) / Alert alert!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful urgency, dramatic language, lighthearted  
   *Use when*: Close colleagues, non-critical alerts, building rapport  
   *Avoid when*: Serious warnings, could seem to minimize importance
   
   **Lively**: Hey! Quick heads up! / Attention everyone!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic, gets attention, urgent but friendly  
   *Use when*: Team announcements, getting group attention, time-sensitive but non-critical
   
   **Empathetic**: I thought you should know / I wanted to make sure you were aware  
   *Characteristics*: Considerate, helpful tone, looks out for others  
   *Use when*: Warning someone helpfully, showing care, preventing problems
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "Heads up" too casual for formal notice to superiors; use "please be advised"
   - *Social Distance*: "Heads up" fine for colleagues; clients need more formal notice
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Critical issues need formal, clear communication; minor items allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: "Heads up" is idiomatic English; literal translation may confuse international audiences
   - *Medium*: Email subject lines allow "Heads up:" informally; formal emails need proper subject
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "Heads up" for critical warnings—too casual for serious matters
   - No warning at all when simple heads up would help colleagues—inconsiderate
   - Overly formal "Please be advised" with close team—creates unnecessary distance
   
   **Cultural Notes**: "Heads up" originally sports term (watch out for ball); now common informal warning; British English might use "Just to let you know"; understood broadly but informal
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Alerting colleague about approaching deadline → **Appropriate Variant**: "Heads up—that report is due Friday" (Casual—colleague, helpful reminder)
   - **Situation 2**: Emailing team about schedule change → **Appropriate Variant**: "Please be aware that meeting times have changed" (Semi-formal—team communication)
   - **Situation 3**: Official notice to clients about policy change → **Appropriate Variant**: "Please be advised that terms of service will be updated on..." (Formal—official, legal)
