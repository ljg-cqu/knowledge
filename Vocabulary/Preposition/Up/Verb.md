1. **Verb**: upgrade
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To improve something to a higher standard or quality; to raise to a better condition
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To promote someone to a higher rank or position
   - *(Sense 3)* (Intransitive) To change to something better, especially on an airplane or in a hotel
   
   **Synonyms**: improve, enhance, elevate, promote, advance
   
   **Antonyms**: downgrade, demote, reduce, lower
   
   **When to Use**: Technology, travel, and workplace contexts; appropriate for describing improvements to systems, positions, or services
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies positive change; don't use if change is lateral or negative
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - upgrade the system
   - upgrade to first class
   - upgrade your skills
   - eligible for an upgrade
   - free upgrade
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (higher level) + grade (quality level)
   
   **Etymology**: American English (early 20th century) → initially railroad/transport terminology → expanded to computing (1980s) → now ubiquitous across domains
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to upgrade my phone next month.
   - They upgraded us to a suite for free!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization must upgrade its infrastructure to remain competitive.
   - Personnel will be upgraded based on performance evaluations.

1. **Verb**: update

   **Antonyms**: outdated, leave unchanged, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: Universal application for making current; essential in technology, information management, and communication

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - update the software
   - keep someone updated
   - provide an update
   - latest update
   - security update
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (to current level) + date (current time)

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you update me on what happened at the meeting?
   - My phone keeps asking me to update the apps.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please update the financial projections to reflect current market conditions.
   - The system automatically updates at scheduled intervals.

1. **Verb**: upload
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To transfer data from a local device to a remote computer or server
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To be transferred in this way
   
   **Synonyms**: transfer, send, transmit, post, push
   
   **Antonyms**: download, retrieve, fetch
   
   **When to Use**: Computing and digital contexts; appropriate in technical documentation, social media, and general technology discussion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Older or non-technical audiences may need clarification; increasingly universal in digital age
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - upload a file
   - upload photos
   - upload content
   - upload speed
   - failed to upload
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (to remote server) + load (transfer)
   
   **Etymology**: Computer science neologism (1970s-80s) → metaphor of sending data "up" from user's device to remote server → opposite of download
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll upload the photos to the cloud later.
   - Did you upload your assignment yet?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Users may upload documents through the secure portal.
   - The system uploads backups automatically each evening.

1. **Verb**: uphold
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To support or maintain something, especially a law, principle, or decision
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To confirm or validate a decision made by someone else, especially in legal contexts
   
   **Synonyms**: support, maintain, sustain, defend, confirm, validate
   
   **Antonyms**: overturn, reject, abandon, violate, undermine
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts involving principles, laws, decisions, or values; common in legal and ethical discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "support" or "defend" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - uphold the law
   - uphold a decision
   - uphold standards
   - uphold justice
   - duty to uphold
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (maintain at level) + hold (keep in position)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → maintain in elevated position → metaphorical extension to abstract principles → now primarily used for supporting laws, decisions, values
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to uphold our end of the agreement.
   - The referee upheld the call despite the protests.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court upheld the lower court's decision.
   - Organizations must uphold ethical standards in all operations.

1. **Verb**: upend
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To turn something upside down or on its end
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To disrupt or overturn completely; to cause radical change to something established
   
   **Synonyms**: overturn, flip, invert (sense 1), disrupt, revolutionize, transform (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: stabilize, maintain, preserve, uphold
   
   **When to Use**: Literal sense for physical objects; metaphorical sense common in business and technology contexts for disruptive change
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Metaphorical use implies significant disruption; use carefully if change is modest
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - upend the market
   - upend expectations
   - upend traditional practices
   - upend the status quo
   - completely upend
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (to inverted position) + end (terminus)
   
   **Etymology**: 16th century → literal meaning of turning objects → modern metaphorical use (20th century) for radical disruption
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new technology totally upended how we do things.
   - He upended the bucket to dump out the water.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Digital transformation has upended traditional business models.
   - The discovery upended prevailing scientific theories.

1. **Verb**: uproot
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To pull a plant including its roots out of the ground
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To force someone to leave their home or usual environment; to displace
   - *(Sense 3)* (Transitive) To destroy or remove something completely
   
   **Synonyms**: extract, pull up (sense 1), displace, relocate (sense 2), eradicate, eliminate (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: plant, establish, root, settle
   
   **When to Use**: Literal gardening contexts; metaphorical use for forced relocation or complete removal of established things
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Implies violence or unwanted change; use "relocate" if change is voluntary
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - uproot a tree
   - uproot your family
   - uproot corruption
   - uproot from home
   - forcibly uproot
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (out of ground) + root (underground plant structure)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → literal botanical meaning → extended metaphorically to people and abstract concepts → emphasizes disruption and displacement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The storm uprooted several trees in our yard.
   - We had to uproot our whole family when I changed jobs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Armed conflict has uprooted millions of people from their homes.
   - The reforms aim to uproot systemic corruption.

1. **Verb**: upstage
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To draw attention away from someone else, especially by performing better or more dramatically
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) In theater, to force another actor to face away from the audience by moving upstage of them
   
   **Synonyms**: overshadow, outshine, eclipse, outperform, steal the spotlight
   
   **Antonyms**: defer to, yield to, support, collaborate with
   
   **When to Use**: Performance contexts or situations where someone deliberately or inadvertently draws attention from others
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Usually carries negative connotation of selfishness or poor etiquette; use carefully
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - upstage someone
   - deliberately upstage
   - try to upstage
   - not to upstage
   - upstage the main event
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (toward back of stage) + stage (performance area)
   
   **Etymology**: Theater terminology (19th century) → literal stage positioning → metaphorical use for drawing attention → now common in non-theatrical contexts
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't try to upstage the bride at her wedding!
   - The side dish totally upstaged the main course.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Minor controversies should not upstage the substantive policy discussion.
   - The actor was criticized for attempting to upstage his co-stars.

1. **Verb**: upkeep
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Noun form used as verb informally) To maintain something in good condition; to perform regular maintenance
   
   **Synonyms**: maintain, preserve, keep up, service, care for
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, abandon, allow to deteriorate
   
   **When to Use**: Primarily used as a noun; verb use is informal and less common; better to use "maintain" or "keep up"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: As a verb, it's non-standard; prefer "maintain" or "keep up with the upkeep" in formal contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - (Noun) expensive upkeep
   - (Noun) require upkeep
   - (Noun) costs of upkeep
   - maintain upkeep
   - ongoing upkeep
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (to standard) + keep (maintain)
   
   **Etymology**: 19th century → primarily noun meaning "maintenance" → occasional informal verb use but not standard
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Old houses really need a lot of upkeep.
   - We can't afford to upkeep such a big property. (informal verb use)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility requires significant upkeep to maintain operational standards. (noun use)
   - Resources must be allocated to maintain proper upkeep of equipment. (noun use)

1. **Verb**: upturn
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To turn something upward or over
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To improve or increase, especially in economics or business
   
   **Synonyms**: turn up, flip (sense 1), improve, rise, increase (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: downturn, decline, decrease, worsen
   
   **When to Use**: Sense 1 is physical; sense 2 common in economic and business contexts for positive trends
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More commonly used as a noun ("an upturn in the market"); verb use is less frequent
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - economic upturn
   - upturn in sales
   - market upturn
   - upturn in fortune
   - recent upturn
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (upward direction) + turn (change direction)
   
   **Etymology**: 19th century → originally physical turning → economic sense developed 20th century → more common as noun than verb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Business has really upturned since the new manager arrived.
   - He upturned the glass to see if anything was inside.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Market indicators suggest conditions will upturn in the coming quarter. (More common: "show an upturn")
   - The economy is beginning to upturn after the recession. (More common: "experience an upturn")

   **Antonyms**: swallow, digest, keep down

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - upchuck your dinner
   - feel like upchucking
   - about to upchuck
   - upchuck everywhere
   - make someone upchuck

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That carnival ride almost made me upchuck.
   - He upchucked after eating too much candy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:
