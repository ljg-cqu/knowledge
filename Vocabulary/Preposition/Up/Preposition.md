1. **Preposition**: up (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* From a lower to a higher point or position; toward a higher level or upward direction
   - *(Spatial)* At or to a higher point on something; further along
   - *(Temporal)* Until a particular time; as far as
   - *(Abstract)* Involved in or engaged with; facing or confronting
   
   **Synonyms**: toward (spatial), upward (spatial), until (temporal), against (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: down, downward, toward a lower point
   
   **When to Use**: Extremely versatile preposition for indicating upward direction, movement along routes toward higher points, progression to a time limit, or engagement with activities/challenges
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid confusion with "above" (position) vs "up" (direction); don't use when "along" is clearer for horizontal movement
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up the street, up the stairs, up the mountain, up the ladder, up to now
   - **Verb + prep**: go up, walk up, run up, climb up, move up, come up, look up
   - **Adjective + prep**: up for (willing), up to (capable of)
   - **Noun + prep**: way up, path up, route up, journey up
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English upp → indicating upward direction
   
   **Etymology**: Old English upp (upward direction) → prepositional use developed in Middle English → highly productive in modern English with spatial, temporal, and abstract meanings
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She walked up the street to meet her friend.
   - Let's go up to the rooftop and watch the sunset.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegation proceeded up the main staircase to the executive floor.
   - Market trends indicate movement up the value chain.

1. **Preposition**: up to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Until; as far as a particular time or point
   - *(Numerical)* As many or as much as; not exceeding
   - *(Abstract)* Being the responsibility or choice of someone
   - *(Abstract)* Capable of; equal to the demands of
   - *(Abstract)* Secretly doing or planning something (usually something bad)
   
   **Synonyms**: until (temporal), as many as (numerical), dependent on (responsibility), capable of (ability), plotting (scheming)
   
   **Antonyms**: from (temporal opposite), incapable of, unable to handle
   
   **When to Use**: Versatile expression for time limits, quantity limits, responsibility assignment, capability assessment, or suspicious activity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Up to" meaning "plotting" is informal and often suspicious in tone; use carefully
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up to now, up to date, up to standard, up to the task, up to no good
   - **Verb + prep**: be up to, get up to, live up to, add up to, amount up to
   - **Adjective + prep**: equal up to
   - **Noun + prep**: everything up to, anything up to
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (extent/limit) + to (reaching point)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition emphasizing extent, limit, or capability threshold
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We can invite up to 50 people to the party.
   - It's up to you to decide what we should do.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility can accommodate up to 500 participants.
   - Successful implementation is up to the dedication of all stakeholders.

1. **Preposition**: up for (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Willing to do or try something; interested in participating
   - *(Abstract)* Being considered or available for something (appointment, award, sale)
   - *(Abstract)* Scheduled or due for something
   
   **Synonyms**: willing to (sense 1), interested in (sense 1), available for (sense 2), due for (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: unwilling to, opposed to, against
   
   **When to Use**: Informal to neutral contexts for expressing willingness, availability, or scheduling
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Primarily conversational; in formal contexts, use "willing to participate" or "available for consideration"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up for discussion, up for sale, up for grabs, up for review, up for election
   - **Verb + prep**: be up for, come up for, put up for
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready up for
   - **Noun + prep**: position up for
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (availability/readiness) + for (purpose)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition indicating readiness or availability for an activity or position
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you up for watching a movie tonight?
   - The house next door is up for sale.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal is up for review by the board of directors.
   - Three positions are up for election this quarter.

1. **Preposition**: up against (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In contact with or very close to something
   - *(Abstract)* Facing or confronting a challenge, opponent, or deadline
   
   **Synonyms**: against (spatial), facing (abstract), confronting (abstract), challenged by
   
   **Antonyms**: away from (spatial), supported by, assisted by
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates physical proximity or metaphorical confrontation with obstacles or deadlines
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound confrontational in abstract sense; ensure context supports challenge framing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up against the wall, up against it, up against a deadline, up against competition
   - **Verb + prep**: be up against, come up against, run up against, push up against
   - **Adjective + prep**: pressed up against
   - **Noun + prep**: position up against, struggle up against
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (intensive) + against (opposition/contact)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition emphasizing closeness or opposition
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're really up against it with this tight deadline.
   - The bookshelf is right up against the wall.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization is up against significant regulatory challenges.
   - Researchers are up against the limitations of current technology.

1. **Preposition**: up on (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Knowledgeable about; well-informed regarding
   - *(Spatial)* Located on top of or at a higher position on something
   
   **Synonyms**: informed about, knowledgeable about (abstract), on top of (spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: ignorant of, unaware of, uninformed about
   
   **When to Use**: Sense 1 is most common - indicating current knowledge or awareness of a topic
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sometimes confused with "upon"; "up on" emphasizes currency of knowledge
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up on current events, up on the latest news, up on technology
   - **Verb + prep**: be up on, catch up on, brush up on, read up on
   - **Adjective + prep**: knowledgeable up on
   - **Noun + prep**: expertise up on
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (current/complete) + on (concerning)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition indicating completeness of knowledge or position
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to get up on the latest software updates.
   - Are you up on what happened at the meeting?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Professionals must remain up on industry developments and regulatory changes.
   - The committee members are well up on the technical specifications.

1. **Preposition**: up in (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* At or to a higher position within something
   - *(Abstract)* Involved or engaged in something (often negative connotations)
   
   **Synonyms**: within (spatial), involved in (abstract), engaged in
   
   **Antonyms**: down in, out of, uninvolved in
   
   **When to Use**: Spatial sense common with elevated locations; abstract sense often implies problematic involvement
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Up in arms" is idiomatic; ensure context matches intended meaning
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up in the air (uncertain), up in arms (angry), up in the attic, up in the mountains
   - **Verb + prep**: be up in, go up in, get up in, climb up in
   - **Adjective + prep**: located up in
   - **Noun + prep**: position up in
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (higher position) + in (within boundaries)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition combining directional and locational elements
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The old photos are up in the attic somewhere.
   - Everyone was up in arms about the policy change.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The equipment is stored up in the upper facility.
   - The proposal remains up in the air pending further review.

1. **Preposition**: up from (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving or located from a lower to higher position relative to something
   - *(Numerical)* Increased or raised from a previous level or amount
   
   **Synonyms**: ascending from, increased from, risen from
   
   **Antonyms**: down from, decreased from, fallen from
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates starting point of upward movement or increase; common in statistical and comparative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ensure direction is clear; "from" indicates origin point
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up from the basement, up from last year, up from previous levels, up from the ground
   - **Verb + prep**: come up from, rise up from, climb up from, increase up from
   - **Adjective + prep**: different up from, higher up from
   - **Noun + prep**: increase up from, growth up from
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (upward direction) + from (origin point)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound prepositional phrase indicating origin point and direction of movement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He came up from the parking garage to meet us.
   - Sales are up from last quarter by 10%.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Revenue increased 15% up from the comparable period.
   - The delegation traveled up from the regional office.

1. **Preposition**: up through (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving upward while passing through something
   - *(Temporal)* Continuing to and including a particular time; until and including
   - *(Hierarchical)* Through successive levels or stages toward a higher position
   
   **Synonyms**: through to (temporal), via ascending levels (hierarchical)
   
   **Antonyms**: down through, down to
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizes progression through intermediate stages while moving upward
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be redundant; sometimes "through" alone is sufficient
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up through the ranks, up through the years, up through the organization, up through Friday
   - **Verb + prep**: move up through, rise up through, progress up through, work up through
   - **Adjective + prep**: promoted up through
   - **Noun + prep**: progression up through, advancement up through
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (upward direction) + through (via intermediate stages)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound prepositional phrase emphasizing continuous progression through levels
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She worked her way up through the company from an intern.
   - The water is seeping up through the floor.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy applies to all grades up through senior management.
   - Candidates may advance up through the certification levels sequentially.

1. **Preposition**: up until (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Continuing to a particular time; right up to but not beyond a specified point
   
   **Synonyms**: until, till, up to
   
   **Antonyms**: from, starting from, beginning with
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizes continuation right to a boundary or limit; slightly more emphatic than "until" alone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Often "until" is sufficient; "up until" adds emphasis but can be redundant in some contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up until now, up until recently, up until that point, up until the deadline
   - **Verb + prep**: continued up until, worked up until, lived up until, lasted up until
   - **Adjective + prep**: valid up until
   - **Noun + prep**: period up until, time up until
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (extent to limit) + until (time boundary)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic temporal phrase emphasizing continuation to a specific time boundary
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I didn't know about it up until yesterday.
   - Everything was fine up until the power went out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulations remained in effect up until the implementation date.
   - Research continued up until the funding period expired.

1. **Preposition**: up with (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In support of; favoring or endorsing
   - *(Abstract)* Tolerating or accepting (usually in negative constructions)
   - *(Spatial)* Moving upward together with someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: in favor of (support), tolerating (acceptance), alongside (spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: down with (opposing), against
   
   **When to Use**: "Up with" for support, "not put up with" for intolerance; context determines meaning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Put up with" is more common than standalone "up with" for tolerance
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up with the times, put up with, come up with, keep up with, catch up with
   - **Verb + prep**: be up with, go up with, come up with (different meaning: devise)
   - **Adjective + prep**: fed up with
   - **Noun + prep**: tolerance up with
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (supporting/maintaining) + with (accompaniment)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition with varied meanings based on verb combinations
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't put up with this noise anymore.
   - Did you come up with any good ideas?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization will not put up with discriminatory practices.
   - Researchers came up with innovative solutions to address the challenge.

1. **Preposition**: up at (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Located at a higher position or place
   - *(Temporal)* Awake or active at a particular time (especially early or late)
   
   **Synonyms**: located at (spatial), awake at (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: down at, asleep at
   
   **When to Use**: Combines upward position with specific location or time reference
   
   **When NOT to Use**: May be regional; "up at" for being awake is more British English
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up at the cabin, up at dawn, up at the office, up at night
   - **Verb + prep**: be up at, stay up at, wake up at, arrive up at
   - **Adjective + prep**: located up at, awake up at
   - **Noun + prep**: house up at, position up at
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (higher position/awake state) + at (location/time point)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition combining directional and locational/temporal specification
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're staying up at my friend's lake house this weekend.
   - I was up at 5 AM to catch my flight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research station is located up at the summit.
   - Personnel must be up at their stations by 0600 hours.

1. **Preposition**: up around (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the vicinity of a higher or more northern location
   - *(Numerical)* Approximately at or near a particular higher number or amount
   
   **Synonyms**: around (approximate), in the vicinity of, near, about
   
   **Antonyms**: down around, exactly
   
   **When to Use**: Informal contexts for approximate location or quantity with upward direction implied
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too casual for formal reports; use "approximately" or "in the vicinity of" instead
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up around $100, up around the corner, up around the north side, up around that area
   - **Verb + prep**: be up around, cost up around, live up around, work up around
   - **Adjective + prep**: located up around
   - **Noun + prep**: price up around, area up around
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (higher/northern direction) + around (approximate location)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal preposition combining directional and approximate locational elements
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They live up around Boston somewhere.
   - It'll probably cost up around fifty bucks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility is located up around the northern district.
   - Estimates place the cost up around the projected maximum.
