1. **Adjective**: interested (comp: more interested, sup: most interested)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Wanting to know more about something or take part in something; showing curiosity or attention (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - Having a personal or financial stake in something
   
   **Synonyms**: curious, engaged, fascinated, intrigued, absorbed
   
   **Antonyms**: uninterested, indifferent, bored, apathetic
   
   **When to Use**: Use "interested" to express curiosity or desire to know more about a topic; commonly followed by "in" to specify what captures attention
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse "interested" with "interesting"; "interested" describes the person experiencing interest, while "interesting" describes the thing that creates interest
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - interested in learning
   - deeply interested in
   - particularly interested in
   - interested party
   - interested in doing something
   
   **Root Analysis**: interest (from Latin interesse "to be between, make a difference") + -ed (past participle suffix forming adjectives)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin interesse "to be between, concern" → Old French interest → Middle English; originally meant "having a legal claim or financial stake," later broadened to "showing curiosity"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm really interested in learning Japanese.
   - Are you interested in joining us for dinner?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is interested in exploring alternative solutions.
   - Participants interested in the study should contact the research team.

1. **Adjective**: involved (comp: more involved, sup: most involved)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Taking part in an activity or situation; participating actively (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - Difficult to understand because of complexity
   
   **Synonyms**: engaged, participating, committed, immersed, entangled
   
   **Antonyms**: uninvolved, detached, disengaged, separate
   
   **When to Use**: Use "involved" to describe active participation or complexity; commonly followed by "in" to specify the activity or situation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using "involved in" when simple "doing" or "working on" would be clearer; can sound unnecessarily formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - involved in politics
   - deeply involved in
   - heavily involved in
   - get involved in
   - involved party
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + volve (from Latin volvere "to roll") + -ed (forming adjectives)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin involvere "to envelop, surround" → Middle English; originally meant "wrapped up in," evolved to mean "taking part in"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's heavily involved in community service.
   - Don't get involved in their argument.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team involved in this study comprises international experts.
   - Stakeholders involved in the decision-making process were consulted.

1. **Adjective**: experienced (comp: more experienced, sup: most experienced)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having knowledge or skill in a particular job or activity gained over time (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: skilled, knowledgeable, expert, seasoned, practiced, proficient
   
   **Antonyms**: inexperienced, novice, unskilled, green, raw
   
   **When to Use**: Use "experienced" to describe someone who has practical knowledge through practice; followed by "in" to specify the domain of expertise
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use for theoretical knowledge alone (use "knowledgeable" instead); requires actual practice or involvement
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - experienced in management
   - highly experienced in
   - experienced professional
   - experienced in dealing with
   - experienced in handling
   
   **Root Analysis**: experience (from Latin experientia "trial, test") + -ed (suffix forming adjectives)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin experiri "to try, test" → experientia "knowledge from practice" → Middle English; denotes learning through practical involvement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's really experienced in dealing with difficult customers.
   - We need someone experienced in web design.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate is experienced in international project management.
   - The team comprised professionals experienced in regulatory compliance.

1. **Adjective**: skilled (comp: more skilled, sup: most skilled)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the ability to do something well, especially because of training or practice (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: proficient, expert, competent, adept, accomplished, capable
   
   **Antonyms**: unskilled, inept, incompetent, inexpert
   
   **When to Use**: Use "skilled" to emphasize high competence in a specific area; followed by "in" or "at" to specify the skill domain
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse with "skillful" (manner of doing) vs. "skilled" (possession of skill)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - skilled in negotiation
   - highly skilled in
   - skilled worker
   - skilled in the art of
   - skilled professional
   
   **Root Analysis**: skill (from Old Norse skil "distinction, knowledge") + -ed (suffix forming adjectives)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse skil "distinction, discernment" → Middle English; evolved to mean "possessing developed ability"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's really skilled in fixing computers.
   - You need to be skilled in communication for this job.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization seeks individuals skilled in data analysis.
   - Candidates should be skilled in project coordination and stakeholder management.

1. **Adjective**: proficient (comp: more proficient, sup: most proficient)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Competent or skilled in doing something; having a high degree of competence (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: skilled, expert, competent, adept, accomplished, capable
   
   **Antonyms**: incompetent, inept, unskilled, unproficient
   
   **When to Use**: Use "proficient" to indicate a high level of skill; followed by "in" or "at" to specify the area of competence; slightly more formal than "skilled"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation about basic skills; implies advanced competence, not beginner level
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - proficient in English
   - highly proficient in
   - proficient in computer programming
   - proficient in using
   - proficient in multiple languages
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + fic (from Latin facere "to make, do") + -ent (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin proficientem "advancing, progressing" → Middle French → English; denotes advancement to high competence
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's proficient in Spanish and French.
   - I'm not very proficient in cooking yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applicants must be proficient in statistical software packages.
   - The team members are proficient in qualitative research methodologies.

1. **Adjective**: rich (comp: richer, sup: richest)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having a lot of money or property; wealthy (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - Containing or providing a large amount of something desirable
   - *(Sense 3)* Having an abundant supply of a particular quality or substance
   
   **Synonyms**: wealthy, affluent, prosperous, abundant, plentiful, full of
   
   **Antonyms**: poor, impoverished, lacking, deficient, scarce
   
   **When to Use**: Use "rich" to describe wealth, abundance, or high content; followed by "in" to specify what is abundant
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in contexts where "wealthy" (for money) or "abundant" (for resources) would be more precise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rich in vitamins
   - rich in history
   - rich in cultural heritage
   - rich in nutrients
   - rich in detail
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English rice "powerful, wealthy," from Proto-Germanic *rikijaz
   
   **Etymology**: Old English rice "powerful, mighty" → "wealthy"; the sense of "abundant in" emerged through metaphorical extension
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This food is rich in protein.
   - The region is rich in natural resources.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The dataset is rich in qualitative insights.
   - The curriculum is rich in interdisciplinary perspectives.

1. **Adjective**: lacking (comp: more lacking, sup: most lacking)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not having enough of something; deficient (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: deficient, wanting, short of, without, devoid of
   
   **Antonyms**: abundant, rich, full of, replete, plentiful
   
   **When to Use**: Use "lacking" to indicate absence or deficiency; followed by "in" to specify what is missing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sounds formal; in casual speech, "missing" or "without" often work better
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lacking in confidence
   - lacking in detail
   - lacking in experience
   - lacking in substance
   - sadly lacking in
   
   **Root Analysis**: lack (from Middle Dutch laken "to be wanting") + -ing (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle Dutch laken "to be deficient" → Middle English; denotes absence or insufficiency
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The plan is lacking in specific details.
   - He seems lacking in confidence lately.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal was deemed lacking in methodological rigor.
   - The report is lacking in empirical evidence.

1. **Adjective**: absorbed (comp: more absorbed, sup: most absorbed)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Intensely engaged or interested in something; completely occupied mentally (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: engrossed, immersed, occupied, preoccupied, captivated, engaged
   
   **Antonyms**: distracted, inattentive, uninterested, disengaged
   
   **When to Use**: Use "absorbed" to describe deep mental engagement or concentration; followed by "in" to specify the object of attention
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use for casual interest; implies deep, complete attention
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - absorbed in thought
   - absorbed in work
   - completely absorbed in
   - deeply absorbed in
   - absorbed in reading
   
   **Root Analysis**: ab- (away) + sorb (from Latin sorbere "to suck in") + -ed (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin absorbere "to swallow up" → Old French → English; metaphorically extended to mental engagement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She was so absorbed in her book she didn't hear me.
   - He gets really absorbed in his video games.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The researchers were absorbed in analyzing the data.
   - Students remained absorbed in the philosophical discussion.

1. **Adjective**: engaged (comp: more engaged, sup: most engaged)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Busy doing something; occupied or involved (gradable, predicative)
   - *(Sense 2)* Having formally agreed to marry someone
   
   **Synonyms**: involved, participating, occupied, busy, absorbed, committed
   
   **Antonyms**: disengaged, uninvolved, free, unoccupied
   
   **When to Use**: Use "engaged" to describe active involvement or participation; followed by "in" to specify the activity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Be aware of the "marriage" sense; context usually clarifies meaning
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - engaged in conversation
   - engaged in business
   - actively engaged in
   - engaged in activities
   - engaged in research
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (in) + gage (from Old French gauge "pledge") + -ed (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French engager "to pledge" → English; evolved to mean "actively involved" beyond original "pledged" sense
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry, I'm engaged in something right now.
   - They're engaged in a heated debate.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization is engaged in community development initiatives.
   - Participants engaged in collaborative problem-solving tasks.

1. **Adjective**: immersed (comp: more immersed, sup: most immersed)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Deeply involved or absorbed in something; completely engaged (gradable, predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: absorbed, engrossed, involved, engaged, steeped, plunged
   
   **Antonyms**: detached, removed, uninvolved, disengaged
   
   **When to Use**: Use "immersed" to describe deep, complete involvement; followed by "in" to specify what one is deeply engaged in; stronger than "involved"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too strong for casual involvement; implies total engagement
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immersed in culture
   - deeply immersed in
   - immersed in work
   - immersed in learning
   - fully immersed in
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (in) + merse (from Latin mergere "to dip, plunge") + -ed (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin immergere "to plunge into" → English; metaphorically extended from physical submersion to mental/emotional engagement
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally immersed in this new series.
   - She's immersed in her studies this semester.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students were immersed in authentic language environments.
   - The research team was immersed in ethnographic fieldwork.
