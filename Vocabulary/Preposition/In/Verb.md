1. **Verb**: include
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have as a part of a whole; to contain or comprise (transitive)
   - To make someone or something part of a group or set (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: contain, comprise, incorporate, encompass, involve, cover
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, leave out, rule out, eliminate
   
   **When to Use**: Use "include" when something is part of a larger whole or when adding elements to a group
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not confuse "include" (part of whole) with "consist of" (made up entirely of); "include" implies there are other parts not mentioned
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - include in the list
   - include as part of
   - include everyone
   - prices include
   - services include
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + clud (from Latin claudere "to close")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin includere "to shut in, enclose" → Old French → English; emphasizes enclosing or containing within
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The price includes breakfast and wifi.
   - Make sure you include everyone in the invitation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study includes participants from diverse demographic backgrounds.
   - The curriculum includes modules on research methodology and ethics.

1. **Verb**: involve
   **Meaning**: 
   - To have or include as a necessary part or result; to require (transitive)
   - To cause someone to take part in something (transitive)
   - *(Sense 3)* To make someone emotionally interested or concerned (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: entail, require, necessitate, include, engage, implicate
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, spare, exempt
   
   **When to Use**: Use "involve" to indicate participation, necessary inclusion, or entanglement; commonly followed by "in"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound passive; consider active alternatives like "participate," "require," or "include" for clarity
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - involve someone in
   - get involved in
   - involve doing something
   - involve risk
   - directly involved in
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + volve (from Latin volvere "to roll")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin involvere "to envelop, surround, wrap up" → Old French → English; emphasizes being wrapped up in something
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The job involves a lot of traveling.
   - Don't involve me in your arguments.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology involves collecting both quantitative and qualitative data.
   - The project involves collaboration among multiple stakeholders.

1. **Verb**: invest
   **Meaning**: 
   - To put money, effort, time, etc. into something to make a profit or achieve a result (transitive/intransitive)
   - To buy shares, property, or goods because you hope their value will increase (transitive/intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: put money into, fund, finance, contribute, devote, commit
   
   **Antonyms**: divest, withdraw, retrieve, save
   
   **When to Use**: Use "invest" for committing resources (money, time, effort) with expectation of returns; followed by "in"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Distinguish financial "invest" from figurative "invest" (time/effort); context usually clarifies
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - invest in stocks
   - invest time in
   - invest in education
   - invest heavily in
   - invest resources in
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + vest (from Latin vestire "to clothe")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin investire "to clothe, surround" → Italian investire "to employ money" → English; metaphor of clothing wealth in assets
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm thinking of investing in real estate.
   - You need to invest more time in your studies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization invests significantly in employee development programs.
   - Governments must invest in sustainable infrastructure initiatives.

1. **Verb**: indicate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To show, point out, or make known; to be a sign of (transitive)
   - To state briefly or indirectly (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: show, demonstrate, signal, suggest, reveal, point to, denote
   
   **Antonyms**: conceal, hide, obscure, suppress
   
   **When to Use**: Use "indicate" to show evidence, point out, or signal something; formal tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound formal; simpler verbs like "show" or "suggest" often work better in casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - indicate that
   - clearly indicate
   - indicate a trend
   - studies indicate
   - results indicate
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (toward) + dic (from Latin dicare "to proclaim, point out") + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin indicare "to point out, show, announce" → English; emphasizes pointing toward or revealing
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The survey indicates that most people prefer remote work.
   - His tone indicated he was annoyed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings indicate a significant correlation between the variables.
   - Performance metrics indicate substantial improvement over the previous quarter.

1. **Verb**: incorporate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To include something as part of a whole; to combine (transitive)
   - To form a company or organization into a legal corporation (transitive/intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: integrate, include, combine, merge, blend, assimilate
   
   **Antonyms**: separate, exclude, isolate, segregate, remove
   
   **When to Use**: Use "incorporate" for formally including or integrating elements into a larger structure; often followed by "into" or "in"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More formal than "include"; use simpler alternatives in casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - incorporate into
   - incorporate feedback
   - incorporate elements
   - incorporate changes
   - incorporate ideas
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + corpor (from Latin corpus "body") + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin incorporare "to form into a body" → Late Latin → English; emphasizes making part of one body
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Try to incorporate more vegetables into your diet.
   - The design incorporates elements from different styles.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The revised framework incorporates stakeholder recommendations.
   - The curriculum incorporates evidence-based pedagogical approaches.

1. **Verb**: indulge
   **Meaning**: 
   - To allow yourself or someone else to have something enjoyable, especially more than is good for them (transitive/intransitive)
   - To satisfy a desire or interest (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: gratify, satisfy, give in to, pamper, spoil, yield to
   
   **Antonyms**: abstain, refrain, deny, resist, deprive
   
   **When to Use**: Use "indulge" for allowing pleasurable excess or satisfying desires; often followed by "in"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can carry negative connotation of excess; context determines whether positive (treating oneself) or negative (overindulgence)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - indulge in
   - indulge yourself
   - indulge a passion
   - indulge in fantasies
   - allow oneself to indulge
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + dulg (from Latin indulgere "to be kind, give way to")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin indulgere "to be courteous, give way to" → English; evolved from "be lenient" to "allow pleasurable excess"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I indulged in some chocolate cake last night.
   - Let's indulge ourselves with a spa day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author indulges in lengthy philosophical digressions.
   - Participants were permitted to indulge in creative experimentation.

1. **Verb**: interfere
   **Meaning**: 
   - To prevent something from succeeding or to prevent someone from doing something; to get involved in a situation where you are not wanted (intransitive)
   - To deliberately involve yourself in a situation where you are not wanted or needed (intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: meddle, intervene, intrude, obstruct, hinder, impede
   
   **Antonyms**: help, assist, facilitate, support, allow
   
   **When to Use**: Use "interfere" for unwanted involvement or obstruction; followed by "in" or "with"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Generally negative; use "intervene" for positive involvement, "interfere" for negative
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - interfere with
   - interfere in someone's business
   - don't interfere
   - interfere with plans
   - interfere with work
   
   **Root Analysis**: inter- (between) + fer (from Latin ferre "to strike, carry")
   
   **Etymology**: Old French s'entreferir "to strike each other" → Latin interferre → English; emphasizes coming between
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Stop interfering in my personal life!
   - The noise is interfering with my concentration.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - External factors may interfere with the experimental outcomes.
   - The policy prohibits interference in internal governance processes.

1. **Verb**: inquire (also: enquire)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To ask for information; to make an inquiry (intransitive/transitive)
   - To investigate; to make a formal examination (intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: ask, question, query, investigate, examine, probe
   
   **Antonyms**: answer, respond, ignore, disregard
   
   **When to Use**: Use "inquire" for formal questioning or investigation; followed by "about" or "into"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: More formal than "ask"; spelling varies (inquire in US, enquire in UK)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inquire about
   - inquire into
   - inquire further
   - inquire within
   - inquire whether
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + quir (from Latin quaerere "to seek, ask")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inquirere "to seek after, search for" → Old French enquerre → English; emphasizes seeking information
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I called to inquire about job openings.
   - Feel free to inquire if you have questions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee will inquire into allegations of misconduct.
   - Interested parties may inquire at the administrative office.

1. **Verb**: increase
   **Meaning**: 
   - To become or make something larger in amount, number, or degree (transitive/intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: grow, rise, expand, escalate, multiply, augment, boost
   
   **Antonyms**: decrease, reduce, diminish, decline, drop, fall
   
   **When to Use**: Use "increase" for upward change in quantity, size, or intensity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Increase" as verb (to increase) vs. noun (an increase); context clarifies usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - increase in size
   - increase rapidly
   - increase significantly
   - increase the amount
   - continue to increase
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + crease (from Latin crescere "to grow")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin increscere "to grow upon, grow over, swell" → Anglo-French encreiss- → English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Traffic has increased a lot in the past year.
   - They're planning to increase prices next month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention increased participant engagement by 35%.
   - Resource allocation must increase to meet growing demands.

1. **Verb**: inhabit
   **Meaning**: 
   - To live in a particular place; to occupy as a residence (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: live in, occupy, reside in, dwell in, populate
   
   **Antonyms**: vacate, leave, abandon, desert, evacuate
   
   **When to Use**: Use "inhabit" for living in or occupying a place; more formal than "live in"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sounds formal or literary; "live in" is more natural in casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inhabit the region
   - inhabit the earth
   - species that inhabit
   - people who inhabit
   - creatures that inhabit
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + habit (from Latin habitare "to dwell")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inhabitare "to dwell in" → Old French → English; emphasizes dwelling within a place
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What kinds of animals inhabit this forest?
   - The tribe has inhabited this area for centuries.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The species inhabits tropical and subtropical regions.
   - Indigenous peoples have inhabited the territory since prehistoric times.
