1. **Adjective**: comparable (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Similar enough in quality, size, or function that two things can reasonably be compared *vis-à-vis* each other.
   - *(Grammar)* Usually predicative and attributive; describes potential for fair comparison.
   
   **Synonyms**: similar, analogous, equivalent (roughly), on a par, commensurate
   
   **Antonyms**: incomparable, unique, vastly different, incommensurate
   
   **When to Use**: To say that items are in the same ballpark *vis-à-vis* scale or quality so that a “like‑for‑like” comparison is meaningful.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When difference is so large that comparison is misleading (*apples and oranges*), or when you want to stress exact equivalence (*identical*).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - broadly comparable
   - comparable vis-à-vis price / risk / quality
   - comparable in size / scope
   - comparable data sets
   
   **Root Analysis**: compare (verb) + -able (capable of)
   
   **Etymology**: From Late Latin *comparabilis*; in English since the 15th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Their offer isn’t really comparable vis-à-vis what we got last year.
   - The two jobs are comparable in pay, but not in stress.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Only projects of comparable scale vis-à-vis budget were included in the analysis.
   - The control and treatment groups were comparable with respect to demographics.

1. **Adjective**: relative (non-gradable when used for type, gradable when used for degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Dependent on or considered in relation to something else; not absolute.
   - *(Grammar)* Attributive or predicative; often followed by *to* when used predicatively.
   
   **Synonyms**: comparative, dependent, contextual, proportional
   
   **Antonyms**: absolute, fixed, independent, unconditional
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing that a judgment or advantage only makes sense *vis-à-vis* some baseline (for example, *relative advantage*, *relative risk*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the property is absolute (for example, *dead/alive*); then *absolute* or *binary* may be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - relative advantage vis-à-vis peers
   - relative risk
   - relative position in the market
   - relative to X
   
   **Root Analysis**: relate (connect) + -ive (having the nature of)
   
   **Etymology**: From Late Latin *relativus* “having reference”; adopted into English philosophical vocabulary.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our success is relative vis-à-vis the overall market mood.
   - Their strength is mostly relative to weak competition.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We assess relative performance vis-à-vis sector benchmarks.
   - Relative bargaining strength vis-à-vis suppliers is analyzed in Section 3.

1. **Adjective**: asymmetric (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not having equal or balanced parts; unequal *vis-à-vis* the other side in power, size, information, or obligations.
   
   **Synonyms**: unbalanced, unequal, lopsided, skewed, one‑sided
   
   **Antonyms**: symmetric, balanced, even, equal, proportional
   
   **When to Use**: Describing structural differences *vis-à-vis* risk, rights, or power (for example, *asymmetric information vis-à-vis users*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When differences are minor or not structural; then *slightly different* or *uneven* may be better.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - asymmetric power vis-à-vis platforms
   - asymmetric obligations
   - asymmetric relationship
   - highly asymmetric negotiation
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (not) + sym (together) + metric (measure)
   
   **Etymology**: From Greek *asymmetria*; technical term in geometry adopted into economics and politics.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The deal is pretty asymmetric vis-à-vis what each side gives up.
   - Users are in a very asymmetric position compared to big platforms.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract creates asymmetric obligations vis-à-vis liability and recourse.
   - Asymmetric information vis-à-vis consumers raises regulatory concerns.

1. **Adjective**: balanced (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Fairly arranged or distributed; not giving one side undue weight *vis-à-vis* another.
   
   **Synonyms**: fair, even, proportionate, equitable, well‑proportioned
   
   **Antonyms**: unbalanced, lopsided, skewed, biased, one‑sided
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about trade‑offs, coverage, or power that feel fair *vis-à-vis* all parties.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need a strict mathematical sense (then *in equilibrium*) or explicitly legal term (*equitable*).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - balanced view vis-à-vis stakeholders
   - balanced obligations and rights
   - balanced portfolio vis-à-vis risk
   - reasonably balanced outcome
   
   **Root Analysis**: balance (state of equilibrium) + -ed (having that quality)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *balance* (scales), then metaphorically to fairness and proportion.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This feels like a more balanced deal vis-à-vis effort and reward.
   - Try to give a balanced view, not just one side.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The agreement seeks a balanced allocation of risks vis-à-vis all signatories.
   - The report offers a balanced assessment vis-à-vis benefits and externalities.

1. **Adjective**: disadvantaged (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an unfavorable position *vis-à-vis* others in terms of power, resources, or opportunity.
   
   **Synonyms**: weaker, underprivileged, marginalized, behind, under‑resourced
   
   **Antonyms**: advantaged, privileged, empowered, well‑positioned
   
   **When to Use**: Describing structural or situational weakness *vis-à-vis* a stronger party or benchmark.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When disadvantage is temporary and small (then *slightly behind*); or when it would stigmatize individuals in sensitive contexts—use with care.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disadvantaged vis-à-vis large incumbents
   - structurally disadvantaged stakeholders
   - disadvantaged communities
   - disadvantaged position in negotiations
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (not) + advantage (benefit) + -ed
   
   **Etymology**: From *advantage* (via French) with negative prefix; used widely in social policy.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As a small startup, we’re disadvantaged vis-à-vis big brands.
   - Users are pretty disadvantaged compared to platforms here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smaller providers are structurally disadvantaged vis-à-vis vertically integrated platforms.
   - The framework protects disadvantaged groups vis-à-vis data‑sharing risks.

1. **Adjective**: advantaged (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a favorable or superior position *vis-à-vis* others.
   
   **Synonyms**: privileged, well‑positioned, stronger, favored, ahead
   
   **Antonyms**: disadvantaged, weaker, marginalized, behind
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing that a party enjoys superior leverage or conditions *vis-à-vis* others.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the advantage is tiny or situational; then *slightly ahead* may sound more neutral.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - structurally advantaged
   - advantaged vis-à-vis smaller players
   - more advantaged position
   - relatively advantaged group
   
   **Root Analysis**: advantage (benefit) + -ed
   
   **Etymology**: Parallel to *disadvantaged*; widely used in sociology and policy discussions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Big platforms are hugely advantaged vis-à-vis small merchants.
   - With that data, you’re advantaged compared to everyone else.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Incumbent firms remain significantly advantaged vis-à-vis new entrants.
   - The tax code leaves some asset classes structurally advantaged vis-à-vis others.

1. **Adjective**: equivalent (gradable in practice, though logic treats it as non‑gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the same value, function, or effect *vis-à-vis* a standard or another item.
   
   **Synonyms**: equal, interchangeable, tantamount, virtually the same
   
   **Antonyms**: unequal, inferior, superior, non‑equivalent
   
   **When to Use**: Saying that option A is effectively the same as B *vis-à-vis* outcome, cost, or rights.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When small differences matter; then *roughly comparable* or *similar* may be safer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - functionally equivalent
   - equivalent vis-à-vis performance
   - roughly equivalent in cost
   - legal equivalent
   
   **Root Analysis**: equi- (equal) + valent (worth)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *aequivalens*; in English since the 15th century, strong in mathematics and law.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These two offers are basically equivalent vis-à-vis money; the difference is culture.
   - The two tools are equivalent for what we need.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The remedies are functionally equivalent vis-à-vis consumer protection.
   - The proposed design is equivalent, vis-à-vis security guarantees, to the baseline.

1. **Adjective**: disproportionate (gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Too large or too small in comparison *vis-à-vis* something else; not in fair proportion.
   
   **Synonyms**: excessive, lopsided, unbalanced, outsized, unequal
   
   **Antonyms**: proportionate, reasonable, balanced, commensurate
   
   **When to Use**: Highlighting that risks, burdens, or benefits are misaligned *vis-à-vis* what would be fair or expected.
   
   **When NOT TO Use**: For minor differences; when you cannot justify the claim of unfairness.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disproportionate impact vis-à-vis vulnerable groups
   - disproportionate burden
   - disproportionate benefit
   - disproportionate risk vis-à-vis reward
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (not) + proportion (proper relation in size) + -ate
   
   **Etymology**: From Medieval Latin *disproportionatus*; now common in legal, ethical, and policy language.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The stress is disproportionate vis-à-vis the pay.
   - That punishment is totally disproportionate to what happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy creates a disproportionate burden vis-à-vis small organizations.
   - Enforcement actions must avoid disproportionate penalties vis-à-vis actual harm.

1. **Adjective**: peer (non-gradable as adjective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having equal status or level *vis-à-vis* others in a defined group (for example, *peer companies*, *peer reviewers*).
   
   **Synonyms**: equivalent‑level, same‑tier, comparable‑status, same‑rank
   
   **Antonyms**: subordinate, superior, non‑comparable, out‑of‑tier
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to entities treated as a reference set for comparison *vis-à-vis* performance, behavior, or norms.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When hierarchy is central (then *senior*/*junior*/ *subordinate* are clearer).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - peer company vis-à-vis size
   - peer group
   - peer benchmark
   - peer institutions
   
   **Root Analysis**: peer (equal in rank) used attributively
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *per* “equal”; adjectival use is modern and business‑driven.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How do we look vis-à-vis our peer companies?
   - Your offer is solid compared to your peer group.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Compensation is evaluated vis-à-vis peer institutions in the same region.
   - Peer organizations vis-à-vis our sector are listed in Appendix B.
