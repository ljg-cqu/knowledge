1. **Linking Word**: vis-à-vis  
   **Function**: Contrastive/relational marker between two entities  
   **Typical Sentence Role**: Preposition or adverb inside the clause, rarely clause‑initial.
   
   **Meaning / Use**: 
   - Signals how one party, idea, or outcome should be viewed *in relation to* another.
   - Often used when talking about power, obligations, leverage, or comparative standing.
   
   **Register**: Semi‑formal → formal; more written than spoken.
   
   **Synonyms**: in relation to, with respect to, relative to, as against, compared with  
   **Antonyms**: independently of, regardless of, separately from
   
   **Position & Grammar**:
   - mid‑clause: *our position vis-à-vis competitors*, *obligations vis-à-vis users*
   - usually links noun phrases; not followed by a full finite clause.
   
   **When to Use**:
   - You want concise, slightly formal language for relational or comparative talk.
   - You are writing reports, analyses, or policy documents.
   
   **When NOT to Use**:
   - Very casual chat (may sound affected).
   - Audiences unfamiliar with French‑origin markers.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:  
   - We’re in a decent spot vis-à-vis the other teams now.  
   - The union is a lot stronger vis-à-vis management after the vote.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:  
   - The firm’s bargaining power vis-à-vis key platforms has declined.  
   - Citizens’ rights vis-à-vis state surveillance remain contested.

1. **Linking Word**: in relation to  
   **Function**: Topic/relational marker  
   **Typical Sentence Role**: Prepositional phrase introducing the dimension or topic.
   
   **Meaning / Use**: 
   - Focuses attention on one aspect or topic (cost, timing, rights) used to evaluate something else.
   
   **Register**: Neutral; common in both spoken and written English.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, as far as … is concerned  
   **Antonyms**: irrespective of, regardless of, apart from
   
   **Position & Grammar**:
   - clause‑initial: *In relation to cost, this option is risky.*
   - mid‑clause: *The salary is good in relation to living costs.*
   
   **When to Use**:
   - You want clear, accessible linking without sounding legalistic.
   - You are introducing the frame for comparison.
   
   **When NOT to Use**:
   - When a simple *about* or *on* is enough in informal speech.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:  
   - In relation to time, this project is pretty heavy.  
   - What do you think in relation to our current workload?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:  
   - In relation to privacy, the proposal remains incomplete.  
   - The report focuses on risks in relation to cross‑border data flows.

1. **Linking Word**: with respect to  
   **Function**: Topic narrowing / aspect marking  
   **Typical Sentence Role**: Prepositional phrase, often clause‑initial.
   
   **Meaning / Use**: 
   - Narrows the claim to a specific dimension: *with respect to accuracy*, *with respect to cost*.
   
   **Register**: Formal; typical in reports, academic, and legal writing.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, in respect of (BrE), in relation to  
   **Antonyms**: irrespective of, ignoring, without regard to
   
   **Position & Grammar**:
   - clause‑initial: *With respect to latency, design B is better.*
   - mid‑clause: *They differ with respect to long‑term stability.*
   
   **When to Use**:
   - You need precise, polite transition into a technical point.
   
   **When NOT to Use**:
   - Casual emails or speech (can sound stiff).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:  
   - With respect to budget, we’re tight this quarter.  
   - I’ve got no concerns with respect to quality, just timing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:  
   - With respect to security, both architectures are equivalent.  
   - Employees’ obligations with respect to confidential data are detailed in section 4.

1. **Linking Word**: relative to  
   **Function**: Comparative marker  
   **Typical Sentence Role**: Prepositional phrase marking the baseline.
   
   **Meaning / Use**: 
   - Emphasizes that a statement only makes sense when compared to a reference point: *high relative to peers*.
   
   **Register**: Neutral → formal; frequent in analytic writing.
   
   **Synonyms**: compared with, in comparison with, vis-à-vis, in proportion to  
   **Antonyms**: absolutely, independently of
   
   **Position & Grammar**:
   - mid‑clause: *Our revenue is small relative to our costs.*
   - follows adjectives: *expensive relative to income*.
   
   **When to Use**:
   - Presenting data, metrics, or rankings.
   
   **When NOT to Use**:
   - When no baseline has been introduced; readers may ask *relative to what?*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:  
   - The rent is high relative to what you earn.  
   - We’re doing well relative to last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:  
   - Returns were modest relative to the level of risk assumed.  
   - Productivity gains were significant relative to the investment.

1. **Linking Word**: compared with / compared to  
   **Function**: Explicit comparison  
   **Typical Sentence Role**: Prepositional phrase; sometimes reduced to *compared with X, …* at clause start.
   
   **Meaning / Use**: 
   - Directly sets one item against another in the reader’s mind.
   
   **Register**: Neutral; widely used.
   
   **Synonyms**: in comparison with, vis-à-vis, relative to  
   **Antonyms**: taken alone, considered in isolation
   
   **Position & Grammar**:
   - clause‑initial: *Compared with last year, results have improved.*
   - mid‑clause: *Our costs are low compared with industry averages.*
   
   **When to Use**:
   - You want clear, learner‑friendly comparison without extra nuance.
   
   **When NOT to Use**:
   - For subtle discourse roles like concession or contrast; use *however, whereas,* etc. there.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:  
   - Compared to the old version, this is way faster.  
   - Our deal is good compared with what others got.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:  
   - Compared with the control group, treated users showed higher engagement.  
   - Costs remain high compared with regional benchmarks.

1. **Linking Word**: in contrast (to)  
   **Function**: Contrastive link between two statements  
   **Typical Sentence Role**: Clause‑initial discourse marker.
   
   **Meaning / Use**: 
   - Highlights clear difference between two items or situations *vis-à-vis* each other.
   
   **Register**: Neutral → formal.
   
   **Synonyms**: by contrast, conversely, whereas (clause linker)  
   **Antonyms**: similarly, in the same way, correspondingly
   
   **Position & Grammar**:
   - usually at the start: *In contrast, plan B carries lower risk.*
   - sometimes followed by *to*: *In contrast to X, Y is cheaper.*
   
   **When to Use**:
   - You want to guide the reader to a sharp difference.
   
   **When NOT to Use**:
   - When differences are minor; *slightly different from* may be more honest.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:  
   - A is risky; in contrast, B is pretty safe.  
   - In contrast to last time, we’re better prepared.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:  
   - In contrast to incumbents, the startup is heavily dependent on one platform.  
   - The two models, in contrast, make opposite assumptions vis-à-vis user behavior.

1. **Linking Word**: conversely  
   **Function**: Signaling an opposite perspective or consequence  
   **Typical Sentence Role**: Clause‑initial adverb.
   
   **Meaning / Use**: 
   - Introduces a statement that reverses direction *vis-à-vis* the previous one.
   
   **Register**: Formal / academic.
   
   **Synonyms**: on the other hand, by contrast, in the opposite direction  
   **Antonyms**: similarly, likewise
   
   **Position & Grammar**:
   - clause‑initial, separated by comma: *Conversely, …*
   
   **When to Use**:
   - Writing analytical text where you want explicit logical contrast.
   
   **When NOT to Use**:
   - Casual speech; *on the other hand* is more natural.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**:  
   - It helps with speed; conversely, it hurts our position vis-à-vis privacy.  
   - We gain flexibility; conversely, we lose leverage.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**:  
   - Conversely, from the regulator’s standpoint vis-à-vis systemic risk, the change is undesirable.  
   - Greater centralization can, conversely, strengthen our position vis-à-vis suppliers.
