1. **Adverb**: relatively (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a certain extent when compared *vis-à-vis* something else; not absolutely.
   
   **Synonyms**: comparatively, somewhat, fairly, to some extent
   
   **Antonyms**: absolutely, completely, extremely (when used without comparison)
   
   **When to Use**: Soften statements by framing them *vis-à-vis* a reference point (for example, *relatively safe vis-à-vis other options*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you mean *in relation to family members* (adjective *relative*), or when comparison baseline is unclear.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - relatively speaking
   - relatively cheap / safe / simple
   - relatively strong vis-à-vis competitors
   
   **Root Analysis**: relative (adjective) + -ly (adverb)
   
   **Etymology**: From *relative* (Latin *relativus*); adverbial form common in 19th‑century prose.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re relatively well‑positioned vis-à-vis the other teams.
   - It’s relatively low‑risk compared to what we tried last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system remains relatively robust vis-à-vis moderate traffic spikes.
   - Returns are relatively modest vis-à-vis sector averages.

1. **Adverb**: comparatively (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - When viewed *vis-à-vis* something else; in a way that shows comparison.
   
   **Synonyms**: relatively, by comparison, in comparison, proportionally
   
   **Antonyms**: absolutely, inherently, intrinsically
   
   **When to Use**: In analytic or formal writing where you want to signal that a judgment holds only in comparison with some benchmark.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual speech (can sound stiff), or when there is no explicit comparison.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - comparatively small / large
   - comparatively low risk vis-à-vis peers
   - comparatively speaking
   
   **Root Analysis**: comparative (relating to comparison) + -ly
   
   **Etymology**: From *comparative*; used as sentence adverb in academic prose.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Comparatively, this option is less painful vis-à-vis our schedule.
   - The new tool is comparatively easy to learn.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The platform is comparatively weak vis-à-vis data‑governance features.
   - Costs remain comparatively high vis-à-vis industry medians.

1. **Adverb**: conversely (type: sentence/discourse)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the opposite way; from the opposite perspective *vis-à-vis* what was just stated.
   
   **Synonyms**: on the other hand, by contrast, in reverse, in the opposite direction
   
   **Antonyms**: similarly, likewise, correspondingly
   
   **When to Use**: To flip perspective *vis-à-vis* an earlier claim (for example, *conversely, from the user’s standpoint…*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you merely add information (use *furthermore* or *in addition*), or in very casual talk (sounds academic).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - conversely, …
   - may conversely lead to
   - conversely framed
   
   **Root Analysis**: converse (to turn about) + -ly
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *conversus* “turned about”; discourse‑marker use well‑established in formal writing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We gain flexibility; conversely, we lose some control vis-à-vis quality.
   - The cloud helps with speed; conversely, it weakens our position vis-à-vis vendors.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Conversely, from the regulator’s standpoint vis-à-vis systemic risk, the change is undesirable.
   - Greater centralization may reduce local autonomy; conversely, it strengthens negotiating power vis-à-vis suppliers.

1. **Adverb**: proportionally (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that keeps the same ratio *vis-à-vis* another quantity; in proportion.
   
   **Synonyms**: correspondingly, commensurately, in proportion, relatively
   
   **Antonyms**: disproportionately, unevenly, unfairly, excessively
   
   **When to Use**: Quantitative, legal, and ethical discussions about how costs, benefits, or penalties scale *vis-à-vis* another factor.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no numeric or logical ratio is intended.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - increase proportionally
   - proportionally higher / lower
   - share costs proportionally vis-à-vis usage
   
   **Root Analysis**: proportion (relative size) + -al + -ly
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *proportio*; adverbial form common in economics and law.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone should contribute proportionally vis-à-vis what they earn.
   - If risk goes up, your buffer should increase proportionally.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Penalties should be imposed proportionally vis-à-vis the severity of non‑compliance.
   - Fees scale proportionally vis-à-vis transaction volume.

1. **Adverb**: disproportionately (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To an extent that is not fair or reasonable *vis-à-vis* something else; out of proportion.
   
   **Synonyms**: excessively, unfairly, unduly, overly, unreasonably
   
   **Antonyms**: proportionately, fairly, evenly, equitably
   
   **When to Use**: To highlight unfair impact or misaligned outcomes across groups or options.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you cannot justify the claim of unfairness; when you only mean *a lot* (use *significantly* instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disproportionately affect
   - disproportionately high / low
   - disproportionately exposed vis-à-vis risk
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (not) + proportion (proper ratio) + -ate + -ly
   
   **Etymology**: From *disproportionate*; widely used in policy and ethics debates.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The costs fall disproportionately on juniors vis-à-vis the benefits.
   - That rule disproportionately hits small teams.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulation disproportionately burdens SMEs vis-à-vis large incumbents.
   - Negative externalities fall disproportionately on communities with little power vis-à-vis decision makers.

1. **Adverb**: respectively (type: sentence/discourse)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the order given for each of two or more items; mapping items in one list *vis-à-vis* items in another.
   
   **Synonyms**: in that order, in corresponding order, each in turn
   
   **Antonyms**: (none direct; misuse leads to confusion rather than an opposite meaning)
   
   **When to Use**: To make clear which number or attribute matches which entity, especially in formal writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual speech (often overkill), when lists don’t line up one‑to‑one.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - A and B scored 60 and 80, respectively.
   - X, Y, and Z correspond to 1, 2, and 3, respectively.
   
   **Root Analysis**: respect (regard) + -ive + -ly
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *respectivus* “looking back at each”; English since the 15th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Plan A and Plan B cost 5k and 8k, respectively.
   - London and Berlin are bigger than their neighboring cities, respectively.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The control and treatment groups contained 42 and 39 participants, respectively.
   - Latency and throughput improved by 12% and 18%, respectively, vis-à-vis the baseline.

1. **Adverb**: symmetrically (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is the same on both sides *vis-à-vis* a center, axis, or counterpart.
   
   **Synonyms**: evenly, equally, mirror‑like, in balance
   
   **Antonyms**: asymmetrically, unevenly, lopsidedly, irregularly
   
   **When to Use**: Describing designs, rights, or obligations that are equal for both sides.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to emphasize productive inequality; then *deliberately asymmetric* might be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - applied symmetrically vis-à-vis parties
   - symmetrically distributed
   - symmetrically structured contract
   
   **Root Analysis**: sym- (together) + metric (measure) + -ally
   
   **Etymology**: From *symmetric* (Greek *symmetria*); used in math, design, and law.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The rules don’t really apply symmetrically vis-à-vis managers and staff.
   - We tried to share the work symmetrically between the two teams.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Obligations should apply symmetrically vis-à-vis buyers and sellers.
   - Access controls are not enforced symmetrically across user groups.

1. **Adverb**: asymmetrically (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is uneven or unbalanced between two sides *vis-à-vis* power, risk, or duties.
   
   **Synonyms**: unevenly, unequally, lopsidedly, disproportionately
   
   **Antonyms**: symmetrically, evenly, fairly, proportionally
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to stress that obligations or benefits do not mirror each other across parties.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the imbalance is minor; when you cannot justify a strong fairness claim.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - asymmetrically distributed
   - applied asymmetrically vis-à-vis groups
   - asymmetrically shared risk
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (not) + symmetric + -ally
   
   **Etymology**: From *asymmetric*; common in political economy and law.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The risks are asymmetrically on our side vis-à-vis the upside.
   - The rules hit us asymmetrically compared to bigger players.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Liabilities are asymmetrically allocated vis-à-vis contractual control.
   - Benefits accrue asymmetrically to shareholders vis-à-vis frontline workers.

1. **Adverb**: vis-à-vis (type: place/abstract relation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Face to face; opposite each other (older use).
   - *(Abstract)* In relation to; with respect to; viewed in comparison with.
   
   **Synonyms**: opposite (spatial), in relation to, with respect to, relative to, as against
   
   **Antonyms**: independently, separately, regardless, apart
   
   **When to Use**: In formal or semi‑formal contexts to succinctly mark how something is viewed *vis-à-vis* another party, risk, or benchmark.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual speech; when readers may find the French spelling distracting; where simpler English prepositions are sufficient.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sit vis-à-vis each other
   - strong vis-à-vis competitors
   - weak vis-à-vis regulation
   - obligations vis-à-vis users
   
   **Root Analysis**: French *vis* (face) + *à* (to) + *vis* (face)
   
   **Etymology**: Borrowed from French; adverbial use (“they sat vis-à-vis”) predates wider prepositional use.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re in a good spot vis-à-vis the other teams right now.
   - The union is stronger vis-à-vis management after the vote.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The firm remains relatively exposed vis-à-vis data‑protection authorities.
   - Households are in a weaker position vis-à-vis energy providers than a decade ago.
