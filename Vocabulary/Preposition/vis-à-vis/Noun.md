1. **Noun**: position (C/U, pl: positions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The place, status, or situation that someone or something occupies, especially *vis-à-vis* others in a comparison, negotiation, or hierarchy.
   - *(Sense 2)* A particular opinion or stance that someone takes on an issue.
   
   **Synonyms**: status, standing, situation, posture, stance
   
   **Antonyms**: insignificance, obscurity, disadvantage, weakness (in relative terms)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about where you stand *vis-à-vis* competitors, partners, risks, or goals; describing both concrete power/status and abstract viewpoints.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you mean just physical place (use *location* or *place*), or when only personal opinion is relevant (often better as *view* or *opinion*).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - market position
   - bargaining position
   - our position vis-à-vis competitors
   - negotiating position
   - position of strength / position of weakness
   
   **Root Analysis**: posit (place, put) + -ion (state or result)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *positio* (placing, situation) via Old French into Middle English; extended from literal placement to abstract status and stance.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What’s our position vis-à-vis the other teams right now?
   - After the new launch, our position in the market feels a lot stronger.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company’s financial position vis-à-vis its main competitors has improved markedly.
   - This memo clarifies our position vis-à-vis the new regulatory guidelines.

1. **Noun**: standpoint (C, pl: standpoints)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A particular way of thinking about something, especially when comparing perspectives *vis-à-vis* other stakeholders or interests.
   
   **Synonyms**: perspective, viewpoint, angle, point of view, outlook
   
   **Antonyms**: neutrality, indifference, lack of perspective
   
   **When to Use**: Highlighting that an evaluation, right, or obligation is being considered from one party’s side *vis-à-vis* another (for example, *from a user’s standpoint vis-à-vis privacy*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you mean overall situation (prefer *position*), or when the focus is on legal status rather than subjective view.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - from a financial standpoint
   - from the user’s standpoint
   - ethical standpoint vis-à-vis data use
   - legal standpoint
   
   **Root Analysis**: stand (remain fixed) + point (location, spot)
   
   **Etymology**: 19th-century English formation based on earlier *point of view*; emphasizes fixed perspective.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - From my standpoint, vis-à-vis time and energy, this project is too expensive.
   - From the customer’s standpoint, the new policy is a step back.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - From a governance standpoint vis-à-vis stakeholders, transparency is non‑negotiable.
   - The paper examines AI deployment from the standpoint of workers vis-à-vis management.

1. **Noun**: counterpart (C, pl: counterparts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that has the same function or status as another in a different place, organization, or context, often considered *vis-à-vis* that other.
   
   **Synonyms**: equivalent, peer, opposite number, parallel, analogue
   
   **Antonyms**: unrelated party, outsider, subordinate (in strongly asymmetric relations)
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to the “other side” *vis-à-vis* you: your counterpart in negotiations, government, partner firms, or other teams.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the relation is clearly hierarchical rather than parallel; then *superior*, *subordinate*, or *report* may be clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - my counterpart in legal / finance
   - foreign counterparts
   - public sector counterparts
   - speak to your counterpart vis-à-vis this issue
   
   **Root Analysis**: counter- (matching, corresponding) + part (thing or role)
   
   **Etymology**: From Middle French *contrepartie* (copy, duplicate) into English in the 15th century; extended to “corresponding person/role” across organizations.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’ll check with my counterpart in marketing vis-à-vis the launch date.
   - Her counterpart in the partner company pushed back on the changes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Government counterparts were consulted vis-à-vis cross‑border data transfers.
   - Each department’s counterpart in the joint venture is listed in Appendix A.

1. **Noun**: relationship (C/U, pl: relationships)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The way in which two or more people, groups, or things are connected and affect each other, especially their relative power, duties, or expectations *vis-à-vis* one another.
   
   **Synonyms**: connection, association, tie, link, dynamic
   
   **Antonyms**: disconnection, separation, isolation, detachment
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the overall pattern of interaction *vis-à-vis* partners, regulators, employees, or concepts (for example, *the relationship between cost and risk*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need a narrower legal word like *obligation*, *liability*, or *contract*; or when you mean simple kinship (then *relative*, *parent*, etc. may be clearer).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the relationship between A and B
   - power relationship vis-à-vis suppliers
   - client relationship
   - long‑term relationship with regulators
   
   **Root Analysis**: relate (connect) + -ship (state or condition)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *relatio* via Old French; in English since the 17th century to describe connections and dependencies.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What’s our relationship vis-à-vis that platform—are we a partner or just a customer?
   - The relationship between price and quality is tricky here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The firm’s relationship vis-à-vis strategic partners is governed by long‑term contracts.
   - We analyze the relationship between latency and throughput vis-à-vis user satisfaction.

1. **Noun**: leverage (U/C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The power to influence people, decisions, or outcomes, especially *vis-à-vis* other parties.
   - *(Finance)* The use of borrowed money; here often extended metaphorically to bargaining power.
   
   **Synonyms**: influence, bargaining power, clout, advantage, sway
   
   **Antonyms**: weakness, vulnerability, lack of influence, disadvantage
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about how much power you have *vis-à-vis* negotiators, platforms, regulators, or competitors.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In strictly financial contexts if you only mean debt ratio (then be explicit: *financial leverage*); in casual speech where simpler words like *influence* work.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - leverage vis-à-vis suppliers
   - strategic leverage
   - lose / gain leverage
   - little leverage in negotiations
   
   **Root Analysis**: lever (tool to lift) + -age (result or amount)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *levier* (lever); figurative sense of “power to influence” emerged in 20th‑century business English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We don’t have much leverage vis-à-vis that platform—they’re huge.
   - This data gives us real leverage in the discussion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smaller vendors often lack bargaining leverage vis-à-vis dominant marketplaces.
   - The consortium gained additional leverage vis-à-vis regulators by coordinating its position.

1. **Noun**: asymmetry (C/U, pl: asymmetries)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A lack of equality or balance between two sides *vis-à-vis* power, information, risk, or obligations.
   
   **Synonyms**: imbalance, disparity, inequality, disproportion, skew
   
   **Antonyms**: symmetry, parity, balance, equivalence
   
   **When to Use**: Analyzing where one party stands in a stronger or weaker position *vis-à-vis* another (for example, information asymmetry between platforms and users).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the difference is small or not structurally important (then *difference* or *gap* may be enough).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - power asymmetry
   - information asymmetry vis-à-vis users
   - asymmetry between buyer and seller
   - asymmetry in obligations
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (not) + sym (together) + metry (measure)
   
   **Etymology**: From Greek *asymmetria* (lack of proportion), entering English technical vocabulary in mathematics and later in economics and politics.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There’s huge asymmetry between us and that big tech company.
   - The info asymmetry vis-à-vis users is pretty scary.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Information asymmetry vis-à-vis end users raises serious accountability questions.
   - The contract entrenches a structural asymmetry between platform and providers.

1. **Noun**: parity (U/C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being equal or equivalent in status, value, or power *vis-à-vis* another party or benchmark.
   
   **Synonyms**: equality, equivalence, balance, symmetry, evenness
   
   **Antonyms**: disparity, imbalance, inequality, asymmetry
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing whether one side has “caught up” with another *vis-à-vis* pay, rights, capabilities, or performance.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you simply mean fairness (then *equity* may be better), or when you talk about exact identity rather than broad equivalence (*identicality*).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pay parity
   - parity with competitors
   - reach parity vis-à-vis peers
   - achieve parity in rights
   
   **Root Analysis**: par (equal) + -ity (state or quality)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *paritas* (equality) via Old French into English; used in law, economics, and politics.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We finally have pay parity vis-à-vis the other team.
   - Their features are now basically at parity with ours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy aims to achieve parity vis-à-vis male and female employees’ benefits.
   - Several smaller providers have reached functional parity with the incumbent.

1. **Noun**: imbalance (C/U, pl: imbalances)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A situation in which two or more things are not in equal or correct proportions *vis-à-vis* each other.
   
   **Synonyms**: disproportion, inequality, asymmetry, skew, tilt
   
   **Antonyms**: balance, parity, symmetry, equilibrium
   
   **When to Use**: Describing unfair or risky conditions *vis-à-vis* workload, power, risk, or rewards between parties or options.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When minor variation is normal (then *variation* or *difference* may be better), or when the concern is strictly numeric ratio (then specify it).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - power imbalance vis-à-vis management
   - imbalance between risk and reward
   - workload imbalance
   - structural imbalance in rights vis-à-vis duties
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (not) + balance (state of being in equilibrium)
   
   **Etymology**: From French *déséquilibre* conceptually, but English *imbalance* formed on *balance* with negating prefix; widely used in social sciences.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There’s a big imbalance between what we give and what we get back.
   - The power imbalance vis-à-vis the platform is obvious.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract perpetuates a pronounced imbalance in obligations vis-à-vis enforcement rights.
   - Policy reforms seek to correct imbalances between data controllers and data subjects.

1. **Noun**: bargaining power (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The ability of a party to obtain favorable terms in a negotiation *vis-à-vis* another party.
   
   **Synonyms**: negotiating power, leverage, clout, influence, pull
   
   **Antonyms**: weakness, lack of leverage, dependency, vulnerability
   
   **When to Use**: In business, labor, or policy contexts when discussing who has the upper hand *vis-à-vis* whom.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Outside of negotiation or price‑setting situations; for general influence, *leverage* or *influence* may be broader.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strong bargaining power vis-à-vis suppliers
   - limited bargaining power
   - shift in bargaining power
   - worker bargaining power vis-à-vis employers
   
   **Root Analysis**: bargain (negotiate terms) + -ing (process) + power (ability to act)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrase from industrial relations and economics, frequent in discussions of labor and market structure.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As a small client, we don’t have much bargaining power vis-à-vis that vendor.
   - Unions give workers more bargaining power against big companies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Platform concentration significantly increases its bargaining power vis-à-vis dependent sellers.
   - Collective action enhanced workers’ bargaining power vis-à-vis management.

1. **Noun**: status (C/U, pl: statuses)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The social, legal, or professional standing of a person or entity *vis-à-vis* others.
   - *(Sense 2)* The current state or condition of something (for example, project status), sometimes compared *vis-à-vis* plan or peers.
   
   **Synonyms**: standing, rank, level, situation, condition
   
   **Antonyms**: insignificance, low rank, subordination, decline
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about where someone or something sits *vis-à-vis* others in hierarchy, rights, or progress.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring purely to job title (use *role* or *title*), or when you need a dynamic process word like *trend*.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - legal status vis-à-vis the state
   - status vis-à-vis competitors
   - project status
   - change in status
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *status* (state, condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Directly from Latin into English in legal and formal registers; still associated with official or high‑register discourse.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What’s our status vis-à-vis that contract—signed or still pending?
   - Their status in the industry has really grown in the last few years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy redefines platform status vis-à-vis content providers.
   - Participant status vis-à-vis control and treatment groups is documented in Table 1.
