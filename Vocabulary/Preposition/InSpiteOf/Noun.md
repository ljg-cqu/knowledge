1. **Noun**: obstacle (C, pl: obstacles)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that blocks progress or makes it difficult to achieve a goal.
   - A problem or barrier that must be overcome for success in spite of difficult conditions.
   
   **Synonyms**: barrier, hindrance, impediment, hurdle, obstruction
   
   **Antonyms**: advantage, aid, assistance, help, facilitator
   
   **When to Use**: When describing concrete or abstract things that people must overcome in order to succeed in spite of circumstances.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you are mainly talking about people who oppose you (use "opposition"), or for very small annoyances (use "difficulty").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overcome obstacles in spite of limited resources
   - face many obstacles in spite of careful planning
   - a major obstacle in spite of strong support
   - obstacles in the way of progress
   
   **Root Analysis**: ob- (against) + sta (stand) + -cle (diminutive noun suffix) → "something standing in the way".
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *obstaculum* (hindrance) → Old French → English *obstacle*, naturally linked with efforts that continue in spite of resistance.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of all the obstacles, she finished her degree.
   - They ran into obstacle after obstacle but kept going in spite of everything.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of significant regulatory obstacles, the organization achieved compliance within the planned timeframe.
   - The team delivered meaningful results in spite of technical obstacles that initially seemed insurmountable.

1. **Noun**: challenge (C, pl: challenges)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A difficult task or situation that tests someone's skill, strength, or determination.
   - A problem that invites effort and growth, often framed positively even when it must be faced in spite of fear or doubt.
   
   **Synonyms**: difficulty, test, trial, hurdle, demanding task
   
   **Antonyms**: ease, simplicity, advantage, opportunity (without difficulty)
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to talk about difficult situations that can be met in a constructive way, especially when success comes in spite of the difficulty.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the situation is catastrophic or overwhelming ("disaster" or "crisis" may be more accurate) rather than simply demanding.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - face challenges in spite of limited support
   - overcome challenges in spite of setbacks
   - rise to the challenge in spite of self‑doubt
   - a serious challenge in spite of good preparation
   
   **Root Analysis**: from Old French *chalenge* (accusation, claim) → extended to "test of ability".
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *chalenge* → Middle English *chalenge* → *challenge*, now widely used for things we take on in spite of their difficulty.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It was a real challenge, but in spite of that we got it done.
   - She enjoys challenges and keeps going in spite of how hard they are.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of numerous operational challenges, service quality remained stable across all regions.
   - The study addresses theoretical challenges that earlier work avoided in spite of their importance.

1. **Noun**: setback (C, pl: setbacks)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A problem or event that delays progress or pushes you back after you have moved forward.
   - A temporary reverse that must be recovered from in spite of disappointment.
   
   **Synonyms**: reversal, delay, hindrance, stumble, complication
   
   **Antonyms**: breakthrough, advance, progress, improvement, leap forward
   
   **When to Use**: When progress is slowed or temporarily reversed, but you expect to continue in spite of the problem.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When something is a complete and final failure ("collapse" or "failure" is stronger), or when the impact is trivial.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - a temporary setback in spite of long‑term progress
   - suffer setbacks but continue in spite of them
   - a serious setback in spite of earlier success
   - recover from setbacks in spite of limited time
   
   **Root Analysis**: set (put) + back (in a backward direction) → to move backwards from a target.
   
   **Etymology**: Formed in English; from the idea of being "set back" from one's previous position, yet continuing in spite of it.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of a few setbacks, the project is going well now.
   - We hit a big setback but decided to continue in spite of it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of early setbacks, the initiative eventually achieved all stated objectives.
   - The organisation recovered quickly from financial setbacks in spite of external market volatility.

1. **Noun**: adversity (U/C, pl: adversities)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Difficult or unpleasant situations; serious hardship or misfortune.
   - The general condition of having to continue in spite of severe problems.
   
   **Synonyms**: hardship, misfortune, difficulty, trouble, affliction
   
   **Antonyms**: prosperity, ease, comfort, good fortune, advantage
   
   **When to Use**: In more formal or literary contexts when speaking about the broad, serious difficulties a person or group faces and works through in spite of them.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For everyday small problems (use "issues" or "problems"), or in very casual conversation where it sounds too dramatic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overcome adversity in spite of limited support
   - grow through adversity in spite of pain
   - triumph over adversity in spite of the odds
   - resilience in adversity
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (toward) + vers (turn) + -ity (state) → "things turned against you".
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *adversitas* (misfortune) → Old French → English *adversity*, often used with stories of success in spite of hardship.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of all the adversity, she stayed hopeful.
   - He went through a lot of adversity but kept moving in spite of it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of considerable adversity, the community demonstrated strong mutual support and long‑term resilience.
   - The biography traces how the subject achieved influence in spite of early life adversity.

1. **Noun**: hardship (C/U, pl: hardships)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Serious difficulty, especially involving lack of money, comfort, or security.
   - Tough living or working conditions that people survive or act through in spite of suffering.
   
   **Synonyms**: suffering, privation, deprivation, adversity, distress
   
   **Antonyms**: comfort, luxury, ease, prosperity, security
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasising how hard conditions are, especially when praising those who keep going in spite of them.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For small inconveniences (sounds exaggerated), or when the main focus is emotional pain rather than external conditions.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - endure hardship in spite of lack of support
   - economic hardship in spite of full‑time work
   - extreme hardship faced in spite of promises
   - hardship and sacrifice in spite of expectations
   
   **Root Analysis**: hard (difficult) + -ship (state or condition) → "state of difficulty".
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *hardshippe* → modern *hardship*, a common noun in narratives of succeeding in spite of tough conditions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of the hardship, they refused to give up.
   - The family went through real hardship in spite of doing everything right.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of prolonged economic hardship, the organisation maintained basic services to vulnerable groups.
   - Participants reported continuing education in spite of significant personal hardship.

1. **Noun**: opposition (U/C, pl: oppositions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Active resistance or disagreement, especially from people or groups.
   - The people who are against a plan or decision that still goes ahead in spite of them.
   
   **Synonyms**: resistance, objection, dissent, antagonism, pushback
   
   **Antonyms**: support, approval, backing, endorsement, cooperation
   
   **When to Use**: When a person, team, or policy is continued or adopted in spite of other people strongly disagreeing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For non‑human barriers (use "obstacle" or "barrier"), or where disagreement is gentle or exploratory.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - face strong opposition in spite of clear benefits
   - proceed in spite of opposition
   - widespread opposition to the plan
   - overcome opposition through results
   
   **Root Analysis**: op- (against) + posit (place) + -ion (noun) → "the act of placing against".
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *oppositio* → Old French → English *opposition*, especially prominent where something is done in spite of critics.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of the opposition, they went ahead with the change.
   - She stuck with her decision in spite of family opposition.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy was implemented in spite of sustained opposition from several stakeholder groups.
   - In spite of internal opposition, leadership maintained the restructuring timeline.

1. **Noun**: pressure (U, sometimes C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Strong influence or demands on someone to act in a particular way; stress caused by expectations or urgency.
   - A feeling of being pushed that someone may withstand in spite of it.
   
   **Synonyms**: stress, strain, coercion, demands, burden
   
   **Antonyms**: ease, relief, freedom, relaxation, calm
   
   **When to Use**: When describing social, emotional, or organisational forces that someone acts in spite of, or resists.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For purely physical obstacles ("barrier"), or when you mean helpful encouragement rather than stressful force.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - under pressure but acting in spite of fear
   - resist pressure in spite of incentives
   - intense pressure in spite of limited control
   - despite the pressure (often followed by "in spite of" examples)
   
   **Root Analysis**: press (push) + -ure (state or result) → "state of being pushed".
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *pressur* → English *pressure*, now used widely for forces that people act in spite of.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She did the right thing in spite of all the pressure.
   - They stayed calm in spite of the pressure from social media.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of intense political pressure, the committee upheld its independent recommendation.
   - The team maintained quality standards in spite of schedule pressure during the final phase.

1. **Noun**: risk (C/U, pl: risks)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The possibility that something bad or harmful will happen; exposure to danger, loss, or failure.
   - A factor that must be accepted or managed when acting in spite of uncertainty.
   
   **Synonyms**: danger, hazard, threat, peril, uncertainty
   
   **Antonyms**: safety, security, certainty, protection, guarantee
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing decisions that go ahead in spite of possible negative outcomes; common in business, health, and everyday choices.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the negative outcome is certain ("loss" or "damage"), or where the tone should be optimistic rather than cautious.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - take the risk in spite of warnings
   - accept the risk in spite of potential loss
   - high risk in spite of safeguards
   - proceed at your own risk in spite of advice
   
   **Root Analysis**: probably from Italian *risco* (danger) → French *risque* → English *risk*.
   
   **Etymology**: Maritime and financial usage in Romance languages → English *risk*, central in discussions of acting in spite of uncertainty.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He knew the risk and went ahead in spite of it.
   - In spite of the risks, they started their own company.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of identified risks, the board approved the proposal subject to strict mitigation measures.
   - The intervention was adopted in spite of residual risk, given its potential benefits for high‑need groups.
