1. **Verb**: persist
   **Meaning**: 
   - To continue firmly in an opinion or course of action in spite of difficulty, opposition, or failure (intransitive).
   - To keep existing or happening over a period of time, even when it would be easier to stop.
   
   **Synonyms**: persevere, continue, keep going, endure, carry on
   
   **Antonyms**: give up, quit, abandon, surrender, yield
   
   **When to Use**: When someone keeps doing something in spite of obstacles, criticism, or repeated setbacks.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the continuation is gentle or flexible rather than stubborn; when you want to avoid the hint of being too rigid.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - persist in spite of repeated failure
   - persist with the plan in spite of criticism
   - persist against the odds
   - problems that persist in spite of treatment
   
   **Root Analysis**: per- (through, thoroughly) + sist (stand) → "stand through" difficulties.
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *persistere* → Old French → English *persist*, strongly tied to acting in spite of obstacles.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She persisted in spite of everyone telling her to stop.
   - The pain persisted in spite of the medicine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of early negative results, the research team persisted with the experimental design.
   - Several issues persisted in spite of multiple rounds of corrective action.

1. **Verb**: persevere
   **Meaning**: 
   - To continue making an effort in spite of difficulty or little chance of success (intransitive).
   - To keep going with courage and patience in spite of obstacles or discouragement.
   
   **Synonyms**: persist, carry on, keep at it, endure, press on
   
   **Antonyms**: give up, abandon, lose heart, surrender, back down
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to praise admirable, patient effort that continues in spite of long‑term difficulty.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech ("keep going" is more natural), or when the behaviour is more stubborn than admirable.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - persevere in spite of adversity
   - persevere through setbacks
   - persevere with the project in spite of delays
   - persevere until success
   
   **Root Analysis**: per- (through) + severus (strict, earnest) → to continue seriously through difficulty.
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *perseverare* → Old French → English *persevere*, often used in motivational "in spite of" contexts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He persevered in spite of failing the exam twice.
   - They persevered in spite of people laughing at their idea.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organisation persevered in spite of severe financial constraints and shifting regulations.
   - In spite of repeated experimental failures, the team persevered until a stable protocol emerged.

1. **Verb**: overcome
   **Meaning**: 
   - To successfully deal with and control a problem or difficulty in spite of its strength (transitive).
   - To defeat an opponent, obstacle, or weakness after a struggle.
   
   **Synonyms**: conquer, defeat, surmount, get over, prevail over
   
   **Antonyms**: succumb to, be defeated by, yield to, be overwhelmed by
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to highlight that someone or something has succeeded in spite of significant obstacles.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When challenges are minor; when the process is ongoing and not yet resolved.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - overcome obstacles in spite of limited time
   - overcome fears in spite of anxiety
   - overcome resistance in spite of strong opposition
   - overcome disadvantages in spite of background
   
   **Root Analysis**: over- (beyond, above) + come (arrive, reach) → "go beyond and get past".
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *ofercuman* → "overcome", central to stories of success in spite of difficulty.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She overcame her shyness in spite of feeling nervous.
   - They overcame a lot of problems in spite of having little help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of resource constraints, the team overcame initial implementation challenges.
   - The organisation overcame structural weaknesses in spite of longstanding cultural resistance.

1. **Verb**: endure
   **Meaning**: 
   - To suffer something difficult or painful patiently and continue in spite of it (transitive/intransitive).
   - To last or continue to exist for a long time, often in spite of changing conditions.
   
   **Synonyms**: withstand, bear, tolerate, survive, persist
   
   **Antonyms**: collapse, give in, break down, perish, succumb
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to stress patience and ongoing existence in spite of pain, hardship, or boredom.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the focus is on active success ("overcome" or "solve" is better), not simply surviving.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - endure hardship in spite of lack of support
   - endure criticism in spite of doing one's best
   - endure long hours in spite of low pay
   - traditions that endure in spite of social change
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (make) + dure (hard, lasting) → "to make hard/last".
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *indurare* (harden) → Old French *endurer* → English *endure*, used for staying firm in spite of hardship.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He endured the training in spite of the pain.
   - They endured years of pressure in spite of wanting to quit.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of repeated crises, the institution endured as a central part of the community.
   - The arrangement endured for decades in spite of political and economic shifts.

1. **Verb**: succeed
   **Meaning**: 
   - To achieve the desired aim or result, often after effort in spite of obstacles (intransitive).
   - To do well in life, work, or a specific task.
   
   **Synonyms**: triumph, prevail, achieve, win, prosper
   
   **Antonyms**: fail, lose, fall short, collapse, be defeated
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to say that someone or something achieved a goal in spite of difficulties, doubts, or poor starting conditions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the outcome is mixed or unclear; where neutral phrasing is needed ("perform well" may be safer).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - succeed in spite of the odds
   - succeed in spite of a slow start
   - succeed where others failed
   - succeed in doing X in spite of Y
   
   **Root Analysis**: suc- (under, up from under) + ceed (go) → "go up after, follow with good result".
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *succedere* (come next, turn out well) → French → English *succeed*, tightly linked to success in spite of difficulty.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She succeeded in spite of everyone doubting her.
   - They succeeded in finishing on time in spite of all the delays.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of external headwinds, the initiative succeeded in reaching all key performance targets.
   - The intervention succeeded in spite of implementation constraints and partial data.

1. **Verb**: continue
   **Meaning**: 
   - To keep doing something without stopping; to carry on in spite of problems or changes (transitive/intransitive).
   - To remain in a particular state or condition.
   
   **Synonyms**: keep going, carry on, persist, proceed, maintain
   
   **Antonyms**: stop, cease, halt, interrupt, abandon
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral, everyday verb for "keep going", especially useful when something goes on in spite of difficulties, criticism, or success.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to emphasise the courage or difficulty of persisting ("persevere" or "endure" is stronger).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - continue in spite of warnings
   - continue working in spite of setbacks
   - continue to grow in spite of the market
   - continue the project in spite of doubts
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, intensive) + tin (hold) + -ue → "hold together, keep going".
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *continuare* (join together, make continuous) → Old French → English *continue*, often paired with "in spite of".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We decided to continue in spite of all the problems.
   - She continued studying in spite of being tired.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of repeated delays, the programme continued according to its revised schedule.
   - The partnership continued in spite of leadership changes on both sides.

1. **Verb**: survive
   **Meaning**: 
   - To stay alive or continue to exist in spite of danger, hardship, or threatening change (intransitive/transitive).
   - To manage to live or continue after someone or something else is gone.
   
   **Synonyms**: endure, outlast, live through, withstand, pull through
   
   **Antonyms**: perish, die, collapse, fail, succumb
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasising bare continuity in spite of strong negative forces; in both literal (life‑or‑death) and metaphorical senses.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the subject is more than just continuing ("thrive" or "prosper" implies success beyond survival).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - survive in spite of the odds
   - survive in spite of serious illness
   - survive a crisis in spite of huge losses
   - survive long enough to in spite of X
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (over, beyond) + vive (live) → "live beyond" threats.
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *supervivere* → Old French → English *survive*, strongly tied to enduring in spite of danger.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The company survived in spite of the recession.
   - He survived in spite of the doctors' low expectations.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of prolonged instability, the institution survived and later stabilised its governance structures.
   - Several local businesses survived in spite of severe supply‑chain disruption.

1. **Verb**: adapt
   **Meaning**: 
   - To change something so that it works better in a new situation (transitive).
   - To change your behaviour or thinking in response to new conditions in spite of discomfort or habit (intransitive).
   
   **Synonyms**: adjust, modify, accommodate, acclimatise, tailor
   
   **Antonyms**: resist, remain rigid, refuse to change, stay fixed
   
   **When to Use**: When people, systems, or organisations change in order to continue in spite of new pressures, constraints, or opportunities.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the change is total and radical ("transform" may be stronger), or when you simply choose something different without pressure.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - adapt in spite of limited resources
   - adapt to change in spite of discomfort
   - adapt strategy in spite of market shocks
   - quickly adapt in spite of initial resistance
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + apt (fit) → "make fit for".
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *adaptare* (adjust, fit to) → French → English *adapt*, central in discussions of coping in spite of change.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She adapted in spite of the culture shock.
   - The team adapted in spite of having almost no time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of regulatory shifts, the organisation adapted its processes without interrupting service delivery.
   - Local communities adapted in spite of climate‑related disruptions to traditional livelihoods.
