1. **Adverb**: nevertheless (type: sentence adverb/discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In spite of that; despite what has just been mentioned, something else remains true.
   - Often used after describing a difficulty, then introducing a contrasting, persistent result that happens in spite of it.
   
   **Synonyms**: nonetheless, however, still, even so, all the same
   
   **Antonyms**: therefore, consequently, thus, accordingly, as a result
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to say that an action or result happens in spite of a problem just mentioned, especially in semi-formal or formal speech and writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk ("but" or "still" is more natural), or when you are explaining a cause (use "therefore" instead of a concessive adverb).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nevertheless continued
   - nevertheless went ahead in spite of the risks
   - nevertheless achieved the goal in spite of everything
   - nevertheless remained calm in spite of the pressure
   
   **Root Analysis**: never (at no time) + the + less (to a smaller degree) → "not at all less (true) for that reason".
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English *never the lesse* → contracted to *nevertheless*, keeping the sense "not any less" true despite the previous point.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It was pouring outside; in spite of that, we went out nevertheless.
   - She was exhausted; nevertheless, she finished the report in spite of the late hour.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sample size was modest; nevertheless, the results in spite of these constraints provide meaningful preliminary evidence.
   - In spite of considerable methodological limitations, the study nevertheless contributes to our understanding of long‑term outcomes.

1. **Adverb**: nonetheless (type: sentence adverb/discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In spite of that; nevertheless; despite the fact just mentioned, the following point still holds.
   - Slightly more emphatic than "nevertheless" and common in formal summaries of results achieved in spite of obstacles.
   
   **Synonyms**: nevertheless, however, even so, still, all the same
   
   **Antonyms**: therefore, hence, thus, as a consequence, accordingly
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to stress that something remains true in spite of strong reasons to expect the opposite, especially in writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very relaxed conversation where a simple "but" or "still" sounds more natural, or when you are emphasising cause and effect.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nonetheless went ahead in spite of concerns
   - nonetheless remained optimistic in spite of the news
   - nonetheless proved effective in spite of limited data
   - nonetheless continued in spite of repeated failures
   
   **Root Analysis**: none (not) + the + less (to a smaller degree) → "not at all less (true)".
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English *none the lesse* → contracted to *nonetheless*, preserving the idea "not less so" despite the previous statement.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone told him it was impossible; he tried in spite of them and succeeded nonetheless.
   - The plan sounded crazy; they went for it nonetheless, in spite of all the warnings.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of significant operational disruptions, the organization nonetheless maintained core service levels.
   - Resource constraints were substantial; nonetheless, the team delivered in spite of these limitations.

1. **Adverb**: still (type: sentence/temporal adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Continuing to happen or be true until and including now; in spite of previously mentioned reasons for it to stop.
   - In concessive use, it highlights that a result occurs in spite of obstacles or expectations to the contrary.
   
   **Synonyms**: nevertheless, yet, even so, nonetheless (in concessive use)
   
   **Antonyms**: no longer, not anymore, already stopped
   
   **When to Use**: Across all registers to show that an action continues or a decision is taken in spite of difficulties, criticisms, or changes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the concessive meaning might be confused with pure time meaning; in very formal writing, prefer "nevertheless" or "nonetheless" for clarity.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - still went ahead in spite of everything
   - still managed to finish in spite of delays
   - still believes it was right in spite of criticism
   - still smiling in spite of the bad news
   
   **Root Analysis**: Proto‑Germanic root meaning "quiet, motionless" → extended to "unchanged, continuing".
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *stille* (quiet, motionless) → adverb meaning "continuing without change", naturally fitting "in spite of" contexts of persistence.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of the rain, we still went for a walk.
   - Everybody doubted her, but she still applied in spite of their comments.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of limited evidence, the hypothesis still warrants careful examination.
   - The organization still achieved its targets in spite of unexpected market shocks.

1. **Adverb**: remarkably (type: degree adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a surprising or striking degree; in a way that is worth remarking on, especially when success happens in spite of obstacles.
   - Often highlights how impressive a result is given difficult conditions.
   
   **Synonyms**: notably, strikingly, extraordinarily, exceptionally, impressively
   
   **Antonyms**: unremarkably, ordinarily, typically, mildly, slightly
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to emphasise that someone or something performs well in spite of disadvantages, constraints, or risk factors.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you are trying to sound strictly neutral or downplay results; in very technical prose where "significantly" or "substantially" is more precise.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - remarkably resilient in spite of adversity
   - performed remarkably well in spite of little preparation
   - remarkably stable in spite of external shocks
   - improved remarkably in spite of limited support
   
   **Root Analysis**: remark (to notice, comment on) + -able (worthy of) + -ly (in a manner) → "in a way worth commenting on".
   
   **Etymology**: From French *remarquable* → English *remarkable* → adverb *remarkably*, used for outcomes that stand out in spite of conditions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of starting late, she did remarkably well on the exam.
   - The meeting went remarkably smoothly in spite of all the last‑minute changes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of severe budgetary restrictions, the program remained remarkably effective across all key indicators.
   - The system behaved remarkably predictably in spite of high input variability.

1. **Adverb**: persistently (type: manner adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that continues firmly and repeatedly in spite of difficulty, opposition, or failure.
   - Describes action that goes on and on in spite of reasons to stop.
   
   **Synonyms**: tenaciously, doggedly, steadily, relentlessly, consistently
   
   **Antonyms**: sporadically, intermittently, occasionally, half‑heartedly
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to highlight ongoing effort in spite of rejection, delays, or criticism; useful in both praise and mild criticism.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the continuation is gentle or flexible rather than stubborn; in very neutral descriptions where "regularly" is enough.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - persistently applied in spite of repeated rejections
   - persistently asked in spite of being ignored
   - persistently worked in spite of setbacks
   - persistently followed up in spite of silence
   
   **Root Analysis**: persist (continue firmly despite difficulty) + -ent + -ly (manner adverb).
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *persistere* → English *persist* → *persistent* → *persistently*, keeping the "in spite of" sense in its core meaning.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He persistently trained in spite of feeling discouraged.
   - She kept emailing persistently in spite of not getting replies at first.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of institutional resistance, the committee persistently advocated for policy change.
   - The team persistently pursued the line of inquiry in spite of early negative results.

1. **Adverb**: steadfastly (type: manner adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a very firm, loyal, and unchanging way, especially in spite of pressure or difficulty.
   - Emphasises faithful commitment and refusal to give up.
   
   **Synonyms**: firmly, unwaveringly, resolutely, loyally, faithfully
   
   **Antonyms**: waveringly, inconsistently, unreliably, hesitantly
   
   **When to Use**: Mainly in formal or literary contexts to praise people or groups who stay committed in spite of opposition, criticism, or risk.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual talk (sounds too heavy), or when flexibility and change are being praised instead of fixed commitment.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - steadfastly supported in spite of criticism
   - steadfastly refused in spite of pressure
   - steadfastly continued in spite of setbacks
   - steadfastly defended in spite of opposition
   
   **Root Analysis**: steadfast (firm, fixed) + -ly (in a manner) → "in a firm, fixed way".
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *stedefæst* (fixed in place) → *steadfast* → *steadfastly*, keeping the idea of not moving in spite of external force.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She steadfastly backed her friend in spite of what others were saying.
   - He steadfastly stuck to his plan in spite of our doubts.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board steadfastly maintained its position in spite of sustained shareholder pressure.
   - In spite of changing fashions, the institution has steadfastly preserved its core values.

1. **Adverb**: eventually (type: time/sentence adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the end; after a period of difficulties, delays, or changes.
   - Often suggests that success or resolution came in spite of many problems along the way.
   
   **Synonyms**: finally, in the end, ultimately, at last, in time
   
   **Antonyms**: immediately, instantly, never, right away
   
   **When to Use**: When describing a result that arrives after setbacks or an "in spite of" journey; common in both speech and writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the timing is exact and should be stated; when nothing difficult preceded the result ("finally" may sound overly dramatic).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - eventually succeeded in spite of early failures
   - eventually recovered in spite of severe illness
   - eventually agreed in spite of strong reservations
   - eventually improved in spite of a slow start
   
   **Root Analysis**: eventual (final, happening as a result) + -ly (adverb).
   
   **Etymology**: From French *éventuel* (happening as a result) → English *eventual* → *eventually*, often used to summarise outcomes achieved in spite of obstacles.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In spite of all the delays, we eventually got there.
   - He kept trying in spite of the setbacks and eventually passed the exam.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of repeated implementation issues, the initiative eventually met all regulatory requirements.
   - The organization eventually stabilized in spite of significant governance failures in the early phase.

1. **Adverb**: tirelessly (type: manner adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - With great energy and persistence, as if without getting tired, especially in spite of long hours or repeated obstacles.
   - Used to praise sustained effort in spite of draining conditions.
   
   **Synonyms**: relentlessly, indefatigably, ceaselessly, energetically, unstintingly
   
   **Antonyms**: lazily, half‑heartedly, sporadically, intermittently, sluggishly
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to highlight admirable, sustained effort in spite of fatigue, resistance, or lack of recognition.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When balance or rest is the goal; in contexts where overwork is being criticised rather than praised.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tirelessly worked in spite of limited resources
   - tirelessly campaigned in spite of low odds
   - tirelessly supported the team in spite of personal stress
   - tirelessly advocated in spite of repeated setbacks
   
   **Root Analysis**: tire (become weary) + -less (without) + -ly (in a manner) → "in a manner as if without tiring".
   
   **Etymology**: Formed in English from *tire* + *-less* → *tireless* → *tirelessly*, commonly collocating with efforts continued in spite of hardship.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She worked tirelessly in spite of feeling exhausted.
   - They tirelessly helped in spite of getting very little thanks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In spite of organisational resistance, the committee tirelessly pursued transparency and accountability reforms.
   - The volunteers tirelessly provided support in spite of scarce funding and limited public attention.
