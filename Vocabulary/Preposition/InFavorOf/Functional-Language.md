1. **Functional Expression**: I'm in favor of [X] (function: expressing support/agreement)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that you support, agree with, or approve of a particular idea, proposal, or action
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I support [X], I back [X], I'm for [X], I approve of [X], I'm behind [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm against [X], I oppose [X], I'm not in favor of [X], I disapprove of [X]
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal contexts when stating your position on an issue; appropriate for meetings, discussions, debates, or written communication where clarity is valued
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual settings where simpler expressions like "I like it" would be more natural; when neutrality is required rather than taking a position
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm strongly in favor of [X] (more emphatic)
   - I'm very much in favor of [X] (more emphatic)
   - I'm not in favor of [X] (negative form)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression balances professionalism with clarity. Using "in favor of" rather than simply "for" adds formality. It's face-saving because it states your position without criticizing opposing views.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - So are we / I am too / I agree (accepting/agreeing)
   - I see your point, but I disagree / I'm actually against it (declining/disagreeing)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "Should we order pizza for the meeting?" B: "I'm in favor of that!"
   - I'm in favor of taking a break before we continue this discussion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I am in favor of implementing the proposed changes to our remote work policy.
   - The senator stated that she is in favor of comprehensive healthcare reform.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd like to voice my support for [X] (function: expressing support formally)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to formally declare or announce your support for something, emphasizing the act of making your position known
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct but polite
   
   **Synonyms**: I'd like to express my support for [X], I wish to support [X], I want to back [X], I'd like to endorse [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'd like to voice my opposition to [X], I must express my concerns about [X]
   
   **When to Use**: In formal meetings, public forums, official statements, or when your support needs to be documented or publicly acknowledged; shows respect for the process
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversations where such formality would sound pompous; when quick, simple agreement is more appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd like to voice my strong support for [X] (emphatic)
   - I want to voice my support for [X] (slightly less formal)
   - May I voice my support for [X]? (requesting permission to speak)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The phrase "voice my support" emphasizes the public declaration aspect. It's appropriate for formal settings where positions are being recorded. Using "I'd like to" softens the directness with politeness.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you for your support / We appreciate that / Noted (acknowledging)
   - Could you elaborate on why? (seeking clarification)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd like to voice my support for Sarah's idea about the team building event.
   - Let me voice my support for this plan before we move forward.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would like to voice my support for the proposed amendment to the bylaws.
   - At this juncture, I wish to voice my unequivocal support for the committee's recommendations.

1. **Functional Expression**: Count me in (function: expressing willingness to participate/support)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that you want to be included or that you support an idea and want to participate
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm in, Include me, I'm on board, You can count on me, Sign me up
   
   **Antonyms**: Count me out, I'm not interested, I'll pass, Leave me out
   
   **When to Use**: In casual, friendly contexts when responding positively to invitations, proposals, or ideas; shows enthusiasm and commitment in an accessible way
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal business meetings, official communications, or with people in authority; too casual for professional documentation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Definitely count me in (emphatic)
   - You can count me in (slightly softer)
   - Count me in for that (specifying what you're supporting)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This is positive politeness—it builds solidarity and shows enthusiasm. The imperative form is softened by the informal, friendly nature of the expression. It implies both support and active participation.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Great! / Awesome! / Perfect! (enthusiastic acceptance)
   - Okay, I'll add you to the list (practical acknowledgment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "We're going to grab lunch after this meeting." B: "Count me in!"
   - If you're organizing a volunteer day, count me in.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - (Generally inappropriate for formal contexts; use "I would be pleased to participate" instead)

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm all for [X] (function: expressing strong support)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate complete agreement with or enthusiasm for something
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I fully support [X], I completely agree with [X], I'm totally behind [X], I'm 100% for [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm against [X], I'm opposed to [X], I'm not for [X]
   
   **When to Use**: In informal to semi-formal contexts when expressing strong, enthusiastic support; appropriate among colleagues, in team discussions, or casual business settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal presentations, written reports to executives, or official statements; when measured, careful support is more appropriate than enthusiasm
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm all for it (using pronoun)
   - I'm totally all for [X] (emphatic with intensifier)
   - We're all for [X] (plural form)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The phrase "all for" suggests complete, unreserved support. It's more enthusiastic than simply "I support," making it useful for building momentum and showing commitment. The informality helps build rapport.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Me too / Same here / I'm glad (agreement)
   - Really? I have some concerns (disagreement with softening)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A: "I think we should start having weekly check-ins." B: "I'm all for that!"
   - I'm all for trying new approaches if they save us time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Our department is all for initiatives that enhance operational efficiency.
   - While informal, this expression occasionally appears in semi-formal contexts: "The committee is all for exploring alternative solutions."

1. **Functional Expression**: You have my backing (function: promising support)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to assure someone that you support them and will help or defend them
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I support you, I'm behind you, You have my support, I've got your back, I'll back you up
   
   **Antonyms**: You're on your own, I can't support you, I won't back you
   
   **When to Use**: When offering support or reassurance to someone undertaking something challenging; appropriate in professional contexts when making commitments
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you cannot actually provide the support promised; this is a commitment, not just an opinion
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You have my full backing (emphatic)
   - You'll have my backing (future tense)
   - You have our backing (collective support)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This is a commissive speech act—you're committing to future support. It's face-saving for the recipient by providing reassurance. More specific than "I agree" because it promises active support.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you, that means a lot / I appreciate it (grateful acceptance)
   - I'm glad to hear that / That's good to know (acknowledgment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't worry about approaching the boss with this idea—you have my backing.
   - If you want to pitch this to the team, you have my backing.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As you proceed with this initiative, please know that you have the full backing of senior leadership.
   - The candidate assured supporters that they would have her backing on this issue.

1. **Functional Expression**: I stand behind [X] (function: expressing firm support/commitment)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate strong, committed support for something or someone, suggesting you will defend or maintain this position
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I support [X], I back [X], I'm committed to [X], I endorse [X], I defend [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I oppose [X], I distance myself from [X], I can't stand behind [X]
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing resolute, principled support; especially appropriate when facing opposition or when commitment needs to be clear; suitable for both personal and organizational statements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When support is tentative or conditional; "stand behind" implies unwavering commitment; avoid in very casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I firmly stand behind [X] (emphatic)
   - I continue to stand behind [X] (emphasizing ongoing support)
   - We stand behind [X] (collective support)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The metaphor of "standing behind" suggests both support and protection. It's often used in situations where there's controversy or challenge. This is a strong commissive—implies you'll defend this position.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I respect that / Good to know where you stand (acknowledging)
   - Even though you've been criticized? (challenging the commitment)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Whatever decision you make, I stand behind you.
   - I stand behind what I said in the meeting yesterday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board stands firmly behind its decision to pursue this strategic direction.
   - Despite recent criticism, I stand behind my recommendation to implement these reforms.

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm inclined to support [X] (function: expressing tendency toward support)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that you are leaning toward supporting something, though perhaps not fully committed yet
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (hedged)
   
   **Synonyms**: I tend to favor [X], I'm leaning toward [X], I'm disposed to support [X], I'm likely to support [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: I'm inclined to oppose [X], I'm hesitant about [X], I'm not inclined to support [X]
   
   **When to Use**: In professional discussions when you want to express support while maintaining flexibility; useful for diplomatic communication or when final decision hasn't been made
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When strong, definite commitment is expected or when hedging would undermine your authority; avoid when clarity is paramount
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm strongly inclined to support [X] (less hedged)
   - I'm somewhat inclined to support [X] (more hedged)
   - I'm inclined to agree (broader application)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This is a hedge—it softens your commitment. Useful for negative politeness (respecting others' autonomy) by not foreclosing discussion. Allows you to change position without losing face.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What would make you more certain? (seeking conditions)
   - That's encouraging (acknowledging qualified support)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm inclined to support the proposal, but I'd like to hear what others think.
   - After hearing the presentation, I'm inclined to vote yes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Based on the evidence presented, I am inclined to support the committee's recommendation.
   - The council appears inclined to support measures that promote sustainable development.

1. **Functional Expression**: I give this my seal of approval (function: expressing formal approval)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate official or authoritative approval, often with a sense of finality
   - *Formality*: neutral (can be slightly humorous or metaphorical)
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: I approve this, This has my approval, I endorse this, I sign off on this, I authorize this
   
   **Antonyms**: I withhold approval, I reject this, This doesn't have my approval
   
   **When to Use**: When in a position of authority and granting approval; can be used semi-seriously in informal contexts or seriously in approval processes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you lack authority to approve; when the language might sound overly pompous for the situation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - This gets my seal of approval (casual variation)
   - I give this my full seal of approval (emphatic)
   - It has the company's seal of approval (organizational version)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The metaphor of "seal of approval" (from wax seals on official documents) adds a semi-formal or sometimes humorous tone. It suggests finality and authority. Context determines whether it's serious or playful.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thank you / Great! / Excellent (acknowledging approval)
   - I'll proceed then (indicating next action)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've reviewed your plan, and it gets my seal of approval.
   - If this restaurant has your seal of approval, I'm happy to try it!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After thorough review, the committee gives this proposal its seal of approval.
   - The project may commence now that it has received the director's seal of approval.
