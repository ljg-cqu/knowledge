1. **Adjective**: supportive (comp: more supportive, sup: most supportive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Giving encouragement or approval to someone or something; showing agreement with a plan, idea, or person (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: encouraging, approving, favorable, backing, endorsing
   
   **Antonyms**: opposed, unsupportive, critical, hostile
   
   **When to Use**: When describing people, attitudes, or environments that show approval or encouragement; appropriate in both personal and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When neutrality is required or when describing active opposition; avoid when the support is only superficial
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supportive environment
   - supportive attitude
   - highly supportive
   - emotionally supportive
   - supportive of the proposal
   
   **Root Analysis**: support (from Latin supportare: sub- "from below" + portare "to carry") + -ive (forming adjectives)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supportare → Old French supporter → English support (14th century) + -ive suffix (creates adjective form)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My friends have been really supportive of my decision to change careers.
   - She's always been supportive when I need someone to talk to.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee adopted a supportive stance toward the proposed environmental reforms.
   - Research indicates that supportive workplace cultures enhance employee retention.

1. **Adjective**: favorable (comp: more favorable, sup: most favorable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing approval or agreement; advantageous or beneficial (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - *(Sense 2)* Likely to lead to success or a good outcome
   
   **Synonyms**: positive, approving, advantageous, beneficial, propitious
   
   **Antonyms**: unfavorable, negative, adverse, disadvantageous
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when describing opinions, conditions, or circumstances that are advantageous; common in business, academic, and legal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual conversation where simpler words like "good" or "positive" would be more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - favorable opinion
   - favorable conditions
   - favorable outcome
   - view favorably
   - favorable impression
   
   **Root Analysis**: favor (from Latin favor "goodwill") + -able (able to be)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin favor → Old French favour → English favor (14th century) + -able suffix
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The weather looks favorable for our camping trip this weekend.
   - She gave a favorable review of the new restaurant downtown.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board issued a favorable response to the merger proposal.
   - Current market conditions remain favorable for sustained economic growth.

1. **Adjective**: pro (non-gradable, attributive only)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In favor of something; supporting a particular side or position (non-gradable, used as prefix or attributive adjective)
   
   **Synonyms**: supporting, for, in favor of, advocating
   
   **Antonyms**: anti, against, opposed, contra
   
   **When to Use**: In discussions of political, social, or ethical issues; often used as a prefix (pro-democracy, pro-environment) or in debate contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When detailed explanation of support is needed; "pro" is too brief for complex positions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pro side of the debate
   - pro arguments
   - pro stance
   - pro forces
   - go pro (different meaning: professional)
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (Latin prefix meaning "for, forward, in favor of")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin pro "for, in favor of, on behalf of" → used in English as prefix and standalone adjective (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm totally pro working from home—it saves so much time.
   - Are you pro or against the new policy?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The senator articulated several pro arguments during the debate.
   - Participants were divided into pro and contra groups for the discussion.

1. **Adjective**: affirmative (comp: more affirmative, sup: most affirmative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Agreeing with or consenting to a statement or request; expressing approval or support (gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - *(Sense 2)* (in formal contexts) Positive rather than negative
   
   **Synonyms**: positive, agreeing, approving, consenting, confirmatory
   
   **Antonyms**: negative, dissenting, disapproving, opposing
   
   **When to Use**: In formal, legal, or official contexts when describing responses, actions, or positions that indicate agreement or approval
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where "yes" or "positive" would sound more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - affirmative answer
   - affirmative action
   - affirmative response
   - affirmative vote
   - respond in the affirmative
   
   **Root Analysis**: affirm (from Latin affirmare: ad- "to" + firmare "make firm") + -ative (forming adjectives)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin affirmare → Old French affermer → English affirm (14th century) + -ative suffix
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When I asked if she wanted to come, she gave an affirmative nod.
   - The answer is affirmative—we're good to go!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee cast an affirmative vote on the resolution.
   - The court issued an affirmative ruling in favor of the plaintiff.

1. **Adjective**: receptive (comp: more receptive, sup: most receptive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Willing to consider or accept new suggestions, ideas, or proposals; open-minded (gradable, predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: open-minded, amenable, willing, responsive, accepting
   
   **Antonyms**: resistant, closed-minded, unreceptive, opposed, hostile
   
   **When to Use**: When describing people or groups who are open to new ideas, feedback, or change; common in professional and educational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing active support rather than passive openness; receptive implies willingness but not necessarily enthusiasm
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - receptive to feedback
   - receptive audience
   - receptive to change
   - receptive mind
   - receptive to new ideas
   
   **Root Analysis**: recept (from Latin recipere "receive") + -ive (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin recipere → receptus → Medieval Latin receptivus → English receptive (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's usually pretty receptive to suggestions about improving the process.
   - The team was receptive to trying out the new software.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The administration has proven receptive to stakeholder concerns regarding transparency.
   - Students demonstrated a receptive attitude toward the revised curriculum.

1. **Adjective**: sympathetic (comp: more sympathetic, sup: most sympathetic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Showing approval of or favor toward an idea or action; supporting or agreeing with something (gradable, predicative or attributive)
   - *(Sense 2)* Showing compassion or understanding for someone's feelings or situation
   
   **Synonyms**: understanding, favorable, well-disposed, supportive, agreeable
   
   **Antonyms**: unsympathetic, opposed, hostile, indifferent, antagonistic
   
   **When to Use**: When describing attitudes that show both emotional understanding and approval; appropriate for discussing both personal and political support
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When clinical objectivity is required; sympathetic suggests emotional involvement that may seem unprofessional in some contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sympathetic to the cause
   - sympathetic ear
   - sympathetic hearing
   - sympathetic audience
   - sympathetic response
   
   **Root Analysis**: sym- (together) + path (feeling) + -etic (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek sympatheia → Latin sympathia → French sympathétique → English sympathetic (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's sympathetic to environmental issues and always votes green.
   - The manager was sympathetic to our request for flexible hours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The judge proved sympathetic to arguments emphasizing rehabilitative justice.
   - The editorial board adopted a sympathetic stance toward the reform proposals.

1. **Adjective**: amenable (comp: more amenable, sup: most amenable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Willing to accept or be influenced by a suggestion; open and responsive to ideas (gradable, usually predicative with "to")
   
   **Synonyms**: agreeable, willing, compliant, receptive, cooperative
   
   **Antonyms**: resistant, opposed, stubborn, unwilling, intractable
   
   **When to Use**: In formal or professional contexts when describing willingness to cooperate or consider proposals; implies tractability and reasonableness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual speech where it may sound overly formal; avoid when describing enthusiastic rather than willing support
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - amenable to suggestion
   - amenable to negotiation
   - amenable to compromise
   - amenable to change
   - amenable to the proposal
   
   **Root Analysis**: amener (from Latin minare "to drive animals") + -able (capable of being)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French amener "to lead to" → Anglo-French amenable → English amenable (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think Dad will be amenable to us borrowing the car if we ask nicely.
   - She's pretty amenable to trying new restaurants.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board members proved amenable to the proposed restructuring plan.
   - Stakeholders remain amenable to negotiations provided transparency is maintained.

1. **Adjective**: inclined (comp: more inclined, sup: most inclined)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having a tendency or preference toward something; favorably disposed (gradable, predicative with "to" + infinitive or "toward/towards")
   
   **Synonyms**: disposed, prone, likely, predisposed, apt
   
   **Antonyms**: disinclined, averse, reluctant, unwilling, opposed
   
   **When to Use**: When describing a tendency, preference, or leaning toward a particular choice or position; suitable for formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing firm commitment rather than tendency; inclined suggests possibility not certainty
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inclined to agree
   - inclined to support
   - favorably inclined
   - naturally inclined
   - inclined toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (toward) + cline (to lean) + -ed (past participle adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inclinare "to bend toward" → Old French encliner → English incline (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm inclined to agree with you on that point.
   - She's not really inclined to go along with the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee appears favorably inclined toward the applicant's proposal.
   - Survey data suggest voters are increasingly inclined to support progressive policies.
