1. **Noun**: support (U/C, pl: supports when countable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Uncountable) Approval, encouragement, or help given to someone or something; the act of supporting
   - *(Sense 2)* (Countable) A person or thing that provides help or encouragement
   
   **Synonyms**: backing, endorsement, approval, assistance, advocacy
   
   **Antonyms**: opposition, resistance, disapproval, obstruction
   
   **When to Use**: In professional, political, and personal contexts when discussing approval, backing, or assistance; appropriate across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to physical support structures without clarifying context; "support" can be ambiguous between emotional/political and physical meanings
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - show support for
   - lend support to
   - voice one's support
   - withdraw support
   - throw one's support behind
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (from below) + port (carry) = carry from below, uphold
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supportare (sub- "from below" + portare "to carry") → Old French supporter → English support (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thanks for your support—it really means a lot to me.
   - We need everyone's support if this project is going to work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative has garnered widespread support from stakeholders across sectors.
   - Public support for the policy reform remains strong according to recent polling data.

1. **Noun**: favor (U, American spelling; favour in British English)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Uncountable) Approval, support, or liking for someone or something; preferential treatment or support
   - *(Sense 2)* A kind or helpful act; something done to help someone
   
   **Synonyms**: approval, support, goodwill, preference, backing
   
   **Antonyms**: disfavor, opposition, disapproval, dislike
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when discussing approval or preferential support; often used in phrases like "in favor of"; appropriate in professional, political, and social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to small acts of kindness (different sense); in very casual contexts where simpler words suffice
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in favor of
   - find favor with
   - look with favor on
   - curry favor with
   - fall out of favor
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin favor "goodwill, inclination, partiality"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin favor → Old French favour → English favor (14th century); American spelling dropped 'u' in 19th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The idea has found favor with most of the team.
   - I hope this proposal is viewed with favor by the committee.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court's decision was rendered in favor of the plaintiff.
   - The policy has gained considerable favor among progressive legislators.

1. **Noun**: endorsement (C/U, pl: endorsements)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable/Uncountable) An act of publicly declaring support or approval for someone or something; an official statement of support
   - *(Sense 2)* (Countable) A signature on a check or document; a celebrity's promotion of a product
   
   **Synonyms**: approval, backing, support, sanction, recommendation
   
   **Antonyms**: rejection, disapproval, condemnation, denunciation
   
   **When to Use**: In formal, professional, and political contexts when discussing official or public support; particularly common in electoral politics and professional recommendations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For casual, private agreement; when informal support rather than official backing is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - give one's endorsement to
   - seek endorsement
   - win endorsement
   - political endorsement
   - full endorsement
   
   **Root Analysis**: endorse (in- "on" + dorsum "back") + -ment (noun suffix) = writing on the back of a document
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin indorsare "to write on the back of" → endorse → endorsement (16th century); meaning expanded to general approval
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Getting her endorsement would really help the project move forward.
   - We're hoping for the manager's endorsement before the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate secured endorsements from three major labor unions.
   - Committee endorsement of the proposal significantly increases its likelihood of implementation.

1. **Noun**: backing (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Uncountable) Support, help, or financial assistance given to a person, organization, or plan; active support
   - *(Sense 2)* Material used to support or strengthen something (physical sense)
   
   **Synonyms**: support, sponsorship, patronage, assistance, funding
   
   **Antonyms**: opposition, resistance, obstruction
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing active, often financial or substantial support; implies more concrete support than mere approval; appropriate in business, political, and project contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For purely verbal or symbolic support; when referring to physical backing material (different sense) without context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - financial backing
   - give backing to
   - have backing of
   - withdraw backing
   - strong backing
   
   **Root Analysis**: back (support from behind) + -ing (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "back" (support) + -ing; emerged in English in 19th century for support meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If we can get the boss's backing, this project is a go.
   - They have backing from several investors.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative has secured backing from prominent venture capital firms.
   - Without legislative backing, implementation of these reforms will prove challenging.

1. **Noun**: approval (U/C, pl: approvals when countable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Uncountable) The belief that something or someone is good or acceptable; positive opinion or support
   - *(Sense 2)* (Countable) Official permission or acceptance
   
   **Synonyms**: endorsement, support, acceptance, sanction, agreement
   
   **Antonyms**: disapproval, rejection, condemnation, refusal
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal contexts when discussing official acceptance or positive judgment; appropriate for organizational, professional, and personal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to informal liking; in contexts requiring more specific terms like "authorization"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - seek approval
   - win approval
   - grant approval
   - stamp of approval
   - meet with approval
   
   **Root Analysis**: approve (ap- "to" + probare "to test, prove good") + -al (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin approbare → Old French aprover → English approve → approval (14th century verb, 17th century noun)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I hope the plan meets with everyone's approval.
   - Getting parental approval for the trip was easier than expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal requires board approval before proceeding to implementation.
   - The legislation has won approval from key stakeholders across the political spectrum.

1. **Noun**: advocacy (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Uncountable) Public support for or recommendation of a particular cause, policy, or course of action; active promotion
   
   **Synonyms**: support, promotion, championing, backing, recommendation
   
   **Antonyms**: opposition, condemnation, resistance
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when discussing active, often public support for causes, policies, or positions; particularly common in legal, political, and social justice contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For passive agreement or weak support; advocacy implies active promotion
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advocacy for
   - policy advocacy
   - public advocacy
   - strong advocacy
   - advocacy group
   
   **Root Analysis**: advocate (ad- "to" + vocare "to call") + -cy (noun suffix indicating state/practice)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin advocare "to call to one's aid" → advocate → advocacy (15th century noun form)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her advocacy for environmental causes is really inspiring.
   - Thanks to his advocacy, the policy finally got attention.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization's advocacy for educational reform has influenced policy at the national level.
   - Effective advocacy requires sustained engagement with stakeholders and policymakers.

1. **Noun**: proponent (C, pl: proponents)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable) A person who supports or advocates for a particular idea, cause, or policy
   
   **Synonyms**: advocate, supporter, champion, backer, promoter
   
   **Antonyms**: opponent, critic, adversary, detractor
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when identifying people who actively support particular positions; common in academic, political, and policy discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For passive supporters or those with weak positions; proponent suggests active advocacy
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - leading proponent of
   - strong proponent
   - proponent of change
   - key proponent
   - vocal proponent
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (for, forward) + ponent (from ponere "to put, place")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin proponere "to put forward" → proponent (16th century) - one who puts forward an idea
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's been a major proponent of the new policy from day one.
   - Who are the main proponents of this approach?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Leading proponents of the theory argue that empirical evidence supports their framework.
   - As a proponent of fiscal reform, the senator has introduced multiple related bills.

1. **Noun**: advocate (C, pl: advocates)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable) A person who publicly supports or recommends a particular cause, policy, or person
   - *(Sense 2)* (Legal) A professional lawyer who speaks in court on behalf of others
   
   **Synonyms**: supporter, proponent, champion, backer, promoter
   
   **Antonyms**: opponent, critic, adversary
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when identifying people who actively promote causes or positions; appropriate across professional, political, and social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For passive supporters; advocate implies active, vocal support
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advocate for change
   - strong advocate
   - vocal advocate
   - tireless advocate
   - advocate of reform
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + vocare (to call) = to call to one's aid
   
   **Etymology**: Latin advocatus "one called to aid" → Old French avocat → English advocate (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Maria has always been an advocate for better work-life balance.
   - He's a strong advocate of using renewable energy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Dr. Chen is a leading advocate for evidence-based educational practices.
   - Civil rights advocates have long championed these legislative reforms.

1. **Noun**: champion (C, pl: champions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable) A person who fights or argues for a cause or on behalf of others; a vigorous supporter or defender
   - *(Sense 2)* A person who has won a competition
   
   **Synonyms**: defender, supporter, advocate, backer, promoter
   
   **Antonyms**: opponent, enemy, detractor
   
   **When to Use**: When describing passionate, dedicated supporters who actively fight for causes; implies vigorous, sometimes combative advocacy; appropriate in political, social, and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For neutral or passive support; champion implies strong, active defense
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - champion of the cause
   - champion of reform
   - become a champion for
   - longtime champion
   - unlikely champion
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin campus "field" → champion (one who fights in the field)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin campus → Late Latin campio "combatant" → Old French champion → English champion (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's been a real champion for remote work options.
   - We need a champion for this initiative who can push it forward.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Senator Williams has emerged as a champion of healthcare accessibility reform.
   - The organization seeks champions who will advocate tirelessly for policy change.

1. **Noun**: sanction (C, usually plural when meaning approval)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable) Official permission or approval for an action; authoritative support
   - *(Sense 2)* (Often plural) A penalty or punishment imposed for breaking a law or rule (opposite meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: approval, authorization, permission, endorsement, consent
   
   **Antonyms**: prohibition, ban, veto (for approval sense); reward (for penalty sense)
   
   **When to Use**: In formal, legal, or official contexts when discussing authorized approval; note: can also mean punishment (context-dependent)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual contexts; be careful as "sanction" has contradictory meanings (approval vs. penalty) depending on context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - give sanction to
   - official sanction
   - receive sanction
   - without sanction
   - moral sanction
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin sanctio (from sancire "to make sacred, ratify")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin sanctio "decree, approval" → French sanction → English sanction (16th century); dual meaning (approval/penalty) creates confusion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Does the plan have official sanction yet?
   - We can't proceed without management's sanction.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee granted its sanction to the proposed research methodology.
   - Legal sanction for the procedure was obtained through the appropriate regulatory channels.
