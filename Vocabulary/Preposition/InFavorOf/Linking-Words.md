1. **Linking Word**: accordingly (type: connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to show that an action or decision is a logical result of what has been stated; therefore, as a result
   - *Function*: Shows cause-effect relationship; signals that what follows is a consequence of supporting something
   
   **Synonyms**: therefore, consequently, thus, as a result, hence
   
   **Antonyms**: nevertheless, however, despite this, in contrast
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal writing or speech when showing logical consequences of support or agreement; common in academic, business, and policy contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where simpler connectors like "so" are more natural; avoid overuse which makes writing sound stilted
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Statement of support], accordingly [action/decision follows]
   - [Position]: Usually sentence-initial with comma, or mid-sentence between commas
   - [Punctuation]: Preceded by semicolon or period; followed by comma when sentence-initial
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't use without establishing cause; don't overuse in casual contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: accord (agreement) + -ing + -ly (in a manner consistent with)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French acorder "to agree" → English accord → accordingly (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm in favor of the new schedule, so accordingly, I'll adjust my plans.
   - The team supports this approach. Accordingly, we'll implement it next week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is in favor of the proposed reforms; accordingly, implementation will commence immediately.
   - Given widespread support for this initiative, the board has accordingly allocated additional resources.

1. **Linking Word**: therefore (type: subordinating connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a logical conclusion or result; for that reason, consequently
   - *Function*: Establishes causal relationship between support/favor and subsequent action or conclusion
   
   **Synonyms**: thus, consequently, hence, accordingly, as a result
   
   **Antonyms**: however, nevertheless, nonetheless, conversely
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal contexts when drawing conclusions from stated support; appropriate across academic, professional, and logical argumentation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech where "so" is more natural; avoid in emotional appeals where logic is secondary
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Statement of support]; therefore, [conclusion/action]
   - [Position]: Sentence-initial (formal), mid-sentence (more flexible), or after semicolon
   - [Punctuation]: Can follow semicolon with comma after, or start new sentence with comma
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't use as coordinating conjunction between two clauses without semicolon
   
   **Root Analysis**: there (Old English þær) + fore (before, in front) = "for that reason"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English þærfore "for that, for that reason" → Middle English therfore → modern therefore
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I support this idea. Therefore, I'm voting yes.
   - She's in favor of the plan; therefore, she'll help implement it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The evidence overwhelmingly supports this approach; therefore, we recommend its immediate adoption.
   - Given that stakeholders are in favor of these measures, therefore the committee endorses their implementation.

1. **Linking Word**: consequently (type: connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a result or effect of something previously mentioned; as a result, therefore
   - *Function*: Signals that what follows is a direct consequence of the support or favor expressed
   
   **Synonyms**: as a result, therefore, thus, accordingly, hence
   
   **Antonyms**: despite this, nevertheless, however, regardless
   
   **When to Use**: In formal writing and speech to show clear cause-effect relationships; particularly common in academic and professional discourse
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where it sounds overly formal; when the causal relationship is weak or unclear
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Statement of support], consequently [resulting action]
   - [Position]: Sentence-initial after semicolon or period; mid-sentence between commas
   - [Punctuation]: Followed by comma when sentence-initial; set off with commas mid-sentence
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't confuse with "subsequently" (time sequence); ensure clear causal link
   
   **Root Analysis**: consequent (following as result) + -ly (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin consequi "to follow after" → consequent → consequently (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're all in favor of flexible hours; consequently, we're implementing the new policy.
   - He backs the proposal. Consequently, he's helping draft the details.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board is in favor of expanding operations; consequently, a feasibility study has been commissioned.
   - Stakeholders have expressed support for enhanced oversight; consequently, new governance protocols will be established.

1. **Linking Word**: thus (type: connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a conclusion or result; in this way, therefore, as a consequence
   - *Function*: Shows logical conclusion or result of support; more formal than "so" or "therefore"
   
   **Synonyms**: therefore, hence, consequently, accordingly, so
   
   **Antonyms**: however, nevertheless, despite this, conversely
   
   **When to Use**: In formal academic, scientific, and professional writing when drawing conclusions; adds scholarly tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where it sounds overly formal or archaic; avoid overuse which makes writing pedantic
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Statement of support]; thus, [conclusion]
   - [Position]: Sentence-initial after semicolon, mid-sentence, or sentence-final
   - [Punctuation]: Usually followed by comma when sentence-initial; can be used without commas mid-sentence
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't overuse in attempt to sound academic; ensure logical connection exists
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English þus "in this way"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English þus → Middle English thus (unchanged meaning since Old English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm in favor of this approach and thus will vote to approve it.
   - The team supports the change; thus, we're moving forward.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee finds merit in the proposal; thus, it recommends approval to the full board.
   - Research supports this methodology; thus, its adoption is warranted for future studies.

1. **Linking Word**: in light of (type: subordinating phrasal preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a reason or consideration that affects the conclusion; because of, considering
   - *Function*: Introduces the basis for support or favorable position; emphasizes evidence or reasoning
   
   **Synonyms**: considering, given, because of, in view of, taking into account
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, regardless of, notwithstanding, irrespective of
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal contexts when explaining rationale for support; emphasizes that decision is evidence-based
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual contexts; when the reasoning is obvious and doesn't need emphasis
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: In light of [evidence/reason], [statement of support]
   - [Position]: Clause-initial or mid-sentence introducing reasoning
   - [Punctuation]: Followed by comma when introducing dependent clause at sentence start
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't confuse with "in the light of" (British); ensure what follows is genuinely relevant evidence
   
   **Root Analysis**: Prepositional phrase: in + light (metaphor: illumination reveals truth) + of
   
   **Etymology**: Metaphorical use of "light" meaning "illumination/understanding" (17th century construction)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In light of your experience, I'm totally in favor of your suggestion.
   - In light of the benefits, I support moving forward with this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In light of compelling evidence, the committee is in favor of adopting these recommendations.
   - In light of stakeholder input, the administration supports comprehensive policy reform.

1. **Linking Word**: given that (type: subordinating conjunction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a reason or fact that supports a conclusion; since, because, considering that
   - *Function*: Introduces premise that leads to support or favorable position
   
   **Synonyms**: since, considering that, seeing that, because, in view of the fact that
   
   **Antonyms**: although, even though, despite the fact that, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal contexts when providing explicit reasoning for support; common in logical argumentation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When reasoning is obvious; in very casual speech where "since" is more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: Given that [reason/fact], [statement of support/favor]
   - [Position]: Typically clause-initial introducing reasoning; can also be mid-sentence
   - [Punctuation]: Followed by comma when introducing dependent clause at sentence beginning
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't use when the given information isn't actually established or accepted
   
   **Root Analysis**: Past participle of "give" used as conjunction: given (accepted as fact) + that
   
   **Etymology**: "Given" (established fact) + "that" (introducing clause) - construction emerged in formal English (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Given that everyone agrees, I'm in favor of going ahead with it.
   - Given that it saves time, I support the new system.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given that empirical evidence supports this approach, we are in favor of its implementation.
   - Given that stakeholders have expressed support, the board is favorably disposed toward the initiative.

1. **Linking Word**: on the grounds that (type: subordinating phrasal conjunction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce the reason or justification for a position or decision; because, for the reason that
   - *Function*: Explicitly states the basis or justification for support
   
   **Synonyms**: because, for the reason that, on the basis that, due to the fact that
   
   **Antonyms**: despite the fact that, although, even though
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts, especially legal, academic, and policy discussions when stating explicit rationale for support
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation where it sounds overly formal; when simple "because" is sufficient
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Statement of support] on the grounds that [justification/reason]
   - [Position]: Usually follows the main statement, introducing justification
   - [Punctuation]: Preceded by comma when joining clauses; introduces dependent clause
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't use for weak or subjective reasons in formal contexts; implies solid justification
   
   **Root Analysis**: Prepositional phrase: on + the grounds (basis, foundation) + that (introducing clause)
   
   **Etymology**: "Grounds" (basis, foundation) metaphor from physical ground/foundation (16th century usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I support the proposal on the grounds that it'll make everyone's life easier.
   - She's in favor of it on the grounds that it's fair to everyone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee is in favor of the amendment on the grounds that it enhances transparency.
   - We support this legislation on the grounds that it addresses critical infrastructure deficiencies.

1. **Linking Word**: for this reason (type: connector/transitional phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce a conclusion or action that follows logically from what has been stated; because of this, therefore
   - *Function*: Connects stated support or favorable position to resulting action or conclusion
   
   **Synonyms**: therefore, because of this, that's why, for this purpose, consequently
   
   **Antonyms**: despite this, even so, nevertheless, regardless
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal writing when making cause-effect connections explicit; adds clarity to logical progression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual speech where simpler connectors like "so" or "that's why" are more natural
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Statement of support]. For this reason, [conclusion/action]
   - [Position]: Sentence-initial after period or semicolon
   - [Punctuation]: Followed by comma when sentence-initial; can use semicolon before it
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Ensure "this reason" clearly refers to something previously stated
   
   **Root Analysis**: Prepositional phrase: for (indicating purpose/cause) + this reason
   
   **Etymology**: Common English construction combining "for" (purpose) with "reason" (cause/explanation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm in favor of the plan. For this reason, I'll vote yes tomorrow.
   - The proposal makes sense. For this reason, we should support it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Stakeholders have expressed overwhelming support for reform. For this reason, the administration is in favor of immediate action.
   - The evidence demonstrates clear benefits. For this reason, the committee endorses widespread adoption.
