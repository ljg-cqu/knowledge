1. **Verb**: support
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To give approval, encouragement, or assistance to someone or something; to be in favor of
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To provide financial or material assistance
   - *(Sense 3)* (Transitive) To bear the weight of; to hold up physically
   
   **Synonyms**: back, endorse, champion, advocate for, uphold
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, resist, undermine, contradict, hinder
   
   **When to Use**: In all contexts when indicating approval, backing, or assistance; versatile verb appropriate from casual to formal registers; one of the most common ways to express being in favor
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When more specific or emphatic language is needed; "support" is somewhat neutral and may lack force in persuasive contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - support a proposal
   - support someone's decision
   - fully support
   - support the initiative
   - continue to support
   
   **Root Analysis**: sup- (from below) + port (carry) = carry from below, uphold
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supportare (sub- "from below" + portare "to carry") → Old French supporter → English support (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I totally support your decision to go back to school.
   - Do you support the idea of having meetings on Fridays?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee supports the implementation of comprehensive reforms.
   - We strongly support initiatives that promote environmental sustainability.

1. **Verb**: endorse
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To declare public approval or support for someone or something; to give official backing
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To sign a check or document
   
   **Synonyms**: support, approve, back, sanction, recommend
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, reject, condemn, denounce, criticize
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when indicating official, public, or strong support; particularly common in political endorsements, professional recommendations, and formal approvals
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For private or informal agreement; endorsement implies public, often official declaration
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - endorse a candidate
   - endorse the proposal
   - fully endorse
   - publicly endorse
   - officially endorse
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (in, on) + dorsum (back) = write on the back of
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin indorsare "to write on the back" → Old French endosser → English endorse (16th century); meaning expanded from signing documents to general approval
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd definitely endorse her for that position—she's great.
   - Can you endorse this approach to the team?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board unanimously endorsed the strategic restructuring plan.
   - Leading economists have endorsed the proposed fiscal policy reforms.

1. **Verb**: advocate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To publicly recommend or support a particular cause, policy, or person; to speak or argue in favor of
   
   **Synonyms**: champion, promote, support, recommend, push for
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, discourage, argue against, condemn
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when indicating active, often passionate support involving public argumentation; implies speaking out and making a case for something
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For passive agreement; advocate implies active promotion and argumentation
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - advocate for change
   - strongly advocate
   - advocate reform
   - advocate a position
   - continue to advocate
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to, toward) + vocare (to call) = call to one's aid
   
   **Etymology**: Latin advocare "to summon to one's aid" → advocate (14th century as noun, 17th century as verb)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've been advocating for better benefits for months now.
   - She really advocates for work-life balance in our department.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The senator has long advocated for comprehensive healthcare reform.
   - Leading scientists advocate immediate action to address climate change.

1. **Verb**: champion
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To fight for, defend, or support a cause or person vigorously and publicly; to act as an advocate
   
   **Synonyms**: advocate for, defend, support, promote, fight for
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, attack, undermine, criticize
   
   **When to Use**: When describing vigorous, dedicated support involving active defense or promotion; implies passionate commitment; appropriate in political, social, and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For weak or passive support; champion implies strong, active defense
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - champion a cause
   - champion reform
   - champion the rights of
   - champion innovation
   - champion change
   
   **Root Analysis**: From noun "champion" (Latin campus "field" → fighter in the field)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin campus → Late Latin campio → Old French champion → English champion (verb use emerged 19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's been championing this approach since day one.
   - Someone needs to champion this idea at the next meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has championed civil rights causes for over five decades.
   - Dr. Rodriguez has championed evidence-based practices throughout her distinguished career.

1. **Verb**: back
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To support someone or something, especially with financial, material, or moral assistance; to give backing to
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive with phrasal forms) To move backward
   
   **Synonyms**: support, endorse, sponsor, finance, stand behind
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, undermine, withdraw from, abandon
   
   **When to Use**: In informal to semi-formal contexts when indicating active support, often with resources; slightly less formal than "support" or "endorse"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal academic or legal writing where more formal verbs are preferred
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - back a proposal
   - back someone up
   - back a candidate
   - back the plan
   - financially back
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English bæc "back, rear"
   
   **Etymology**: Old English bæc → verb form emerged (support from behind) in 16th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll back whatever decision you make on this.
   - Are you willing to back this project with your team?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The venture capital firm has agreed to back the innovative startup.
   - Key stakeholders have pledged to back the comprehensive reform initiative.

1. **Verb**: approve
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To officially agree to or accept something as satisfactory; to give formal consent or positive judgment
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive with "of") To believe that something or someone is good or acceptable
   
   **Synonyms**: accept, authorize, sanction, endorse, ratify
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, disapprove, veto, deny, refuse
   
   **When to Use**: In formal and semi-formal contexts when indicating official acceptance or positive judgment; common in organizational, regulatory, and decision-making contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For enthusiastic support (approve is relatively neutral); when informal agreement is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approve a request
   - approve of something
   - approve the plan
   - formally approve
   - approve unanimously
   
   **Root Analysis**: ap- (to) + probare (test, prove good) = prove acceptable
   
   **Etymology**: Latin approbare "to assent to as good" → Old French aprover → English approve (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I definitely approve of your plan to take some time off.
   - Did the boss approve your vacation request?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board approved the budget allocation for the fiscal year.
   - Regulatory authorities approved the merger subject to certain conditions.

1. **Verb**: favor (favour in British English)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To prefer or support one person, group, or option over others; to show preference for
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To treat with partiality or give advantage to
   
   **Synonyms**: prefer, support, choose, opt for, lean toward
   
   **Antonyms**: disfavor, oppose, reject, discriminate against
   
   **When to Use**: When indicating preference or support among alternatives; can imply partiality; appropriate in formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When impartiality is required or valued; favoring can suggest bias
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - favor an approach
   - favor a solution
   - favor one option over another
   - strongly favor
   - tend to favor
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin favor "goodwill, partiality"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin favor → Old French favour → English favor (14th century as noun, verb use later)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I favor the second option—it seems simpler.
   - Which approach do you favor for this project?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee favors a phased implementation strategy.
   - Recent polling indicates voters favor candidates with moderate positions.

1. **Verb**: promote
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To support or actively encourage a cause, venture, or aim; to advocate for or publicize
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To raise someone to a higher position or rank
   - *(Sense 3)* (Transitive) To advertise or market
   
   **Synonyms**: advocate, support, advance, encourage, champion
   
   **Antonyms**: oppose, hinder, discourage, suppress, demote
   
   **When to Use**: When indicating active support involving advocacy, publicity, or advancement; implies working to increase acceptance or success
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For passive agreement; promote implies active efforts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - promote a cause
   - promote reform
   - actively promote
   - promote awareness
   - promote change
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (forward) + movere (to move) = move forward
   
   **Etymology**: Latin promovere "to move forward" → promote (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm happy to promote your idea at the next meeting.
   - Let's promote this initiative across all teams.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The foundation promotes literacy programs in underserved communities.
   - Government agencies actively promote policies that support small businesses.

1. **Verb**: uphold
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To support, maintain, or defend something, especially a law, principle, or decision; to confirm or sustain
   
   **Synonyms**: maintain, support, sustain, defend, preserve
   
   **Antonyms**: overturn, reject, violate, abandon, undermine
   
   **When to Use**: In formal contexts when supporting principles, decisions, or standards; particularly common in legal contexts for confirming decisions; implies principled support
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For supporting individuals or casual preferences; uphold is used for principles, decisions, standards
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - uphold a decision
   - uphold the law
   - uphold principles
   - uphold values
   - uphold standards
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (upward) + hold (grasp, maintain) = hold up, maintain
   
   **Etymology**: Old English combination of up + hold → uphold (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to uphold the standards we agreed on.
   - I'll uphold whatever decision the team makes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The appellate court upheld the lower court's ruling on all grounds.
   - This organization is committed to upholding the highest ethical standards.

1. **Verb**: sanction
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To give official permission or approval for an action; to authorize
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To impose a penalty on (opposite meaning—context dependent)
   
   **Synonyms**: approve, authorize, permit, endorse, ratify (for approval sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: prohibit, forbid, veto, ban (for approval sense); reward (for penalty sense)
   
   **When to Use**: In formal, official, or legal contexts when granting official approval or authorization; note the confusing dual meaning (approve vs. penalize)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual contexts; be careful of ambiguity as "sanction" can mean both approve and penalize depending on context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - officially sanction
   - sanction an action
   - sanction a policy
   - formally sanction
   - legally sanction
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin sanctio (from sancire "to make sacred, ratify")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin sancire → sanctio → French sanction → English sanction (16th century); confusing dual meaning emerged over time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did the committee sanction this approach?
   - We can't proceed until management sanctions the plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The regulatory body sanctioned the proposed procedural modifications.
   - International law sanctions humanitarian intervention under specific circumstances.
