1. **Verb**: add (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To join or unite something to something else so as to increase its quantity, size, or importance
   - (Intransitive) To combine numbers in arithmetic to find a total
   
   **Synonyms**: append, attach, include, incorporate, supplement
   
   **Antonyms**: subtract, remove, take away, delete, exclude
   
   **When to Use**: All contexts from casual to formal; basic action of including extra items; mathematical contexts; general addition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When multiplication or other operations are meant; context clarifies usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - add to something
   - add something to
   - add up
   - add on
   - add value
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ad- (to, toward) + -dere (to put)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin addere (to add, join) → Old French ajouter → Middle English adden → modern "add"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you add milk to the shopping list?
   - Add these numbers together.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study adds valuable insights to existing literature.
   - Researchers added control variables to improve model specification.

1. **Verb**: supplement (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To add an extra element or amount to something in order to complete it or make it more effective
   - To provide something additional to make up for a deficiency
   
   **Synonyms**: complement, add to, enhance, augment, reinforce
   
   **Antonyms**: reduce, diminish, subtract from, weaken
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; emphasizing completion or enhancement; academic, professional, and medical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (use "add to" instead); when simple addition without enhancement is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supplement something with
   - supplement income
   - supplement diet
   - supplement existing
   - supplement knowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin sub- (from below) + -plement (from plere, to fill)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplēre (to fill up, complete) → supplementum → English verb "supplement"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He supplements his salary with freelance work.
   - I supplement my diet with vitamins.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings supplement existing research on cognitive development.
   - Participants supplemented their income through part-time employment.

1. **Verb**: augment (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make something greater by adding to it; to increase in size, number, or degree
   - More formal than "increase" or "add to"
   
   **Synonyms**: increase, enlarge, enhance, boost, expand
   
   **Antonyms**: reduce, diminish, decrease, lessen, curtail
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts; emphasizing significant increase or enhancement; professional and technical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "increase" or "boost"); very common contexts where simpler terms suffice
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - augment something with
   - augment income
   - augment capacity
   - augment resources
   - augment knowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin aug- (increase) + -ment (verb-forming suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin augēre (to increase) → augmentāre → Old French augmenter → English "augment"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She augments her income by tutoring students.
   - Technology can augment our abilities.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention augmented participants' problem-solving capabilities.
   - Additional funding will augment existing research infrastructure.

1. **Verb**: expand (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To make something larger in size, number, or importance
   - (Intransitive) To become larger in size, number, or importance; to increase in scope
   
   **Synonyms**: enlarge, extend, increase, broaden, develop
   
   **Antonyms**: contract, reduce, shrink, narrow, diminish
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; emphasizing growth in scope or size; business, academic, and general contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple addition without growth is meant; when "extend" (length/time) is more precise
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - expand on something
   - expand into
   - expand upon
   - expand horizons
   - expand knowledge
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ex- (out) + -pand (from pandere, to spread)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin expandere (to spread out) → expanded → English "expand"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you expand on that idea?
   - The company is expanding into new markets.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research expands upon previous findings in the field.
   - Organizations expand their operations to meet increasing demand.

1. **Verb**: extend (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To make something longer or larger in space or time; to offer or give
   - (Intransitive) To cover a certain area or continue for a particular distance or period
   
   **Synonyms**: prolong, lengthen, stretch, expand, broaden
   
   **Antonyms**: shorten, reduce, curtail, contract, limit
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; emphasizing lengthening in time or space; offering something (extend an invitation); formal and informal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When width or general size (not length) is the focus; context-dependent
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - extend to
   - extend beyond
   - extend deadline
   - extend invitation
   - extend offer
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ex- (out) + -tend (from tendere, to stretch)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin extendere (to stretch out) → extended → English "extend"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we extend the deadline?
   - The fence extends all the way to the road.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The university extended the submission deadline by two weeks.
   - This research extends existing theoretical frameworks.

1. **Verb**: append (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To add something as an attachment or supplement; to attach or affix something to something larger or more important
   - Formal/technical term
   
   **Synonyms**: attach, add, affix, adjoin, annex
   
   **Antonyms**: detach, remove, separate, delete
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and technical contexts; computing (append to file); academic writing; emphasizing attachment to existing content
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "add" or "attach"); when "add" is clearer and less formal
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - append to
   - append document
   - append data
   - append information
   - append signature
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ad- (to) + -pend (from pendere, to hang)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin appendere (to hang upon, attach) → Old French appendre → English "append"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll append the document to the email.
   - Just append your signature at the bottom.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Additional analyses are appended to the main report.
   - The researcher appended supplementary materials to the manuscript.

1. **Verb**: incorporate (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To include something as part of a whole; to combine or unite into one body
   - To form a legal corporation
   
   **Synonyms**: include, integrate, combine, merge, embed
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, separate, remove, extract, segregate
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts; emphasizing integration into a whole; business, academic, and technical writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "include" or "add"); when items remain separate rather than integrated
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - incorporate into
   - incorporate within
   - incorporate elements
   - incorporate feedback
   - incorporate changes
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin in- (into) + -corpor (from corpus, body) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin incorporāre (to form into a body) → Late Latin → English "incorporate"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should incorporate your suggestions into the plan.
   - The recipe incorporates fresh herbs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The revised framework incorporates stakeholder feedback.
   - Researchers incorporated multiple methodological approaches.

1. **Verb**: include (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To contain or comprise as part of a whole; to make part of a whole or set
   - To allow someone to share in an activity or privilege
   
   **Synonyms**: contain, comprise, encompass, incorporate, involve
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, leave out, except, remove
   
   **When to Use**: All registers from casual to formal; most common and neutral term for making something part of something else
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When emphasizing attachment rather than membership (use "attach" or "append"); always clear in context
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - include in
   - include with
   - include among
   - include as part of
   - services include
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin in- (in) + -clude (from claudere, to close)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin inclūdere (to shut in, enclose) → included → English "include"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't forget to include me in the group chat.
   - The price includes breakfast.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis includes data from multiple sources.
   - The curriculum includes both theoretical and practical components.

1. **Verb**: enhance (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To increase or improve the quality, value, or extent of something; to intensify or make more prominent
   - Emphasizes improvement rather than just addition
   
   **Synonyms**: improve, augment, boost, increase, strengthen
   
   **Antonyms**: diminish, reduce, weaken, degrade, impair
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; emphasizing quality improvement; marketing, academic, and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple addition without quality improvement is meant; very casual speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - enhance quality
   - enhance performance
   - enhance value
   - enhance experience
   - enhance understanding
   
   **Root Analysis**: Anglo-Norman en- (make) + -hance (from Old French haucier, to raise)
   
   **Etymology**: Anglo-Norman French enhaucer (to raise, elevate) → English "enhance"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These spices really enhance the flavor.
   - Good lighting enhances the room.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention enhanced participants' cognitive abilities.
   - Technology enhances educational outcomes.

1. **Verb**: complement (transitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To add to something in a way that completes or improves it; to go well together with something
   - Note: different from "compliment" (to praise)
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, supplement, enhance, accompany, match
   
   **Antonyms**: contradict, clash with, conflict with, detract from
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts; emphasizing completion or harmonious addition; note spelling vs. "compliment"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When expressing praise (use "compliment"); casual contexts may use simpler alternatives
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - complement each other
   - complement something with
   - complement existing
   - perfectly complement
   - complement the
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin com- (together) + -plement (from plere, to fill)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin complēmentum → English noun/verb "complement"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The wine complements the meal perfectly.
   - Your skills complement mine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Qualitative data complement quantitative findings.
   - The framework complements existing theoretical models.

1. **Verb**: accumulate (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To gather or acquire an increasing amount of something over time
   - (Intransitive) To increase in quantity or number over time; to build up
   
   **Synonyms**: gather, collect, amass, build up, stockpile
   
   **Antonyms**: disperse, dissipate, scatter, distribute, deplete
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; emphasizing gradual increase over time; financial, academic, and general contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When sudden or one-time addition is meant (use "add"); when decrease is occurring
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accumulate over time
   - accumulate wealth
   - accumulate knowledge
   - accumulate evidence
   - accumulate debt
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ac- (to) + -cumulate (from cumulus, heap)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin accumulāre (to heap up) → accumulated → English "accumulate"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've accumulated a lot of books over the years.
   - Dust accumulates if you don't clean regularly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Researchers accumulated extensive data over the five-year study period.
   - Evidence accumulates supporting the efficacy of this intervention.

1. **Verb**: broaden (transitive/intransitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To make something wider or more extensive in scope
   - (Intransitive) To become wider or more extensive
   
   **Synonyms**: widen, expand, extend, enlarge, increase
   
   **Antonyms**: narrow, restrict, limit, confine, reduce
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; emphasizing expansion in scope or range; often used with abstract concepts (knowledge, perspectives)
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When physical length (not width) is meant (use "lengthen"); context clarifies usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - broaden horizons
   - broaden perspective
   - broaden scope
   - broaden understanding
   - broaden appeal
   
   **Root Analysis**: broad (adjective: wide) + -en (verb-forming suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English brād (broad) → broaden (verb form with -en suffix)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Travel really broadens your mind.
   - This course will broaden your skills.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The revised curriculum broadens students' analytical capabilities.
   - International collaboration broadens research perspectives.
