1. **Noun**: addition (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable) A person or thing added or joined; something extra
   - (Uncountable) The action or process of adding something to something else
   
   **Synonyms**: supplement, extra, appendage, enhancement, augmentation
   
   **Antonyms**: subtraction, removal, deletion, reduction
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to extra items or the process of adding; formal and informal contexts; mathematical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing multiplication (different operation); when "supplement" or "extra" would be clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in addition to
   - make an addition
   - addition to the family
   - latest addition
   - welcome addition
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ad- (to, toward) + -dition (from dare, to give)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin additio (adding, increase) → Old French addicion → Middle English → modern "addition"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The baby is a wonderful addition to our family.
   - This sauce is a great addition to the meal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal represents a significant addition to existing research.
   - The addition of control variables improved model accuracy.

1. **Noun**: supplement (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something added to complete a thing, make up for a deficiency, or extend or strengthen the whole
   - A separate section added to a book, newspaper, or magazine
   
   **Synonyms**: addition, complement, addendum, appendix, extra
   
   **Antonyms**: reduction, core, essence, main body
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to items that complete or enhance something primary; academic, medical, and publishing contexts; emphasizing complementary function
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing primary or standalone items; casual contexts may use simpler terms like "extra"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - dietary supplement
   - food supplement
   - vitamin supplement
   - supplementary material
   - act as a supplement
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin sub- (under, up from below) + -plement (from plere, to fill)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin supplementum (something added to fill up) → Old French → English "supplement"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I take a vitamin supplement every morning.
   - The magazine has a travel supplement this month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants received a nutritional supplement throughout the intervention period.
   - The document includes a supplement containing detailed methodological procedures.

1. **Noun**: extra (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An item, amount, or person beyond what is usual or strictly necessary; something additional
   - A person hired to appear in a film or television show, typically in a non-speaking role
   
   **Synonyms**: addition, bonus, supplement, add-on, optional item
   
   **Antonyms**: necessity, essential, requirement, standard item
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to bonus or optional items; informal and semi-formal contexts; commercial contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing (use "addition" or "supplement"); when emphasizing essential items
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - optional extras
   - included extras
   - cost extra
   - little extras
   - added extras
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin extra (outside, beyond)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin extra (outside) → used as noun meaning "something beyond what is necessary"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The car comes with several extras like heated seats.
   - They threw in some extras for free.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The package includes several extras at no additional cost.
   - Optional extras are available for purchase.

1. **Noun**: extension (C/U)
   **Meaning**:

   - (Uncountable) The action of extending or the state of being extended
   
   **Synonyms**: addition, enlargement, expansion, prolongation, annex
   
   **Antonyms**: reduction, contraction, shortening, limitation
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to physical additions to buildings; time extensions; conceptual extensions; formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple "addition" would be clearer; context-dependent usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - extension of time
   - file extension
   - extension cord
   - house extension
   - grant an extension
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ex- (out) + -tension (from tendere, to stretch)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin extensio (stretching out) → Old French → English "extension"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're building an extension on the back of the house.
   - Can I get an extension on the deadline?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The university approved an extension for dissertation submission.
   - The extension of the existing framework accommodates new variables.

1. **Noun**: appendix (C, pl: appendices or appendixes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A section or table of subsidiary matter at the end of a book or document
   - (Medical) A tube-shaped organ attached to the large intestine
   
   **Synonyms**: supplement, addendum, attachment, annex (for document sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: main text, body, core content
   
   **When to Use**: Academic and formal writing; referring to supplementary sections at the end of documents; medical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to integrated content (not separate); casual conversation outside academic contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - see appendix
   - included in the appendix
   - appendix A/B/C
   - detailed in the appendix
   - refer to appendix
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ad- (to, toward) + -pendix (from pendere, to hang)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin appendix (something attached) → English "appendix" (supplement to text)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check the appendix for the full list.
   - All the technical details are in the appendix.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Detailed statistical analyses are provided in Appendix A.
   - The survey instrument is reproduced in the appendix.

1. **Noun**: addendum (C, pl: addenda)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An item of additional material added at the end of a book or other publication
   - Something added or to be added
   
   **Synonyms**: supplement, appendix, addition, postscript, attachment
   
   **Antonyms**: original text, main body, core document
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts; referring to material added after original publication; legal and business documents; note: plural is "addenda"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; when "addition" or "extra" would be simpler; very technical term
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - issue an addendum
   - addendum to the report
   - addendum to the contract
   - include as addendum
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin ad- (to) + -dendum (gerundive of dare, to give - "that which must be added")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin addendum (that which is to be added) → English technical/academic term
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They sent out an addendum with the corrections.
   - Check the addendum for updates.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - An addendum to the contract specifies revised terms.
   - The addendum contains supplementary data supporting the primary analysis.

1. **Noun**: complement (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thing that completes or brings to perfection; the amount or number needed to make a group complete
   - Note: different from "compliment" (expression of praise)
   
   **Synonyms**: addition, supplement, completion, counterpart, accompaniment
   
   **Antonyms**: contradiction, opposite, detractor
   
   **When to Use**: Describing items that complete or enhance something; emphasizing mutual completion; formal and semi-formal contexts; note spelling difference from "compliment"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When expressing praise (use "compliment"); when simple addition without completion is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - perfect complement
   - complement to something
   - ideal complement
   - natural complement
   - complement each other
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin com- (together) + -plement (from plere, to fill)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin complementum (that which fills up or completes) → English "complement"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Wine is a perfect complement to the meal.
   - Your skills are a great complement to the team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Qualitative data serve as a complement to quantitative findings.
   - The theoretical framework provides a complement to empirical observations.

1. **Noun**: accompaniment (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable) Something that supplements or complements something else
   - (Countable) A musical part that supports or partners a solo instrument, voice, or group
   - (Uncountable) The action of accompanying
   
   **Synonyms**: addition, supplement, complement, adjunct, side dish (culinary)
   
   **Antonyms**: main element, solo, standalone, primary component
   
   **When to Use**: Culinary contexts (side dishes); musical contexts; describing items that go together; formal and informal uses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing primary elements; when "addition" is clearer
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - accompaniment to
   - perfect accompaniment
   - musical accompaniment
   - without accompaniment
   - serve as accompaniment
   
   **Root Analysis**: accompany (verb) + -ment (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French accompagnier → English "accompany" → "accompaniment" (noun form)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Rice is a good accompaniment to curry.
   - He played the piano accompaniment for her song.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The dish is served with seasonal vegetables as accompaniment.
   - Orchestral accompaniment enhances the vocal performance.

1. **Noun**: bonus (C, pl: bonuses)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An extra payment or benefit given in addition to what is usual or expected
   - An additional feature or benefit
   
   **Synonyms**: extra, addition, premium, perk, benefit
   
   **Antonyms**: penalty, deduction, standard payment, basic package
   
   **When to Use**: Financial contexts (extra payment); general contexts (extra benefit); emphasizing unexpected or welcome additions; informal and semi-formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic writing; when describing expected standard items
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bonus payment
   - added bonus
   - bonus feature
   - receive a bonus
   - as a bonus
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin bonus (good)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin bonus (good) → used in English as noun meaning "extra good thing given"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The hotel had a pool, which was a nice bonus.
   - I got a bonus at work this year!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Employees receive performance-based bonuses annually.
   - The package includes several bonus features at no additional cost.

1. **Noun**: enhancement (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Countable) An improvement or augmentation in quality, value, or attractiveness
   - (Uncountable) The action of enhancing or the state of being enhanced
   
   **Synonyms**: improvement, augmentation, enrichment, upgrade, boost
   
   **Antonyms**: degradation, reduction, deterioration, decline
   
   **When to Use**: Describing improvements or increases in quality; technical and formal contexts; emphasizing value addition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing simple addition without quality improvement; very casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - enhancement of
   - performance enhancement
   - image enhancement
   - quality enhancement
   - provide enhancement
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (make) + -hance (from Old French haucier, to raise) + -ment (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Anglo-Norman French enhaucer (to raise, elevate) → English "enhance" → "enhancement"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new lighting is a real enhancement to the room.
   - This app has lots of enhancements compared to the old version.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention resulted in significant enhancement of cognitive function.
   - Technological enhancements improve operational efficiency.
