1. **Preposition**: including (type: simple preposition, derived from present participle)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Having as part of a whole; containing or encompassing within a larger category or set
   - Used to introduce specific examples or items that are part of what has been mentioned
   - Indicates that the named items are contained within or are members of a larger group
   
   **Synonyms**: such as (in context of examples), with (in context of accompaniment), comprising (formal), encompassing (formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, except for, apart from, but not, without
   
   **When to Use**: Introducing examples within a category; specifying particular members of a group; listing items that are part of a package or set; all registers from casual to formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the list is complete and exhaustive (use "consisting of" instead); when you want to exclude items (use "excluding" or "except"); when the items mentioned are NOT part of the whole
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - including [noun phrase] (everyone including the CEO)
     - including [list of items] (several countries including France, Germany, and Italy)
     - including but not limited to [examples] (legal/formal)
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (including is not a dependent preposition with specific verbs)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A (including typically follows complete noun phrases rather than forming fixed collocations)
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + clud- (close, shut) + -ing (present participle suffix functioning as preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin includere (to shut in, enclose, contain) → include (verb, 15th century) → including (present participle used prepositionally, 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone's invited, including you!
   - The package costs $50, including shipping.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team comprised scholars from multiple disciplines, including sociology, psychology, and anthropology.
   - All employees, including senior management, must complete the mandatory training.

2. **Preposition**: including (in range specifications)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal/Spatial)* Used to specify that endpoints or boundaries are part of the range being discussed
   - Indicates that the limits mentioned are contained within the scope
   
   **Synonyms**: inclusive of (formal), through (in some contexts), up to and including
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, up to but not including, through but excluding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal specifications, date ranges, numerical intervals, technical documentation, legal contexts requiring precision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When boundaries are excluded from the range; casual contexts where ambiguity is acceptable
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - from [date] to [date] including [endpoint]
     - ages [X] to [Y] including [Y]
     - chapters 1-5 including chapter 5
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: date range including, age group including
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Extension of general "including" meaning to precise range specification (19th-20th century technical usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The sale runs Monday through Friday including Friday.
   - Everyone aged 5 to 12 including 12-year-olds gets in free.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study period encompasses 2015-2020, including both endpoints for data collection.
   - Participants aged 18-65 years, including those at the upper age limit, were eligible for enrollment.

3. **Preposition**: including (emphasizing membership)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Used to emphasize that a particular person, group, or item is counted as part of a total
   - Highlights the inclusion of someone or something that might be unexpected, notable, or important
   
   **Synonyms**: even (for emphasis), counting (in numerical contexts), with [X] counted
   
   **Antonyms**: not counting, excluding, apart from
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing notable participation or membership; headcounts and enumerations; drawing attention to specific inclusions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the inclusion is completely expected and unremarkable; can be redundant if inclusion is obvious
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - including [notable person/group]
     - [number] people including [specific person]
     - including myself/yourself/himself/herself
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: attendees including, participants including, members including
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic use developed from general inclusion meaning (18th-19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Five of us went, including Sarah who usually hates camping.
   - Everyone contributed, including me!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The delegation comprised 12 representatives, including the Minister of Foreign Affairs.
   - All stakeholders, including those who previously expressed reservations, endorsed the proposal.

4. **Preposition**: including (in pricing and commercial contexts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - commercial)* Indicating that certain costs, services, or items are part of the stated price or package
   - Shows what is covered without additional charge
   
   **Synonyms**: with [X] included, inclusive of (formal), covering, with
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, not including, plus (indicating addition), extra
   
   **When to Use**: Pricing statements, commercial offers, package descriptions, sales contexts, contract terms
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When items require additional payment; when exclusions need to be stated (use "not including" or "excluding")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - [price] including [costs/items]
     - including all fees/taxes/charges
     - package including [services/items]
   - **Verb + prep**: priced including, offered including, quoted including
   - **Adjective + prep**: available including, free including
   - **Noun + prep**: price including, cost including, package including
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Commercial usage developed from general inclusion sense (19th-20th century with modern commerce)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The room costs $150 a night including breakfast.
   - It's $20 including tax.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The quoted price is $50,000 including installation, training, and first-year maintenance.
   - The comprehensive package is priced at €2,500 including all applicable taxes and service charges.

5. **Preposition**: including (in legal and formal hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - legal)* Used in legal documents to provide examples while explicitly not limiting scope to those examples
   - Introduces representative instances without claiming exhaustiveness
   
   **Synonyms**: such as (less formal), inter alia (Latin legal term), among other things
   
   **Antonyms**: consisting solely of, limited exclusively to, only
   
   **When to Use**: Legal documents, contracts, formal policies, terms and conditions, situations requiring legal precision
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts; when complete enumeration is required; informal communication
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - including but not limited to [examples]
     - including without limitation [examples]
     - including, inter alia, [examples] (very formal)
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (appears in formal clauses)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: rights including, obligations including, remedies including
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Legal usage codified 19th-20th century to prevent unintended limitation through examples
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - [Rarely used in casual contexts due to legalistic nature]
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The Company may terminate for cause, including but not limited to material breach, fraud, or insolvency.
   - Participants must comply with all regulations, including but not limited to safety standards, ethical guidelines, and data protection laws.

6. **Preposition**: including (in academic enumeration)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - academic)* Used to introduce specific examples that illustrate a general category in scholarly writing
   - Provides concrete instances of abstract concepts or general statements
   
   **Synonyms**: such as, for example, notably, particularly
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, except, apart from
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, research papers, literature reviews, formal analysis, scholarly presentations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When examples are not truly representative; when complete lists are required
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - [general category] including [specific examples]
     - multiple [noun] including [examples]
     - various [noun] including [examples]
   - **Verb + prep**: examined including, studied including, analyzed including
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: factors including, variables including, theories including, methods including
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Academic usage standardized in scholarly English (19th-20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The study looked at several factors including age and income.
   - Many countries participated including the major powers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research employed multiple methodologies, including ethnographic observation, semi-structured interviews, and survey instruments.
   - Various theoretical frameworks informed the analysis, including social constructionism, critical theory, and phenomenology.

7. **Preposition**: including (with percentages and proportions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - quantitative)* Used to specify what is counted within a stated percentage or proportion
   - Clarifies what elements are encompassed in numerical statements
   
   **Synonyms**: counting, with [X] counted, comprising
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding, not counting, without
   
   **When to Use**: Statistical reports, research findings, demographic data, quantitative analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the included elements are obvious; when exclusions need emphasis
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - [percentage] including [category]
     - [number] participants including [subgroup]
     - [proportion] of [group] including [specific members]
   - **Verb + prep**: totaling including, comprising including, numbering including
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: percentage including, total including, population including
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Quantitative usage developed with statistical methods (19th-20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - About 60% of students passed, including some who barely studied.
   - There were 30 people there including kids.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Approximately 73% of respondents expressed satisfaction, including 45% who indicated high satisfaction.
   - The sample comprised 500 participants including 280 women and 220 men across age ranges from 18 to 75 years.

8. **Preposition**: including (sentence-initial for emphasis)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - rhetorical)* Placed at the beginning of a sentence to emphasize what follows or to introduce significant examples
   - Creates emphasis by fronting the inclusion statement
   
   **Synonyms**: notably (for emphasis), particularly, even, especially
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding (when fronted), except
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing, rhetorical emphasis, when highlighting particularly important inclusions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where mid-sentence placement is more natural; when no special emphasis is needed
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - Including [notable item], [main clause]
     - Including [person/group], [statement about total/group]
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A (sentence-initial position)
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: N/A (appears before main clause)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Rhetorical fronting developed in formal English style (18th-19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Including you, there are five of us going.
   - Including tips, the bill was over $100.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Including the most recent data, the evidence strongly supports the hypothesis.
   - Including contributions from external stakeholders, the total funding reached $5 million.

9. **Preposition**: including (with "among others" for non-exhaustive lists)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - hedging)* Used together with "among others" to explicitly signal that examples given are representative, not complete
   - Academic and formal hedging strategy to avoid claims of exhaustiveness
   
   **Synonyms**: such as among other things, inter alia
   
   **Antonyms**: consisting exclusively of, only, limited to
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, formal reports, when providing examples without claiming completeness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the list IS actually complete; casual contexts where such precision is unnecessary
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - including [examples], among others
     - including, among others, [examples]
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: scholars including, studies including, countries including
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Academic hedging pattern developed 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We visited many cities, including Paris and London, among others.
   - Several people helped, including John and Mary, among others.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research drew on multiple theoretical frameworks, including social constructionism and critical theory, among others.
   - Distinguished scholars participated, including Professor Smith and Dr. Jones, among others.

10. **Preposition**: including (in parenthetical clarifications)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract - clarification)* Used in parentheses or set off by dashes/commas to add clarifying examples
   - Provides additional specification without disrupting main sentence flow
   
   **Synonyms**: such as (in parentheses), for example, like
   
   **Antonyms**: excluding (in parentheses), except
   
   **When to Use**: Technical writing, clarifications, when examples are supplementary rather than essential to sentence meaning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When examples are central to the sentence meaning; when parenthetical interruption disrupts readability
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: 
     - [statement] (including [examples])
     - [statement]—including [examples]—[continuation]
     - [statement], including [examples], [continuation]
   - **Verb + prep**: N/A
   - **Adjective + prep**: N/A
   - **Noun + prep**: features (including), services (including), items (including)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Same as above (in- + clud- + -ing)
   
   **Etymology**: Parenthetical usage developed with complex sentence structures (19th-20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Several features (including voice control) are now available.
   - Many factors—including weather and timing—affected the outcome.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system offers multiple capabilities (including real-time data processing and predictive analytics) that enhance operational efficiency.
   - Various stakeholders—including regulatory agencies, industry partners, and community representatives—participated in the consultation process.
