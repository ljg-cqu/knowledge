1. **Adjective**: inclusive (comparative: more inclusive, superlative: most inclusive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Including or covering all items, categories, or services mentioned; not excluding any part of a group or range
   - *(Gradable)* Designed to include all people or types without discrimination; open to everyone
   
   **Synonyms**: comprehensive, all-encompassing, all-inclusive, complete, total
   
   **Antonyms**: exclusive, selective, restricted, limited
   
   **When to Use**: When describing policies, prices, ranges, or environments that include everything or everyone; formal and professional contexts; diversity and equality discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing selective or exclusive situations; avoid in contexts where exclusivity is valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - inclusive policy
   - inclusive approach
   - fully inclusive
   - inclusive environment
   - inclusive of all costs
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + clud- (close, shut) + -ive (adjective suffix, characterized by)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin includere (to shut in, enclose) → inclusive (16th century) meaning "tending to include"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The resort offers all-inclusive packages including meals and activities.
   - We're trying to create a more inclusive workplace where everyone feels welcome.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has implemented inclusive hiring practices to ensure equal opportunities.
   - The quoted price is inclusive of all taxes and service charges.

1. **Adjective**: included (not gradable - used predicatively or attributively)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Contained as part of a whole; counted as part of a group or total
   - Being part of something larger or more comprehensive
   
   **Synonyms**: incorporated, encompassed, contained, comprised, covered
   
   **Antonyms**: excluded, omitted, left out, separate
   
   **When to Use**: When specifying what is part of a package, group, price, or set; all registers; business and commercial contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When something is explicitly excluded or requires additional payment; avoid when clarifying exceptions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - included in the price
   - everything included
   - batteries included
   - included as standard
   - services included
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + clud- (close, shut) + -ed (past participle adjective)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin includere (to enclose) → included (past participle form) used as adjective from 14th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Breakfast is included in the room rate, so you don't need to pay extra.
   - Is shipping included or do I need to add that separately?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All necessary equipment is included in the standard package.
   - The fees included in this estimate cover both materials and labor.

1. **Adjective**: comprehensive (comparative: more comprehensive, superlative: most comprehensive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Including or dealing with all or nearly all elements, aspects, or details; thorough and complete
   - *(Gradable)* Covering a wide range of subjects, items, or information
   
   **Synonyms**: complete, thorough, exhaustive, all-inclusive, extensive
   
   **Antonyms**: partial, incomplete, limited, narrow, selective
   
   **When to Use**: Academic and professional contexts; describing reports, studies, insurance, or examinations; emphasizing thoroughness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "complete" or "full" would be simpler; avoid overuse which may sound pretentious
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - comprehensive coverage
   - comprehensive study
   - comprehensive report
   - comprehensive approach
   - comprehensive list
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together, with) + prehens- (seize, grasp) + -ive (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin comprehendere (to seize together, include) → comprehensive (17th century) meaning "including much or all"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The guidebook gives you comprehensive information including maps, hotels, and restaurants.
   - I need comprehensive insurance that covers everything including theft and damage.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee conducted a comprehensive review including all relevant documentation.
   - This comprehensive analysis includes data from multiple sources and time periods.

1. **Adjective**: all-encompassing (not gradable or minimally gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Including everything or everyone; covering all aspects completely without exception
   - Complete and total in scope or extent
   
   **Synonyms**: comprehensive, all-inclusive, exhaustive, complete, total
   
   **Antonyms**: partial, limited, narrow, restricted, selective
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic writing; emphasizing completeness; describing theories, systems, or approaches that cover everything
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where simpler alternatives exist; may sound overly formal in everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - all-encompassing approach
   - all-encompassing view
   - all-encompassing system
   - all-encompassing theory
   - all-encompassing solution
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (entire, whole) + en- (make, cause to be) + compass (surround, contain) + -ing (adjective suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Compass from Latin com- (together) + passus (step) → encompass (encircle, contain) → all-encompassing (20th century compound)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The app is an all-encompassing solution including calendar, notes, and task management.
   - They want an all-encompassing vacation package including flights, hotels, and tours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The framework provides an all-encompassing methodology including both qualitative and quantitative approaches.
   - The legislation represents an all-encompassing reform including provisions for environmental protection and economic development.

   - broad perspective
   - broad scope

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   - She has broad interests including art, science, and sports.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study employed a broad methodology including both quantitative and qualitative techniques.
   - The policy framework encompasses a broad spectrum of issues including economic, social, and environmental concerns.

1. **Adjective**: all-inclusive (not gradable - compound adjective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Including everything, especially all costs or all people; with no additional charges or exclusions
   - Comprehensive and covering all necessary items or services
   
   **Synonyms**: comprehensive, inclusive, complete, total, full-service
   
   **Antonyms**: exclusive, selective, partial, à la carte
   
   **When to Use**: Travel and hospitality contexts; describing packages or deals; business contexts; emphasizing complete coverage
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When items are sold separately; contexts where selectivity is valued
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - all-inclusive resort
   - all-inclusive package
   - all-inclusive price
   - all-inclusive deal
   - all-inclusive vacation
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (entire, every) + inclusive (in- + clud- + -ive)
   
   **Etymology**: Modern compound from "all" + "inclusive" (20th century), popularized by tourism industry
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We booked an all-inclusive resort including meals, drinks, and activities.
   - The all-inclusive package covers everything including airport transfers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization offers all-inclusive membership plans including access to all facilities and programs.
   - The conference fee is all-inclusive, including accommodation, meals, and workshop materials.
