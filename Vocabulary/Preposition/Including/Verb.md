1. **Verb**: include
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To contain or comprise as part of a whole; to have something or someone as one of the parts or members
   - To make someone or something part of a group, set, or category
   
   **Synonyms**: contain, comprise, incorporate, encompass, embrace
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, leave out, remove, bar
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; describing what is part of something; specifying contents or members; professional, academic, and everyday contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When something is explicitly excluded; when referring to the whole rather than parts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - include someone in
   - include as part of
   - doesn't include
   - be included in
   - include everything
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + clud- (close, shut)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin includere (to shut in, enclose, contain) → Old French inclure → English include (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The price includes shipping and handling.
   - Don't forget to include me in the group chat!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The comprehensive package includes all necessary services and materials.
   - The study includes participants from diverse demographic backgrounds.

1. **Verb**: incorporate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To include something as part of a whole; to combine or unite with something already existing
   - To take in or contain as part of a larger entity
   
   **Synonyms**: integrate, include, absorb, assimilate, embody
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, separate, remove, isolate, extract
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts; describing integration of elements; business and academic writing; emphasizing active combination
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation where "include" or "add" are simpler; when passive containment rather than active integration is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - incorporate into
   - incorporate elements of
   - be incorporated in
   - incorporate feedback
   - incorporate changes
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (into) + corpor- (body) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin incorporare (to form into a body, unite) → Late Latin → English incorporate (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We should incorporate your ideas into the plan.
   - The recipe incorporates several international flavors.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The revised framework incorporates stakeholder feedback and recent empirical findings.
   - The curriculum incorporates experiential learning opportunities including internships and field research.

1. **Verb**: encompass
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To include comprehensively; to cover or deal with a range of subjects or areas
   - To contain or include entirely within scope
   
   **Synonyms**: include, comprise, cover, embrace, incorporate
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, narrow, restrict, limit
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic writing; emphasizing breadth or comprehensiveness; describing scope
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (sounds overly formal); when referring to simple containment
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - encompass a range of
   - encompass all aspects
   - broadly encompass
   - encompass the scope
   - encompass both
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (make, cause to be) + compass (circle, surround)
   
   **Etymology**: compass (from Latin com- "together" + passus "step") → encompass (surround, contain, 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The project encompasses everything from design to marketing.
   - Her interests encompass both arts and sciences.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research encompasses multiple disciplines including sociology, psychology, and anthropology.
   - The policy framework encompasses diverse considerations including environmental, economic, and social factors.

1. **Verb**: comprise
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To consist of; to be made up of specified parts or elements
   - To include or contain; to form the whole of something
   
   **Synonyms**: consist of, be composed of, contain, include, constitute
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, lack, be excluded from
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing; describing composition; academic and professional contexts; when listing what makes up a whole
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (prefer "is made up of" or "includes"); note: "is comprised of" is disputed usage—prefer "comprises" or "is composed of"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - comprise several parts
   - comprise a total of
   - the team comprises
   - comprise the majority
   - comprise various elements
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + prehens- (seize, grasp)
   
   **Etymology**: French compris (past participle of comprendre "to comprise") → English comprise (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The team comprises five people from different departments.
   - The package comprises everything you need to get started.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sample comprises 1,200 participants including both treatment and control groups.
   - The curriculum comprises core courses and elective options including specialized seminars.

1. **Verb**: contain
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To have or hold within; to have something as a part or as contents
   - To include or encompass within specified limits
   
   **Synonyms**: include, hold, comprise, incorporate, encompass
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, lack, omit, be without
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; describing physical or abstract contents; technical specifications; everyday and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to exclusion; when membership rather than containment is emphasized
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - contain information
   - contain elements of
   - doesn't contain
   - be contained in
   - contain both
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together, with) + ten- (hold)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin continere (to hold together, contain) → Old French contenir → English contain (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The box contains all the parts you need.
   - This product doesn't contain any artificial ingredients.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The dataset contains comprehensive information including demographic and behavioral variables.
   - The document contains provisions addressing multiple scenarios including breach and termination.

1. **Verb**: cover
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To deal with or include; to extend over or deal with a range of subjects or areas
   - To include in scope or treatment
   
   **Synonyms**: include, address, deal with, encompass, treat
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, skip, ignore, neglect
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; describing scope of discussion, research, insurance, or services; both formal and casual
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When physical covering is meant (context-dependent); when containment rather than treatment is emphasized
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cover all aspects
   - cover a range of
   - cover the basics
   - coverage includes
   - cover both
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin cooperire (to cover completely) via Old French
   
   **Etymology**: Latin cooperire → Old French covrir → English cover (13th century, abstract "include/deal with" sense developed later)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The class covers everything from basics to advanced topics.
   - Does your insurance cover dental work?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The literature review covers major theoretical frameworks including social constructionism and systems theory.
   - The policy covers various scenarios including both routine operations and emergency situations.

1. **Verb**: embrace
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To include or contain, especially willingly or enthusiastically; to accept or support a belief, theory, or change
   - To take in or encompass a range of items or ideas
   
   **Synonyms**: include, encompass, adopt, incorporate, accept
   
   **Antonyms**: reject, exclude, refuse, resist, deny
   
   **When to Use**: Formal writing; emphasizing willing inclusion or acceptance; intellectual or philosophical contexts; figurative use
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Technical contexts requiring precision; when referring to physical embrace; when neutrality is needed (embrace implies positive acceptance)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - embrace change
   - embrace diversity
   - embrace the concept
   - embrace new ideas
   - embrace a range of
   
   **Root Analysis**: em- (in) + brace (arms, from Latin bracchia)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French embracer (to clasp in the arms) → English embrace (14th century, figurative "include/accept" sense developed 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The company is trying to embrace diversity and new perspectives.
   - We should embrace new technologies including AI and automation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theoretical framework embraces multiple perspectives including positivist and interpretivist approaches.
   - Contemporary pedagogy embraces diverse methodologies including collaborative learning and technology integration.

1. **Verb**: feature
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To include as an important or prominent part; to have as a characteristic or component
   - To give special prominence to something included
   
   **Synonyms**: include, present, showcase, offer, display
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, lack, be without
   
   **When to Use**: Marketing and promotional contexts; describing contents or characteristics; emphasizing notable inclusions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When items are not prominent or noteworthy; very formal academic writing (prefer "include" or "present")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - feature prominently
   - feature in
   - features include
   - be featured in
   - feature both
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin factura (making, formation) via Old French faiture
   
   **Etymology**: Old French faiture → feature (distinctive characteristic, 14th century; verb use "to include prominently" from 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new phone features an amazing camera.
   - The concert features several famous artists including some Grammy winners.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The exhibition features works from multiple periods including Renaissance and Baroque masterpieces.
   - The conference features presentations including keynote addresses and panel discussions.

1. **Verb**: involve
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To include as a necessary part or result; to require as a necessary accompaniment
   - To include someone in an activity or decision
   
   **Synonyms**: include, entail, require, necessitate, encompass
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, omit, leave out, exempt
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; describing what is necessary or required; participation contexts; both formal and casual
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple containment rather than necessary connection is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - involve doing something
   - get involved in
   - be involved in
   - involve everyone
   - involve both
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in) + volv- (roll)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin involvere (to envelop, roll in) → involve (14th century, sense of "include" developed 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The job involves working with people from different countries.
   - Let's involve everyone in the decision, including the new team members.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology involves multiple data collection techniques including surveys and interviews.
   - Effective implementation involves various stakeholders including administrators, faculty, and students.

1. **Verb**: list
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To write or name items one by one; to include in a list or enumeration
   - To specify items that are part of something
   
   **Synonyms**: enumerate, itemize, catalog, specify, detail
   
   **Antonyms**: omit, exclude, overlook, skip
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; creating inventories or enumerations; formal documentation; specifications
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When items should not be explicitly enumerated; when summary rather than detail is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - list all items
   - be listed in
   - list separately
   - list includes
   - list both
   
   **Root Analysis**: Germanic lista (border, strip, list)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French liste (border, list) → English list (register, roll, 17th century verb use)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you list everything we need including snacks and drinks?
   - The menu lists several options including vegetarian choices.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The document lists all requirements including technical specifications and compliance standards.
   - Table 1 lists demographic characteristics including age, gender, and educational attainment.

1. **Verb**: offer
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To make available; to provide or present something that includes specified features or benefits
   - To include as part of what is provided
   
   **Synonyms**: provide, furnish, supply, present, give
   
   **Antonyms**: withhold, deny, refuse, take away
   
   **When to Use**: Commercial contexts, service descriptions, all registers, emphasizing what is available
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing mandatory inclusion rather than optional provision
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - offer services
   - offer a range of
   - be offered including
   - offer both
   - offer everything
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin offerre (to present, bestow)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin offerre (ob- "to" + ferre "bring") → Old French offrir → English offer (Old English, reinforced by French)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We offer various packages including all-inclusive options.
   - The gym offers lots of classes including yoga and Pilates.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The institution offers comprehensive programs including undergraduate, graduate, and professional degrees.
   - The service package offers multiple benefits including 24/7 support and regular system updates.

1. **Verb**: provide
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To make available; to supply or furnish something that includes particular items or services
   - To include as part of what is supplied
   
   **Synonyms**: supply, furnish, offer, give, include
   
   **Antonyms**: withhold, deny, refuse, take away, deprive
   
   **When to Use**: All registers; describing what is supplied or made available; formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to containment rather than active supply
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - provide access to
   - provide services
   - provide information
   - be provided including
   - provide both
   
   **Root Analysis**: pro- (before, for) + vid- (see)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin providere (to foresee, attend to) → provide (15th century, sense of "supply" dominant)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The hotel provides everything you need including towels and toiletries.
   - Can you provide more details including dates and costs?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The framework provides comprehensive guidance including implementation strategies and evaluation criteria.
   - The platform provides multiple functionalities including data visualization and predictive analytics.
