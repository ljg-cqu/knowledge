1. **Original Expression**: The package includes everything you need, including flights and accommodation.
   **Context**: Describing what is contained in a travel package or offer
   
   **Core Intent**: To specify that all necessary items or services are part of the package
   
   **Casual**: The package has everything you need, flights and accommodation included.
   *Characteristics*: Shorter sentence, dropped "including" for "included" at end, conversational tone
   *Use when*: Talking to friends, informal sales conversations, social media posts
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The package encompasses all necessary components, including flights and accommodation.
   *Characteristics*: More formal vocabulary ("encompasses"), maintains professional structure
   *Use when*: Business emails, customer service, standard marketing materials
   
   **Formal**: The package comprises all requisite elements, inclusive of flights and accommodation.
   *Characteristics*: Formal vocabulary ("comprises," "requisite," "inclusive of"), structured syntax
   *Use when*: Legal documents, formal contracts, official company communications
   
   **Humorous**: The package is all-you-can-travel, including the flying part and a place to crash!
   *Characteristics*: Playful language ("all-you-can-travel," "place to crash"), casual metaphor
   *Use when*: Friendly marketing, social media, casual promotional materials
   *Avoid when*: Professional client communications, serious business contexts
   
   **Lively**: The package has it all—yes, including your flights AND accommodation!
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic tone, em dash for emphasis, capitalized "AND"
   *Use when*: Sales pitches, promotional events, energetic presentations
   
   **Empathetic**: We understand you need everything taken care of, so the package includes both flights and accommodation.
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges customer needs ("we understand"), reassuring tone
   *Use when*: Customer service situations, addressing concerns, building trust
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status clients may expect formal variants; equals allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: First-time customers need semi-formal; returning customers accept casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-value purchases warrant formal; routine purchases allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect formality in business; others prefer friendliness
   - *Medium*: Written contracts require formal; verbal conversations allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "including" repeatedly in one sentence: "The package includes everything including flights including meals"—redundant
   - Using overly formal language in casual contexts: "Your weekend getaway comprises..."—sounds stiff
   - Being too casual in contracts: "Everything's in there, even flights"—lacks legal precision
   
   **Cultural Notes**: British English may prefer "inclusive of" in formal contexts; American English commonly uses "including" across all registers
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Writing a legal contract for corporate travel → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("comprises all requisite elements")
   - **Situation 2**: Chatting with a friend about vacation plans → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("has everything, flights included")
   - **Situation 3**: Sending a professional email to a new client → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("encompasses all necessary components")

1. **Original Expression**: The study covers various topics, including climate change and biodiversity.
   **Context**: Academic or research context describing scope of study
   
   **Core Intent**: To specify particular topics that are part of a larger set
   
   **Casual**: The study looks at different topics, like climate change and biodiversity.
   *Characteristics*: "looks at" instead of "covers," "like" instead of "including"
   *Use when*: Informal presentations, casual academic discussions, student conversations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The study addresses various topics, including climate change and biodiversity.
   *Characteristics*: Neutral verb ("addresses"), standard academic structure
   *Use when*: Academic emails, conference abstracts, general research presentations
   
   **Formal**: The study encompasses diverse subject matter, inclusive of climate change and biodiversity.
   *Characteristics*: Elevated vocabulary ("encompasses," "subject matter," "inclusive of")
   *Use when*: Journal articles, formal academic writing, grant proposals
   
   **Humorous**: The study digs into all sorts of stuff, including the big ones like climate change and biodiversity!
   *Characteristics*: Casual language ("digs into," "stuff," "big ones"), exclamation
   *Use when*: Informal academic talks, public engagement, science communication to general audiences
   *Avoid when*: Formal publications, grant applications, peer-reviewed contexts
   
   **Lively**: The study tackles so many fascinating topics—including climate change and biodiversity!
   *Characteristics*: Action verb ("tackles"), enthusiasm marker ("fascinating"), em dash
   *Use when*: Engaging presentations, public lectures, promotional materials for research
   
   **Empathetic**: Recognizing the urgency of environmental issues, the study addresses critical topics including climate change and biodiversity.
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges importance ("recognizing the urgency," "critical"), context-setting
   *Use when*: Advocacy-oriented research, policy briefs, stakeholder communications
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Peer audiences allow casual; senior academics expect formal
   - *Social Distance*: Conference presentations allow semi-formal; journal articles require formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Grant applications demand formal; internal seminars allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Different academic cultures have varying formality expectations
   - *Medium*: Published papers require formal; oral presentations allow more flexibility
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "like" in formal academic writing: "topics like climate change"—too casual for publications
   - Over-formalizing oral presentations: "encompasses diverse subject matter"—sounds stilted when spoken
   - Inconsistent register within one document: mixing "covers" and "encompasses"—maintain consistency
   
   **Cultural Notes**: British academic English tends toward more formal registers; American academic style allows slightly more casual language, especially in presentations
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Writing a journal article abstract → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("encompasses diverse subject matter")
   - **Situation 2**: Presenting to undergraduate students → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("looks at topics like...")
   - **Situation 3**: Conference presentation to mixed academic audience → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("addresses various topics")

1. **Original Expression**: The price is $500, including tax and shipping.
   **Context**: Commercial transaction describing total cost
   
   **Core Intent**: To clarify that the stated price covers all costs with no hidden fees
   
   **Casual**: It's $500 total, tax and shipping included.
   *Characteristics*: Contracted "it's," "total" for clarity, "included" at end
   *Use when*: Casual sales, online marketplace listings, text messages, informal quotes
   
   **Semi-Formal**: The price is $500, including all taxes and shipping fees.
   *Characteristics*: Clear structure, "all" for emphasis, "fees" for specificity
   *Use when*: Professional invoices, business emails, standard retail communications
   
   **Formal**: The total cost amounts to $500, inclusive of all applicable taxes and shipping charges.
   *Characteristics*: "amounts to" for formality, "inclusive of," legal precision ("applicable," "charges")
   *Use when*: Contracts, formal quotes, legal documents, corporate billing
   
   **Humorous**: You pay $500 and that's it—no sneaky extra charges, tax and shipping are in there!
   *Characteristics*: Reassuring tone ("that's it"), casual language ("sneaky"), conversational structure
   *Use when*: Consumer-friendly marketing, relationship-building sales, social media
   *Avoid when*: Legal documents, formal contracts, corporate communications
   
   **Lively**: Just $500—and yes, that includes EVERYTHING: tax, shipping, the works!
   *Characteristics*: "Just" minimizes cost, capitalization for emphasis, enthusiastic punctuation
   *Use when*: Sales presentations, promotional materials, special offers
   
   **Empathetic**: We know hidden fees are frustrating, so our price of $500 includes tax and shipping—no surprises.
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges customer pain point, reassuring language ("no surprises")
   *Use when*: Customer service, trust-building situations, responding to pricing concerns
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: B2B transactions with corporate clients require formal language
   - *Social Distance*: New customers need clear semi-formal; repeat customers accept casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Large purchases warrant formal documentation; small purchases allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some markets expect very explicit pricing breakdowns
   - *Medium*: Legal contracts demand formal; verbal quotes allow casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Ambiguity about what's included: "including some fees"—be specific
   - Too casual in legal contexts: "Everything's in there"—lacks precision for contracts
   - Over-formalizing simple transactions: "The cost amounts to..."—excessive for $20 purchases
   
   **Cultural Notes**: European pricing typically shows VAT-inclusive prices by law; U.S. often shows pre-tax prices, so "including tax" is particularly important to specify
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Drafting a formal business contract → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("inclusive of all applicable taxes and shipping charges")
   - **Situation 2**: Responding to customer inquiry via email → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("including all taxes and shipping fees")
