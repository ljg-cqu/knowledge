1. **Functional Expression**: According to [source], ... (function: citing/attributing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to cite a source or attribute information to someone or something
   - *Formality*: formal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (distances speaker from claim)
   
   **Synonyms**: As [source] states, As reported by [source], [Source] indicates that, Based on [source], [Source] suggests that
   
   **Antonyms**: I believe that, In my opinion, I claim that
   
   **When to Use**: When citing external sources, attributing information to others, presenting research findings, distancing yourself from a claim, formal writing or speaking
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When presenting your own original ideas, when you want to take ownership of a statement, in very casual conversation (sounds stilted), when the source is obvious or irrelevant
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - According to recent studies/research
   - According to experts/specialists
   - According to the report/article/data
   - According to X (person's name)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Creates epistemic distance—the speaker presents information without necessarily endorsing it; essential for academic integrity and avoiding plagiarism; shows respect for intellectual property
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That's interesting. What else did [source] say?"
   - Questioning: "What source is that from? Is it reliable?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to my mom, I should be wearing a jacket.
   - According to the weather forecast, it's going to rain tomorrow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to recent epidemiological studies, early intervention significantly improves outcomes.
   - According to the financial report, revenues exceeded projections by 15 percent.

1. **Functional Expression**: As per your request, ... (function: acknowledging/responding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that an action is being taken in response to someone's request or instruction
   - *Formality*: formal/very formal
   - *Directness*: direct acknowledgment
   
   **Synonyms**: As you requested, As requested, Following your request, In response to your request, Per your request
   
   **Antonyms**: Without being asked, On my own initiative, Unsolicited
   
   **When to Use**: Formal business correspondence, responding to official requests, showing compliance with instructions, acknowledging authority or hierarchy
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal), when you want to show initiative rather than compliance, in creative or collaborative contexts (may sound bureaucratic)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As per your instructions
   - As per your email
   - As per our discussion
   - As per the agreement
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Common in Indian English and formal business English; considered overly formal or outdated by some American English speakers; shows deference and compliance; reduces sense of initiative or creativity
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Thank you for following up on that."
   - Neutral: "Received, thanks."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As per your request, here are the files you wanted.
   - As per usual, he showed up late. (idiomatic use)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As per your request of March 15, please find attached the quarterly financial statements.
   - As per the terms stipulated in Section 3.2, payment is due within thirty days.

1. **Functional Expression**: Based on [evidence/information], ... (function: reasoning/concluding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to show that a conclusion or decision is founded on specific evidence or information
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: direct (shows logical reasoning)
   
   **Synonyms**: Given [evidence], Considering [information], In light of [facts], Drawing from [data], Judging by [evidence]
   
   **Antonyms**: Regardless of [evidence], Ignoring [information], Without considering
   
   **When to Use**: Presenting conclusions, explaining reasoning, showing evidence-based thinking, academic or analytical contexts, decision-making explanations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no evidence supports your claim, when reasoning is intuitive or emotional, when you want to emphasize opinion over facts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Based on the evidence/data/facts
   - Based on what I've seen/heard
   - Based on my experience
   - Based on current information
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals rational, evidence-based thinking; increases credibility; creates space to revise conclusions if new evidence emerges ("based on current information" implies openness to revision)
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: "That makes sense given the evidence."
   - Challenging: "But have you considered other factors?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Based on what you told me, I think you should go for it.
   - Based on past experience, this probably won't work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Based on the experimental results, we conclude that the hypothesis is supported.
   - Based on market analysis, we recommend expanding into the Asian market.

1. **Functional Expression**: In accordance with [rule/standard], ... (function: stating compliance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that something follows or complies with a specific rule, regulation, or standard
   - *Formality*: formal/very formal
   - *Directness*: direct compliance statement
   
   **Synonyms**: In compliance with, Following, Pursuant to, In conformity with, According to
   
   **Antonyms**: In violation of, Contrary to, Despite, In defiance of
   
   **When to Use**: Legal documents, regulatory contexts, formal policies, official communications, emphasizing compliance with authority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (too formal), creative or flexible contexts, when you want to emphasize initiative rather than compliance
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - In accordance with policy/regulations/law
   - In strict accordance with
   - In full accordance with
   - In accordance with the agreement
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Emphasizes formal compliance and adherence to authority; protects against liability by explicitly stating rule-following; common in legal and regulatory language; may sound bureaucratic if overused
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Good, that ensures we're compliant."
   - Questioning: "Are we sure that's the correct interpretation?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In accordance with what we agreed, I'm sending the report today.
   - Everything was done in accordance with your instructions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In accordance with Article 5.3 of the bylaws, the board shall convene quarterly meetings.
   - All procedures were conducted in strict accordance with institutional ethical guidelines.

1. **Functional Expression**: [Source] indicates/suggests/shows that ... (function: reporting findings)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to report what a source says or reveals, often in academic or professional contexts
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: direct reporting with epistemic hedging
   
   **Synonyms**: [Source] demonstrates, [Source] reveals, [Source] reports, [Source] finds, [Source] states
   
   **Antonyms**: [Source] contradicts, [Source] denies, I claim (without source)
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, research reports, presenting others' findings, maintaining objectivity, showing evidence-based reasoning
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Presenting your own original claims, very casual conversation, when you want to strongly endorse a claim (these verbs hedge commitment)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Research indicates that...
   - The data suggest that...
   - Studies show that...
   - Evidence demonstrates that...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Verb choice matters: "shows" is stronger than "suggests" which is stronger than "indicates"; allows hedging of commitment to claims; maintains academic objectivity; proper for evidence-based discourse
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That's a significant finding."
   - Questioning: "What's the confidence level on that?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The forecast shows it's going to rain.
   - Your test results indicate everything is normal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Recent meta-analyses indicate that the intervention produces moderate effect sizes.
   - The quarterly report demonstrates consistent growth across all market segments.

1. **Functional Expression**: Following [event/rule], ... (function: indicating sequence or compliance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that something comes after an event (temporal) or follows a rule/procedure (compliance)
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: direct (temporal or compliance)
   
   **Synonyms**: After [event], According to [rule], In accordance with [procedure], Subsequent to [event]
   
   **Antonyms**: Before, Prior to, Preceding, Contrary to, Ignoring
   
   **When to Use**: Describing sequences of events, indicating compliance with procedures, formal instructions, process descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "after" would be clearer for temporal meaning, when the sequence is obvious, in very casual contexts (may sound formal)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Following the meeting/discussion/incident
   - Following standard protocol/procedure
   - Following these guidelines/instructions
   - Following this approach
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Ambiguous between temporal and compliance meanings—context must clarify; more formal than "after" for temporal meaning; emphasizes adherence when used for procedures
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That makes sense as a next step."
   - Clarifying: "Do you mean after the meeting or according to the plan?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Following our conversation, I'll send you the information.
   - Following the instructions, I put it together in ten minutes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Following the board meeting, several policy changes were implemented.
   - All experiments were conducted following established ethical protocols.

1. **Functional Expression**: In line with [goal/policy/expectation], ... (function: showing alignment)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to show that something aligns with or matches expectations, policies, or goals
   - *Formality*: neutral/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct alignment statement
   
   **Synonyms**: Consistent with, In accordance with, Aligned with, Matching, In keeping with
   
   **Antonyms**: Contrary to, At odds with, Inconsistent with, Deviating from
   
   **When to Use**: Showing strategic alignment, demonstrating consistency with goals, explaining rationale for decisions, organizational communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When independence from established norms is valued, in highly formal legal contexts (use "in accordance with"), very casual conversation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - In line with our goals/objectives
   - In line with expectations/predictions
   - In line with current trends/thinking
   - In line with policy/strategy
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Common in business and organizational contexts; less legalistic than "in accordance with"; emphasizes harmony and strategic fit; popular in corporate communication
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Good to see consistency in our approach."
   - Questioning: "How does this align with our other priorities?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In line with what we discussed, I've updated the plan.
   - The changes are in line with what customers have been asking for.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In line with strategic objectives, the organization is prioritizing digital transformation.
   - These findings are in line with previous research in the field.

1. **Functional Expression**: [Source] claims/argues/contends that ... (function: reporting opinions/arguments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to report someone's argument or claim, often with implicit uncertainty or distance
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: direct reporting with epistemic distance
   
   **Synonyms**: [Source] asserts, [Source] maintains, [Source] posits, [Source] proposes, [Source] believes
   
   **Antonyms**: [Source] proves, [Source] demonstrates (stronger commitment), [Source] denies
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, reporting contested claims, maintaining objectivity, presenting arguments you may not endorse, critical analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Presenting established facts (use "shows" or "demonstrates"), when you want to endorse a claim strongly, very casual conversation
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Critics argue that...
   - The author claims that...
   - Opponents contend that...
   - Proponents maintain that...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: These verbs signal that a claim is being made but not necessarily proven; creates epistemic distance; useful for presenting multiple perspectives; "claims" can imply skepticism, "argues" is more neutral, "contends" suggests debate
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: "That's a valid argument."
   - Disagreeing: "I'm not convinced by that claim."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He claims he didn't do it, but I'm not sure I believe him.
   - Some people argue that social media is harmful.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Smith (2019) argues that traditional models fail to account for cultural variation.
   - Critics contend that the methodology introduces systematic bias.

1. **Functional Expression**: Consistent with [finding/theory/principle], ... (function: showing compatibility)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to show that something aligns with or doesn't contradict established findings, theories, or principles
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct compatibility statement
   
   **Synonyms**: In line with, Compatible with, In agreement with, Aligned with, Conforming to
   
   **Antonyms**: Inconsistent with, Contrary to, At odds with, Contradicting
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, scientific reporting, showing that new findings fit with existing knowledge, demonstrating logical coherence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Claiming strong causal relationships (use "based on" instead), very casual contexts, when independence from prior work is emphasized
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Consistent with previous research/findings
   - Consistent with theoretical predictions
   - Consistent with expectations
   - Fully consistent with / Largely consistent with
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Weaker than "based on"—shows compatibility without claiming causation; essential in academic discourse to position new findings within existing literature; allows hedging when fit is imperfect
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That strengthens the overall evidence."
   - Questioning: "Are there alternative explanations?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What you're saying is consistent with what I heard from others.
   - Her behavior is consistent with someone under stress.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These results are consistent with the hypothesis that cognitive load affects recall.
   - The observed patterns are consistent with theoretical predictions of the model.

1. **Functional Expression**: As [source] points out/notes/observes, ... (function: highlighting observation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to draw attention to someone's observation or insight, often to emphasize an important point
   - *Formality*: neutral/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct reporting with emphasis
   
   **Synonyms**: As [source] mentions, As [source] highlights, As [source] emphasizes, [Source] correctly notes
   
   **Antonyms**: Ignoring [source]'s point, Contrary to what [source] says
   
   **When to Use**: Academic writing, emphasizing important insights, showing engagement with sources, building on others' observations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Presenting claims that need strong evidence (use "demonstrates" instead), when you disagree with the source, very formal legal writing
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - As several scholars have pointed out
   - As the report notes
   - As critics have observed
   - As [name] correctly points out
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Often signals agreement or endorsement of the observation; creates dialogue with sources; "correctly" adds explicit endorsement; useful for building arguments on others' insights
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: "That's an important observation."
   - Neutral: "I see, what are the implications?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As you pointed out earlier, we need to be more careful.
   - As my teacher always notes, practice makes perfect.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As Johnson (2020) points out, previous models failed to account for temporal dynamics.
   - As the committee notes in its report, funding constraints pose significant challenges.

1. **Functional Expression**: Per [rule/standard/agreement], ... (function: citing authority)

   - Used to cite a rule, standard, or agreement as authority for an action or requirement
   - *Formality*: formal/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct citation of authority
   
   **Synonyms**: According to, As per, Following, Under, In accordance with

   - Per our discussion/conversation
   - Per company policy
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Very common in business email; Latin origin gives formal feel; some consider it jargon; perfectly acceptable for rates (per capita, per unit); creates clear reference to authority
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Understood, I'll follow that guideline."
   - Clarifying: "Which section specifically?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Per your email, I've updated the schedule.
   - The speed limit is 60 miles per hour.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Per Section 3.4 of the operating manual, all equipment must be inspected monthly.
   - Compensation is calculated at $50 per hour, per the terms of the contract.

1. **Functional Expression**: [Source] reveals/demonstrates/establishes that ... (function: reporting discoveries)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to report that a source shows or proves something, often with emphasis on new information
   - *Formality*: neutral/formal
   - *Directness*: direct reporting with strong epistemic commitment
   
   **Synonyms**: [Source] shows, [Source] proves, [Source] illustrates, [Source] evidences
   
   **Antonyms**: [Source] obscures, [Source] fails to show, [Source] questions
   
   **When to Use**: Reporting strong findings, emphasizing discovery, scientific communication, when evidence is compelling
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Reporting weak or preliminary findings (use "suggests"), presenting your own claims, overstating evidence strength
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Research reveals that...
   - Analysis demonstrates that...
   - The study establishes that...
   - Evidence reveals...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Stronger than "indicates" or "suggests"; "reveals" emphasizes disclosure of hidden information; "demonstrates" emphasizes proof; "establishes" emphasizes definitive determination; choose based on evidence strength
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That's a significant discovery."
   - Questioning: "How robust is that evidence?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The investigation revealed who was responsible.
   - Your example demonstrates exactly what I was trying to explain.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The meta-analysis demonstrates significant treatment effects across all studies.
   - Archaeological evidence reveals extensive trade networks in the ancient period.
