1. **Original Expression**: According to recent research, exercise improves cognitive function.
   **Context**: Academic citation of source material
   
   **Core Intent**: To cite research findings and attribute information to a source
   
   **Casual**: Research says exercise is good for your brain.
   *Characteristics*: Simple verb "says," conversational vocabulary, informal attribution
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, informal discussions, general conversation
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Studies show that exercise improves cognitive function.
   *Characteristics*: Neutral verb "show," clear and professional, general attribution
   *Use when*: Business presentations, workplace discussions, general professional contexts
   
   **Formal**: According to recent research, exercise improves cognitive function.
   *Characteristics*: Academic phrasing "according to," precise attribution, scholarly tone
   *Use when*: Academic papers, formal presentations, research reports, scholarly articles
   
   **Humorous**: Word on the street is that exercise actually makes you smarter—who knew?
   *Characteristics*: Colloquial expression "word on the street," playful tone with rhetorical question
   *Use when*: Informal presentations, friendly discussions, breaking tension
   *Avoid when*: Academic contexts, serious health discussions, professional reports
   
   **Lively**: Exciting new research reveals that exercise boosts brainpower!
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic adjectives "exciting," "boosts," exclamation point for energy
   *Use when*: Popular science writing, motivational contexts, engaging presentations
   
   **Empathetic**: Recent research confirms what many have experienced—exercise truly helps our minds work better.
   *Characteristics*: Validating language "confirms what many have experienced," inclusive "our minds"
   *Use when*: Health counseling, patient education, supportive communication
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status audiences require formal; peers allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: Academic journals require formal; blog posts allow semi-formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Scientific claims require formal attribution; general health tips allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Anglo-American academic culture values explicit citation
   - *Medium*: Written academic work requires formal; spoken conversation allows casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "they say" without specifying who "they" are—too vague for formal contexts
   - Overusing "according to" in casual conversation—sounds stilted and overly formal
   - Omitting attribution entirely when citing research—leads to plagiarism or unclear claims
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Anglo-American academic culture highly values explicit source attribution and considers failure to cite as serious academic misconduct. Some cultures have different norms around knowledge ownership.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Academic journal article → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("According to recent research")—requires scholarly precision
   - **Situation 2**: Conversation with friend about health → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("Research says")—natural and accessible
   - **Situation 3**: Corporate wellness presentation → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("Studies show")—professional but accessible
