1. **Expression**: in the following days/weeks/months/years (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - During the time period that comes after the present or reference point
   - *Fixedness*: medium (can vary time unit: days, weeks, months, years)
   
   **Synonyms**: in the coming days/weeks, over the next days/weeks, in the days/weeks ahead, in subsequent days/weeks
   
   **Antonyms**: in the previous days/weeks, in the preceding days/weeks, in recent days/weeks
   
   **When to Use**: Use in both formal and informal contexts when referring to near-future time periods; common in planning, predictions, announcements; creates clear temporal frame
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be vague about exact timing; when precision required, specify exact dates; "following" may be ambiguous (immediately following or generally subsequent?)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in the days/weeks following [event]
   - over the following days/weeks
   - throughout the following days/weeks
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Following" naturally combines with time period nouns to create forward-looking temporal frames; the semantic compatibility between "following" (temporal succession) and time units creates natural partnership
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (preposition) + the (article) + following (adjective) + time period noun
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We'll finalize everything in the next few weeks.
   - Let me know in the coming days what you decide.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Implementation will occur in the following months according to the established timeline.
   - In the following weeks, the committee will review all submitted proposals.

1. **Expression**: following in someone's footsteps (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Doing the same thing as someone else, especially someone successful or respected; pursuing the same career or path
   - *Literal meaning*: Walking behind someone in their footprints
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Emulating someone's career path or life choices
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiom; variations uncommon)
   
   **Synonyms**: emulating, following someone's lead, following someone's example, walking in someone's footsteps, taking after someone
   
   **Antonyms**: forging one's own path, breaking the mold, going one's own way, diverging from
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing career choices, life paths, or behaviors that emulate another person; common in biographical, professional, family contexts; often implies respect or admiration
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply lack of originality if tone is negative; don't use when person is deliberately choosing different path; may carry pressure/expectation connotations in some contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - following in the footsteps of [specific person]
   - walking in someone's footsteps
   - following closely in someone's footsteps
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Medieval origin when travelers literally followed footprints in snow/mud for safe passage; metaphorically extended to mean following someone's example or life path; the physical tracking metaphor naturally extends to abstract guidance-following
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow (verb) + in (preposition) + footsteps (compound noun: foot + steps)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's following in her dad's footsteps and becoming a doctor too.
   - I guess I'm following in my sister's footsteps by moving to New York.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The young researcher is following in the footsteps of her distinguished mentor.
   - Following in his predecessor's footsteps, the new director emphasized innovation and collaboration.

1. **Expression**: the following day/morning/evening/week (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The day/time period immediately after the one mentioned or implied
   - *Fixedness*: medium (can substitute different time units)
   
   **Synonyms**: the next day/morning, the day/morning after, the subsequent day/morning
   
   **Antonyms**: the previous day/morning, the preceding day/morning, the day/morning before
   
   **When to Use**: Use in narrative, reports, and formal writing to indicate the time period immediately after; creates clear temporal sequence; more formal than "next day" but less formal than "subsequent day"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In casual conversation, "next day" often sounds more natural; can be ambiguous if reference point unclear; ensure readers know which day/time is being "followed"
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - early/late the following morning
   - on the following day
   - by the following week
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Following" naturally pairs with time period nouns to indicate immediate succession; conventional in narrative and formal temporal sequencing; frequent co-occurrence in written English
   
   **Root Analysis**: the (article) + following (adjective) + time period noun
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We partied all night, and the next morning I felt terrible.
   - He called me the day after to apologize.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The meeting was held on Tuesday, with decisions implemented the following week.
   - Participants were contacted the following day to complete post-intervention assessments.

1. **Expression**: following suit (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Doing the same thing as someone else has just done; conforming to another's example
   - *Literal meaning*: In card games, playing a card of the same suit as the one led
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Imitating another's action or decision
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed idiom from card-playing)
   
   **Synonyms**: doing the same, following someone's lead, following along, imitating, conforming
   
   **Antonyms**: breaking with tradition, going against the grain, doing the opposite, diverging
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing mimicking behavior or decisions; common in business, social contexts; can be neutral or slightly negative (implying lack of independence); often describes multiple people/entities making same choice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can imply lack of independent thought; avoid when praising original thinking; may suggest herd mentality in some contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - followed suit
   - following suit with [specific action]
   - quickly/immediately following suit
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originates from card games where players must "follow suit" by playing a card of the same suit as the first card played; extended metaphorically to any situation where someone copies another's action; first recorded use: early 1800s
   
   **Root Analysis**: following (gerund of follow) + suit (from French "suite", sequence/series; in cards, set of same symbol)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When Sarah quit, three other people on the team did the same thing.
   - He started working from home, and soon everyone else was too.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - After the market leader announced price increases, competitors quickly followed suit.
   - Several European nations imposed sanctions, with additional countries following suit within days.

1. **Expression**: in the wake of (type: collocation/phrasal preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Following as a consequence or result of; coming after and affected by
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed phrasal preposition; rarely varied)
   
   **Synonyms**: following, after, as a result of, in the aftermath of, subsequent to
   
   **Antonyms**: before, prior to, leading up to, in advance of
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing events or actions that follow as consequences of significant events; common in journalism, formal writing; often used for negative or disruptive events; implies causal connection
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too dramatic for minor or routine events; implies significant impact; avoid for neutral or positive events where "after" or "following" more appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in the immediate wake of
   - in the wake of recent [event]
   - directly in the wake of
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Nautical metaphor from the track left by a ship in water; anything in the wake is following and affected by the ship's passage; extended to mean consequences following significant events; emphasizes both temporal succession and causal impact
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (preposition) + the (article) + wake (noun: track left in water) + of (preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After the scandal, the CEO resigned.
   - Because of the pandemic, everyone started working remotely.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the wake of the financial crisis, regulatory frameworks were substantially revised.
   - In the wake of these findings, the organization implemented comprehensive policy reforms.

1. **Expression**: as follows (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the following manner; in this way (used to introduce a list or explanation)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (completely fixed expression; no variation)
   
   **Synonyms**: in the following manner, as shown below, listed below, the following, as such
   
   **Antonyms**: as mentioned above, as previously stated, as described earlier
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing immediately before lists, procedures, or detailed explanations; creates clear signpost that details will follow; standard in academic, legal, business writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual writing; sounds stilted in conversation; avoid in creative or narrative writing; don't use if no list or details actually follow
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - The [items] are as follows
   - [Action] is as follows
   - The procedure is as follows
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Grammatically unusual construction (singular verb "is" with plural "follows"); evolved as fixed formal phrase in legal and academic English; the apparent grammatical irregularity signals its status as formulaic expression; high frequency in procedural and instructional discourse
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (conjunction/preposition) + follows (verb, third person singular of "follow")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Here's what you need to do: [list]
   - This is how it works: [explanation]
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee's recommendations are as follows: first, immediate implementation...; second, quarterly reviews...
   - The experimental procedure was as follows: samples were prepared using...

1. **Expression**: following through (on something) (type: phrasal verb/collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completing an action or commitment; carrying something through to completion
   - *Fixedness*: medium (phrasal verb with consistent meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: completing, finishing, carrying through, seeing through, honoring commitments
   
   **Antonyms**: abandoning, giving up on, failing to complete, dropping
   
   **When to Use**: Use when discussing completing commitments, promises, or plans; common in professional, personal development contexts; implies responsibility and reliability; can be verb or noun form ("follow-through")
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundancy ("following through to completion"); context often makes meaning clear without extra emphasis
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - follow through with [plan/commitment]
   - lack of follow-through (noun form)
   - failed to follow through
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Extended from sports (golf, tennis) where "follow-through" means continuing motion after striking; metaphorically applied to completing any action fully; the physical completion metaphor naturally extends to abstract task completion
   
   **Root Analysis**: follow (verb) + through (particle indicating completion)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He always says he'll help but never actually does it.
   - Make sure you finish what you started this time!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization demonstrated strong follow-through on strategic initiatives.
   - Managers must follow through on commitments to maintain team trust.

1. **Expression**: the following applies (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The information, rules, or conditions stated below are relevant or applicable
   - *Fixedness*: medium (can vary subject: "the following applies/pertains/refers")
   
   **Synonyms**: this applies, the below is relevant, this pertains to, this concerns
   
   **Antonyms**: the following does not apply, this is not relevant, this excludes
   
   **When to Use**: Use in policies, instructions, documentation to clarify scope and relevance; essential for legal, procedural, administrative writing; ensures readers understand applicability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual contexts; can sound bureaucratic; in conversational settings, simpler language ("this is for...") works better
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - the following applies to [specific group]
   - all of the following applies
   - the following specifically applies
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Institutional discourse requires clear scope definition; "following" + "applies" combination creates standard formula for introducing applicable rules/conditions; high frequency in policy and procedural documents creates strong conventional partnership
   
   **Root Analysis**: the (article) + following (adjective/noun) + applies (verb: to be relevant/applicable)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is for everyone who signed up before Friday.
   - Here's what you need to know if you're a new member.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The following policy applies to all full-time employees hired after January 1, 2024.
   - The following requirements apply exclusively to doctoral candidates.

1. **Expression**: in the following manner/way (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the way that will be described or shown; using the method about to be explained
   - *Fixedness*: medium (can substitute manner/way/fashion)
   
   **Synonyms**: in this way, as follows, like this, in this manner, as shown below
   
   **Antonyms**: in a different manner, alternatively, differently, contrary to this
   
   **When to Use**: Use in instructional, procedural, or formal descriptive writing to introduce methods or processes; common in academic, technical, scientific writing; creates clear transition to explanation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual instructions; can be wordy ("do it like this" is simpler); avoid if manner is obvious from context
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - proceed in the following manner
   - arranged in the following manner
   - calculated in the following manner
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Following" + manner/way creates formal structure for introducing procedures; semantic compatibility between "following" (what comes next) and "manner" (method) creates natural partnership; conventional in instructional and scientific discourse
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (preposition) + the (article) + following (adjective) + manner/way (noun)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Here's how you do it: [instructions]
   - Do it like this: [demonstration]
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sample was prepared in the following manner: first, the substrate was cleaned...
   - Participants were recruited in the following manner: advertisements were placed in...
