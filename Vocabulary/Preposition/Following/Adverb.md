1. **Adverb**: subsequently (type: time, sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a later time; afterwards; following in time or order
   - Used to indicate that something happened after something else in chronological sequence
   - *Position*: Typically sentence-initial or mid-sentence; can also appear at end
   
   **Synonyms**: afterwards, later, thereafter, then, next
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, earlier, formerly, beforehand, prior
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to show temporal sequence; common in academic, legal, professional contexts; emphasizes chronological order; adds formality to narrative
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; use "later" or "after that" in everyday speech; can sound stilted in informal contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - subsequently discovered/found/revealed
   - subsequently became/developed
   - was subsequently arrested/charged
   - subsequently published/released
   - and subsequently
   
   **Root Analysis**: subsequent (following) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "subsequens" (following after) + English adverbial suffix "-ly"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We met in college and got married a few years later.
   - After that, things just got weird.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data was collected in June and subsequently analyzed over the following three months.
   - The defendant was subsequently charged with multiple counts of fraud.

1. **Adverb**: afterwards (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a later or following time; after an event or time mentioned
   - Following in temporal sequence; in the time that follows
   - *Position*: Typically sentence-initial or sentence-final; occasionally mid-sentence
   
   **Synonyms**: later, subsequently, thereafter, then, after
   
   **Antonyms**: beforehand, previously, earlier, before, prior
   
   **When to Use**: Use in both formal and informal contexts to indicate later time; versatile across registers; common in narrative and descriptive writing; natural in conversation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: American English often prefers "afterward" (no 's'); avoid redundancy with other time markers
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - shortly afterwards
   - immediately afterwards
   - soon afterwards
   - long afterwards
   - years afterwards
   
   **Root Analysis**: after + -ward(s) (directional suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "æfter" (after, later) + "-weard" (directional suffix indicating movement or direction)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's grab dinner first and catch a movie afterwards.
   - We talked for hours, and afterwards I felt so much better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants completed the survey immediately afterwards to assess retention.
   - The conference concluded at noon, and workshops were held afterwards.

1. **Adverb**: thereafter (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - After that time; from that time onward; following that event
   - Used to indicate continuing from a point in time onwards
   - *Position*: Sentence-initial or mid-sentence; formal tone marker
   
   **Synonyms**: afterwards, subsequently, from then on, after that, thenceforth
   
   **Antonyms**: theretofore, previously, before that, earlier
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal, legal, or academic writing to indicate time from a point forward; common in contracts, legal documents; emphasizes continuation; sounds archaic in casual speech
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (use "after that" or "from then on"); too formal for everyday use; can sound pretentious in informal contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - shortly thereafter
   - immediately thereafter
   - and thereafter
   - for years thereafter
   - from that point thereafter
   
   **Root Analysis**: there + after (compound adverb)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English combining "there" (at that point/place) + "after" (following) → developed specialized temporal meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After we graduated, we kind of lost touch.
   - From that point on, everything changed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy was enacted in 2015 and remained in effect thereafter.
   - The company was founded in 1990 and expanded rapidly thereafter.

1. **Adverb**: later (type: time, comparative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a time in the future or after the time being considered
   - At a subsequent point in time; afterwards
   - *Position*: Sentence-initial, mid-sentence, or sentence-final; highly flexible
   
   **Synonyms**: afterwards, subsequently, eventually, in time, eventually
   
   **Antonyms**: earlier, sooner, before, previously, prior
   
   **When to Use**: Use in all contexts (formal and informal) to indicate future or subsequent time; most common and versatile time adverb; works across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be vague about timing; when precision needed, specify exact time; don't confuse with "latter" (second of two)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - see you later
   - later that day/week/year
   - sooner or later
   - later on
   - not until later
   
   **Root Analysis**: late + -er (comparative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "læt" (late) + comparative suffix "-er"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll call you later when I'm done with work.
   - Later that night, we all went out for drinks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings are discussed in greater detail later in this paper.
   - Participants were contacted later to complete a follow-up survey.

1. **Adverb**: then (type: time, sequence, sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At that time; at the time mentioned
   - *(Sense 2)* After that; next in order or sequence
   - *(Sense 3)* In that case; therefore (logical consequence)
   - *Position*: Highly flexible—sentence-initial, mid-sentence, or sentence-final
   
   **Synonyms**: at that time, next, afterwards, subsequently, in that case
   
   **Antonyms**: now, currently, at present, before
   
   **When to Use**: Use across all registers to indicate time, sequence, or logical consequence; extremely versatile; one of most common temporal/sequential adverbs; essential for narrative
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous between temporal and logical meanings; context must clarify which meaning intended
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - and then
   - just then
   - even then
   - back then
   - now and then
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "þanne/þænne" (at that time)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "þanne" (at that time, then) → related to "that" and "the" (demonstrative forms)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had lunch and then went shopping.
   - If you're not coming, then I'll go alone.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data was collected, and then a statistical analysis was performed.
   - If the hypothesis is correct, then the results should demonstrate correlation.

1. **Adverb**: next (type: time, sequence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - On the first or soonest occasion after the present; immediately following
   - In the position or place immediately following
   - *Position*: Sentence-initial, mid-sentence, or sentence-final; versatile placement
   
   **Synonyms**: afterwards, then, subsequently, following that, after that
   
   **Antonyms**: previously, before, earlier, last, prior
   
   **When to Use**: Use in all contexts to indicate immediate succession in time or order; extremely common; works across all registers; essential sequencing word
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can be ambiguous (next Tuesday might mean upcoming Tuesday or Tuesday after); when precision required, be more specific
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - what happened next
   - next time
   - do this next
   - next up
   - who's next
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "niehst" (nearest, next)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "niehst" (superlative of "neah", near) → developed temporal meaning from spatial proximity
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What should we do next?
   - Next, add the flour and mix well.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Next, we examine the implications of these findings.
   - The sample was next subjected to centrifugation at 3000 rpm.

1. **Adverb**: consecutively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that follows continuously without interruption; in unbroken sequence
   - One after another in order, with no gaps
   - *Position*: Typically mid-sentence or sentence-final; modifies verb
   
   **Synonyms**: successively, sequentially, continuously, in succession, in a row
   
   **Antonyms**: intermittently, sporadically, discontinuously, randomly
   
   **When to Use**: Use when emphasizing unbroken sequence or continuity; common in formal contexts, sports, technical writing; highlights the continuous nature
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; in everyday conversation, use "in a row" or "straight"; avoid when sequence has gaps
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - numbered consecutively
   - run consecutively (legal: sentences)
   - occur consecutively
   - follow consecutively
   - three times consecutively
   
   **Root Analysis**: consecutive (unbroken sequence) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "consecutivus" (following in order) + English adverbial suffix "-ly"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She won the championship three years in a row.
   - Number these pages starting from one.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Pages should be numbered consecutively throughout the document.
   - The defendant was sentenced to serve three terms consecutively.

1. **Adverb**: successively (type: manner, time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that follows in order without interruption; one after another in sequence
   - Repeatedly in succession; in consecutive instances
   - *Position*: Mid-sentence or sentence-final; modifies verb or clause
   
   **Synonyms**: consecutively, sequentially, in succession, one after another, repeatedly
   
   **Antonyms**: simultaneously, concurrently, at once, randomly, sporadically
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to emphasize sequential progression; common in academic, technical contexts; highlights ordered repetition or gradual progression
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation; avoid when events are simultaneous or random; don't confuse with "successfully"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - successively higher/lower
   - successively refined
   - applied successively
   - occur successively
   - improve successively
   
   **Root Analysis**: successive (following in order) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin "successivus" (following in order) + English suffix "-ly"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She tried again and again, each time getting better.
   - The prices went up year after year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The model was successively refined through iterative testing procedures.
   - Each layer was successively applied and allowed to dry completely.

1. **Adverb**: accordingly (type: sentence, manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that is appropriate to the particular circumstances
   - *(Sense 2)* As a result; consequently; therefore (used to introduce logical conclusion)
   - *Position*: Typically sentence-initial (with comma) or mid-sentence; signals consequence
   
   **Synonyms**: consequently, therefore, thus, hence, appropriately
   
   **Antonyms**: inappropriately, regardless, nevertheless, nonetheless
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to show logical consequence or appropriate response; common in academic, business, professional contexts; indicates reasoned conclusion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual speech; in conversation use "so" or "that's why"; can sound bureaucratic if overused
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - and accordingly
   - accordingly adjusted
   - plan accordingly
   - act accordingly
   - respond accordingly
   
   **Root Analysis**: according + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "acorder" (to agree, be in harmony) → "according" + English suffix "-ly"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The weather looks bad, so we should probably stay home.
   - He was rude to everyone, so nobody wanted to work with him.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data suggests a significant correlation; accordingly, we recommend further investigation.
   - Resource allocation was adjusted accordingly to reflect changing priorities.

1. **Adverb**: thence (type: time, place, archaic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - From that place; from there (spatial)
   - *(Sense 2)* From that time; from then onwards (temporal)
   - *(Sense 3)* For that reason; therefore (logical)
   - *Position*: Sentence-initial or mid-sentence; archaic and literary
   
   **Synonyms**: from there, from that point, therefore, hence, consequently
   
   **Antonyms**: hither, here, to that place
   
   **When to Use**: Use only in legal documents, very formal academic writing, or literary/archaic contexts; indicates source point (spatial/temporal); now mostly obsolete except in legal language
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in modern general writing; sounds archaic and pretentious; use "from there" or "therefore" instead; only appropriate in specific legal or literary contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - from thence
   - thence to
   - and thence
   - thence onwards
   - proceeded thence
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "þanon" (from that place)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "þanon/þonan" (from that place, thence) → related to "then" and "the"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We drove to the lake and then headed to the mountains from there.
   - After that, things got better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The property extends to the river and thence northward to the county boundary.
   - The theory was established in ancient Greece and thence transmitted to medieval scholars.
