1. **Functional Expression**: Following [noun/gerund], [subject] [verb]... (function: sequencing/transitioning)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that an action or event occurs after another specified action or event
   - *Formality*: formal/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: After [noun/gerund], Subsequent to [noun], Upon completion of [noun], Once [subject] [verb]
   
   **Antonyms**: Before [noun/gerund], Prior to [noun], Until [subject] [verb]
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal and semi-formal writing to create clear temporal connections between events; common in business communication, academic writing, procedural documentation; indicates clear cause-and-effect or sequence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual conversation (use "after" instead); can sound bureaucratic if overused; avoid in creative or narrative writing where varied language preferred
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Following this/that [more emphatic]
   - Immediately following [adds urgency]
   - Following upon [very formal, archaic]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This construction follows formal negative politeness strategies by maintaining professional distance; indicates logical, ordered thinking; common in institutional and organizational discourse
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - When will that occur? → Following the completion of phase one
   - What's the timeline? → Following approval, implementation begins immediately
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After we finish dinner, we can watch a movie.
   - Once you're done with that, come find me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Following the board's approval, the project will proceed to the implementation phase.
   - Following completion of the assessment, participants were debriefed.

1. **Functional Expression**: In the following [time period/section/manner] (function: introducing/referencing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce or reference what comes next in sequence or what will be presented
   - *Formality*: formal/semi-formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: In the next [noun], In the upcoming [noun], In the subsequent [noun], Below, As follows
   
   **Antonyms**: In the previous [noun], In the preceding [noun], Above, As mentioned earlier
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing to introduce upcoming content, data, or timeframes; essential in academic writing, reports, presentations; creates clear organizational structure; guides reader through document
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual writing; in conversation, use "next" or "coming up"; can be redundant if context is clear
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - The following shows/demonstrates [introduces evidence]
   - Consider the following [directs attention]
   - Note the following [emphasizes importance]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This phrase serves as discourse organizer, helping readers/listeners anticipate what's coming; maintains formal register while being accessible; shows organizational competence
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What should I focus on? → Please review the following points
   - When does this happen? → In the following weeks, we'll see results
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check out these next few points.
   - Over the next couple weeks, we'll finish up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the following section, we examine the methodological framework employed.
   - The following table presents the demographic characteristics of participants.

1. **Functional Expression**: What follows is... (function: introducing/presenting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce and present upcoming information, explanation, or content
   - *Formality*: semi-formal/formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: The following is..., Next is..., Coming up is..., Below is..., Presented here is...
   
   **Antonyms**: What preceded was..., Previously discussed was..., Earlier was...
   
   **When to Use**: Use when introducing complex information, transitioning between topics, or presenting structured content; common in presentations, reports, academic writing; creates anticipation and focus
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formulaic for narrative writing; avoid overuse (sounds repetitive); in casual contexts, omit entirely or use "here's" instead
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What follows below is [emphasizes location]
   - What follows next is [emphasizes sequence]
   - All that follows is [emphasizes completeness]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This construction creates a pause that signals importance; helps audience prepare to receive information; shows organized thinking; useful for managing information flow
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Can you explain? → What follows is a detailed breakdown
   - What's the plan? → What follows is our proposed approach
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Here's what we're thinking...
   - So next up is the fun part!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - What follows is a comprehensive analysis of the experimental results.
   - What follows below is a detailed description of the methodology employed.

1. **Functional Expression**: As follows (function: introducing list/explanation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used immediately before presenting a list, explanation, or detailed information
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: In the following manner, As shown below, Listed below, As such
   
   **Antonyms**: As mentioned above, As previously stated, As described earlier
   
   **When to Use**: Use in formal writing immediately before lists, procedures, or detailed explanations; creates clear expectation that details follow; standard in business, legal, academic contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual communication; avoid in narrative or creative writing; can sound stilted in spoken language
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - The steps are as follows [introduces procedure]
   - The requirements are as follows [introduces criteria]
   - The findings are as follows [introduces results]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This phrase functions as formal signpost; indicates completeness and authority; suggests definitive, organized presentation; conveys professionalism and precision
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What are the requirements? → The requirements are as follows: [list]
   - How do we proceed? → The process is as follows: [steps]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Here's what you need: [list]
   - This is how it works: [explanation]
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research questions are as follows: first, to examine...; second, to determine...
   - The committee's recommendations are as follows: immediate implementation of...

1. **Functional Expression**: Following on from [topic/discussion/point] (function: continuing/extending)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to continue a discussion or build upon a previous point; indicates logical connection
   - *Formality*: semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (collaborative)
   
   **Synonyms**: Building on [topic], Continuing from [point], Expanding on [discussion], Further to [topic]
   
   **Antonyms**: Moving away from [topic], Aside from [topic], Setting aside [topic]
   
   **When to Use**: Use in discussions, meetings, or correspondence to show continuity with previous contributions; common in British English; demonstrates active listening and collaborative thinking
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less common in American English (use "building on" instead); avoid when introducing entirely new topic; can seem forced in written communication
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Following on from what [name] said [attributes to speaker]
   - Following on from earlier [references previous discussion]
   - Following on from that point [shows logical connection]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression employs positive politeness by acknowledging others' contributions; builds conversational cohesion; shows respect for previous speakers; particularly valued in collaborative cultures
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - [Someone shares idea] → Following on from that, I'd add...
   - [Topic discussed] → Following on from this discussion, we should consider...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Like you said earlier, we should also...
   - Going off what you mentioned, maybe we could...
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Following on from the previous presentation, our analysis suggests...
   - Following on from Dr. Smith's remarks, I would like to examine...

1. **Functional Expression**: In the wake of [event/development] (function: explaining consequences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to describe events or actions that occur as a consequence or result of something
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (causal relationship)
   
   **Synonyms**: Following [event], After [event], As a result of [event], In the aftermath of [event]
   
   **Antonyms**: Before [event], Prior to [event], Leading up to [event]
   
   **When to Use**: Use when describing consequences or responses to significant events; common in journalism, formal analysis, historical writing; implies causal connection; often used for negative events
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual contexts; can sound overdramatic for minor events; implies significant impact (don't use for routine occurrences)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - In the immediate wake of [emphasizes recency]
   - In the wake of recent [connects to current events]
   - Directly in the wake of [emphasizes causation]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression carries connotations of disruption or significant change; implies the initial event was impactful; often used in crisis communication or change management contexts
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - How did you respond? → In the wake of the incident, we implemented...
   - What changed? → In the wake of these findings, we adjusted...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - After what happened, we had to change everything.
   - Because of that incident, we now do things differently.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In the wake of the financial crisis, regulatory frameworks were substantially revised.
   - In the wake of these findings, the organization implemented comprehensive reforms.

1. **Functional Expression**: To follow up on [topic/conversation/request] (function: continuing/checking progress)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to return to a previous discussion, request, or action item to check status or provide additional information
   - *Formality*: semi-formal/formal
   - *Directness*: direct but polite
   
   **Synonyms**: To follow through on, To circle back to, To revisit, To check in on, Regarding [topic]
   
   **Antonyms**: To initiate, To introduce, To begin discussing, To raise for the first time
   
   **When to Use**: Use in professional communication to check progress, provide updates, or continue previous discussions; common in emails, meetings, project management; shows accountability and attention to detail
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound bureaucratic if overused; in very casual contexts, simpler language ("checking on") works better; avoid if no prior discussion exists
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm following up on [identifies specific item]
   - Just following up to see [adds checking purpose]
   - As a follow-up to [references earlier communication]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression demonstrates professionalism and responsibility; employs negative politeness by maintaining appropriate distance; shows project management competence; culturally valued in task-oriented business cultures
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Did you receive my email? → I'm following up on my previous message
   - What's the status? → Following up on our discussion, has there been progress?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Hey, just checking—did you get a chance to look at that?
   - Wanted to see if you had any updates on what we talked about.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I am writing to follow up on our previous correspondence regarding the proposal.
   - Following up on last week's meeting, I wanted to provide an update on implementation progress.

1. **Functional Expression**: Following [possessive] lead/example (function: imitating/agreeing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate doing the same as someone else; adopting someone else's approach or decision
   - *Formality*: semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (collegial)
   
   **Synonyms**: Taking [possessive] lead, Following suit, Like [person], In line with [person], Similarly to [person]
   
   **Antonyms**: Diverging from [person], Taking a different approach, Contrary to [person]
   
   **When to Use**: Use when aligning with someone else's decision or action; shows respect for their judgment; common in collaborative settings, consensus-building; demonstrates team cohesion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can diminish your own agency if overused; avoid when you need to demonstrate independent thinking; may seem deferential in contexts requiring assertiveness
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Following your lead on this [defers to expertise]
   - Following their example [emulates behavior]
   - Following in their footsteps [suggests longer-term pattern]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression employs positive politeness by showing respect and agreement; builds rapport; useful in hierarchical situations; can be strategic face-saving when agreeing with authority
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What do you think? → I'm following Sarah's lead on this one
   - Which approach? → Following your example, I'll use the same method
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm just going to do what she does.
   - Let's do it the way they did.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Following the committee chair's lead, I move to approve the proposal.
   - Following the precedent established by our colleagues, we recommend similar implementation.

1. **Functional Expression**: The following applies/applies to... (function: clarifying scope/relevance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to specify what information, rules, or conditions are relevant to particular situations or people
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: This applies to, The below pertains to, This concerns, This is relevant to
   
   **Antonyms**: This does not apply to, This excludes, This is not relevant to
   
   **When to Use**: Use in policy documents, instructions, legal writing, or any context requiring clarity about scope and applicability; ensures understanding of who/what is affected
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual instructions; can sound bureaucratic; in conversational contexts, use simpler language ("this is for people who...")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - The following applies only to [restricts scope]
   - All of the following applies to [emphasizes completeness]
   - The following specifically applies to [emphasizes particularity]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This construction prioritizes clarity and precision; reduces ambiguity about scope; demonstrates attention to detail; valued in legal and regulatory contexts where precision is critical
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Does this affect me? → The following applies to all employees in...
   - Who needs to do this? → The following applies to anyone who...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is for everyone who registered by Friday.
   - Here's what you need to know if you're in the pilot program.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The following policy applies to all full-time employees hired after January 1, 2024.
   - The following guidelines apply exclusively to research involving human participants.

1. **Functional Expression**: Please see/find the following (function: directing attention)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used in written communication to direct recipient's attention to information presented below or attached
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: direct but polite (imperative softened by "please")
   
   **Synonyms**: Below you will find, Attached is, Here is, Presented below is, I am providing
   
   **Antonyms**: Above was presented, Previously sent, As mentioned earlier, Already provided
   
   **When to Use**: Use in business correspondence, emails, formal reports to guide reader to specific information; creates clear organizational signpost; common in professional communication
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual emails to colleagues; can sound stiff with friends; unnecessary if context makes location obvious
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Please find the following attached [for attachments]
   - Please see the following details [emphasizes specificity]
   - Please note the following [emphasizes importance]
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Softened imperative ("please") employs negative politeness to minimize imposition; shows professional courtesy; standard in business email conventions; demonstrates organizational skills
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Can you send details? → Please see the following breakdown
   - Where's the information? → Please find the following attached
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Here's the info you wanted.
   - Check out the details below.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please see the following agenda items for tomorrow's board meeting.
   - Please find the following documents attached for your review.
