1. **Original Expression**: "After this, we need to discuss the next steps"  
   **Context**: Indicating sequence in planning or discussion contexts
   
   **Core Intent**: To signal transition to discussion of subsequent actions or plans
   
   **Casual**: After this, let's talk about what happens next  
   *Characteristics*: Contracted forms, simple vocabulary, direct phrasing  
   *Use when*: Friends, close colleagues, informal meetings, casual planning
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Following this, we should discuss the next steps  
   *Characteristics*: Clear structure, professional vocabulary, balanced tone  
   *Use when*: General workplace, team meetings, standard business communication
   
   **Formal**: Subsequent to this discussion, we shall examine the forthcoming phases  
   *Characteristics*: Sophisticated vocabulary ("subsequent," "examine," "forthcoming"), modal verb "shall"  
   *Use when*: Board meetings, official presentations, formal proposals, executive communication
   
   **Humorous**: Once we're done here, we can tackle what comes next—hopefully with coffee!  
   *Characteristics*: Conversational tone with light self-deprecating humor  
   *Use when*: Building rapport, team bonding, creative brainstorming sessions  
   *Avoid when*: Serious topics, crisis management, formal presentations
   
   **Lively**: Alright! Next up, let's dive into what happens after this!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic language ("dive into"), enthusiasm markers ("Alright!"), exclamation  
   *Use when*: Motivating teams, dynamic presentations, energizing discussions
   
   **Empathetic**: I know this is a lot to take in. After we finish here, we can walk through the next steps together  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of difficulty, supportive language ("together"), gentle pacing  
   *Use when*: Explaining complex processes, supporting stressed team members, change management
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status → formal; peers → semi-formal; subordinates → semi-formal/casual
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/new colleagues → semi-formal; established relationships → casual acceptable
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Strategic planning → formal/semi-formal; routine coordination → casual/semi-formal
   - *Cultural Context*: Corporate culture varies; hierarchical organizations prefer formal
   - *Medium*: Written reports → formal; emails → semi-formal; verbal → more flexibility
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "after this" repeatedly sounds choppy; vary with "next," "following this," "then"
   - Overusing "shall" in semi-formal contexts sounds pretentious
   - Mixing registers: "Subsequent to this, let's talk about..." combines formal/casual awkwardly
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Anglo-American business culture increasingly accepts semi-formal in most contexts; Asian and European corporate cultures may expect more formal register in hierarchical settings
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Team standup meeting with peers → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Semi-formal ("After this, let's talk about...")
   - **Situation 2**: Presenting to C-suite executives → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Subsequent to this analysis...")
   - **Situation 3**: Training new employees through complex process → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("After we finish this section, we'll walk through...")

1. **Original Expression**: "What happens next?"  
   **Context**: Inquiring about subsequent events or actions in sequence
   
   **Core Intent**: To request information about what will occur after the current point
   
   **Casual**: So what's next? / What do we do now?  
   *Characteristics*: Simple, direct question; contracted forms; conversational  
   *Use when*: Friends, casual work settings, informal planning, relaxed environments
   
   **Semi-Formal**: What are the next steps? / What follows from here?  
   *Characteristics*: Complete sentences, neutral vocabulary, professional but accessible  
   *Use when*: Standard workplace, general business meetings, professional emails
   
   **Formal**: What are the subsequent phases of this initiative? / What actions follow this stage?  
   *Characteristics*: Sophisticated vocabulary ("subsequent," "initiative," "phases"), complete formal structure  
   *Use when*: Formal presentations, written reports, executive briefings, official documentation
   
   **Humorous**: So what's on the menu after this adventure?  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor ("menu," "adventure"), light tone  
   *Use when*: Lightening mood, building team rapport, creative sessions  
   *Avoid when*: Crisis situations, serious strategic planning, formal contexts
   
   **Lively**: Okay, so what's coming up next? I'm ready to jump in!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiasm markers, action-oriented language ("jump in"), forward momentum  
   *Use when*: Motivating situations, brainstorming, energizing team discussions
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand this might feel overwhelming. Can we go over what comes next at a pace that works for you?  
   *Characteristics*: Validation of feelings, collaborative language ("can we"), accommodation of pace  
   *Use when*: Training situations, supporting anxious team members, explaining complex changes
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Asking superior → formal question form; asking peer → casual acceptable
   - *Social Distance*: New relationship → semi-formal; established rapport → casual fine
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Critical decisions → formal/semi-formal; routine matters → any register
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct cultures (US, Netherlands) accept casual; indirect cultures prefer formal
   - *Medium*: Face-to-face → more casual acceptable; written → more formal expected
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - "What's subsequent?" sounds unnatural; "subsequent" works better as adjective
   - Using overly formal register when seeking clarification can create distance
   - Casual register with unfamiliar authority figures appears disrespectful
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In Anglo-American contexts, asking clarifying questions is valued; in some Asian cultures, repeated questions may suggest the speaker wasn't clear initially
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Confused during peer-led training → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("So what's next?")
   - **Situation 2**: Following up on executive decision → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("What are the subsequent implementation phases?")
   - **Situation 3**: Supporting team member through change → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("Can we go through what comes next together?")

   *Characteristics*: Simple temporal markers, conversational structure  
   *Use when*: Informal planning, casual updates, friendly correspondence
   
   **Semi-Formal**: In the coming weeks/months / During the upcoming period  
   *Characteristics*: Professional temporal references, clear structure  
   *Use when*: Business updates, project timelines, professional emails, team communication

   *Characteristics*: Sophisticated vocabulary ("ensuing," "subsequent"), formal structure

   **Lively**: In the exciting weeks ahead! / As we move forward over the next few months!  
   *Characteristics*: Positive framing ("exciting"), forward-looking enthusiasm, exclamations  
   *Use when*: Motivational contexts, launching initiatives, building momentum

   **Context Examples**:

   - **Situation 2**: Annual report future outlook section → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Throughout the ensuing fiscal year")

   **Context**: Making decisions or plans contingent on future events
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate dependency on subsequent events or information
   
   **Casual**: Depending on what happens next / It depends on what's next  
   *Characteristics*: Simple conditional language, conversational structure  
   *Use when*: Informal planning, casual discussions, friendly advice
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Contingent on subsequent developments / Based on upcoming outcomes  
   *Characteristics*: Professional conditional language, clear logical structure  
   *Use when*: Business planning, project management, professional decision-making
   
   **Formal**: Predicated upon ensuing circumstances / Conditional upon subsequent developments  
   *Characteristics*: Sophisticated vocabulary ("predicated," "ensuing"), formal logical structure  
   *Use when*: Legal documents, formal proposals, executive decision frameworks, academic writing
   
   **Humorous**: We'll cross that bridge when we get to it (assuming the bridge is still there!)  
   *Characteristics*: Common idiom with playful parenthetical qualifier, self-aware humor  
   *Use when*: Acknowledging uncertainty with levity, building rapport in planning  
   *Avoid when*: High-stakes decisions, risk-averse stakeholders, formal planning documents
   
   **Lively**: Let's see where this takes us and adapt as we go!  
   *Characteristics*: Adventurous framing, action-oriented, enthusiasm for flexibility  
   *Use when*: Agile environments, creative projects, motivating adaptable thinking
   
   **Empathetic**: I know the uncertainty is difficult. We'll make decisions together as things unfold  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment of discomfort, collaborative framing, reassurance

   - *Power Distance*: Proposing to leadership → formal; discussing with peers → semi-formal/casual
   - *Social Distance*: Stakeholders → formal/semi-formal; team members → casual acceptable
   - *Gravity of Situation*: High-stakes decisions → formal; routine adaptations → any register
   - *Cultural Context*: Risk-averse cultures prefer formal; uncertainty-tolerant cultures more casual
   - *Medium*: Contracts/formal agreements → formal; planning discussions → semi-formal

   - Being too casual about serious dependencies makes you seem unprepared
   - Overusing formal language for routine contingencies sounds inflexible
   - Not clearly specifying what "next" refers to creates confusion
