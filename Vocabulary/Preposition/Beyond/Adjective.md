**Note**: "Beyond" is primarily used as a preposition and is not a standard adjective in English. However, it appears in compound adjectives and adjective-like prepositional phrases. Below are relevant adjective patterns related to "beyond":

1. **Adjective**: beyond-normal (compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Exceeding or surpassing what is considered typical, standard, or ordinary; outside the range of normal expectations or parameters
   
   **Synonyms**: extraordinary, exceptional, abnormal, unusual, atypical
   
   **Antonyms**: normal, ordinary, typical, standard, average
   
   **When to Use**: Used in technical, medical, or analytical contexts to describe phenomena, measurements, or behaviors that exceed established norms; often hyphenated when used attributively
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation where simpler adjectives like "unusual" or "extraordinary" would be clearer; may sound overly technical in informal settings
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beyond-normal temperatures
   - beyond-normal stress levels
   - beyond-normal performance
   - beyond-normal circumstances
   - beyond-normal capabilities
   
   **Root Analysis**: beyond (preposition) + normal (Latin normalis "according to a rule")
   
   **Etymology**: Modern compound formation combining preposition "beyond" with adjective "normal" → primarily used in technical and scientific English from 20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The heat this summer has been beyond-normal—way hotter than usual.
   - His beyond-normal strength makes him perfect for the team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients exhibited beyond-normal cardiac activity during the trial.
   - The equipment operates under beyond-normal pressure conditions.

1. **Adjective**: beyond-human (compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Exceeding human capabilities, understanding, or limitations; superhuman or transcendent
   
   **Synonyms**: superhuman, transcendent, extraordinary, supernatural, god-like
   
   **Antonyms**: human, ordinary, mortal, limited
   
   **When to Use**: Used to describe abilities, intelligence, or qualities that surpass human limitations; common in science fiction, philosophy, and discussions of AI or enhanced capabilities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing achievable human excellence; inappropriate when discussing normal human variation; can sound hyperbolic in everyday contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - beyond-human abilities
   - beyond-human intelligence
   - beyond-human comprehension
   - beyond-human strength
   - beyond-human perception
   
   **Root Analysis**: beyond (preposition) + human (Latin humanus "of man")
   
   **Etymology**: Modern compound combining "beyond" with "human" → popularized in science fiction and philosophical discourse from mid-20th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That AI has beyond-human chess skills—no one can beat it.
   - Some people think meditation can unlock beyond-human consciousness.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research explores beyond-human cognitive enhancement through neural interfaces.
   - Artificial intelligence systems demonstrate beyond-human pattern recognition capabilities.

1. **Adjective**: far-beyond (compound, intensified)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Significantly or substantially exceeding a particular standard, limit, or expectation; far surpassing
   
   **Synonyms**: far exceeding, well beyond, vastly surpassing, significantly exceeding
   
   **Antonyms**: well within, far below, significantly under
   
   **When to Use**: Used to emphasize the degree to which something exceeds limits; adds intensity to the basic "beyond" concept; often appears in comparative or evaluative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundancy—don't use with other intensifiers; inappropriate when precise measurements are needed; can sound informal or colloquial
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - far-beyond expectations
   - far-beyond the average
   - far-beyond previous records
   - far-beyond normal limits
   - far-beyond initial projections
   
   **Root Analysis**: far (Old English feorr "distant") + beyond (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic compound combining intensifier "far" with "beyond" → develops naturally in English as emphatic construction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her cooking skills are far-beyond anything I could do.
   - That price is far-beyond my budget!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project's costs proved far-beyond initial estimates.
   - Results demonstrated efficacy far-beyond the control group's performance.
