1. **Verb**: go beyond
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To exceed, surpass, or extend further than a particular limit, standard, or expectation
   - *(Sense 2)* To explore or consider additional factors outside a specific scope or boundary
   
   **Synonyms**: surpass, exceed, transcend, outstrip, outdo
   
   **Antonyms**: stay within, limit oneself to, restrict to, confine to
   
   **When to Use**: Used when describing actions that exceed expectations, limitations, or boundaries; common in discussions of achievement, exploration, or analysis that extends past initial parameters
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when simple "exceed" would be clearer; don't use redundantly with other words that already express surpassing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go beyond expectations
   - go beyond the call of duty
   - go beyond the surface
   - go beyond the basics
   - go beyond what is required
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (Old English gān, "to move") + beyond (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb combining "go" (movement verb) with preposition "beyond" → developed as natural English construction expressing movement past limits
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She really went beyond what I expected—the project was amazing!
   - Let's go beyond just talking about it and actually do something.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research goes beyond previous studies by incorporating longitudinal data.
   - Organizations must go beyond compliance to achieve true sustainability.

1. **Verb**: extend beyond
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To reach or stretch further than a particular point, boundary, or limit in space, time, or scope
   
   **Synonyms**: reach beyond, stretch beyond, continue past, span beyond
   
   **Antonyms**: stop at, terminate at, end at, remain within
   
   **When to Use**: Used when describing physical extension, temporal duration, or conceptual reach that surpasses a defined boundary; common in academic, technical, and descriptive contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when "reach" alone would suffice; don't use when describing containment rather than extension
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - extend beyond the borders
   - extend beyond the original scope
   - extend beyond one's expertise
   - extend beyond the deadline
   - influence extends beyond
   
   **Root Analysis**: extend (Latin ex- "out" + tendere "to stretch") + beyond (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Combines Latin-derived "extend" with Germanic "beyond" → common in formal English from 17th century onwards
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The wifi signal extends beyond our backyard now.
   - His influence extends way beyond this town.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The implications of this policy extend beyond the immediate economic sector.
   - Cultural practices may extend beyond their original geographical boundaries.

1. **Verb**: look beyond
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To consider factors, possibilities, or perspectives outside immediate or obvious boundaries; to think past surface-level observations
   
   **Synonyms**: see past, consider beyond, think past, look past
   
   **Antonyms**: focus solely on, limit consideration to, fixate on
   
   **When to Use**: Used when encouraging broader perspective, deeper analysis, or consideration of additional factors; common in advice, strategic thinking, and analytical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing literal vision; inappropriate when narrow focus is actually needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - look beyond appearances
   - look beyond the obvious
   - look beyond the numbers
   - look beyond immediate concerns
   - look beyond the surface
   
   **Root Analysis**: look (Old English lōcian) + beyond (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Phrasal verb combining native English "look" with "beyond" → used metaphorically from Middle English onwards
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need to look beyond his attitude and see the good work he does.
   - Try looking beyond the price tag—quality matters too.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Investors must look beyond short-term fluctuations to assess long-term value.
   - Educators should look beyond test scores to evaluate student development.

1. **Verb**: reach beyond
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To extend one's influence, abilities, or physical presence further than a particular limit; to achieve impact outside expected boundaries
   
   **Synonyms**: extend beyond, stretch beyond, expand past, transcend
   
   **Antonyms**: confine to, restrict to, limit to
   
   **When to Use**: Used when describing achievement, influence, or physical extension that surpasses normal range; common in discussions of achievement, impact, and physical capability
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid redundancy when "reach" alone is sufficient; don't use when describing failure to attain
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reach beyond one's grasp
   - reach beyond limitations
   - reach beyond borders
   - reach beyond expectations
   - reach beyond the target audience
   
   **Root Analysis**: reach (Old English rǣcan "to stretch out") + beyond (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Germanic verb "reach" combined with "beyond" → emphasizes extension past normal reach
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His music reaches beyond his local fanbase now.
   - Can you reach beyond that shelf to grab the box?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The campaign's message reached beyond its intended demographic.
   - Scientific discoveries often reach beyond their original applications.

1. **Verb**: move beyond
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To progress past a particular point, stage, or limitation; to transcend or leave behind a previous state or condition
   
   **Synonyms**: progress past, advance beyond, transcend, leave behind, get past
   
   **Antonyms**: remain at, stay stuck in, dwell on, fixate on
   
   **When to Use**: Used when describing personal growth, intellectual development, or progression past obstacles; common in psychological, developmental, and strategic contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when physical movement is meant without metaphorical meaning; don't use dismissively of important issues that deserve attention
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move beyond the past
   - move beyond initial reactions
   - move beyond blame
   - move beyond basic understanding
   - move beyond comfort zones
   
   **Root Analysis**: move (Latin movēre "to move") + beyond (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin-derived "move" with Germanic "beyond" → developed metaphorical sense of emotional/intellectual progression
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's time to move beyond that argument and forgive each other.
   - We need to move beyond just complaining and find solutions.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations must move beyond traditional hierarchical structures.
   - The field has moved beyond early theoretical frameworks to embrace complexity.
