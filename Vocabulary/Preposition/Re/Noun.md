1. **Noun**: reference (C, pl: references)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A mention of or allusion to something; a way of directing the reader to another document, person, or source
   
   **Synonyms**: mention, citation, pointer, allusion, cross-reference
   
   **Antonyms**: omission, silence, disregard
   
   **When to Use**: When talking about documents, numbers, or sources that other texts or emails refer to, for example "your reference number" or "in reference to your email".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you simply mean "information" (use "details" or "information"), or when the focus is on respect (use "regard" or "respect").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in reference to your email
   - reference number
   - make reference to
   - with reference to the above
   - cross-reference
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + fer (carry) + -ence (state/result)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *referre* (to carry back, refer) → French *référence* → English "reference" (mentioning or directing attention to something).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you have the reference number for that order?
   - I saw a reference to your work in that article.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - In reference to your email dated 5 May, we enclose our response.
   - Please quote this reference on all future correspondence re your account.

1. **Noun**: regard (U/C, pl: regards in set phrases)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (U) Attention or consideration given to something; (in phrases) respect or concern about something
   
   **Synonyms**: consideration, respect, concern, attention
   
   **Antonyms**: disregard, neglect, indifference
   
   **When to Use**: Often in fixed prepositional phrases like "with regard to" or "in this regard" when introducing or connecting topics in formal English.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: As a direct synonym of "about" in casual speech (learners may overuse "regard" awkwardly).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with regard to
   - in this regard
   - in regard to
   - pay regard to
   - send my regards
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + gard (look, heed)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *regarder* (to look at) → English "regard" (consideration, respect).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Say hi to your family—send them my regards.
   - I don't have much regard for that idea.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regard to your recent enquiry, please see the information below.
   - In this regard, the findings are particularly significant.

1. **Noun**: respect (U/C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (U) Consideration or concern for something; (in phrases) a particular aspect or detail of something
   
   **Synonyms**: regard, consideration, aspect, facet
   
   **Antonyms**: disrespect, disregard, contempt
   
   **When to Use**: Frequent in prepositional phrases like "with respect to" and "in respect of" to introduce aspects or topics in formal writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: To mean politeness or admiration only (that is another sense); here the focus is on "aspect" or "connection".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with respect to
   - in respect of
   - in this respect
   - in no respect
   - in many respects
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + spect (look) → "look back at" → consideration
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *respectus* (looking back, consideration) → Old French → English "respect".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In many respects it's a good plan.
   - With respect to timing, we need to decide soon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With respect to your complaint re delivery delays, we enclose a detailed response.
   - The policy differs in several key respects from previous guidance.

1. **Noun**: matter (C, pl: matters)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A subject or situation that you have to think about, deal with, or discuss
   
   **Synonyms**: issue, subject, affair, case, topic
   
   **Antonyms**: non-issue, triviality (in some contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: Very common in formal letters and legal/business writing with `re`: "the above matter", "this matter", "a matter re your account".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want a more concrete word like "problem", "question", or "task" in casual conversation.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in the matter of
   - this matter
   - the above matter
   - serious matter
   - resolve the matter
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *materia* (substance, subject)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *materia* → Old French *matiere* → English "matter".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's not a big matter—I'll handle it.
   - There's a small matter re your payment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We are writing re the above matter and request your reply within 14 days.
   - The court heard submissions in the matter of Smith v Jones.

1. **Noun**: issue (C, pl: issues)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An important subject or problem that people are discussing or arguing about
   
   **Synonyms**: problem, concern, matter, question, topic
   
   **Antonyms**: solution, answer, non-issue
   
   **When to Use**: To talk about problems or topics that emails, memos, or policies are written re (about), especially in professional and public contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For very small, everyday things where "problem" or "thing" sounds more natural in casual speech.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - key issue
   - main issue
   - raise an issue
   - address an issue
   - issues re compliance / safety / quality
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *exire* (go out) → "outcome, point that comes out"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *exitus* (outcome) → Old French → English "issue".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There are a few issues re the new schedule.
   - What's the main issue here?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This report summarises key issues re data protection and access control.
   - Several outstanding issues re the contract remain unresolved.

1. **Noun**: correspondence (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Letters, emails, or messages exchanged between people or organisations
   
   **Synonyms**: communication, letters, email exchange, mail
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, lack of communication
   
   **When to Use**: In formal writing when referring collectively to letters and emails re (about) a particular matter.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For a single email or message ("email" may be clearer); in very casual speech.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - correspondence re this matter
   - previous correspondence
   - keep a copy of all correspondence
   - extensive correspondence re
   
   **Root Analysis**: co- (together) + respond (answer) + -ence (state)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin-based French *correspondance* → English "correspondence".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's been a lot of email correspondence re that booking.
   - I kept all our correspondence re the repairs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please quote the reference number in all future correspondence re your account.
   - Copies of correspondence re this matter are attached for your information.

1. **Noun**: subject line (C, pl: subject lines)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The line in an email that briefly states what the message is about
   
   **Synonyms**: subject, email title, heading (informal)
   
   **Antonyms**: body text, content (in contrast to the subject line)
   
   **When to Use**: When teaching or discussing how to write clear business emails, especially ones that use `Re:` plus a concise topic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For letters without an explicit "Subject:" line; for document headings in general (use "title" or "heading").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - clear subject line
   - subject line re [topic]
   - change the subject line
   - misleading subject line
   
   **Root Analysis**: subject (topic) + line (one line of text)
   
   **Etymology**: Modern email terminology; "subject" + "line".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Your subject line re the party made me curious.
   - Can you change the subject line so it's clearer what the email is re?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Use a specific subject line re each project to keep email threads organised.
   - The email's subject line re compliance updates should include the relevant policy code.
