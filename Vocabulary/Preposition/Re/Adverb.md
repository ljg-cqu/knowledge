1. **Adverb**: specifically (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to narrow the focus to a particular detail or aspect of a broader topic
   
   **Synonyms**: particularly, exactly, precisely, explicitly, in particular
   
   **Antonyms**: generally, broadly, vaguely, loosely
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to make clear that a question, comment, or clause is re (about) a very specific part of something, not the whole.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you are giving only a general overview; when you do not care which exact part is in focus.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - specifically about
   - specifically re
   - ask specifically about
   - more specifically
   - designed specifically for
   
   **Root Analysis**: specific (particular) + -ally (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *species* (kind) → "specific" → "specifically" meaning "in a particular way".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm asking specifically about your plans this weekend.
   - I'm worried specifically re the extra costs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This section deals specifically re compliance obligations.
   - Participants were asked specifically about previous experience.

1. **Adverb**: particularly (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a greater extent than usual; especially
   
   **Synonyms**: especially, notably, in particular, singularly
   
   **Antonyms**: barely, hardly, slightly, generally
   
   **When to Use**: To emphasise one aspect re (about) a topic as more important or noticeable than others.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need objective measurement instead of emphasis; when all aspects are equally important.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - particularly about
   - particularly re
   - particularly concerned about
   - particularly relevant to
   - not particularly interested
   
   **Root Analysis**: particular (specific) + -ly (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *particularis* (of a part) → "particularly".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not particularly worried about that email.
   - I'm particularly interested re your remote-work policy.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We are particularly concerned re data protection.
   - These findings are particularly relevant in relation to small firms.

1. **Adverb**: directly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a clear, straight, and honest way, without going through something else
   
   **Synonyms**: straightforwardly, plainly, immediately, explicitly
   
   **Antonyms**: indirectly, vaguely, obliquely
   
   **When to Use**: When describing statements, rules, or effects that are clearly and immediately re (about) a specific topic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When diplomacy, softening, or indirectness is needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - talk directly about
   - speak directly about
   - directly related to
   - directly re
   - address directly
   
   **Root Analysis**: direct (straight) + -ly (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *directus* (straight) → English "directly".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's talk directly about the problem.
   - This message is directly re your complaint.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report speaks directly about risks re supply chain disruption.
   - The regulation applies directly to matters re cross-border data transfers.

1. **Adverb**: explicitly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a very clear and direct way, leaving no doubt about what is meant
   
   **Synonyms**: clearly, plainly, specifically, unambiguously
   
   **Antonyms**: implicitly, vaguely, indirectly, ambiguously
   
   **When to Use**: When stating that a document, contract, or policy openly mentions something re (about) a topic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: If you want to keep something implied or soft; when understatement is preferred.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - explicitly about
   - explicitly re
   - explicitly state
   - explicitly mention
   - explicitly refer to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + plic (fold) + -itly (in a manner) → "unfolded clearly"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *explicitus* (unfolded) → English "explicit" → "explicitly".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He explicitly said the email was about schedules.
   - The message explicitly mentions you re the role.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract explicitly refers re liability for delays.
   - The guidance explicitly states what is and is not covered.

   **Antonyms**: narrowly, precisely, specifically
   
   **When to Use**: When giving a high-level view re (about) a topic without going into fine detail.

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - broadly about

   - broadly consistent with
   - broadly speaking
   
   **Root Analysis**: broad (wide) + -ly (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English *brād* (broad) → English "broadly".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The book is broadly about life in big cities.
   - The talk was broadly re career choices.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results are broadly consistent with earlier work re market volatility.
   - The strategy is broadly about long-term resilience rather than short-term gains.

   **Common Phrases**:

   **Examples [Casual]**:

1. **Adverb**: formally (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that follows official or traditional rules and uses serious language
   
   **Synonyms**: officially, ceremonially, properly, according to protocol
   
   **Antonyms**: informally, casually, colloquially
   
   **When to Use**: When describing communication or actions re (about) a topic that follow formal procedures, such as letters beginning with `Re:` or official written notices.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual chats or internal messages where an official tone would feel stiff.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - formally notify
   - formally respond re
   - formally raise concerns re
   - communicate formally about
   
   **Root Analysis**: formal (official style) + -ly (in a manner)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *forma* (shape, form) → English "formal" → "formally".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We don't need to write formally about this—just send a quick note.
   - They decided not to complain formally re the delay.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We hereby formally notify you re the proposed changes.
   - Employees may formally raise concerns re safety through the HR portal.
