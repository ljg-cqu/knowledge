1. **Linking Word**: regarding (type: connector/prepositional marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Means "about", "concerning", or "in relation to" a specific topic
   - *Function*: Topic-introducing connector, especially in formal emails and letters
   
   **Synonyms**: concerning, about, in relation to, with respect to, as regards
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: In formal or semi-formal writing to introduce what your message is re (about), especially in the first sentence or subject line.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation ("about" is more natural); do not overuse at the start of every sentence.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: Regarding [topic], [clause]
   - [Position]: sentence-initial or mid-sentence before a noun phrase
   - [Punctuation]: comma after introductory "Regarding [topic]"; no comma when mid-sentence
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Avoid double-topic markers like *"Regarding about your email"*; use one only.
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again/back) + gard (look) + -ing (prepositional form)
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "regard" (look at, consider) → prepositional use "regarding" meaning "in relation to".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Regarding that trip, are you still going?
   - Regarding your text earlier, yes, I'm free Friday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regarding your application re the post of Project Manager, we are pleased to inform you...
   - Regarding your enquiry re invoice 4821, please see the details below.

1. **Linking Word**: concerning (type: connector/prepositional marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Means "about" or "relating to" a particular subject, often used in formal writing
   - *Function*: Formal topic-introducing connector; similar to `re` and "regarding"
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, about, in relation to, with regard to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: In official letters, reports, or announcements when introducing what the document is re (about).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very informal speech; when "concerning" might be misread as the adjective meaning "worrying".
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: Concerning [topic], [clause]
   - [Position]: sentence-initial or mid-sentence
   - [Punctuation]: comma after introductory phrase; no comma mid-sentence
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't write *"concerning about"*; choose one marker only.
   
   **Root Analysis**: concern (relate to) + -ing
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *concernere* (relate to) → "concern" → "concerning".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We had a meeting concerning the new schedule.
   - I got a call concerning your order.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Concerning your request re flexible working, we need further details.
   - The committee issued a statement concerning recent changes re fees.

1. **Linking Word**: with respect to (type: phrasal connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Means "concerning" or "in relation to" a particular aspect
   - *Function*: Aspect-focusing connector; highlights which part of a topic you are talking about
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, in respect of, as regards, in relation to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: In formal reports, policies, and academic writing to specify a particular dimension that is re (about) the discussion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual talk (sounds very formal); where a simple "about" will do.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Clause] with respect to [aspect]
   - [Position]: mid-sentence or sentence-initial ("With respect to [aspect], [clause]")
   - [Punctuation]: comma after sentence-initial phrase
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't confuse with polite formula "with all due respect".
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + respect (regard) + to
   
   **Etymology**: From noun "respect" (regard) → fixed phrasal preposition.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's too formal to say "with respect to" in a normal chat—use "about" instead.
   - The rule is mainly with respect to safety.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings are robust with respect to changes in sample size.
   - With respect to your complaint re delays, we propose the following steps.

1. **Linking Word**: in relation to (type: phrasal connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Means "about" or "connected with" something, often for comparisons or explanations
   - *Function*: Relationship-indicating connector; links one item to another
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, with respect to, in connection with
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: When explaining how one thing is re (about or connected to) another in reports, analyses, or briefings.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Where a simpler preposition ("to", "for") is enough; in very informal chat.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Clause] in relation to [reference point]
   - [Position]: mid-sentence or sentence-final
   - [Punctuation]: comma optional depending on emphasis
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Avoid redundancy like *"in relation to regarding"*.
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + relation (connection) + to
   
   **Etymology**: From noun "relation" → phrasal preposition.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How does this decision work in relation to what we agreed before?
   - It's cheap in relation to the quality.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Performance was evaluated in relation to agreed targets.
   - Several questions arise in relation to costs re long-term maintenance.

1. **Linking Word**: as regards (type: phrasal connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Means "with regard to" or "as far as it concerns" a topic
   - *Function*: Topic-introducing connector, similar to `re` and "regarding"
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, as for, with regard to, concerning
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: In neutral to formal writing and speech, often when you move from one subtopic to another that is re (about) the main subject.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Highly informal chat (may sound old-fashioned); modern emails may prefer "regarding".
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: As regards [topic], [clause]
   - [Position]: sentence-initial
   - [Punctuation]: comma after "As regards [topic]"
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Do not write *"as regards to"*.
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the role of) + regards (looks at)
   
   **Etymology**: From verb "regard" in older constructions; now a fixed phrasal preposition.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - As regards the party, I'm still not sure if I can come.
   - As regards your message, I'll reply later.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As regards your application re funding, a decision will be made next month.
   - As regards staffing levels, further discussion is required.

1. **Linking Word**: with regard to (type: phrasal connector)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Means "concerning" or "about" a particular subject
   - *Function*: Formal topic-introducing connector; very frequent in business English
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, in regard to, in respect of, concerning
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: In letters, reports, and emails when you want to connect your statement clearly to a specific matter re (about) which you are writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual settings; do not confuse with "with respects to" (incorrect).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: With regard to [topic], [clause]
   - [Position]: sentence-initial or mid-sentence
   - [Punctuation]: comma after introductory phrase
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Avoid redundant combinations like *"with regard to regarding"*.
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + regard (consideration) + to
   
   **Etymology**: From noun "regard"; established formula in formal English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - With regard to your text, sure, I'm in.
   - It's a bit formal to say "with regard to" in normal chat—"about" is fine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - With regard to your complaint re delivery delays, we have investigated the matter.
   - With regard to your enquiry re course credits, please see the attached note.

1. **Linking Word**: in respect of (type: phrasal connector, chiefly BrE)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Means "concerning" or "with reference to"; common in legal and official English
   - *Function*: Formal connector marking the area to which a statement applies
   
   **Synonyms**: with respect to, regarding, in relation to, about
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: In legal texts, contracts, and very formal letters when specifying what a clause or obligation is re (about).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Everyday conversation; informal emails; American English that prefers "with respect to" or "regarding".
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [Clause] in respect of [topic]
   - [Position]: mid-sentence
   - [Punctuation]: usually no comma
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Don't say *"in respect to"* when you mean this formula; standard is "in respect of".
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + respect (regard) + of
   
   **Etymology**: From noun "respect"; entrenched in legal and bureaucratic English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *Too formal for normal chat—use "about" or "regarding" instead.*
   - The clause is in respect of holiday pay—that just means "about holiday pay".
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The employer's liability in respect of personal injury is limited as set out below.
   - Payments in respect of overtime will be calculated monthly.
