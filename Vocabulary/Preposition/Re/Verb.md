1. **Verb**: refer
   **Meaning**: 
   - To mention or allude to someone or something; to direct someone's attention to a source or document
   
   **Synonyms**: mention, allude to, cite, point to, direct to
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, omit, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: When explaining that a text, email, or person is talking about something, especially in formal phrases like "refer to the email re..." or "as referred to above".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For informal "talk about" in everyday speech when simpler verbs ("talk", "say") are enough.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - refer to
   - as referred to above
   - refer the reader to
   - refer to your email re [topic]
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + fer (carry) → "carry back" (the reader's attention)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *referre* → Old French *referer* → English "refer".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She kept referring to that movie re relationships.
   - I'll refer to your message re the trip when I plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please refer to our email dated 10 April re your outstanding balance.
   - The report refers to several regulations re data protection.

1. **Verb**: relate (to)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be connected with or about something; to show or describe a connection between things
   
   **Synonyms**: connect to, concern, be about, pertain to, involve
   
   **Antonyms**: differ from, be unrelated to
   
   **When to Use**: When explaining how two pieces of information are re (about) each other, especially in academic or analytical writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For telling personal stories ("relate" meaning "tell" is different and less common in modern English).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - relate to
   - how A relates to B
   - related to
   - questions re how these ideas relate
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + lat (carry) → "carry back" or "link"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *referre/relatus* influences "relate" via French; now meaning "show connection".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This chapter relates to what we talked about yesterday.
   - How does this relate to our earlier discussion re time management?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings relate directly to policies re remote work.
   - We must clarify how these metrics relate to existing targets.

1. **Verb**: concern
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be about something; to affect or involve someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: be about, involve, affect, relate to, regard
   
   **Antonyms**: exclude, ignore, bypass
   
   **When to Use**: In formal definitions and policy language describing what a document or rule is re (about): "This policy concerns...".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing emotional worry ("concern" as a noun or verb of emotion is different, though related).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - concerns (is about)
   - as far as it concerns
   - matters that concern
   - questions re matters that concern staff safety
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + cern (separate, sift) → "distinguish, have to do with"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *concernere* (to mix/relate) → Old French → English "concern".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The film mainly concerns family relationships.
   - This email concerns your booking re next week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This policy concerns data retention and access control.
   - The report concerns issues re cross-border data transfers.

1. **Verb**: address
   **Meaning**: 
   - To deal with or give attention to a problem, question, or topic
   
   **Synonyms**: deal with, tackle, handle, respond to, take up
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, neglect, avoid
   
   **When to Use**: In professional and academic writing when explaining what a document or meeting is re (about): "address concerns re...".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For physical addressing (writing an address on an envelope) unless clearly specified.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - address an issue
   - address concerns re
   - address questions re
   - address topics related to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + dress (direct)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *adrecier* (straighten, direct) → English "address".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to address the problems re communication.
   - Let's address your questions re the trip.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This guideline addresses key concerns re the new system.
   - The workshop will address questions re implementation timelines.

1. **Verb**: cite
   **Meaning**: 
   - To mention something as an example or proof; to quote a passage or source
   
   **Synonyms**: quote, refer to, mention, reference, draw on
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, suppress, conceal
   
   **When to Use**: In academic and legal writing when citing sources re (about) a topic; often followed by references and footnotes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "mention" or "talk about" is enough.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cite a source
   - cite evidence
   - frequently cited re
   - widely cited re climate change / inequality
   
   **Root Analysis**: cit (call, arouse) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin *citare* (to summon) → Old French → English "cite".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They cited a few examples re bad service.
   - She likes to cite studies re sleep and health.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The paper cites several key articles re deep learning.
   - Courts often cite earlier decisions re similar facts.

1. **Verb**: correspond (to/with)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To match or be similar; to have a direct relationship with something else
   
   **Synonyms**: match, align with, be equivalent to, map onto
   
   **Antonyms**: differ from, conflict with, contradict
   
   **When to Use**: When explaining how numbers, items, or sections are re each other (tied together) in tables, contracts, or technical documents.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For everyday "write emails" meaning ("correspond with someone"); that is a different, though related, sense.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - correspond to
   - correspond with
   - section A corresponds to figure 1 re layout
   - colours correspond to categories
   
   **Root Analysis**: co- (together) + respond (answer) → "answer to, match"
   
   **Etymology**: Latin-based via French; English "correspond" meaning "answer, be similar".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The labels correspond to the colours on the chart.
   - These steps correspond to the notes we sent re the process.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Each item in column B corresponds to a requirement re data handling.
   - The categories correspond closely to those used in prior work re risk assessment.

1. **Verb**: reply
   **Meaning**: 
   - To answer someone in speech or writing; to respond to a message, email, or question
   
   **Synonyms**: respond, answer, write back, get back to
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, remain silent, withhold a reply
   
   **When to Use**: For email and message communication, especially in contexts where the email software adds `Re:` automatically to show that a message is a reply.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need a more formal phrase like "respond to" in academic writing.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reply to an email
   - quick reply
   - no reply
   - thank you for your reply re [topic]
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + ply (fold) → "fold back" → answer
   
   **Etymology**: Old French *reployer* (fold back, answer) → English "reply".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I haven't replied to his email re the trip yet.
   - Thanks for replying so fast re the budget.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Thank you for your reply re our enquiry about licence terms.
   - Please reply re your availability by Friday.
