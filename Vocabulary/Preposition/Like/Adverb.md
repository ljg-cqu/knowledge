1. **Adverb**: likely (type: degree/sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Probably; with a high degree of probability or expectation
   - *(Sentence adverb)* Used to express probability or expectation about the truth of a statement
   - *Position: most commonly sentence-initial or mid-position; can also follow "very", "most", "more"*
   
   **Synonyms**: probably, presumably, apparently, seemingly, in all likelihood
   
   **Antonyms**: unlikely, improbably, doubtfully
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing probability or likelihood of something happening; appropriate in both predictions and assessments; suitable for formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when certainty is required; not suitable when making definite statements or guarantees
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - most likely
   - very likely
   - more than likely
   - quite likely
   - likely to happen
   
   **Root Analysis**: like (similar) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from Old Norse "líkligr" (probable). Adverb form developed 14th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She'll most likely show up around 8 pm.
   - It's likely going to rain tomorrow, so bring an umbrella.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed changes will likely require extensive legislative review.
   - Analysts predict that market volatility is likely to continue throughout the quarter.

1. **Adverb**: likewise (type: sentence/linking)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Similarly; in the same way; also
   - *(Sentence adverb)* Used to indicate that what follows is similar to what came before
   - *Position: typically sentence-initial, can be mid-position for emphasis*
   
   **Synonyms**: similarly, also, in the same way, equally, correspondingly
   
   **Antonyms**: conversely, differently, on the contrary, however
   
   **When to Use**: When drawing parallels or indicating similarity between two statements or situations; appropriate for adding information that mirrors previous content; suitable in formal and academic writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when contrasting ideas; not suitable for introducing opposing viewpoints or exceptions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - and likewise
   - likewise with
   - do likewise
   - respond likewise
   - feel likewise
   
   **Root Analysis**: like (similar) + wise (manner, way)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from "like" + "wise" (OE wīse meaning "manner"). First recorded use 15th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I enjoyed the movie; my friends felt likewise.
   - She thanked him for his help, and he responded likewise.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The first hypothesis was confirmed; likewise, the second hypothesis yielded positive results.
   - European markets declined sharply; Asian markets performed likewise during trading hours.

1. **Adverb**: unlikely (type: degree/sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Probably not; with a low degree of probability or expectation
   - *(Sentence adverb)* Used to express improbability or low expectation about something happening
   - *Position: most commonly sentence-initial, mid-position, or as complement after "be"*
   
   **Synonyms**: improbably, doubtfully, hardly, scarcely, not likely
   
   **Antonyms**: likely, probably, presumably, certainly
   
   **When to Use**: When expressing low probability or improbability of an event or situation; appropriate for making predictions or assessments; suitable in formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when discussing something that has already happened or is certain; not suitable when strong denial is needed
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - highly unlikely
   - very unlikely
   - extremely unlikely
   - seem unlikely
   - appear unlikely
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + like (similar) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: English → from "un-" (negative prefix) + "likely". First recorded use 16th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's highly unlikely she'll change her mind now.
   - They're unlikely to finish the project on time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee deemed it unlikely that funding would be approved before fiscal year-end.
   - Economic recovery appears unlikely without significant policy intervention.
