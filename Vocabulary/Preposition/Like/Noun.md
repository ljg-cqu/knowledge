1. **Noun**: like (C, pl: likes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Something that one enjoys or prefers; a preference or favorable feeling
   - *(Social media context)* An expression of approval or support for content, typically indicated by clicking a button
   
   **Synonyms**: preference, favorite, interest, passion, approval
   
   **Antonyms**: dislike, aversion, hatred, antipathy
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing personal preferences, social media engagement, expressing tastes and interests; appropriate in informal and semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal academic or professional writing; technical contexts requiring precise terminology
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - likes and dislikes
   - get more likes
   - share your likes
   - similar likes
   - accumulate likes
   
   **Root Analysis**: like (verb: to enjoy) → nominalized to noun form
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "līcian" (to please) → Middle English "liken" → Modern English "like" (noun form from 19th century; social media sense from 2000s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We have similar likes and dislikes when it comes to music.
   - Her post got thousands of likes within an hour!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The survey assessed participant preferences across multiple categories.
   - User engagement metrics indicate substantial approval for the new feature.

1. **Noun**: likeness (C/U, pl: likenesses)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Resemblance or similarity in appearance; the quality of being similar
   - *(Sense 2)* A portrait or representation of someone
   
   **Synonyms**: resemblance, similarity, semblance, image, representation
   
   **Antonyms**: difference, dissimilarity, contrast, unlikeness
   
   **When to Use**: Formal descriptions of similarity, art criticism, portrait descriptions, philosophical or literary contexts discussing representation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations (use "similarity" or "looks like"); technical scientific contexts requiring precise terminology
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bear a likeness to
   - striking likeness
   - family likeness
   - capture someone's likeness
   - physical likeness
   
   **Root Analysis**: like (similar) + -ness (quality, state)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gelīcnes" → Middle English "liknesse" → Modern English "likeness" (12th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The statue has an amazing likeness to the real person.
   - There's definitely a family likeness between the siblings.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The portrait captures a remarkable likeness of the subject in both appearance and spirit.
   - Scholars have noted the structural likeness between these two architectural traditions.

1. **Noun**: likelihood (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The probability or chance that something will happen; the state of being likely
   
   **Synonyms**: probability, chance, prospect, possibility, odds
   
   **Antonyms**: impossibility, improbability, unlikelihood
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing probabilities, risk assessment, predictions, formal and semi-formal contexts, academic and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When certainty is meant (use "certainty" instead); very casual contexts where simpler words suffice
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in all likelihood
   - increase the likelihood
   - likelihood of success
   - strong likelihood
   - assess the likelihood
   
   **Root Analysis**: likely (probable) + -hood (state, condition)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "likelihed" → Modern English "likelihood" (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's the likelihood of it raining tomorrow?
   - There's a strong likelihood she'll get the promotion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Statistical analysis indicates a high likelihood of correlation between these variables.
   - The likelihood of project completion within the proposed timeframe appears minimal.

1. **Noun**: liking (U, occasionally C: pl: likings)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A feeling of regard or fondness for someone or something; preference or taste
   - *(In phrases)* "To one's liking" means according to one's preference or satisfaction
   
   **Synonyms**: fondness, affection, taste, preference, partiality
   
   **Antonyms**: dislike, aversion, distaste, antipathy
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing preferences or fondness, discussing tastes, both formal and informal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When strong love or deep emotion is meant (too mild); very technical contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - to one's liking
   - take a liking to
   - have a liking for
   - develop a liking
   - not to someone's liking
   
   **Root Analysis**: like (verb: enjoy) + -ing (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "līcung" → Middle English "liking" → Modern English "liking" (gerund/noun from 14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've really taken a liking to this new coffee shop.
   - Is the room temperature to your liking?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee found the proposal to their liking and approved funding.
   - The candidate developed a liking for constitutional law during her undergraduate studies.

1. **Noun**: unlikelihood (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The quality or state of being unlikely; improbability
   
   **Synonyms**: improbability, doubtfulness, implausibility, remote chance
   
   **Antonyms**: likelihood, probability, certainty, strong possibility
   
   **When to Use**: Formal discussions of improbable events, risk assessment, academic and professional contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations (use "it's unlikely" instead); when discussing impossibility (use "impossibility")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - given the unlikelihood
   - despite the unlikelihood
   - unlikelihood of occurrence
   - the sheer unlikelihood
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + likely (probable) + -hood (state, condition)
   
   **Etymology**: English formation → un- + likelihood (17th century coinage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Given the unlikelihood of rain, we decided not to bring umbrellas.
   - The plan's success seems doubtful at best.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The unlikelihood of such convergent evolution occurring independently warrants further investigation.
   - Analysts emphasize the unlikelihood of market recovery within the current quarter.

1. **Noun**: dislike (C/U, pl: dislikes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A feeling of distaste, aversion, or mild hostility toward something or someone
   
   **Synonyms**: aversion, distaste, antipathy, disinclination, disapproval
   
   **Antonyms**: like, liking, fondness, preference, affection
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing negative preferences, discussing aversions, appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When strong hatred is meant (too mild); "dislike" is milder than "hatred"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - likes and dislikes
   - take a dislike to
   - strong dislike
   - express dislike
   - personal dislikes
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (opposite, negative) + like (enjoy)
   
   **Etymology**: English formation → dis- (Latin negative prefix) + like (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have a strong dislike for horror movies—they're too scary!
   - She took an instant dislike to the new manager.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants expressed significant dislike for the proposed policy changes.
   - The author's dislike of modernist architecture is evident throughout the critique.
