1. **Functional Expression**: Would you like [X]? / Would you like to [verb]? (function: offering/inviting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to offer something to someone or invite them to do something
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite question form)
   
   **Synonyms**: Do you want [X]?, Can I offer you [X]?, Care to [verb]?, How about [X]?
   
   **Antonyms**: (Not applicable—no opposite for offering)
   
   **When to Use**: Polite offers in professional contexts, service situations, hosting guests, formal invitations; shows respect and gives the other person easy refusal option
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual situations with close friends (overly formal); urgent situations requiring direct action; when informality is culturally expected
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would you care to [verb]? (more formal)
   - Do you want [X]? (less formal)
   - Would you like some [X]? (offering substances)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses conditional "would" to create social distance and politeness; allows easy refusal without face-threat; standard hospitality language
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: Yes, please / I'd love to / That would be nice
   - Declining: No, thank you / I'm fine, thanks / Maybe later
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would you like some coffee? I'm making a pot.
   - Would you like to join us for dinner tonight?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would you like to review the proposal before our meeting tomorrow?
   - Would you like me to arrange a conference call with the stakeholders?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd like to [verb] / I would like [noun] (function: requesting/expressing preference)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to state what you want or request something
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (softened with conditional)
   
   **Synonyms**: I want to [verb], I wish to [verb], I'd prefer to [verb], Could I [verb]?
   
   **Antonyms**: I don't want to [verb], I'd rather not [verb]
   
   **When to Use**: Making polite requests, stating preferences professionally, ordering in service contexts, expressing desires in formal situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Emergencies requiring direct commands; very casual contexts with intimates; when appearing uncertain would weaken authority
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I would like to [verb] (more formal, written)
   - I'd love to [verb] (more enthusiastic)
   - I'd really like to [verb] (adding emphasis)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Conditional "would" creates politeness through hypothetical framing; less imposing than direct "I want"; standard professional register
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Granting: Certainly / Of course / I'll arrange that
   - Refusing: I'm afraid that's not possible / Unfortunately, we can't / Perhaps an alternative...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd like to try that new restaurant sometime.
   - I'd like a coffee, please—black, no sugar.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I would like to schedule a meeting to discuss the quarterly results.
   - I would like to request additional resources for this project.

1. **Functional Expression**: It's like [X] / It's (just) like [verb]-ing (function: comparing/explaining through analogy)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to explain or clarify something by comparing it to something more familiar
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (straightforward comparison)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's similar to [X], It's comparable to [X], It resembles [X], Think of it as [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: It's different from [X], It's unlike [X], It's nothing like [X]
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining concepts to others, making ideas more concrete, teaching, clarifying through comparison; especially useful when dealing with abstract or unfamiliar concepts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal academic writing (use "resembles" or "is analogous to"); when precision is critical and analogies might mislead; legal contexts requiring exact definitions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It's kind of like [X] (more tentative)
   - It's exactly like [X] (stronger similarity)
   - Think of it like [X] (more instructional)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Common teaching and explanatory device; creates shared understanding through familiar reference points; can make complex ideas accessible
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Understanding: Oh, I see! / That makes sense / Got it
   - Still confused: I'm not sure I follow / Can you give another example?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Learning to code is like learning a new language—frustrating at first but rewarding.
   - It's like trying to solve a puzzle with missing pieces.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system operates in a manner analogous to cellular respiration.
   - The structure may be compared to a hierarchical tree with multiple branches.

1. **Functional Expression**: If you like (function: hedging/softening)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to present something as optional or to soften a suggestion
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (minimizes imposition)
   
   **Synonyms**: If you want, If you prefer, If you wish, Should you choose to
   
   **Antonyms**: You must, You should, You have to
   
   **When to Use**: Making suggestions without imposing, offering options, showing flexibility, respecting autonomy; appropriate when giving advice or alternatives
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When giving mandatory instructions; emergency situations; when clarity and directness are essential; positions of authority requiring clear direction
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - If you'd like (more formal)
   - If you like, you can [verb] (offering option)
   - You can, if you like (emphasizing permission)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Classic hedging device; reduces face-threat of suggestions; maintains recipient's autonomy; common in British English
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: Yes, I think I will / That sounds good
   - Declining: No, I'm fine / I'll pass / Maybe another time
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can borrow my car, if you like.
   - We could grab lunch after, if you like.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - You may submit your revisions by Friday, if you like, or we can extend the deadline.
   - We can schedule a follow-up meeting, if you like, to address remaining concerns.

1. **Functional Expression**: Something like [X] / [Number] or something like that (function: approximating/hedging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to give an approximate figure, description, or example without claiming exactness
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (deliberately vague for caution)
   
   **Synonyms**: Approximately [X], Around [X], Roughly [X], About [X], More or less [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: Exactly [X], Precisely [X], Specifically [X]
   
   **When to Use**: When exact figures aren't available or necessary, giving rough estimates, avoiding over-commitment, hedging claims, casual descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Contexts requiring precision (legal, scientific, financial reporting); when vagueness undermines credibility; formal presentations where accuracy is expected
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Or something like that (following a suggestion)
   - Something along those lines (similar concept)
   - Somewhere around [X] (approximating location/quantity)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Useful hedging strategy to avoid false precision; signals uncertainty or casual estimation; protects speaker from being wrong about specifics
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Seeking precision: Can you be more specific? / Do you know the exact number?
   - Accepting approximation: That's close enough / Okay, roughly [X]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It'll cost something like $200, maybe more.
   - There were 30 people there, or something like that.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project will require approximately six months to complete.
   - Initial estimates suggest a budget in the region of $500,000.

1. **Functional Expression**: What's [X] like? / What does [X] look like? (function: asking for description)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to request a description or characterization of someone or something
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (straightforward question)
   
   **Synonyms**: How would you describe [X]?, Can you describe [X]?, How is [X]?, Tell me about [X]
   
   **Antonyms**: (Not applicable—no opposite for questions)
   
   **When to Use**: Seeking information about unfamiliar people, places, experiences, or things; gathering details before making decisions; showing interest in others' experiences
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When asking about subjective quality (use "How is/was...?"); very formal contexts (use "Could you characterize...?")
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What's it like to [verb]? (asking about experience)
   - What does it feel like? (asking about sensation)
   - What's he/she like? (asking about personality)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Versatile question form for eliciting descriptions; can refer to physical appearance, personality, experiences, or characteristics; conversational and natural
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Describing: It's [adjective] / He's [characteristics] / It looks like [description]
   - Comparing: It's like [comparison] / Similar to [X]
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's your new boss like? Is she nice?
   - What's the weather like there? Should I bring a jacket?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - What is the regulatory environment like in that jurisdiction?
   - Could you describe what the implementation process looks like?
