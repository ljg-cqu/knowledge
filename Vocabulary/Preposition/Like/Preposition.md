1. **Preposition**: like (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial/Abstract)* Similar to; having the same characteristics or qualities as
   - *(Abstract)* In the manner of; in the way that
   - *(Abstract)* Such as; for example (introducing examples)
   
   **Synonyms**: similar to (in context of similarity), such as (in context of examples), in the manner of (in context of manner), resembling (in context of appearance)
   
   **Antonyms**: unlike, different from, contrary to, as opposed to
   
   **When to Use**: Comparisons, giving examples informally, describing similarities, casual and semi-formal speech; introducing hypothetical scenarios
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal academic writing (use "such as" for examples); after linking verbs (use "as" or "as if" instead: "It looks as if..." not "It looks like..."); when "as" is grammatically required in comparisons of roles
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: like a bird, like magic, like clockwork
   - **Verb + prep**: sounds like, looks like, feels like, seems like
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare with adjectives; "like" is preposition not dependent preposition)
   - **Noun + prep**: something like, anything like, nothing like
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "gelīc" (having the same form)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "gelīc" → Middle English "lik/lyke" → Modern English "like" (preposition use from 13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She runs like the wind—incredibly fast!
   - I want a car like yours, something sporty and fun.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The mechanism operates in a manner similar to hydraulic systems.
   - Several metropolitan areas, such as Tokyo and London, face comparable challenges.

1. **Preposition**: unlike (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Different from; not similar to; in contrast to
   - *(Abstract)* Not typical or characteristic of (someone's usual behavior)
   
   **Synonyms**: different from, in contrast to, as opposed to, contrary to, dissimilar to
   
   **Antonyms**: like, similar to, akin to, resembling
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing differences, contrasting two things, showing deviation from norms or expectations; appropriate in formal and informal writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When highlighting similarities; common learner error is using "unlike" with a verb ("unlike he does"—incorrect; use "unlike him")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: unlike before, unlike anything else, unlike other cases
   - **Verb + prep**: (rare; "unlike" typically begins phrases rather than follows verbs)
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not applicable)
   - **Noun + prep**: behavior unlike X, something unlike X
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + like (similar)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → un- (negative prefix) + like (15th century as preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Unlike my brother, I'm terrible at sports.
   - That's so unlike you—normally you're super organized!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Unlike previous methodologies, this approach incorporates qualitative analysis.
   - The current administration's policy differs substantially from, or is unlike, that of its predecessor.

1. **Preposition**: nothing like (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Completely different from; not at all similar to; bearing no resemblance to
   
   **Synonyms**: completely different from, not remotely similar to, bears no resemblance to, totally unlike
   
   **Antonyms**: just like, exactly like, identical to, very similar to
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing extreme difference, contradicting comparisons, stressing dissimilarity; appropriate in all registers though more common informally
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When subtle or moderate differences are meant; formal academic writing prefers "differs substantially from"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: nothing like before, nothing like the original, nothing like expected
   - **Verb + prep**: is nothing like, looks nothing like, sounds nothing like
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not applicable)
   - **Noun + prep**: result nothing like X, outcome nothing like Y
   
   **Root Analysis**: no + thing (complete negation) + like (similar to)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound phrase (17th century onward)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The sequel was nothing like the first movie—totally disappointing.
   - She looks nothing like her profile picture!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The experimental results were nothing like predicted outcomes, requiring theoretical revision.
   - Contemporary practices bear no resemblance to, or are nothing like, historical precedents.

1. **Preposition**: something like (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Approximately; roughly; used to indicate an estimate or approximation
   - *(Abstract)* Somewhat similar to; bearing some resemblance to
   
   **Synonyms**: approximately, about, around, roughly, more or less
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly, precisely, specifically
   
   **When to Use**: Giving rough estimates, avoiding precision, hedging numerical claims, casual descriptions of similarity
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Contexts requiring precision (legal, scientific, financial reporting); formal documents where accuracy is critical
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: something like 50, something like three hours, something like this
   - **Verb + prep**: costs something like, takes something like, looks something like
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not applicable)
   - **Noun + prep**: (used before noun: something like a miracle)
   
   **Root Analysis**: some + thing (indefinite) + like (similar to/approximately)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound phrase (19th century for approximation sense)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It'll take something like two hours to get there, maybe less.
   - We need something like 50 volunteers for the event.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project will require approximately six months for completion.
   - Initial estimates suggest a budget in the region of $2 million.

1. **Preposition**: just like (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Exactly similar to; in exactly the same way as; precisely resembling
   
   **Synonyms**: exactly like, the same as, identical to, precisely as, in the same manner as
   
   **Antonyms**: completely unlike, totally different from, nothing like
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing strong or exact similarity, making vivid comparisons, showing parallels; casual and semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal academic writing (use "in the same manner as" or "identically to"); when approximate similarity is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: just like before, just like me, just like clockwork
   - **Verb + prep**: works just like, sounds just like, looks just like
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not applicable)
   - **Noun + prep**: behavior just like X, response just like Y
   
   **Root Analysis**: just (exactly) + like (similar to)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound → just (14th c., from Latin) + like (OE gelīc)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You sound just like your father when you say that!
   - The new phone works just like the old one, so no learning curve.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The mechanism functions in precisely the same manner as previous models.
   - The results mirror exactly those obtained in earlier trials.

1. **Preposition**: more like (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* More accurately described as; closer in resemblance to; used to correct or refine a previous statement
   
   **Synonyms**: more accurately, rather, or more precisely, closer to
   
   **Antonyms**: less like, exactly, not at all like
   
   **When to Use**: Self-corrections, refining descriptions, improving accuracy of comparisons; informal and semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Initial statements in formal writing (be accurate from the start); contexts where self-correction undermines credibility
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: more like X than Y, more like 10 than 5
   - **Verb + prep**: is more like, sounds more like, feels more like
   - **Adjective + prep**: (not applicable)
   - **Noun + prep**: description more like X, situation more like Y
   
   **Root Analysis**: more (greater degree) + like (similar to)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound (20th century common usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's not sadness, more like frustration, you know?
   - The meeting took 10 minutes—well, more like 30, actually.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The phenomenon may be more accurately characterized as oscillation rather than decline.
   - The timeline reflects three months, though a more precise estimate would be four to five months.

1. **Preposition**: anything like (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* At all similar to; in any way resembling (typically used in questions and negatives)
   - *(Abstract)* Nearly as much as; to any comparable degree
   
   **Synonyms**: at all like, in any way similar to, remotely like, at all comparable to
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly like, identical to, completely like
   
   **When to Use**: Questions about similarity, negative statements emphasizing difference, comparisons of degree; casual and semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Positive statements (use "something like"); formal academic writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: anything like before, anything like expected, anything like this
   - **Verb + prep**: is anything like, looks anything like, costs anything like
   - **Adjective + prep**: (used with adjectives in comparative: anything like as good as)
   - **Noun + prep**: (question: anything like X?)
   
   **Root Analysis**: any + thing (indefinite) + like (similar to)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound phrase (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is the new restaurant anything like the old one?
   - Today wasn't anything like as busy as yesterday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results do not bear any resemblance to previous findings.
   - Current conditions are not remotely comparable to those of the preceding decade.
