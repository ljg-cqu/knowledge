1. **Verb**: like
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To find someone or something pleasant, attractive, or satisfactory; to enjoy
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To wish for or want; to prefer (often with "would" or "should")
   - *(Sense 3)* (Intransitive - social media) To indicate approval or support for online content by clicking a button
   
   **Synonyms**: enjoy, appreciate, love, fancy, prefer, care for
   
   **Antonyms**: dislike, hate, detest, despise, loathe
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing preferences, enjoyment, or approval; describing tastes and interests; making polite requests with "would like"; all registers from casual to formal
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When strong passion is meant (use "love"); when professional neutrality is required (use "prefer" or "find satisfactory")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - like to do something
   - would like to [verb]
   - if you like
   - like it or not
   - I'd like to think
   
   **Root Analysis**: From Old English "līcian" (to please, be pleasing)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "līcian" → Middle English "liken" → Modern English "like" (verb form since 8th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I really like this new coffee shop—great atmosphere!
   - Do you like horror movies? I can't stand them.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We would like to express our appreciation for your continued support.
   - Participants indicated they like the flexibility of the proposed schedule.

1. **Verb**: dislike
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To regard with distaste or aversion; to find unpleasant or disagreeable
   
   **Synonyms**: disapprove of, object to, be averse to, take exception to, not care for
   
   **Antonyms**: like, enjoy, appreciate, favor, prefer
   
   **When to Use**: Expressing negative preferences or mild aversion; appropriate in all registers; more polite than "hate"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When strong hatred is meant (use "hate" or "detest"); "dislike" is relatively mild
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strongly dislike
   - dislike intensely
   - come to dislike
   - take a dislike to
   - make oneself disliked
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (opposite, reversal) + like (enjoy)
   
   **Etymology**: English formation → dis- (Latin negative prefix) + like (verb form from 16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I really dislike getting up early on weekends.
   - She dislikes people who are constantly late.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Survey respondents disliked the proposed changes to the benefits package.
   - The author clearly dislikes modernist approaches to literary criticism.

1. **Verb**: liken
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To compare someone or something to another; to describe as similar (typically followed by "to")
   
   **Synonyms**: compare to, equate with, draw parallels with, analogize to, parallel
   
   **Antonyms**: contrast with, differentiate from, distinguish from
   
   **When to Use**: Formal or literary contexts when making comparisons or analogies; analytical writing; appropriate in formal and academic registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversations (use "compare to" or "it's like"); when informal language is preferred
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - liken something to something
   - be likened to
   - can be likened to
   - have been likened to
   - often likened to
   
   **Root Analysis**: like (similar) + -en (verb-forming suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "liknen" → Modern English "liken" (14th century; less common in modern usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - People often liken her voice to that of a famous singer.
   - He likened the experience to running a marathon.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Critics have likened the author's prose style to that of Virginia Woolf.
   - The phenomenon may be likened to osmosis in biological systems.

1. **Verb**: unlike (archaic/rare as verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive - social media) To remove a previously given "like" from content; to retract approval
   - *(Archaic)* To cease to like; to regard with distaste
   
   **Synonyms**: remove like, retract approval, withdraw support
   
   **Antonyms**: like, approve, support
   
   **When to Use**: Social media contexts when removing approval; technical discussions of social media features; archaic sense rarely used in modern English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: General preference discussions (use "dislike"); formal writing (archaic sense essentially obsolete)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unlike a post
   - unlike and unfollow
   - can unlike
   - accidentally unlike
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (reversal) + like (approve)
   
   **Etymology**: English formation → un- + like (verb). Modern social media sense from 2000s; archaic sense from 16th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I accidentally liked her photo and then had to quickly unlike it!
   - You can always unlike posts if you change your mind.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - User engagement metrics track both likes and unlikes to assess content performance.
   - Platform analytics distinguish between sustained approval and users who subsequently unlike content.

1. **Verb**: mislike (archaic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To dislike or disapprove of; to regard with displeasure (archaic/obsolete)
   
   **Synonyms**: dislike, disapprove of, object to, find disagreeable
   
   **Antonyms**: like, approve, favor
   
   **When to Use**: Historical texts, Shakespearean English, archaic literary contexts; essentially obsolete in modern English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Contemporary speech and writing (use "dislike"); modern contexts where it will sound pretentious or confusing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - mislike greatly (archaic)
   - much misliked (archaic)
   
   **Root Analysis**: mis- (badly, wrongly) + like (regard)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "misliken" → obsolete by 18th century
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Not used in modern casual speech)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I mislike this matter greatly," declared the nobleman in the historical novel.
   - Early English texts reveal that subjects misliked the unpopular tax measures.
