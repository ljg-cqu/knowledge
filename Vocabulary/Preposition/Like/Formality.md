1. **Original Expression**: I like your idea  
   **Context**: Expressing approval or appreciation for someone's suggestion or proposal
   
   **Core Intent**: To express positive opinion about someone's suggestion
   
   **Casual**: I like it! / That sounds good / I'm into it  
   *Characteristics*: Simple verb choice, informal vocabulary, contractions, enthusiastic tone  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, close colleagues, casual meetings, relaxed brainstorming
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I appreciate your idea / That's a good suggestion / I think that could work well  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral professional language, complete sentences, measured enthusiasm  
   *Use when*: Regular workplace interactions, team meetings, professional emails, polite colleagues
   
   **Formal**: I find your proposal quite compelling / Your suggestion has considerable merit / I concur with your recommendation  
   *Characteristics*: Academic vocabulary ("compelling", "merit", "concur"), complete grammatical structures, restrained tone  
   *Use when*: Executive meetings, academic settings, formal presentations, written proposals, authority figures
   
   **Humorous**: I dig it! / That's pretty groovy / I'm vibing with that idea  
   *Characteristics*: Playful slang, dated or trendy expressions, casual enthusiasm  
   *Use when*: Friendly team environments, creative brainstorming, building rapport, appropriate informality  
   *Avoid when*: Serious strategic decisions, performance reviews, client meetings, cross-cultural contexts
   
   **Lively**: I love that! / That's brilliant! / I'm totally on board with that!  
   *Characteristics*: Intensifiers ("totally", "brilliant"), exclamation marks, energetic vocabulary  
   *Use when*: Motivating teams, celebrating ideas, energetic discussions, positive feedback sessions
   
   **Empathetic**: I really appreciate how you're thinking about this / I can see the value in your perspective / Your idea resonates with me  
   *Characteristics*: Validation language ("appreciate", "value", "resonates"), acknowledgment of effort  
   *Use when*: Supporting team members, encouraging participation, validating contributions, building trust
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Use "I concur" with superiors; "I like it" with peers; "That's interesting" when subordinate
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/new colleagues require semi-formal; close colleagues allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Strategic decisions require formal; routine ideas allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures value restraint (formal); others value directness (casual)
   - *Medium*: Written communication typically one level more formal than spoken
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "I like it" in formal proposals—sounds childish and unprofessional
   - Using "I concur" with peers—creates unnecessary distance and sounds pretentious
   - Over-enthusiasm ("I love it!") when serious analysis is expected—undermines credibility
   
   **Cultural Notes**: North American workplace culture generally accepts casual enthusiasm; European/Asian business contexts often prefer more restrained, formal expressions
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Junior colleague suggesting idea in team meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Lively ("I like that approach!" or "Great idea!")
   - **Situation 2**: Responding to CEO's strategic proposal in board meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("I find the proposal compelling and well-reasoned")
   - **Situation 3**: Email to external client about their suggestion → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("I appreciate your suggestion and believe it could work well")

1. **Original Expression**: It's like comparing apples and oranges  
   **Context**: Explaining that two things are too different to be meaningfully compared
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that a comparison is invalid or inappropriate due to fundamental differences
   
   **Casual**: You can't really compare those / They're totally different things / Not the same thing at all  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary, conversational structure, negations  
   *Use when*: Informal discussions, casual debates, friendly conversations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: These are fundamentally different / That's not a valid comparison / They aren't comparable  
   *Characteristics*: Precise vocabulary, neutral tone, clear logical structure  
   *Use when*: Professional discussions, business meetings, workplace analysis, reports
   
   **Formal**: Such a comparison lacks analytical validity / These entities are categorically distinct / One cannot meaningfully juxtapose these disparate concepts  
   *Characteristics*: Academic language ("juxtapose", "disparate", "validity"), complex syntax, abstract nouns  
   *Use when*: Academic writing, research papers, formal debates, scholarly presentations
   
   **Humorous**: That's like comparing pizza and taxes—both exist, but that's where it ends / Apples and doorknobs, my friend  
   *Characteristics*: Absurd comparisons, playful exaggeration, conversational asides  
   *Use when*: Lightening mood in discussions, making points memorable, friendly debates  
   *Avoid when*: Serious analytical contexts, formal arguments, when humor might offend
   
   **Lively**: Whoa, those are worlds apart! / Come on, that's not even close! / Those are completely different universes!  
   *Characteristics*: Intensifiers, emphatic language, spatial metaphors ("worlds apart")  
   *Use when*: Energetic debates, passionate discussions, emphasizing strong differences
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand why you might see a connection, but they're actually quite different / I see where you're coming from, though these differ in important ways  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledgment phrases, softening language, validating before correcting  
   *Use when*: Teaching contexts, mentoring, gentle correction, maintaining relationships while disagreeing
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Use formal variants with authority; semi-formal with equals; empathetic when correcting subordinates
   - *Social Distance*: Professional strangers need formal/semi-formal; friends allow casual/humorous
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Critical analysis requires formal; casual debate allows humor
   - *Cultural Context*: Direct cultures (US) accept "not comparable"; indirect cultures need empathetic framing
   - *Medium*: Written analysis requires more formal variants; spoken allows casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using the idiom "apples and oranges" in formal academic writing—too colloquial
   - Being too blunt ("That's wrong") without acknowledging the other person's perspective—damages rapport
   - Overusing emphatic language in professional contexts—sounds unprofessional
   
   **Cultural Notes**: The "apples and oranges" idiom is primarily English; direct translation doesn't work cross-culturally. Many cultures prefer more indirect disagreement.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Student comparing unrelated concepts in class → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("I see the connection you're making, but these differ fundamentally")
   - **Situation 2**: Colleague making false equivalence in strategy meeting → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("These situations aren't directly comparable due to different contexts")
   - **Situation 3**: Academic paper analyzing flawed comparison → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Such juxtaposition lacks methodological rigor given the disparate nature of the phenomena")

1. **Original Expression**: If you like  
   **Context**: Offering someone a choice or suggesting an optional course of action
   
   **Core Intent**: To indicate that something is optional or to politely offer an alternative
   
   **Casual**: If you want / Sure, if you want to / Up to you  
   *Characteristics*: Short, simple constructions, direct language, minimal politeness markers  
   *Use when*: Friends, family, close colleagues, casual suggestions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: If you prefer / If that works for you / If you'd like to  
   *Characteristics*: Polite phrasing, slight deference to other's choice, professional courtesy  
   *Use when*: Standard workplace interactions, professional emails, polite offers to colleagues
   
   **Formal**: Should you so desire / If you deem it appropriate / At your discretion  
   *Characteristics*: Elevated vocabulary, formal constructions, high deference to other's authority  
   *Use when*: Formal correspondence, addressing superiors, legal/official contexts, ceremonial situations
   
   **Humorous**: If you're feeling fancy / If that's your jam / If you're so inclined  
   *Characteristics*: Playful vocabulary, lighthearted tone, mildly archaic ("so inclined") used ironically  
   *Use when*: Friendly banter, casual team interactions, rapport-building  
   *Avoid when*: Important decisions, serious offers, professional distance required
   
   **Lively**: Absolutely, if you'd like! / Go for it if you want! / Feel free!  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic encouragement, exclamation marks, permission-giving language  
   *Use when*: Encouraging participation, welcoming team members, positive team culture
   
   **Empathetic**: Whatever feels right for you / Only if you're comfortable with that / No pressure—whatever you prefer  
   *Characteristics*: Reassuring language, explicit removal of pressure, comfort-oriented phrasing  
   *Use when*: Sensitive situations, supporting someone's autonomy, reducing anxiety about choices
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: "At your discretion" to superiors; "if you like" to peers; "if you want" to subordinates
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers need formal/semi-formal; close relationships allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Important decisions need clear formal offer; trivial choices allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: High-context cultures need more formal deference; low-context allow directness
   - *Medium*: Written offers typically more formal than spoken
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "if you want" to superiors—sounds too casual and can appear dismissive
   - Using "should you so desire" with peers—sounds overly formal and creates distance
   - Overusing empathetic framing in routine situations—can seem patronizing
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Offering choices requires different levels of formality across cultures. British English often uses "if you like" more formally than American English.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Offering colleague coffee in break room → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("Want some coffee? If you want, I'm making a pot")
   - **Situation 2**: Proposing optional meeting time to client → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("We can meet at 2pm, if that works for you")
   - **Situation 3**: Board member offering CEO choice of strategies → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("You may pursue either option, at your discretion")
