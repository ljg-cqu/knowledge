1. **Noun**: exclusion (U/C, pl: exclusions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of deliberately not including someone or something; the state of being excluded
   - *(Sense 2)* A thing that is excluded or not included in a category, contract, or insurance policy
   
   **Synonyms**: omission, rejection, barring, prohibition, elimination
   
   **Antonyms**: inclusion, incorporation, admission, acceptance, integration
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts when discussing social dynamics, policies, contracts, insurance terms, or systematic omissions. Common in sociology, legal documents, and policy discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When a softer term is needed for sensitive situations (consider "oversight" for accidents). Casual conversation may prefer "leaving someone out."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - social exclusion
   - exclusion zone
   - policy exclusions
   - systematic exclusion
   - grounds for exclusion
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + clus (close, shut) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin excludere (to shut out) → exclusio → Old French exclusion → English exclusion (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His exclusion from the group really hurt his feelings.
   - Check the policy exclusions before you file a claim.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Social exclusion contributes to health disparities among marginalized populations.
   - The contract specifies several exclusions from warranty coverage.

1. **Noun**: exception (C, pl: exceptions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that does not follow a general rule or pattern; something excluded from a general statement
   - *(Sense 2)* The action of excluding something from a general statement or rule
   
   **Synonyms**: anomaly, deviation, special case, irregularity, exemption
   
   **Antonyms**: rule, norm, standard, typical case
   
   **When to Use**: When pointing out cases that don't fit general patterns. Common in all registers—scientific discourse, legal writing, everyday conversation. Used in the phrase "with the exception of."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When no general rule has been established (the term presupposes a norm). Avoid overuse that weakens general statements.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - make an exception
   - with the exception of
   - without exception
   - prove the exception
   - exception to the rule
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + cept (take, seize) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin exceptio (act of taking out) → Old French exception → English exception (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone's coming to the party, with the exception of Mike.
   - Can you make an exception just this once?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data demonstrate a consistent pattern with few exceptions.
   - This case constitutes a notable exception to established precedent.

1. **Noun**: omission (C/U, pl: omissions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of not including something or failing to do something; something that has been left out
   - *(Sense 2)* (Legal) Failure to act when there is a duty to do so
   
   **Synonyms**: exclusion, oversight, deletion, neglect, leaving out
   
   **Antonyms**: inclusion, addition, insertion, incorporation
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and professional contexts when discussing mistakes, editorial decisions, legal duties, or gaps in accounts. More neutral than "exclusion" (can be accidental).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When deliberate exclusion needs emphasis (use "exclusion"). Casual speech may prefer "leaving something out."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - notable omission
   - sins of omission
   - omission from the record
   - significant omissions
   - error of omission
   
   **Root Analysis**: o- (variant of ob-, away) + miss (send) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin omittere (to let go, disregard) → omissio → Old French omission → English omission (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I noticed a few omissions in your report—you forgot to mention the budget.
   - That's a pretty big omission; how did you miss it?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report contains several significant omissions regarding environmental impacts.
   - Liability may arise from omissions as well as from positive acts.

1. **Noun**: exemption (C/U, pl: exemptions)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Official permission not to do or pay something that others must do or pay; the state of being exempt
   - *(Sense 2)* A person or type of income that is exempt from taxation
   
   **Synonyms**: immunity, exception, release, dispensation, waiver
   
   **Antonyms**: obligation, liability, requirement, duty
   
   **When to Use**: Legal, tax, and administrative contexts when discussing official releases from duties or payments. Common in tax law, military service discussions, and regulatory compliance.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual exclusions (too formal/legal). When no formal authority grants the release (use "excuse" or "exception").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tax exemption
   - claim an exemption
   - religious exemption
   - exemption status
   - qualify for exemption
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + empt (buy, take) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin exemptio (a taking out, removal) → Old French exemption → English exemption (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He got a medical exemption from gym class.
   - Do we qualify for any tax exemptions?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization has applied for 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status.
   - Religious exemptions from vaccination requirements remain controversial.

1. **Noun**: rejection (C/U, pl: rejections)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The act of refusing to accept, use, or believe something or someone; the state of being rejected
   - *(Sense 2)* Something or someone that has been rejected
   
   **Synonyms**: refusal, dismissal, denial, exclusion, rebuff
   
   **Antonyms**: acceptance, approval, inclusion, selection, embrace
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing active refusal or negative evaluation. Common in academic publishing, job applications, social relationships, and medical contexts (organ rejection). Stronger negative connotation than "exclusion."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When neutral exclusion is meant (use "exclusion" or "omission"). Be sensitive in interpersonal contexts as term carries emotional weight.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - face rejection
   - rejection letter
   - organ rejection
   - outright rejection
   - fear of rejection
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + ject (throw) + -ion (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin rejectio (a throwing back) → rejection (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I got another rejection from the publisher—so discouraging!
   - Nobody likes dealing with rejection, but it's part of life.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study examined factors predicting manuscript rejection in peer review.
   - Fear of social rejection significantly influences adolescent behavior patterns.

1. **Noun**: discrimination (U, rarely C in plural)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The unjust or prejudicial treatment of different categories of people, especially on grounds of race, age, sex, or disability; the act of excluding people unfairly
   - *(Sense 2)* (Positive sense, now rare) The ability to judge what is of good quality; discernment
   
   **Synonyms**: prejudice, bias, inequity, unfair treatment, exclusion
   
   **Antonyms**: equality, fairness, impartiality, equal treatment
   
   **When to Use**: Legal, social justice, and human rights contexts when discussing unfair treatment. Always negative in modern usage (Sense 1). Be specific about type (racial discrimination, age discrimination, etc.).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing legitimate differentiation (use "distinction" or "differentiation"). The older positive sense (discernment) is now archaic—use "discernment" instead.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - racial discrimination
   - face discrimination
   - discrimination lawsuit
   - reverse discrimination
   - employment discrimination
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart) + crimin (separate, distinguish) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin discriminare (to distinguish) → discriminatio → English discrimination (17th century, negative sense became dominant in 20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She faced discrimination at work because of her accent.
   - That's straight-up discrimination—you should report it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legislation prohibits discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation.
   - Evidence of systematic discrimination emerged from the employment data analysis.

1. **Noun**: segregation (U, occasionally C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action of setting someone or something apart from others; the enforced separation of different racial, ethnic, or social groups
   - *(Sense 2)* (Genetics) The separation of alleles during gamete formation
   
   **Synonyms**: separation, isolation, division, apartness, exclusion
   
   **Antonyms**: integration, inclusion, unity, desegregation, mixing
   
   **When to Use**: Historical and social contexts discussing forced separation by race, gender, or other characteristics. Also used in technical contexts (genetics, urban planning). Strong negative connotations from racial segregation history.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For voluntary or neutral separation (use "separation" or "division"). Casual use may sound inappropriately weighty given historical associations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - racial segregation
   - residential segregation
   - school segregation
   - gender segregation
   - de facto segregation
   
   **Root Analysis**: se- (apart) + greg (flock, group) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin segregare (to set apart from the flock) → segregatio → English segregation (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My grandparents lived through segregation in the South.
   - There's still a lot of residential segregation in this city.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Educational outcomes reflect patterns of residential segregation and resource inequality.
   - The study examined mechanisms of occupational segregation by gender.

1. **Noun**: isolation (U, occasionally C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The state of being alone or separated from others; the act of isolating something
   - *(Sense 2)* (Medicine) The separation of infected patients to prevent disease spread
   - *(Sense 3)* (Chemistry) The extraction of a substance in pure form
   
   **Synonyms**: separation, seclusion, solitude, detachment, quarantine
   
   **Antonyms**: connection, integration, community, contact, inclusion
   
   **When to Use**: Social, medical, geographical, and technical contexts. Can be neutral (medical isolation), negative (social isolation), or positive (peaceful isolation). Common in psychology, public health, and geography.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exclusion by others is meant (use "exclusion"). "Isolation" often implies separation from a group, sometimes self-imposed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - social isolation
   - in isolation (considered alone)
   - isolation ward
   - splendid isolation
   - geographic isolation
   
   **Root Analysis**: isol(a) (island) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Italian isolato (made into an island) → French isolation → English isolation (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Working from home can lead to feelings of isolation.
   - He lived in isolation in a cabin in the woods.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Social isolation among elderly populations constitutes a significant public health concern.
   - The policy was developed in isolation without stakeholder consultation.
