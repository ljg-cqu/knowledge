1. **Verb**: exclude
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To deliberately not include someone or something; to prevent someone from entering a place or taking part in an activity
   - *(Sense 2)* To decide that something is not possible or that something cannot happen
   
   **Synonyms**: omit, leave out, bar, shut out, rule out
   
   **Antonyms**: include, incorporate, admit, embrace, welcome
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and semi-formal contexts when discussing deliberate omissions, access restrictions, or eliminating possibilities. Common in academic, legal, policy, and administrative discourse.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When accidental omission occurred (use "overlook" or "miss"). Casual conversation may prefer "leave out."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exclude from consideration
   - exclude someone from something
   - exclude the possibility
   - deliberately exclude
   - systematically exclude
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + clud/clus (close, shut)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin excludere (to shut out, keep out) → Old French exclure → English exclude (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They excluded him from the group chat—pretty harsh.
   - Can we exclude dessert from the budget? We're running low.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study excluded participants with pre-existing conditions.
   - We cannot exclude the possibility of measurement error.

1. **Verb**: omit
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To leave out or fail to include something; to not do something that should be done
   - *(Sense 2)* (Formal) To fail to perform a duty or obligation
   
   **Synonyms**: leave out, exclude, skip, drop, neglect
   
   **Antonyms**: include, add, insert, incorporate
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and academic contexts when discussing what has been left out of documents, accounts, or actions. More neutral than "exclude" (can be accidental or deliberate).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When deliberate exclusion needs emphasis (use "exclude"). Very casual speech may prefer "leave out" or "skip."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - omit details
   - omit to mention
   - omit from the record
   - accidentally omit
   - deliberately omit
   
   **Root Analysis**: o- (variant of ob-, away) + mit (send, let go)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin omittere (to let go, disregard, neglect) → English omit (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think you omitted a few important facts from your story.
   - Don't omit the part about what happened next!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report omits critical information regarding environmental impacts.
   - Several key studies were omitted from the meta-analysis.

1. **Verb**: except
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive, formal/archaic) To exclude something or someone from a general statement or category
   - Note: Now rare as a verb; primarily used as preposition/conjunction
   
   **Synonyms**: exclude, exempt, leave out
   
   **Antonyms**: include, incorporate
   
   **When to Use**: Very formal or legal contexts, typically in passive constructions ("X is excepted from Y"). Modern usage almost always prefers "exclude" or uses "except" as preposition.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Modern general writing (archaic as verb). Use preposition form or verb "exclude" instead.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - be excepted from
   - present company excepted (fixed phrase)
   - excepted from the rule
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + cept (take, seize)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin excipere (to take out) → Old French excepter → English except (14th century as verb, now primarily preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare in casual speech; use preposition form or "exclude")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Certain categories of workers are excepted from overtime requirements.
   - Present company excepted, most people disagree with that view.

1. **Verb**: segregate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To separate people or things from a larger group and treat them differently, especially because of race, sex, or religion
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive, Genetics) (Of alleles) to separate during meiosis
   
   **Synonyms**: separate, isolate, set apart, divide, partition
   
   **Antonyms**: integrate, unite, combine, mix, desegregate
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing enforced separation, particularly in historical or social justice contexts. Strong negative connotations due to racial segregation history. Also technical use in genetics.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For neutral or voluntary separation (use "separate"). Casual use may sound inappropriately heavy given historical weight.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - racially segregate
   - segregate by gender
   - segregated facilities
   - deliberately segregate
   - historically segregated
   
   **Root Analysis**: se- (apart) + greg (flock, group) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin segregare (to set apart from the flock) → English segregate (16th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They used to segregate schools by race—that was so wrong.
   - Why are they segregating the groups this way?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Historical policies systematically segregated communities along racial lines.
   - The data were segregated by demographic characteristics for analysis.

1. **Verb**: isolate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To separate someone or something from other people or things; to cause to be alone
   - *(Sense 2)* (Medicine) To separate an infected person to prevent disease spread
   - *(Sense 3)* (Chemistry/Biology) To obtain a substance in pure form
   
   **Synonyms**: separate, set apart, quarantine, seclude, detach
   
   **Antonyms**: integrate, connect, unite, include, incorporate
   
   **When to Use**: Scientific, medical, social, and psychological contexts. Can be neutral (technical isolation), negative (social isolation), or positive (isolating problems for analysis).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exclusion by others is the focus (use "exclude"). "Isolate" often implies physical or analytical separation.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - isolate the problem
   - isolate a variable
   - feel isolated
   - isolate oneself
   - isolate the pathogen
   
   **Root Analysis**: isol(a) (island) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Italian isolato (made into an island) → Latin insulatus → English isolate (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tend to isolate myself when I'm stressed.
   - They isolated the leak to one specific pipe.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study isolated the effects of the intervention from confounding factors.
   - Infected patients must be isolated to prevent transmission.

1. **Verb**: exempt
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To officially allow someone not to do or pay something that others must do or pay; to grant freedom from an obligation
   
   **Synonyms**: excuse, release, free, spare, grant immunity
   
   **Antonyms**: require, obligate, subject to, hold liable
   
   **When to Use**: Legal, administrative, and tax contexts when granting official releases from obligations. Requires authority to grant the exemption. Formal register.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Informal favors (use "excuse" or "let off"). When no official authority exists to grant exemption.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exempt from tax
   - exempt someone from something
   - be exempted from
   - exempt from liability
   - exempt from requirements
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + empt (buy, take, remove)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin eximere (to take out, remove) → exemptus → Old French exempter → English exempt (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The teacher exempted me from the final exam since I had an A.
   - Can't we exempt ourselves from this rule just once?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The legislation exempts small businesses from certain reporting requirements.
   - Non-profit organizations are typically exempted from federal income taxation.

1. **Verb**: bar
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To prevent someone from entering a place or participating in something; to prohibit or forbid officially
   - *(Sense 2)* To fasten with a bar; to physically obstruct
   
   **Synonyms**: exclude, prohibit, ban, forbid, block
   
   **Antonyms**: allow, admit, permit, welcome, include
   
   **When to Use**: When describing official prohibitions or physical blocking. Common in legal contexts (barred from practicing), access control, and regulations. Implies authority to enforce the exclusion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual suggestions (too strong). When temporary exclusion is meant (use "exclude temporarily").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bar from entry
   - bar someone from something
   - legally barred
   - bar the door
   - bar from participation
   
   **Root Analysis**: From noun "bar" (barrier, obstruction)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French barre (barrier, rod) → English bar (noun and verb, 13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They barred him from the club after that incident.
   - Security barred us from entering without ID.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The court barred the defendant from contacting the plaintiff.
   - Participants were barred from discussing the study with others.

1. **Verb**: reject
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To refuse to accept, use, or believe something; to dismiss as inadequate or faulty
   - *(Sense 2)* To refuse to give someone love, care, or attention; to rebuff
   - *(Sense 3)* (Medicine) (Of the body) to fail to accept transplanted tissue or organ
   
   **Synonyms**: refuse, decline, turn down, dismiss, repudiate
   
   **Antonyms**: accept, approve, embrace, welcome, adopt
   
   **When to Use**: When describing active refusal or negative evaluation. Common in academic publishing, job applications, decision-making, and medical contexts. Stronger and more decisive than "exclude."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When neutral exclusion is appropriate (use "exclude" or "not select"). Be sensitive in personal contexts due to emotional weight.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reject an application
   - reject a proposal
   - reject the hypothesis
   - outright reject
   - reject the offer
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + ject (throw)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reicere/rejicere (to throw back) → English reject (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They rejected my application without even an interview.
   - Don't reject the idea before you've heard the whole plan.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The peer reviewers rejected the manuscript due to methodological flaws.
   - The null hypothesis was rejected at the p < 0.05 significance level.
