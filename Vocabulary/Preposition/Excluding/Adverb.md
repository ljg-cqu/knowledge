1. **Adverb**: exclusively (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that excludes others; solely and only for a particular person or purpose.
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence or end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: solely, only, purely, singularly, uniquely
   
   **Antonyms**: inclusively, jointly, collectively, broadly
   
   **When to Use**: When emphasizing that something applies to one specific thing/person and nothing/nobody else. Common in business, legal, and social contexts to emphasize restriction or specificity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when inclusivity is important. Don't overuse for emphasis as it can sound pretentious in casual conversation.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - focus exclusively on
   - available exclusively
   - work exclusively with
   - deal exclusively in
   - exclusively for members
   
   **Root Analysis**: exclusive (ex- + clud + -ive) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin excludere (to shut out) → exclusive → exclusively (18th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's been focusing exclusively on his music career lately.
   - This offer is exclusively for our loyal customers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team works exclusively with publicly available datasets.
   - The funding will be allocated exclusively to environmental conservation projects.

1. **Adverb**: exceptionally (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To an unusual degree; extremely or remarkably.
   - *(Sense 2)* In a way that forms an exception; unusually.
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence before adjective/adverb, or sentence-initial for emphasis
   
   **Synonyms**: extremely, remarkably, extraordinarily, unusually, outstandingly
   
   **Antonyms**: ordinarily, typically, normally, averagely, moderately
   
   **When to Use**: When intensifying positive qualities or emphasizing that something deviates significantly from the norm. Common in praise, performance reviews, and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overuse as an intensifier (loses impact). Don't use with negative qualities unless emphasizing severity ("exceptionally bad" sounds formal/clinical).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exceptionally well
   - exceptionally talented
   - exceptionally high
   - exceptionally rare
   - perform exceptionally
   
   **Root Analysis**: exceptional (ex- + cept + -ion + -al) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin exceptio (exception) → exceptional → exceptionally (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's exceptionally good at problem-solving—best on our team.
   - The weather has been exceptionally nice this week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The candidate performed exceptionally well across all assessment criteria.
   - This intervention has proven exceptionally effective in controlled studies.

1. **Adverb**: selectively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that involves careful choosing; applying only to certain things and not others.
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence or end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: carefully, discriminatingly, specifically, particularly, deliberately
   
   **Antonyms**: indiscriminately, randomly, universally, broadly, comprehensively
   
   **When to Use**: When describing targeted or careful selection processes. Common in scientific, technical, and strategic contexts. Neutral or positive connotation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when implying unfair bias (use "discriminatorily" instead). Don't use when complete coverage is implied.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - selectively choose
   - apply selectively
   - breed selectively
   - listen selectively
   - enforce selectively
   
   **Root Analysis**: selective (se- + lect + -ive) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin selectus (chosen) → selective → selectively (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I selectively ignore advice that doesn't make sense to me.
   - She reads the news selectively to avoid getting overwhelmed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The intervention was selectively applied to high-risk populations.
   - Resources must be allocated selectively to maximize impact.

1. **Adverb**: discriminatorily (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that shows prejudice or unfair treatment toward different categories of people or things.
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence or end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: unfairly, prejudicially, partially, unjustly, biasedly
   
   **Antonyms**: fairly, impartially, equitably, justly, even-handedly
   
   **When to Use**: In legal, academic, or social justice contexts when criticizing unfair practices. Always carries negative connotation in modern usage.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for neutral differentiation. Avoid in casual conversation (sounds formal/legalistic). Not appropriate for describing legitimate selection criteria.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - act discriminatorily
   - applied discriminatorily
   - discriminatorily enforced
   - operate discriminatorily
   - treat discriminatorily
   
   **Root Analysis**: discriminatory (dis- + crimin + -ate + -ory) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin discriminare (to distinguish) → discriminatory → discriminatorily (20th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're applying the rules discriminatorily—some people get away with it, others don't.
   - The policy has been enforced discriminatorily against certain groups.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The evidence suggests that the regulation was applied discriminatorily.
   - Practices that operate discriminatorily violate equal protection principles.

1. **Adverb**: restrictively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that imposes limitations or controls; preventing freedom or flexibility.
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence or end-position
   
   **Synonyms**: limitingly, constrainingly, narrowly, rigidly, strictly
   
   **Antonyms**: liberally, broadly, permissively, flexibly, generously
   
   **When to Use**: When describing the manner in which rules, policies, or interpretations limit freedom or options. Can be neutral (technical description) or critical.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when describing reasonable safety measures (sounds critical). Not common in casual conversation (sounds formal).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - interpret restrictively
   - applied restrictively
   - define restrictively
   - operate restrictively
   - regulate restrictively
   
   **Root Analysis**: restrictive (re- + strict + -ive) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin restrictus (bound back) → restrictive → restrictively (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're interpreting the rules way too restrictively if you ask me.
   - The policy is being applied restrictively in some departments.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The statute has been interpreted restrictively by lower courts.
   - When regulations are applied restrictively, innovation may be stifled.

1. **Adverb**: separately (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Apart from something else; individually rather than together.
   - *(Sense 2)* In a way that involves separation or distance.
   - *Position*: typically end-position, sometimes sentence-initial for emphasis
   
   **Synonyms**: individually, independently, apart, discretely, distinctly
   
   **Antonyms**: together, jointly, collectively, unitedly, combined
   
   **When to Use**: When describing things done or considered individually rather than as a group. Common in instructions, analysis, and organizational contexts. Neutral tone.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when unity is important or when separation has negative connotations (use "independently" for positive frame).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sold separately
   - charged separately
   - consider separately
   - packaged separately
   - treated separately
   
   **Root Analysis**: separate (se- + par + -ate) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin separatus (set apart) → separate → separately (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The batteries are sold separately, not included in the box.
   - Can we talk about each issue separately instead of all at once?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Each variable must be analyzed separately to isolate its effects.
   - The components are packaged separately to prevent contamination.

1. **Adverb**: partially (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To a limited extent; not completely or entirely.
   - *Position*: typically mid-sentence before verb/adjective, or sentence-initial
   
   **Synonyms**: partly, incompletely, somewhat, to some extent, in part
   
   **Antonyms**: completely, fully, entirely, totally, wholly
   
   **When to Use**: When indicating incomplete application, coverage, or truth. Common in academic, technical, and explanatory contexts. Neutral or slightly negative tone.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when complete success/coverage is claimed. Don't use when binary states (yes/no) are more appropriate than degrees.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - partially correct
   - partially completed
   - partially funded
   - partially responsible
   - only partially
   
   **Root Analysis**: partial (part + -ial) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin partialis (relating to a part) → partial → partially (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm only partially done with my homework—still have math left.
   - The problem is partially fixed, but there are still some bugs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hypothesis was partially supported by the experimental data.
   - Funding for the project has been partially secured through grants.
