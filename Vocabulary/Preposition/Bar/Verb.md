1. **Verb**: bar
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To officially or physically prevent someone from entering, doing something, or having something; to exclude or forbid
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To block a path or opening with a bar or similar object
   
   **Synonyms**: block, prohibit, forbid, exclude, shut out, ban
   
   **Antonyms**: allow, admit, permit, enable, grant access
   
   **When to Use**: Use *bar* when emphasizing strong prevention or exclusion, often by rule or physical obstacle: *bar someone from entry, bar access, bar discussion of a topic*.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech where *"stop"* or *"keep out"* sounds more natural; do not confuse with *"bar"* as a noun (*pub* or *rod*).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - bar someone from (doing) something
   - be barred from entry
   - bar access to
   - bar the door / bar the gate
   
   **Root Analysis**: bar (rod, barrier) → verb *bar* (to put a bar across; to obstruct)
   
   **Etymology**: From noun *bar* "rod, barrier"; verbal use *"to bar"* meaning "to fasten with a bar" is recorded from Middle English, later extended to legal and abstract exclusion.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They barred him from coming back to the club.
   - We had to bar the door during the storm.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Applicants with unresolved compliance issues are barred from participating in the program.
   - Security protocols bar remote access from unregistered devices.

1. **Verb**: block
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To stop something from moving through or along something; to obstruct
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To prevent something from happening or succeeding
   
   **Synonyms**: obstruct, bar, hinder, stop, choke, clog
   
   **Antonyms**: clear, unblock, enable, facilitate, open
   
   **When to Use**: Use *block* for both physical and figurative contexts: *block the road, block access, block a proposal, block a request*.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid for formal legal prohibitions (*ban, prohibit* may be more precise); do not use where simple *delay* or *slow* is meant.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - block the view
   - block the road/entrance
   - block a plan/decision
   - block a user/account
   
   **Root Analysis**: block (solid piece, obstruction) → verb meaning "to stop or obstruct"
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *bloc* (log, block of wood) → verb sense "obstruct, make into a block" in English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A parked truck was blocking the exit.
   - If he keeps spamming, just block him.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal was blocked by the supervisory board.
   - Fire doors must not be blocked by equipment or storage.

1. **Verb**: obstruct
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To block a road, passage, or view so that people or things cannot move or be seen easily
   - *(Sense 2)* (Transitive) To make it difficult for something to happen or develop
   
   **Synonyms**: block, impede, hinder, bar, interfere with
   
   **Antonyms**: clear, facilitate, aid, ease, assist
   
   **When to Use**: Use *obstruct* in more formal or technical contexts when something *gets in the way* of movement, visibility, or progress (e.g. *obstruct justice, obstruct airflow, obstruct negotiations*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech where *block* is simpler; do not use when you only mean *"to disagree"* or *"to critique"*.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - obstruct the view
   - obstruct justice
   - obstruct progress
   - obstruct an investigation
   
   **Root Analysis**: ob- (against) + struct (build, from Latin *struere* "to pile up")
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *obstruere* (to build up against, block) → Latin *obstructus* → English *obstruct*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That tree branch is obstructing the sign.
   - I don't want to obstruct you—tell me if I'm in the way.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Any attempt to obstruct the investigation will lead to disciplinary action.
   - The new development must not obstruct established drainage channels.

1. **Verb**: forbid
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To order someone not to do something; to say that something must not happen
   
   **Synonyms**: prohibit, ban, bar, disallow, veto
   
   **Antonyms**: allow, permit, authorize, sanction, approve
   
   **When to Use**: Use *forbid* when someone in authority explicitly *bars* an action: parents forbidding children to do something, rules forbidding smoking, laws forbidding certain conduct.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Sounds strong or old‑fashioned in some casual contexts; for softer discouragement use *discourage* or *advise against*.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - forbid someone to do something
   - be strictly forbidden
   - rules forbid
   - the law forbids
   
   **Root Analysis**: for- (completely) + bid (command, from Old English *beodan* "to command")
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *forbeodan* (to prohibit) → modern *forbid*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My parents forbade me to stay out that late.
   - The school forbids phones in class.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The constitution forbids the enactment of such discriminatory laws.
   - Company policy strictly forbids unauthorized access to client data.

1. **Verb**: preclude
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To prevent something from happening or make it impossible; to rule out in advance
   
   **Synonyms**: prevent, rule out, bar, exclude, forestall, obviate
   
   **Antonyms**: allow, enable, permit, admit, leave open
   
   **When to Use**: Use *preclude* in formal or academic writing when emphasizing that circumstances or prior decisions *close off* certain possibilities (e.g. *preclude participation, preclude the need for*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for most everyday conversation; in speech, *"rule out"* or *"make impossible"* is often clearer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - preclude the possibility of
   - preclude someone from doing something
   - does not preclude
   - effectively preclude
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + clud (close, from Latin *claudere* "to shut")
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *praecludere* (to close off beforehand) → English *preclude*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A tight budget might preclude us from taking that trip.
   - His injury precludes him from playing this season.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The small sample size precludes definitive conclusions.
   - Prior commitments preclude her participation in the current phase of the project.

1. **Verb**: exclude
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To deliberately keep someone or something out; to prevent someone from taking part
   - *(Sense 2)* To leave something out of consideration or calculation
   
   **Synonyms**: bar, leave out, shut out, omit, rule out
   
   **Antonyms**: include, admit, welcome, incorporate, count
   
   **When to Use**: Use *exclude* when talking about who or what is *kept outside* a group, calculation, or discussion: *exclude from school, exclude from analysis, exclude certain variables*.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when non‑deliberate absence is meant (then use *"miss"* or *"not include"*). For social situations, be sensitive: *exclude* often implies unfairness.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exclude from
   - systematically exclude
   - explicitly exclude
   - exclude the possibility
   - feel excluded
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + clud (close, from Latin *claudere* "to shut")
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *excludere* (to shut out) → English *exclude*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I hate feeling excluded from the conversation.
   - They excluded one weird result from the graph.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study excluded participants who did not provide informed consent.
   - The revised criteria explicitly exclude short‑term contracts from coverage.
