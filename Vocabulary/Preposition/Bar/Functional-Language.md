1. **Functional Expression**: Bar unforeseen circumstances, we will start on time (function: hedging/forecasting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Announces a plan or forecast while explicitly carving out a small exception for truly unexpected events
   - *Formality*: formal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect hedge (states commitment but leaves a narrow escape route)
   
   **Synonyms**: Unless something unexpected happens, Assuming nothing unusual occurs, Provided there are no unforeseen issues, If all goes to plan
   
   **Antonyms**: We will start on time no matter what, We guarantee an on‑time start under all conditions
   
   **When to Use**: Use in professional updates, project planning, or public announcements when you want to show confidence while acknowledging that rare *bars* (unexpected obstacles) could still appear.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual speech ("unless something comes up" is simpler); avoid when stakeholders need an unconditional guarantee.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Bar any delays, we should finish by Friday.
   - Bar unexpected setbacks, the release will proceed as scheduled.
   - Bar major objections, the proposal will be approved.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses prepositional *bar* to frame a small exception space; sounds concise and slightly formal, common in British English and written updates.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "That sounds reasonable" / "Understood—barring surprises."
   - Probing: "What would count as an unforeseen circumstance?" / "How likely are such delays?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Bar any emergencies, I'll be there by seven.
   - Bar something weird happening, the game should go ahead.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Bar unforeseen circumstances, the migration will complete over the weekend.
   - Bar any significant objections from regulators, the transaction will close this quarter.

1. **Functional Expression**: Bar a few minor issues, everything went smoothly (function: qualifying/softening)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Gives an overall positive evaluation while acknowledging small exceptions
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: balanced—clear praise with honest caveats
   
   **Synonyms**: Apart from a few minor issues, With a few small exceptions, Overall it went smoothly, aside from a couple of glitches
   
   **Antonyms**: Everything went perfectly, There were major problems throughout
   
   **When to Use**: Use in debriefs, status updates, and reviews when you want to highlight success but still mention small bars (glitches, frictions) that learners should notice.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid if issues were major or systemic; then *"despite serious problems"* or *"although we hit major obstacles"* is more honest.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Bar a couple of delays, the rollout was smooth.
   - Bar one or two bugs, the release went well.
   - Bar a few edge cases, the model behaved as expected.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Frames problems as exceptions to a positive rule; helps maintain morale while still signalling areas to improve.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Curious: "What were the minor issues?" / "Anything we should fix for next time?"
   - Reassuring: "Glad to hear it was smooth overall."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Bar a few hiccups with the sound system, the event was great.
   - Bar a couple of typos, your report looks solid.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Bar a few minor configuration problems, the deployment proceeded as planned.
   - Bar isolated anomalies, the data align with prior expectations.

1. **Functional Expression**: Bar none, this is our best option (function: emphasizing/judging)
   **Meaning**: 
   - States that something is the best (or worst, strongest, etc.) with no exceptions at all
   - *Formality*: neutral, slightly idiomatic
   - *Directness*: very direct and emphatic
   
   **Synonyms**: without exception, by far, hands down, unquestionably the best
   
   **Antonyms**: one of several good options, not necessarily the best, comparable to others
   
   **When to Use**: Use *bar none* after a strong evaluative statement when you want to say it applies to *every* comparison case (*the toughest exam I've taken, bar none*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in careful academic writing where such absolute claims are rarely provable; avoid if you want to leave room for debate.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - the toughest challenge I've faced, bar none
   - she's the most reliable teammate we have, bar none
   - bar none, that's the clearest explanation I've seen
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Functions as a conversational intensifier; uses the preposition *bar* literally (*except*) plus *none* to say *"except none"*.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreeing: "Totally, I feel the same" / "I can't think of anything better."
   - Softening: "It's great, but I'm not sure I'd say bar none."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's the best burger in town, bar none.
   - She's the nicest manager I've worked with, bar none.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This remains, bar none, the most comprehensive dataset currently available.
   - In terms of risk reduction, the proposed design is, bar none, the strongest candidate.

1. **Functional Expression**: All bar one of us agreed (function: reporting outcomes)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Summarizes a vote or opinion as nearly unanimous, with exactly one exception
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct but concise
   
   **Synonyms**: Everyone except one agreed, All but one of us agreed, There was one dissenting voice
   
   **Antonyms**: The group was split, Only a few agreed, Opinion was evenly divided
   
   **When to Use**: Use in minutes, summaries, or narratives when you want to highlight both strong consensus and the fact that one person was barred from the majority position (or chose to stand apart).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid if highlighting the lone dissenter might expose them unnecessarily; in sensitive contexts, anonymize or shift focus.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - All bar one voted in favour.
   - All bar one department met the deadline.
   - Targets were reached in all bar one market.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: *All bar one* is compact and slightly formal; often used in journalistic or analytic styles.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Clarifying: "Who was the one?" / "What were their reasons?"
   - Summarizing: "So basically everyone agreed."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - All bar one of us wanted pizza.
   - The talks were smooth—everyone bar one person was on board.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The motion carried, with all bar one member voting in support.
   - All bar one of the proposed milestones were accepted without modification.

1. **Functional Expression**: Bar that one issue, it's solid (function: balancing praise and criticism)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Signals that apart from a single problem, overall quality is good
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct but mitigated by strong positive frame
   
   **Synonyms**: Except for that one issue, Overall it's solid, With that single caveat, it's strong
   
   **Antonyms**: That issue ruins the whole thing, It's full of problems, It's not solid at all
   
   **When to Use**: Use in feedback conversations or reviews where you want to encourage someone while clearly pointing to a specific bar (blocking problem) to fix.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: If there are multiple serious issues; then *"several major issues"* should be named. Avoid if the "one issue" is actually critical enough to make it *not* solid.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Bar that detail, the argument holds up.
   - Bar that section, the draft is in good shape.
   - Bar that risk, the plan looks sound.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The structure *"Bar X, Y"* puts the exception first, then reaffirms the positive evaluation; useful for concise, balanced feedback.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Curious: "Which issue exactly?" / "What should I fix first?"
   - Reassured: "Glad it's solid overall—I'll work on that part."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Bar that one typo on the front page, your CV looks great.
   - Bar the ending, the movie was solid.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Bar that methodological limitation, the study is well designed.
   - Bar the unresolved dependency, the proposal appears robust.
