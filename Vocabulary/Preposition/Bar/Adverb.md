1. **Adverb**: strictly (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that must be obeyed completely; with no exceptions allowed
   - *(Sense 2)* Exactly or only, in a narrow or precise sense
   
   **Synonyms**: rigidly, rigorously, firmly, severely, exactly
   
   **Antonyms**: loosely, leniently, flexibly, broadly
   
   **When to Use**: Use *strictly* to emphasize that rules or conditions *bar* any exceptions (*strictly prohibited, strictly no refunds*), or that a statement is true only in a narrow sense (*strictly speaking*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you want to suggest flexibility or openness; overuse can make communication sound harsh.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - strictly forbidden
   - strictly limited to
   - strictly no...
   - strictly speaking
   
   **Root Analysis**: strict (tight, rigid) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *strictus* (drawn tight) → English *strict* → *strictly* (rigidly, with no loosening).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Smoking is strictly forbidden in here.
   - Strictly speaking, it's not a bug, it's a feature.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Access to the archive is strictly controlled.
   - Participation is strictly limited to pre‑registered attendees.

1. **Adverb**: categorically (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a very clear and direct way that leaves no doubt or exception; absolutely
   
   **Synonyms**: absolutely, unequivocally, flatly, unconditionally, decisively
   
   **Antonyms**: tentatively, conditionally, partially, ambiguously
   
   **When to Use**: Use *categorically* with verbs of denying or rejecting (e.g. *categorically deny, categorically refuse*) when you want to draw a hard bar against a claim or possibility.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for most casual talk; avoid when nuance or open‑mindedness is needed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - categorically deny
   - categorically reject
   - categorically rule out
   - categorically state
   
   **Root Analysis**: category (class, type) + -ical (relating to) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Greek *katēgorein* (to assert, accuse) → *category* → *categorical* → *categorically* (without qualification).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I categorically refuse to do that.
   - He categorically denied touching the settings.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The organization categorically denies all allegations of misconduct.
   - Current evidence allows us to categorically rule out several alternative explanations.

1. **Adverb**: absolutely (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Completely; totally; used for emphasis
   - *(Sense 2)* In a way that is not limited by conditions or exceptions
   
   **Synonyms**: completely, totally, entirely, utterly, wholly
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, somewhat, partly, relatively
   
   **When to Use**: Use *absolutely* to strengthen statements that imply strong bars or limits (*absolutely no smoking, absolutely impossible, absolutely essential*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Overuse in casual speech can weaken its force; avoid in precise academic writing when a more measured adverb (*"highly, strongly"*) is appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - absolutely no...
   - absolutely necessary
   - absolutely essential
   - absolutely impossible
   
   **Root Analysis**: absolute (complete, not limited) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *absolutus* (set free, complete) → *absolute* → *absolutely*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's absolutely no way I'm doing that.
   - This rule is absolutely non‑negotiable.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - There is absolutely no provision for late submissions.
   - Data integrity is absolutely critical to the validity of the results.

1. **Adverb**: exclusively (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Only; and not shared with others; to the exclusion of all else
   
   **Synonyms**: solely, only, purely, entirely, just
   
   **Antonyms**: inclusively, jointly, partly, additionally
   
   **When to Use**: Use *exclusively* when something is reserved for one group or purpose, or when a resource is *barred* to others (e.g. *exclusively for members, used exclusively for research*).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when you actually mean *"mainly"* or *"primarily"*; *exclusively* means *"with no other"*.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - exclusively for
   - focus exclusively on
   - available exclusively to
   - used exclusively in
   
   **Root Analysis**: exclusive (shutting others out) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *excludere* (shut out) → *exclusive* → *exclusively*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This area is exclusively for staff.
   - I shop almost exclusively online now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The grant may be used exclusively for approved project activities.
   - Access to this dataset is reserved exclusively for authorized researchers.

1. **Adverb**: solely (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Only; not involving or including anyone or anything else
   
   **Synonyms**: only, exclusively, purely, entirely, alone
   
   **Antonyms**: jointly, partly, partially, together, collectively
   
   **When to Use**: Use *solely* to emphasize that responsibility, decision‑making power, or focus is placed on a single person, factor, or purpose (*solely responsible, based solely on*). It often highlights a bar against considering other influences.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when multiple causes or agents are involved; in those cases, *primarily* or *largely* may be more accurate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - solely responsible for
   - based solely on
   - depend solely on
   - used solely for
   
   **Root Analysis**: sole (only, single) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *solus* (alone) → Old French *soul* → English *sole* → *solely*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm solely responsible for booking the flights.
   - The delay wasn't solely his fault.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Admission decisions are based solely on academic merit and documented potential.
   - The interpretation should not rest solely on preliminary correlation analyses.
