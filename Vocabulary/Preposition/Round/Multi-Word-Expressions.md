1. **Expression**: go round in circles (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To keep discussing the same things without making any progress or reaching a decision
   - *Literal meaning*: Moving in circular paths; *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Making no progress in discussion or action
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: make no progress, get nowhere, spin one's wheels, go around in circles, repeat oneself
   
   **Antonyms**: make progress, move forward, advance, reach a decision, break through
   
   **When to Use**: Describing unproductive discussions, repetitive thinking, or stalled decision-making; frustration with lack of progress; casual to semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing productive iteration or refinement; in contexts where "circling back" is intentional and valuable; when literal circular motion is meant
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - going around in circles (American English variant)
   - running in circles
   - talking in circles
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Metaphor from physical circular motion that returns to starting point without forward progress; visual image of futility; dates to 19th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "gan" (go) + "round" (circular) + "in circles" (circular paths)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We've been going round in circles for an hour—let's just make a decision!
   - I feel like I'm going round in circles with this problem.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee has been going round in circles without reaching consensus.
   - To avoid going round in circles, I propose we establish clear decision criteria.

1. **Expression**: round of applause (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A period of clapping by a group of people to show approval or appreciation
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: applause, ovation, clapping, hand (give a big hand), acclamation
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, booing, jeering, disapproval
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and informal events; performances, speeches, achievements; suggesting appreciation; universal across cultures
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In written descriptions unless reporting actual applause; when other forms of appreciation are more appropriate (standing ovation is stronger)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - big round of applause
   - warm round of applause
   - huge round of applause
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Round" suggests complete circle/group participation; conventional usage dating to 18th century; high co-occurrence frequency in performance contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: "round" (complete, circular) + "of" (partitive) + "applause" (clapping)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's give her a big round of applause for all her hard work!
   - The performance ended with a huge round of applause.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please join me in a round of applause for our keynote speaker.
   - The presentation concluded to a warm round of applause from the audience.

1. **Expression**: round the clock (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - All day and all night without stopping; continuously for 24 hours
   - *Literal meaning*: Circling the clock face; *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Continuous operation
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed)
   
   **Synonyms**: 24/7, around the clock, continuously, non-stop, all day and night
   
   **Antonyms**: part-time, occasionally, during business hours, intermittently
   
   **When to Use**: Describing continuous operations, services, or work; emphasizing dedication or availability; business, healthcare, emergency services contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When operations actually have breaks or limited hours; when exaggeration would be misleading
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - around the clock (more common in American English)
   - 24/7 (modern equivalent)
   - round-the-clock (hyphenated adjective form)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Clock metaphor represents complete 24-hour cycle; originated in mid-20th century with increasing industrialization and shift work
   
   **Root Analysis**: "round" (complete cycle) + "the clock" (24-hour period)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The hospital has nurses working round the clock.
   - We've been working round the clock to finish this project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility provides round-the-clock security monitoring.
   - Medical staff worked round the clock during the crisis.

1. **Expression**: round trip (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A journey to a place and back again, typically by the same route
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed, especially in transportation contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: return journey, return trip, there and back, two-way trip
   
   **Antonyms**: one-way trip, single journey, outbound only
   
   **When to Use**: Transportation contexts (flights, trains, buses); travel planning; ticket purchasing; indicating complete journey
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing only one direction of travel; when multiple stops make "round" inaccurate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - round-trip ticket (hyphenated adjective form)
   - return trip (British English equivalent)
   - roundtrip (sometimes one word in American English)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Round" indicates circular completion back to origin; standard transportation industry term; conventional usage since early 20th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: "round" (returning to starting point) + "trip" (journey)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - How much is a round trip to Boston?
   - The round trip takes about 6 hours total.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please book a round-trip ticket departing Monday and returning Friday.
   - The round-trip distance is approximately 500 kilometers.

1. **Expression**: do the rounds (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (of news, jokes, illness) To be passed from person to person; to circulate
   - *(Sense 2)* (of a person) To make a series of visits or inspections
   - *Fixedness*: strong
   
   **Synonyms**: circulate, spread, go around, make the rounds, be passed around
   
   **Antonyms**: remain private, stay contained, be kept secret
   
   **When to Use**: Describing information spread (gossip, news, jokes); describing inspection tours (doctor, manager, security); British and American English
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For one-time communications; when spread is controlled or official; when literal circular motion is meant
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - make the rounds (American English preference)
   - going the rounds (present progressive)
   - did the rounds (past tense)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Rounds" from medical practice of doctor visiting each patient; extended to mean any systematic visiting or circulation; 18th-century origin
   
   **Root Analysis**: "do" (perform) + "the rounds" (circular visits)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That joke has been doing the rounds at the office all week.
   - There's a nasty flu doing the rounds right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The rumor did the rounds before official confirmation was issued.
   - The chief executive makes the rounds of regional offices quarterly.

1. **Expression**: round up (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To gather people or animals together in one place
   - *(Sense 2)* To increase a number to the nearest whole number or convenient figure
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed verb-particle combination)
   
   **Synonyms**: gather, assemble, collect, bring together (Sense 1); raise to, approximate upward (Sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: scatter, disperse, separate (Sense 1); round down, lower (Sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Gathering people for meetings or activities (Sense 1); mathematical approximations upward (Sense 2); casual to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal writing (use "assemble" for Sense 1); when exact figures required (Sense 2); when "round up" has negative law enforcement connotations
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - round someone/something up
   - rounding up
   - rounded up
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Originally from American West cattle herding; "up" intensifies gathering action; extended to numbers (upward adjustment); high frequency in informal contexts
   
   **Root Analysis**: "round" (gather in circular formation) + "up" (completive particle)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you help me round up the kids for dinner?
   - Let's round this number up to 100 to make calculations easier.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Please assemble the team members in the conference room (preferred formal alternative for Sense 1).
   - The figure was rounded up to the nearest thousand for reporting purposes.

1. **Expression**: round down (type: phrasal verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To decrease a number to the nearest whole number or convenient figure below the actual value
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed verb-particle combination)
   
   **Synonyms**: decrease to nearest, reduce to, approximate downward, lower to
   
   **Antonyms**: round up, increase to, raise to
   
   **When to Use**: Mathematical and financial contexts; when lower estimate is conservative or safer; accounting, statistics, everyday calculations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact precision is required; in legal contexts requiring exact figures; when rounding up is mandated
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - round something down
   - rounding down
   - rounded down to
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Mathematical term; "down" indicates downward adjustment; conventional usage in accounting and mathematics; complements "round up"
   
   **Root Analysis**: "round" (adjust to convenient figure) + "down" (directional decrease)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If you round down 4.7 to the nearest whole number, you get 4.
   - Let's round down the estimate to be on the safe side.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Values were rounded down to two decimal places for consistency.
   - The calculation requires rounding down to the nearest integer.

1. **Expression**: round of drinks (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A serving of drinks bought for all members of a group at the same time
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed in social drinking contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: drinks for everyone, a set of drinks, drinks all around
   
   **Antonyms**: individual drink, one drink, separate tabs
   
   **When to Use**: Social drinking contexts (pubs, bars, parties); offering to buy drinks for a group; British and Irish culture especially; reciprocal social practice
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In professional settings where alcohol is inappropriate; when individuals prefer to pay separately; in formal written contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - buy a round (verb phrase)
   - it's my round (possessive, indicates whose turn)
   - another round (requesting repeat)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Round" implies each person in circle receives one; British pub culture convention; reciprocal social obligation; high frequency co-occurrence
   
   **Root Analysis**: "round" (complete circle of people) + "of drinks" (drinks for all)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's my turn—I'll get the next round of drinks.
   - He bought a round of drinks to celebrate his promotion.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts*
   - Refreshments were provided for all attendees (formal equivalent).

1. **Expression**: roundabout way (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An indirect method of saying or doing something; not straightforward
   - *Fixedness*: medium to strong
   
   **Synonyms**: indirect way, circuitous route, indirect method, oblique approach
   
   **Antonyms**: direct way, straightforward manner, blunt approach, direct method
   
   **When to Use**: Describing indirect communication or methods; criticizing lack of directness; explaining diplomatic or tactful approaches; neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When praising efficient methods; when directness is the goal; in cultures strongly valuing indirect communication (may be seen as criticism)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - in a roundabout way (most common prepositional phrase)
   - through a roundabout way
   - by a roundabout route
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Roundabout" (literally: circular route) metaphorically represents indirect approach; conventional pairing with "way/manner"; medium-high co-occurrence
   
   **Root Analysis**: "roundabout" (circular, indirect) + "way" (method, manner)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She told me in a roundabout way that she didn't want to go.
   - Stop being so roundabout—just say what you mean!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The information was conveyed in a roundabout manner through intermediaries.
   - Rather than direct inquiry, he approached the matter in a roundabout way.

1. **Expression**: all year round (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Throughout the entire year; in every season without interruption
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed temporal expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: year-round, throughout the year, all year long, twelve months a year, continuously
   
   **Antonyms**: seasonally, part of the year, periodically, occasionally
   
   **When to Use**: Describing continuous operations, availability, or conditions across all seasons; tourism, business, climate descriptions; neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For seasonal activities; when significant seasonal variation exists; when intermittent operation is accurate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - year-round (hyphenated adjective form)
   - round the year (less common)
   - all the year round (British English, more formal)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "All year" (complete annual period) + "round" (circular, continuous cycle); emphasizes completeness; conventional phrase since 19th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: "all" (complete) + "year" (annual period) + "round" (circular cycle)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The beach is beautiful all year round, even in winter.
   - They keep the pool heated so you can swim all year round.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The resort offers all-year-round accommodation and activities.
   - This region experiences mild temperatures all year round.
