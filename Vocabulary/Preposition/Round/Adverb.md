1. **Adverb**: around (type: manner/place)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a circular direction or position; on all sides; encircling.
   - *(Sense 2)* Approximately; about (used with numbers, times, amounts).
   - *(Sense 3)* In or to various places; here and there.
   - *Can appear in front, mid, or end position depending on meaning*
   
   **Synonyms**: round, about, approximately, roughly, surrounding
   
   **Antonyms**: precisely, exactly, directly, straight
   
   **When to Use**: For circular movement, approximate quantities, general location, or scattered distribution. Use in both casual and formal contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when precision is required (use "exactly" instead). Don't use for linear movement (use "along" or "through").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - walk around
   - turn around
   - around here
   - around noon
   - look around
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (prefix: on, in) + round (circular)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "around" from "a-" + "round" (14th century). Originally meant "in a circle," extended to mean "approximately" or "in the vicinity."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's meet around 3 o'clock, give or take.
   - I looked around but couldn't find my keys anywhere.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conference will commence around mid-morning.
   - Participants were distributed around the perimeter of the facility.

1. **Adverb**: round (type: manner/direction)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a circular direction or course; so as to rotate or revolve.
   - *(Sense 2)* To or at a particular place, especially someone's home (British English).
   - *(Sense 3)* On all sides; surrounding.
   - *Primarily end position; some mid-position uses*
   
   **Synonyms**: around, about, circularly, in a circle
   
   **Antonyms**: straight, directly, linearly
   
   **When to Use**: For circular or rotating motion, especially in British English. Common with verbs of motion and change.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In American English, "around" is generally preferred. Avoid when describing linear or direct movement.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - turn round
   - spin round
   - come round
   - go round
   - all year round
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (from Old English "rund" = circular)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "rund" → Middle English "round" (used as adverb from 13th century). Closely related to "around" but more common in British English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Spin round three times and point!
   - Come round to my place later.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility operates round the clock throughout the year.
   - Please turn round to face the audience.

1. **Adverb**: roundly (type: manner/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a thorough, vigorous, or emphatic manner; severely or completely.
   - *(Sense 2)* In a circular or rounded manner (less common).
   - *Typically mid or end position*
   
   **Synonyms**: thoroughly, severely, emphatically, vigorously, completely
   
   **Antonyms**: mildly, partially, gently, incompletely
   
   **When to Use**: For emphasis when describing criticism, condemnation, defeat, or rejection. More common in formal or written contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (may sound overly formal). Don't use for literal circular motion (use "in circles" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - roundly criticized
   - roundly condemned
   - roundly defeated
   - roundly rejected
   - roundly denounced
   
   **Root Analysis**: round + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "round" + "-ly" (15th century). Originally meant "in a round manner," later specialized to mean "thoroughly" or "severely."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His proposal was roundly rejected by everyone at the meeting.
   - The team was roundly beaten 5-0.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The policy was roundly criticized by independent experts.
   - The defendant's testimony was roundly contradicted by physical evidence.

1. **Adverb**: circularly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a circular manner; moving in or forming a circle.
   - *(Sense 2)* In a way that is circular (illogical, using conclusion as premise).
   - *Primarily end position; technical or formal usage*
   
   **Synonyms**: in circles, round and round, in a circle, round
   
   **Antonyms**: linearly, straight, directly, straightforwardly
   
   **When to Use**: In technical, scientific, or formal contexts when describing circular motion, patterns, or reasoning. Common in physics, mathematics, and logic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual conversation (too formal or technical). For everyday circular motion, use "in circles" or "round and round."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - move circularly
   - arranged circularly
   - rotate circularly
   - argue circularly
   - reason circularly
   
   **Root Analysis**: circular + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "circular" + "-ly" (17th century). Technical term from geometry and logic.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The satellites orbit circularly around the planet.
   - You're arguing circularly—that doesn't prove anything!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The particles were arranged circularly to maximize interaction.
   - The reasoning proceeds circularly, undermining the conclusion's validity.

1. **Adverb**: roundabout (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In an indirect way; not straightforwardly or directly.
   - *End position or adverbial phrase ("in a roundabout way")*
   
   **Synonyms**: indirectly, circuitously, obliquely, in a roundabout way
   
   **Antonyms**: directly, straightforwardly, bluntly, frankly
   
   **When to Use**: When describing indirect communication, circuitous routes, or non-direct approaches. Can imply evasiveness or diplomacy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when directness is valued or expected. Don't use as standalone adverb—typically used in phrase "in a roundabout way."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in a roundabout way
   - say something roundabout
   - come to the point roundabout
   - explain roundabout
   - approach roundabout
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (circular) + about (approximately, around)
   
   **Etymology**: From "round" + "about" (17th century). Originally referred to circular routes, extended to mean indirect speech or action.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He told me in a roundabout way that I wasn't invited.
   - Stop being so roundabout—just say what you mean!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The speaker approached the sensitive topic in a roundabout manner.
   - Information was conveyed roundabout through intermediaries.

1. **Adverb**: approximately (type: degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Close to a particular number, time, or amount but not exactly; roughly.
   - *Typically front or mid position; formal register*
   
   **Synonyms**: roughly, about, around, nearly, close to
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly, precisely, definitely, specifically
   
   **When to Use**: For estimates, rounded numbers, or inexact measurements. Common in formal, academic, technical, and scientific contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when exact figures are required or available. Too formal for casual conversation (use "about" or "around" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approximately equal
   - approximately correct
   - approximately the same
   - cost approximately
   - measure approximately
   
   **Root Analysis**: approximat(e) (Latin "approximatus" = brought near) + -ly (adverb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "approximare" (ad- "to" + proximus "nearest") → "approximate" + "-ly" (17th century). Formal term for estimation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There were approximately 50 people at the party.
   - It takes approximately two hours to get there.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sample contained approximately 15% impurities.
   - Participants numbered approximately 200 across three cohorts.
