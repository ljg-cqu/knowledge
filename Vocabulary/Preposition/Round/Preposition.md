1. **Preposition**: round (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* On all sides of; encircling; so as to encircle or enclose
   - *(Spatial)* In various directions from a center; here and there in or near
   - *(Spatial)* So as to reach the other side of; moving in a circular path past
   - *(Temporal)* Approximately at (a particular time)
   
   **Synonyms**: around (in all senses), about, surrounding, encircling
   
   **Antonyms**: through (for "past" sense), away from, straight to
   
   **When to Use**: British English preference; describing encircling position, scattered location, or approximate time; both literal and figurative spatial relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In American English writing (prefer "around"); when precise location or time is required; when linear direction is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: round the corner, round the clock, round here, round the world
   - **Verb + prep**: walk round, look round, go round, turn round
   - **Adjective + prep**: (less common with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: journey round, trip round
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (from Old English "rund" = circular)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "rund" (circular) → Middle English "round" (used as preposition from 13th century). British English retains this form; American English prefers "around."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's meet round 7 o'clock, okay?
   - There's a great café just round the corner from here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants gathered round the conference table for discussions.
   - The facility operates round the clock to ensure continuous service.

1. **Preposition**: around (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* On all sides of; encircling; surrounding
   - *(Spatial)* In or to various places within or near; scattered throughout
   - *(Spatial)* So as to move in a circular path; past or beyond
   - *(Temporal)* Approximately at (a particular time)
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; about; on the subject of
   
   **Synonyms**: round, about, surrounding, near, circa (for time)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, straight to, through, exactly at
   
   **When to Use**: Universal English (American and British); describing encircling, approximate location/time, or topic; most versatile positional preposition
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact position or time required; when "through" indicates penetration rather than circumvention
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: around the corner, around here, around noon, around the issue
   - **Verb + prep**: walk around, look around, turn around, gather around
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare with adjectives)
   - **Noun + prep**: trip around, tour around, way around
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on, in) + round (circular)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "around" from "a-" (prefix) + "round" (14th century). Developed as spatial preposition, extended to temporal and abstract uses.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll be there around 5 PM, maybe a bit earlier.
   - Let's walk around the park and talk.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Approximately 200 participants gathered around the main auditorium.
   - The discussion centered around three key policy proposals.

1. **Preposition**: about (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In various directions from a place; here and there in or near (British English)
   - *(Spatial)* All around; surrounding (British English, less common)
   - *(Temporal)* Approximately at (a particular time)
   - *(Abstract)* On the subject of; concerning
   - *(Abstract)* Engaged in; occupied with
   
   **Synonyms**: around, concerning, regarding, approximately, relating to
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly, precisely, away from (depending on sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing topic/subject (most common); approximate time; scattered position (British); universal English usage
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precise location or time is essential; American English increasingly prefers "around" for spatial uses
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: about here (British), about time, talk about, think about
   - **Verb + prep**: talk about, think about, worry about, care about
   - **Adjective + prep**: concerned about, worried about, excited about, sure about
   - **Noun + prep**: book about, discussion about, question about, concern about
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + bout (around, from Old English "butan" = outside)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "abutan" (on the outside of) → Middle English "about" (13th century). Originally spatial, extended to temporal and abstract meanings.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll see you about 3 o'clock.
   - What are you talking about?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The conference will address concerns about climate policy.
   - Data collection occurred about midday across all sites.

1. **Preposition**: surrounding (type: simple/participial)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* All around; encircling on all sides
   - Often functions as adjective but also used prepositionally
   
   **Synonyms**: around, round, encircling, encompassing
   
   **Antonyms**: within, inside, central to
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing complete encirclement; formal and technical contexts; geographic, architectural, or scientific descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "around"); when partial encirclement is meant; when position isn't literally surrounding
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: surrounding the area, surrounding the issue, areas surrounding [place]
   - **Verb + prep**: (less common as most verbs take "around")
   - **Adjective + prep**: (typically "surrounding" is itself the adjective)
   - **Noun + prep**: land surrounding, debate surrounding, controversy surrounding
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (over, above) + round (circular) + -ing (participial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: French "suronder" (overflow) → English "surround" + "-ing" (17th century). Participial form used as preposition/adjective.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The hills surrounding the town are beautiful.
   - There's a lot of controversy surrounding this decision.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The tissue surrounding the organ showed signs of inflammation.
   - Legislation surrounding data protection has evolved significantly.

1. **Preposition**: circa (type: simple, Latin)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Approximately; about; around (used with dates)
   - Formal/academic preposition borrowed from Latin
   
   **Synonyms**: around, about, approximately, roughly
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly, precisely, definitely, in
   
   **When to Use**: Historical dates in academic writing; formal publications; museum labels; scholarly citations; when emphasizing approximation of historical timing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; contemporary dates (use "around"); non-academic writing; when exact dates are known
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: circa 1500, circa 500 BCE, built circa 1920
   - **Verb + prep**: (typically follows "was," "occurred," "dated")
   - **Adjective + prep**: dated circa, built circa, written circa
   - **Noun + prep**: painting circa, manuscript circa, artifact circa
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "circa" = around, about
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "circa" (around, about) borrowed directly into English (19th century) for scholarly and historical purposes. Abbreviation: c. or ca.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - *Typically not used in casual contexts; prefer "around"*
   - The painting is from around 1600 (more appropriate casual form)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The manuscript is dated circa 1450.
   - Construction commenced circa 800 CE, according to archaeological evidence.

1. **Preposition**: around/round (in phrasal preposition combinations) (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Forms compound prepositions with other words to express complex spatial or abstract relationships
   - Examples: "all around," "round about"
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout, near, in the vicinity of (depending on combination)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, distant from, far from
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing distributed position, approximate location, or scattered arrangement; various formality levels depending on specific phrase
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simple preposition suffices; when precision is required; in highly formal academic writing (prefer more specific prepositions)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: all around the world, round about here, scattered around the room
   - **Verb + prep**: look all around, search around for
   - **Adjective + prep**: distributed all around
   - **Noun + prep**: people all around, things scattered around
   
   **Root Analysis**: various combinations of "all/round/about" + "around/round"
   
   **Etymology**: Compound formations from Middle English onward, combining intensifiers with spatial prepositions for emphasis.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There are cafés all around this neighborhood.
   - He lives somewhere round about Manchester.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The phenomenon has been observed in regions around the Mediterranean.
   - Samples were collected from sites distributed around the perimeter.
