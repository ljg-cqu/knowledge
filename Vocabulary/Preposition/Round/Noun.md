1. **Noun**: round (C, pl: rounds)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A circular or cylindrical shape or object
   - *(Sense 2)* One stage in a competition or series of events
   - *(Sense 3)* A regular series of visits or inspections
   - *(Sense 4)* A single shot or ammunition unit for a firearm
   - *(Sense 5)* A serving of drinks for all members of a group
   
   **Synonyms**: circle, stage, series, bout (depending on sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: straight line, angle, finish/end (for competition sense)
   
   **When to Use**: Sports competitions, ammunition contexts, social drinking situations (British), medical or security patrols, geometric descriptions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specific technical terms are more appropriate (e.g., "stage" in formal competition rules, "bullet" for ammunition)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - next round
   - final round
   - round of applause
   - round of drinks
   - do the rounds
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (from Latin "rotundus" = circular)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "rotundus" → Old French "roond" → Middle English "round" (noun use from 14th century). Extended from geometric shape to various metaphorical uses.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our team made it to the third round of the tournament!
   - It's my round—what's everyone drinking?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The selection process consists of three rounds of interviews.
   - Security personnel conduct regular rounds of the premises.

1. **Noun**: roundabout (C, pl: roundabouts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (British) A circular intersection where traffic moves in one direction around a central island
   - *(Sense 2)* (British) A rotating circular platform in a playground; merry-go-round
   - *(Sense 3)* (British) A carousel at a fair
   
   **Synonyms**: traffic circle, rotary, circle (American English for Sense 1); merry-go-round, carousel (Sense 2-3)
   
   **Antonyms**: straight road, intersection, crossroads (for Sense 1)
   
   **When to Use**: British English traffic contexts; playground equipment; fairground attractions; describing circular structures or systems
   
   **When NOT to Use**: American English for traffic (use "traffic circle" or "rotary"); when specific technical traffic engineering terms are required
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - approach the roundabout
   - exit the roundabout
   - roundabout ahead
   - mini roundabout
   - magic roundabout
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (circular) + about (around)
   
   **Etymology**: From "round" + "about" (late 18th century for playground sense, early 20th century for traffic sense). Originally described circular motion or structures.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Take the second exit at the roundabout.
   - The kids love playing on the roundabout at the park.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposed roundabout will improve traffic flow at this junction.
   - Installation of a roundabout reduced accident rates by 40 percent.

1. **Noun**: surroundings (U/plural, no singular form)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The things and conditions around a person or thing; the environment
   
   **Synonyms**: environment, setting, milieu, vicinity, environs
   
   **Antonyms**: interior, inside, center
   
   **When to Use**: Describing physical environment, atmosphere, or context; formal and informal contexts; discussing adaptation or awareness
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to specific individual objects (use "objects" or "items"); when "environment" is more technically appropriate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - natural surroundings
   - immediate surroundings
   - familiar surroundings
   - unfamiliar surroundings
   - take in the surroundings
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (over, around) + round (circular) + -ings (plural noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From "surround" (French "suronder" = overflow) + "-ings" suffix (17th century). Originally meant "things that surround."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I love the peaceful surroundings of this countryside cottage.
   - Take a moment to look at your surroundings—it's beautiful here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients recover more quickly in pleasant surroundings.
   - The organism adapts to changes in its surroundings through evolutionary processes.

1. **Noun**: roundness (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The quality or state of being round; circularity
   
   **Synonyms**: circularity, curvature, sphericity, rotundity
   
   **Antonyms**: angularity, flatness, straightness, sharpness
   
   **When to Use**: Geometric descriptions, aesthetic discussions, technical or scientific contexts describing shape properties
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "round shape" is more natural; when precision about type of roundness is needed (circular vs. spherical)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the roundness of the earth
   - perfect roundness
   - degree of roundness
   - fullness and roundness
   - appreciate the roundness
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (circular) + -ness (noun suffix indicating quality)
   
   **Etymology**: From "round" + "-ness" (15th century). Abstract noun indicating quality of being round.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The roundness of the ball makes it roll easily.
   - I love the roundness of her face—it's so cute!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hypothesis concerns the perfect roundness of planetary orbits.
   - Measurements indicated significant variations in roundness across samples.

1. **Noun**: circle (C, pl: circles)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A round plane figure whose boundary consists of points equidistant from a fixed center
   - *(Sense 2)* A group of people with shared interests, profession, or acquaintances
   - *(Sense 3)* A curved upper tier of seats in a theater
   
   **Synonyms**: ring, round, disc (Sense 1); group, coterie, clique (Sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: square, line, triangle (Sense 1); outsider, individual (Sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Geometric contexts, social groups, theater seating, describing circular shapes or patterns
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For three-dimensional round objects (use "sphere"); when other geometric shapes are more accurate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - draw a circle
   - vicious circle
   - inner circle
   - come full circle
   - social circle
   
   **Root Analysis**: circl (from Latin "circulus" = small ring) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "circulus" (diminutive of "circus" = ring) → Old French "cercle" → Middle English "circle" (14th century). Originally geometric term, extended to social groups.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Draw a circle around the correct answer.
   - She moves in very different social circles than I do.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The theorem applies to all points within the circle's circumference.
   - He was highly regarded within academic circles.

1. **Noun**: sphere (C, pl: spheres)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A round three-dimensional object; a globe or ball
   - *(Sense 2)* An area of activity, interest, or expertise; a domain
   - *(Sense 3)* A social class or group
   
   **Synonyms**: ball, globe, orb (Sense 1); domain, field, realm, area (Sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: cube, plane, flat surface (Sense 1)
   
   **When to Use**: Geometric and scientific contexts for 3D round objects; describing domains of activity or influence; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For 2D circular shapes (use "circle"); in casual conversation where "ball" is more natural
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sphere of influence
   - political sphere
   - private sphere
   - public sphere
   - celestial sphere
   
   **Root Analysis**: spher (from Greek "sphaira" = ball, globe) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Greek "sphaira" → Latin "sphaera" → Old French "espere" → Middle English "sphere" (14th century). Originally astronomical term, extended to domains and social classes.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The earth is shaped like a sphere, not a perfect ball.
   - That's outside my sphere of knowledge—you'd better ask an expert.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The particle exhibits properties consistent with a perfect sphere.
   - This matter falls within the sphere of the ethics committee's authority.
