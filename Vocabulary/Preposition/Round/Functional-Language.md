1. **Functional Expression**: "Let's go round to [place/person's]" (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggesting visiting someone's place or going somewhere together (British English)
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (uses "let's" to include speaker)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's go to [place], Why don't we visit [person], How about going over to [place], Let's head to [place]
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's stay here, I'm going alone, You go without me
   
   **When to Use**: Casual invitations among friends or close colleagues; British English; social contexts; including yourself in the plan
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal professional settings; American English (use "go over to" instead); when making solo plans; hierarchical relationships with superiors
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Come round to my place (more direct invitation)
   - Pop round to see them (more casual, spontaneous)
   - Let's swing by (American English equivalent)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Minimizes imposition through inclusive "let's"; British cultural norm for casual social invitations; softer than direct invitation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Sure, let's go!" / "Sounds good to me" / "When?"
   - Declining: "I can't today, maybe next time" / "I'd love to but I'm busy"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Let's go round to Sarah's after work—she said she's making pizza."
   - "Want to go round to the pub for a quick drink?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Perhaps we could visit their office to discuss this matter" (more appropriate formal equivalent)
   - "I suggest we reconvene at the alternate location" (very formal equivalent)

1. **Functional Expression**: "Could you round this up/down to [number]?" (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Requesting someone to adjust a number to the nearest convenient figure
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (uses modal "could")
   
   **Synonyms**: Can you make this approximately [number], Would you mind approximating to [number], Please adjust this to [number]
   
   **Antonyms**: Give me the exact number, Don't round it, Keep it precise
   
   **When to Use**: Mathematical, financial, or data contexts; when precision isn't critical; professional settings; instructing calculations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact figures are legally required; in critical financial decisions; when rounding would significantly distort data
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Round this to the nearest [unit] (more specific)
   - Could you give me a round number? (less precise instruction)
   - Let's round this off at [number] (more collaborative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Modal "could" softens request; implies understanding that rounding is acceptable; commonly used in accounting, statistics, and presentations
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Sure, rounded up that's [number]" / "Okay, approximately [number]"
   - Clarifying: "Round to what decimal place?" / "Up or down?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "The bill is $47.30—could you just round it up to $50?"
   - "Can you round those percentages to whole numbers? Makes it easier to read."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "For presentation purposes, could you round these figures to the nearest thousand?"
   - "Would you mind rounding these calculations to two decimal places?"

1. **Functional Expression**: "I'll come round to your way of thinking" (function: agreeing/accepting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Acknowledging gradual acceptance of someone's viewpoint or opinion
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (indicates process, not immediate agreement)
   
   **Synonyms**: I'll eventually agree with you, I'm starting to see your point, You're winning me over, I'm coming around to that view
   
   **Antonyms**: I'll never agree, I strongly disagree, That doesn't make sense to me
   
   **When to Use**: Showing openness to persuasion; diplomatic disagreement softening; acknowledging good arguments; professional or personal discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you fundamentally disagree; when appearing uncertain hurts your position; in contexts requiring immediate decision
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm coming round to that idea (present progressive, more immediate)
   - I might come round to it eventually (more hesitant)
   - You've brought me round (completed action, fully convinced)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Saves face for both parties; indicates respectful consideration; British English preference; shows intellectual flexibility
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Encouraging: "Take your time" / "Think about it" / "I appreciate you considering it"
   - Accepting: "That's all I ask" / "Fair enough"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I wasn't sure about moving, but I'm coming round to the idea."
   - "Your argument makes sense—I think I'll come round to your view eventually."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "While initially skeptical, I am gradually coming round to this proposal."
   - "The committee appears to be coming round to the revised framework."

1. **Functional Expression**: "Let's round off the meeting with [activity/summary]" (function: concluding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Suggesting a concluding activity or statement for a meeting or event
   - *Formality*: neutral to semi-formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (uses "let's" to involve others)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's conclude with [activity], Let's finish by [doing something], To wrap up, To close
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's continue with, There's more to discuss, We're not done yet
   
   **When to Use**: Facilitating meetings or events; transitioning to conclusion; summarizing key points; professional and social contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When important matters remain unresolved; when people have urgent concerns; prematurely ending discussions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - To round things off (more formal)
   - Let's wrap this up with (more casual)
   - In conclusion (more formal, academic)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals transition to conclusion; allows for satisfying closure; collaborative tone through "let's"; common in meeting facilitation
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Good idea" / "Agreed" / "Perfect"
   - Objecting: "Actually, I have one more point" / "Before we finish, can I add something?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Let's round off the meeting with a quick summary of action items."
   - "How about we round things off with a team photo?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "To round off today's session, I invite each participant to share key takeaways."
   - "We shall round off the conference with a closing keynote address."

1. **Functional Expression**: "It's round about [time/amount]" (function: estimating)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Providing an approximate time, amount, or location without claiming exactness
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct (but with hedge for imprecision)
   
   **Synonyms**: It's around [amount], It's approximately [amount], It's about [amount], Roughly [amount]
   
   **Antonyms**: It's exactly [amount], Precisely [amount], It's definitely [amount]
   
   **When to Use**: Informal estimates; when exact information unavailable or unnecessary; casual conversation; giving directions or time approximations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Professional reports requiring precision; legal or financial contexts; when exact information is available and expected
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Roundabout [amount] (more British)
   - Something like [amount] (more casual)
   - In the region of [amount] (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Two-word hedge ("round about") emphasizes approximation; British English preference; protects speaker from commitment to exact figure
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Okay, that works" / "Gotcha" / "Thanks"
   - Clarifying: "Can you be more specific?" / "Is that a rough estimate?"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "What time should I come over?" "Round about 7 o'clock."
   - "How much will it cost?" "Round about $200, maybe a bit less."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "The estimate is approximately £200" (preferred formal alternative)
   - "The arrival time is around 19:00 hours" (more formal)

1. **Functional Expression**: "Can we go another round on this?" (function: requesting continuation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Requesting to revisit, discuss, or work through something again
   - *Formality*: neutral to informal
   - *Directness*: indirect (uses question form and modal)
   
   **Synonyms**: Can we discuss this again, Should we revisit this, Could we review this once more, Can we take another look at this
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's move on, That's settled, We're done with this, I think we've covered it
   
   **When to Use**: When feeling uncertain or unsatisfied with current conclusion; requesting additional discussion; team collaboration; decision-making processes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When decision is final; when repeating will not add value; when time is limited; when appearing indecisive is problematic
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - One more round of discussion (more formal)
   - Give it another go (more casual)
   - Revisit this topic (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Another round" metaphor from games/boxing suggests iterative process; acknowledges previous attempt; seeks permission to continue
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Accepting: "Sure, what's still unclear?" / "Of course, let's dive in"
   - Declining: "I think we've covered it thoroughly" / "Let's move forward for now"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'm still not 100% sure about this plan—can we go another round on it?"
   - "Before we finalize, can we do another round of edits?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Would it be possible to conduct another review of these specifications?"
   - "I propose we undertake a further round of consultations before proceeding."
