1. **Verb**: round
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make or become round in shape
   - *(Sense 2)* To go around something; to pass around the other side of
   - *(Sense 3)* To express (a number) as a round figure (with "up," "down," or "off")
   - *Transitive and intransitive uses depending on sense*
   
   **Synonyms**: curve, bend, circle (Sense 1); pass, circle, go around (Sense 2); approximate, estimate (Sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: straighten, flatten, square (Sense 1); approach directly (Sense 2); specify exactly (Sense 3)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing shape changes, navigation around objects, mathematical approximations; neutral register; various contexts from everyday to technical
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When precision about exact shape or number is required; when other verbs are more specific
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - round the corner
   - round off
   - round up
   - round down
   - round to the nearest
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (from Latin "rotundus" = circular)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "rotundus" → Old French "roond" → Middle English "round" (verb use from 14th century). Extended from making circular to various related actions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The car rounded the corner at high speed.
   - Can you round that number up to 50?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Edges were rounded to enhance ergonomic properties.
   - Values were rounded to two decimal places for consistency.

1. **Verb**: surround
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be or come on all sides of; to encircle
   - *(Sense 2)* To be associated with (a situation, event, or person); to characterize the circumstances of
   - *Transitive verb*
   
   **Synonyms**: encircle, encompass, enclose, ring, circle
   
   **Antonyms**: expose, open, leave clear, free
   
   **When to Use**: Describing physical encirclement, circumstances, or context; military, security, design, and descriptive contexts; neutral to formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When position is adjacent but not encircling; when "beside" or "near" is more accurate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - surround by
   - surround with
   - surrounded on all sides
   - mystery surrounding
   - controversy surrounding
   
   **Root Analysis**: sur- (over, beyond) + round (circular) from French "suronder"
   
   **Etymology**: Old French "suronder" (overflow) → Middle English "surrounden" (15th century). Originally meant "overflow," later "encircle."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The house is surrounded by beautiful gardens.
   - There's so much mystery surrounding her disappearance.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Security forces surrounded the building to prevent escape.
   - Significant controversy surrounds the proposed legislation.

1. **Verb**: circle
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move in a circle around something
   - *(Sense 2)* To draw a circle around something to highlight or select it
   - *Transitive and intransitive*
   
   **Synonyms**: orbit, revolve around, encircle, loop, go around
   
   **Antonyms**: approach directly, move in a straight line, go through
   
   **When to Use**: Describing circular motion (birds, planes, vehicles), marking or highlighting, indirect approaches; neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When movement is not circular; when "surround" (stationary encirclement) is more accurate
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - circle around
   - circle back
   - circle overhead
   - circle the answer
   - circle the wagons
   
   **Root Analysis**: circl (from Latin "circulus" = small ring) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "circulare" (form a circle) → English "circle" as verb (15th century). From geometric noun to action verb.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The plane circled the airport waiting for permission to land.
   - Circle the correct answer on your test paper.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The satellite circles the Earth approximately every 90 minutes.
   - Please circle all items requiring immediate attention.

1. **Verb**: revolve
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move in a circular orbit around a central point or axis
   - *(Sense 2)* (of thoughts, discussions) To have as the main focus or concern; to center on
   - *Intransitive primarily; transitive with "around"*
   
   **Synonyms**: rotate, orbit, turn, spin (Sense 1); center on, focus on, concern (Sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: remain stationary, stand still (Sense 1); ignore, overlook (Sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Astronomical contexts, mechanical rotation, central themes or concerns; slightly more formal than "go around"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation where "go around" suffices; when rotation on own axis (use "rotate"); when movement is irregular or non-circular
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - revolve around
   - revolve about
   - life revolves around
   - world doesn't revolve around you
   - revolve in orbit
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again, back) + volv (Latin "volvere" = roll, turn)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "revolvere" (roll back, unroll) → Old French "revolver" → English "revolve" (14th century). Originally "turn over," specialized to circular motion.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The Earth revolves around the Sun once a year.
   - His whole life revolves around his work—he needs to relax!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The celestial bodies revolve in elliptical orbits according to Kepler's laws.
   - The debate revolves around fundamental questions of resource allocation.

1. **Verb**: rotate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To turn or cause to turn around a central point or axis
   - *(Sense 2)* To take turns in a particular order or sequence (jobs, positions)
   - *Transitive and intransitive*
   
   **Synonyms**: spin, turn, revolve, twist (Sense 1); alternate, take turns, cycle (Sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: remain stationary, keep still (Sense 1); keep in same position, fix permanently (Sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Mechanical rotation, job/duty rotation, agriculture (crop rotation), systematic turn-taking; technical and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When orbital motion around external point (use "revolve"); casual conversation about turning (use "turn" or "spin")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rotate clockwise/counterclockwise
   - rotate positions
   - rotate crops
   - rotate duties
   - rotate tires
   
   **Root Analysis**: rotat (Latin "rotare" = turn in a circle) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "rotare" (turn like a wheel, from "rota" = wheel) → English "rotate" (17th century). Technical term from Latin, especially in mechanics and astronomy.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Rotate the picture 90 degrees so it's right-side up.
   - We rotate who does the dishes every week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The shaft rotates at 3000 revolutions per minute.
   - Staff members rotate through different departments to gain comprehensive experience.

1. **Verb**: spin
   **Meaning**: 
   - To turn or cause to turn rapidly around a central point
   - *(Sense 2)* To present information in a particular way, especially to give a favorable impression
   - *Transitive and intransitive*
   
   **Synonyms**: whirl, rotate, revolve, twirl (Sense 1); slant, angle, skew (Sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: stop, halt, stabilize (Sense 1); present objectively, report factually (Sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Rapid rotation, dizziness, yarn/thread production, political/PR contexts (Sense 2); casual to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Slow rotation (use "turn" or "rotate"); when "revolve" around external point is more accurate; when objectivity is valued (Sense 2)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - spin round
   - spin out of control
   - spin the wheel
   - spin a story
   - spin doctor
   
   **Root Analysis**: spin (from Old English "spinnan" = draw out and twist fiber)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English "spinnan" (draw out and twist fibers) → Middle English "spinnen" (extended to rapid rotation by 15th century). Sense 2 developed late 20th century from political PR.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The child spun round and round until she was dizzy.
   - Don't spin this story—just tell me what really happened!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The turbine spins at optimal velocity to generate maximum output.
   - Communications teams attempted to spin the negative publicity favorably.

1. **Verb**: encircle
   **Meaning**: 
   - To form a circle around; to surround completely
   - *Transitive verb*
   
   **Synonyms**: surround, circle, encompass, ring, enclose
   
   **Antonyms**: open, expose, free, release
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts emphasizing complete surrounding; military tactics, geography, relationships; more formal than "surround"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "surround" or "circle"); when encirclement is partial; when position is adjacent rather than enclosing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - encircle completely
   - encircle the enemy
   - encircled by
   - encircle with
   - tightly encircle
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (make, put in) + circle
   
   **Etymology**: "en-" prefix + "circle" (16th century). Formed to emphasize the action of making/forming a circle around something.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The mountains encircle the valley on all sides.
   - Protesters encircled the building.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Forces successfully encircled the enemy position, preventing retreat.
   - The ancient walls encircle the historic city center.

1. **Verb**: circumnavigate
   **Meaning**: 
   - To sail or travel completely around (something, especially the world or an island)
   - *Transitive verb; formal/technical*
   
   **Synonyms**: sail around, travel around, circle, navigate around, go around
   
   **Antonyms**: cross directly, go through, traverse
   
   **When to Use**: Maritime and aerial navigation, exploration, geographic achievements; formal and technical contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (use "sail around" or "go around"); land travel (though technically applicable, less common); short distances
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - circumnavigate the globe
   - circumnavigate the world
   - circumnavigate an island
   - first to circumnavigate
   - successfully circumnavigate
   
   **Root Analysis**: circum- (around) + navigat (Latin "navigare" = sail) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin "circum" (around) + "navigare" (sail) → English "circumnavigate" (17th century). Technical term from Age of Exploration.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Magellan's crew was the first to circumnavigate the globe.
   - We circumnavigated the island in about six hours.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The expedition successfully circumnavigated the Antarctic continent.
   - Modern technology enables solo sailors to circumnavigate the world with greater safety.
