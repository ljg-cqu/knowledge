1. **Linking Word**: all round (type: discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In all respects; considering all aspects; comprehensively
   - *Function*: Summarizing multiple aspects; emphasizing completeness
   
   **Synonyms**: overall, in all respects, on the whole, altogether, comprehensively
   
   **Antonyms**: partially, in some respects, incompletely, narrowly
   
   **When to Use**: When summarizing overall assessment; emphasizing comprehensive consideration; making general evaluations; both casual and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When discussing specific aspects only; when precision about particular dimensions is needed; in mathematical or technical exact statements
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: It was [evaluation] all round / All round, [independent clause]
   - [Position]: sentence-final (most common) or sentence-initial (for emphasis)
   - [Punctuation]: comma after when sentence-initial
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Confusing with "all around" (American spelling); using when discussing single aspect
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (complete) + round (comprehensive, covering all sides)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound from "all" + "round" (18th century). Originally meant "on all sides," extended to mean "in every respect."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The party was a great success all round—everyone had fun and the food was amazing.
   - All round, I think we made the right choice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The initiative has been beneficial all round, improving both efficiency and morale.
   - All round, the evidence supports the proposed hypothesis.

1. **Linking Word**: around (type: discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Approximately; about (used to indicate rough quantity, time, or degree)
   - *Function*: Hedging; indicating approximation; softening numerical claims
   
   **Synonyms**: approximately, about, roughly, circa, nearly
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly, precisely, definitely, specifically
   
   **When to Use**: When providing estimates; softening numerical commitments; indicating approximate times or quantities; both casual and semi-formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal academic writing (use "approximately"); when exact figures are required; in legal or financial precise contexts
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [around + number/time] / [verb] + around [number]
   - [Position]: immediately before number/time expression
   - [Punctuation]: no special punctuation required
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Overuse making statements seem vague; using in contexts requiring precision
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (prefix: on, in) + round (circular, approximate)
   
   **Etymology**: From "a-" + "round" (14th century). Extended from spatial meaning to temporal and quantitative approximation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll be there around 5 PM, maybe a bit earlier.
   - It costs around $50, give or take.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project timeline is around six months (though "approximately" preferred in formal writing).
   - Participation rates were around 75% across cohorts.

1. **Linking Word**: round about (type: discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Approximately; about (emphasizes approximation more than "around")
   - *Function*: Hedging with emphasis on imprecision; British English preference
   
   **Synonyms**: approximately, around, about, roughly, more or less
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly, precisely, definitely, specifically
   
   **When to Use**: Casual estimates in British English; emphasizing uncertainty or approximation; conversational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: American English (less common); formal writing; when precision is expected; professional reports
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [round about + number/time]
   - [Position]: immediately before number/time expression
   - [Punctuation]: no special punctuation required
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Using in formal documents; spelling as "roundabout" (the adjective/noun)
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (circular, approximate) + about (approximately)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound from "round" + "about" (16th century). Reinforcing compound emphasizing approximation.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "When are you leaving?" "Round about noon, I reckon."
   - It happened round about this time last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The incident occurred approximately midday (preferred formal alternative)
   - Estimates suggest around 50 participants (semi-formal alternative)

1. **Linking Word**: year-round / all year round (type: discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Throughout the entire year; in every season; continuously
   - *Function*: Indicating temporal continuity; emphasizing no seasonal variation
   
   **Synonyms**: throughout the year, annually, continuously, all year long, perennially
   
   **Antonyms**: seasonally, occasionally, periodically, intermittently
   
   **When to Use**: Describing continuous operations, availability, or conditions; tourism, business operations, weather descriptions; neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing seasonal or periodic activities; when variation across seasons is important to note
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [noun/verb] + year-round / [subject] + [verb] + all year round
   - [Position]: typically follows verb or modifies noun (year-round operation)
   - [Punctuation]: hyphenated when used as adjective before noun (year-round); no hyphen in "all year round"
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Inconsistent hyphenation; using for seasonal activities
   
   **Root Analysis**: year (annual period) + round (circular, continuous cycle)
   
   **Etymology**: Compound from "year" + "round" (19th century). Metaphor of circular, continuous cycle.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The pool is open year-round, even in winter.
   - We go hiking all year round, no matter the weather.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The facility maintains year-round operations to ensure continuous service.
   - Tourism has become a year-round industry in this region.

1. **Linking Word**: round and round (type: discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In circles; repeatedly; continuously in a circular motion or pattern
   - *Function*: Emphasizing repetition, circular motion, or lack of progress
   
   **Synonyms**: in circles, repeatedly, continuously, around and around, over and over
   
   **Antonyms**: straight ahead, progressively, forward, linearly
   
   **When to Use**: Describing circular physical motion; metaphorically describing repetitive or unproductive activity; both literal and figurative contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing productive iteration; in contexts requiring neutral description of repetition
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [subject] + [go/spin/turn] + round and round
   - [Position]: typically follows verb of motion
   - [Punctuation]: no special punctuation; can be set off by commas for emphasis
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Using when iteration is productive (implies lack of progress); confusing with "around and around"
   
   **Root Analysis**: round (circular) + and + round (repeated for emphasis)
   
   **Etymology**: Reduplication for emphasis (15th century). Repetition emphasizes continuous circular motion.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The kids were spinning round and round until they got dizzy.
   - We keep going round and round on this issue without resolving anything.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The discussion has proceeded round and round without reaching consensus.
   - The carousel rotates round and round continuously.

1. **Linking Word**: the other way round (type: discourse marker)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the opposite order, position, or manner; reversed
   - *Function*: Indicating reversal or opposite arrangement; British English
   
   **Synonyms**: the other way around, vice versa, conversely, in reverse, oppositely
   
   **Antonyms**: in the same order, similarly, identically, as stated
   
   **When to Use**: Correcting order or sequence; presenting alternative perspective; clarifying opposite arrangement; British English preference
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In American English writing (use "the other way around"); when the order is correct as stated
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - [Clause structure]: [statement], or [the other way round]
   - [Position]: sentence-final or independent clause
   - [Punctuation]: preceded by comma or semicolon when contrasting
   - [Common errors to avoid]: Using in American English contexts; forgetting article "the"
   
   **Root Analysis**: the other (opposite) + way (manner) + round (direction/orientation)
   
   **Etymology**: British English compound phrase (18th century). "Round" indicates directional reversal.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I thought you were helping me, but it was the other way round!
   - No, put the fork on the left and knife on the right—you've got it the other way round.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The data suggests causation flows the other way round.
   - Conventional wisdom held that A caused B; however, the relationship may be the other way round.
