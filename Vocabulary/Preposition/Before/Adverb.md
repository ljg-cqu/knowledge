1. **Adverb**: before (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At an earlier time; previously; in the past; on a previous occasion
   - Already; by a particular point in time; earlier than a reference point
   - Ahead in time or position; in front (spatial/temporal)
   
   **Synonyms**: previously, earlier, formerly, in the past, already, beforehand
   
   **Antonyms**: after, afterward, later, subsequently, since
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating prior occurrence, past experience, or temporal precedence; common in all registers; can occupy various sentence positions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simultaneity is meant (use "while" or "during"); avoid ambiguity between temporal and spatial senses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - never before
   - long before
   - just before
   - as before
   - seen before
   - mentioned before
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + fore (earlier/in front)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English beforan (earlier in time/position) → Middle English → Modern English adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Have we met before?
   - I've never been here before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This phenomenon has not been observed before in controlled conditions.
   - As stated before, the methodology requires modification.

1. **Adverb**: beforehand (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In advance; ahead of time; before an event or action occurs; preparatorily
   - At an earlier time than something else; with advance preparation
   
   **Synonyms**: in advance, ahead of time, previously, earlier, preparatorily, pre-emptively
   
   **Antonyms**: afterward, subsequently, later, after the fact, in hindsight
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing advance preparation, planning, or knowledge; scheduling contexts; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing reactions or responses (use "afterward"); when exact moment is important (be specific)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - know beforehand
   - prepare beforehand
   - arrange beforehand
   - inform beforehand
   - plan beforehand
   - warn beforehand
   
   **Root Analysis**: before (earlier) + hand (archaic temporal marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English (14th century) combining "before" + "hand" → adverb of preparation
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You should have told me beforehand!
   - Let's get everything ready beforehand.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants must register beforehand to ensure adequate resources.
   - All materials were distributed beforehand for preliminary review.

1. **Adverb**: previously (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At an earlier time; before now or before a particular time mentioned; formerly
   - On a prior occasion; in the past; at a time before the present
   
   **Synonyms**: before, earlier, formerly, at an earlier time, in the past, beforehand
   
   **Antonyms**: later, subsequently, afterward, following, next
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and neutral contexts; academic writing, professional communication; referring to past states or events
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (prefer "before" or "earlier"); when ongoing state is meant (use "formerly")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - previously mentioned
   - previously discussed
   - previously reported
   - as previously stated
   - previously unknown
   - previously described
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + via (way) + -ous (adjective) + -ly (adverb)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praevius (going before) → French → English "previous" + -ly → formal temporal adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I told you this previously.
   - We've met previously, haven't we?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As previously mentioned, the methodology requires rigorous validation.
   - Previously unreported findings suggest alternative interpretations.

1. **Adverb**: earlier (type: time/degree)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At a time before the present or before a specified time; previously; sooner
   - In the earlier part of a period or text; previously in the discussion
   
   **Synonyms**: before, previously, sooner, beforehand, at an earlier time
   
   **Antonyms**: later, afterward, subsequently, eventually
   
   **When to Use**: General temporal references; comparing times; all registers; can modify verbs or serve as sentence adverb
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "earliest" (superlative) is meant; distinguish from "early" (at beginning of period)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - earlier today
   - earlier this week
   - mentioned earlier
   - discussed earlier
   - as stated earlier
   - no earlier than
   
   **Root Analysis**: early + -er (comparative suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ærlīce (early) + comparative -er → temporal adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I saw him earlier at the store.
   - Didn't we talk about this earlier?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - As demonstrated earlier, the correlation is statistically significant.
   - Earlier research established the theoretical framework.

1. **Adverb**: formerly (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In the past; at an earlier time; before now; once but no longer
   - Previously; in times past; at a time before the current state
   
   **Synonyms**: previously, once, earlier, before, in the past, at one time
   
   **Antonyms**: currently, presently, now, at present, nowadays
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating past state contrasted with present; formal and neutral registers; emphasizes change from past to present
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When continuity exists (not for ongoing situations); avoid when simple past reference suffices
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - formerly known as
   - formerly occupied
   - formerly held
   - formerly called
   - formerly believed
   - formerly used
   
   **Root Analysis**: former (earlier) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English former (first of two) + -ly → temporal contrast adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He formerly worked in finance.
   - This was formerly a factory.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The territory was formerly administered by colonial authorities.
   - These practices, formerly considered standard, are now deemed unethical.

1. **Adverb**: prior (type: time - primarily formal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Before; beforehand; at an earlier time; in advance
   - Previously; ahead of a reference point in time
   
   **Synonyms**: before, beforehand, previously, earlier, in advance
   
   **Antonyms**: after, afterward, subsequently, later
   
   **When to Use**: Very formal contexts; typically used with "to" as compound preposition ("prior to"); legal and academic writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; standalone adverb use is rare (usually part of "prior to")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prior to (more common as compound preposition)
   - occurring prior
   - arranged prior
   - (More common as adjective: "prior experience")
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin prior (former, first)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prior (former) → English → formal temporal adverb (less common than adjectival use)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare as standalone adverb in casual use)
   - We met prior to the conference. (more common: "before the conference")
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Documents must be submitted prior to the deadline.
   - Consent was obtained prior to data collection.

1. **Adverb**: ahead (type: spatial/temporal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Further forward in position or time; in front; in advance
   - Before someone or something in sequence, progress, or development
   - In or into the future; forward; at a point yet to come
   
   **Synonyms**: forward, in front, before, in advance, beforehand
   
   **Antonyms**: behind, after, backward, later
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating spatial or temporal priority; progress, planning, advancement; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When past reference is meant (use "before"); distinguish spatial from temporal senses
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - go ahead
   - look ahead
   - plan ahead
   - ahead of time
   - ahead of schedule
   - straight ahead
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + head (front)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "a hed" (at the head/front) → Modern English spatial/temporal adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Go ahead, I'll catch up later.
   - We need to plan ahead for this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Organizations must look ahead to anticipate market changes.
   - The project is proceeding ahead of schedule.

1. **Adverb**: already (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Before now or before a specified time; by this time; so soon
   - Previously; even before the expected time; sooner than anticipated
   
   **Synonyms**: by now, by this time, previously, before now, even now
   
   **Antonyms**: not yet, still not, later, eventually
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating completion before expected time; expressing surprise at earliness; mid-sentence position common
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When ongoing action is meant (use "still"); distinguish from "all ready" (completely prepared)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - already done
   - already finished
   - already mentioned
   - already late
   - have already
   - already here
   
   **Root Analysis**: all (completely) + ready (prepared/at time)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English "al redy" (completely at the time) → Modern English temporal adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you leaving already?
   - I've already eaten, thanks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system has already been tested in multiple environments.
   - As already noted, several limitations affect interpretation.

1. **Adverb**: yet (type: time - negative/interrogative contexts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Up until now or up until a particular time; so far; by now
   - Still; continuing from an earlier time; up to this point
   - *(In questions/negatives)* Until now; before now; by this time
   
   **Synonyms**: so far, up to now, thus far, until now, still (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: already, no longer, previously
   
   **When to Use**: Questions and negative sentences about time up to present; formal and informal registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Simple affirmative statements about past (use "already" or "before"); note contrast with positive contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - not yet
   - yet to come
   - yet again
   - have yet to
   - as yet
   - not yet decided
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English giet, gēt (still)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English gēt, gīet → Middle English → Modern temporal/concessive adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Have you finished yet?
   - I haven't decided yet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results have not yet been validated through peer review.
   - We have yet to determine the optimal approach.

1. **Adverb**: first (type: time/sequence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Before anything or anyone else; initially; at the beginning; to begin with
   - For the first time; on the first occasion; originally
   - Before doing something else; as the first step
   
   **Synonyms**: initially, firstly, to begin with, at the outset, originally, before anything else
   
   **Antonyms**: last, finally, lastly, ultimately, at the end
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating chronological priority, sequence, initial occurrence; all registers; various sentence positions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When not discussing sequence or priority; distinguish from ordinal number use
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - first of all
   - first and foremost
   - at first
   - first time
   - first thing
   - come first
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English fyrst (foremost)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English fyrst (foremost, earliest) → superlative of fore → sequence adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - First, let me explain what happened.
   - When did you first meet her?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - First, we must establish clear evaluation criteria.
   - The phenomenon was first observed in controlled laboratory conditions.

1. **Adverb**: initially (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At the beginning; at first; at the start; in the early stages
   - Originally; in the initial phase; before subsequent developments
   
   **Synonyms**: at first, originally, in the beginning, at the outset, firstly, to begin with
   
   **Antonyms**: finally, ultimately, eventually, in the end, later
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and neutral contexts; describing first stages, original states, preliminary phases; academic and professional writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (prefer "at first"); when final state is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - initially thought
   - initially planned
   - initially believed
   - initially designed
   - initially expected
   - initially proposed
   
   **Root Analysis**: initial (first) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin initialis (of the beginning) → English initial + -ly → temporal sequence adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Initially, I wasn't sure about this.
   - The plan was initially different.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The hypothesis was initially formulated to address observed anomalies.
   - Initially, participants demonstrated resistance to the intervention.

1. **Adverb**: preliminarily (type: time/manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a preliminary manner; as a first step; introductorily; tentatively
   - Before the main action or matter; in preparation; provisionally
   
   **Synonyms**: tentatively, provisionally, initially, as a preliminary step, preparatorily
   
   **Antonyms**: finally, conclusively, definitively, ultimately
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts; research, planning, early stages; emphasizing tentative or preparatory nature
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (prefer "at first" or "tentatively"); when final conclusions are meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - preliminarily assessed
   - preliminarily examined
   - preliminarily determined
   - preliminarily concluded
   - preliminarily tested
   
   **Root Analysis**: preliminary (preparatory) + -ly (adverbial suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: French préliminaire + English -ly → formal temporal/manner adverb
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare in casual use; prefer "tentatively" or "at first")
   - We've preliminarily agreed to meet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Data were preliminarily analyzed using descriptive statistics.
   - Findings were preliminarily reported at the conference.
