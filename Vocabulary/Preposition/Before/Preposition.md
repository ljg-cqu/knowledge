1. **Preposition**: before (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Earlier than a particular time, event, or action; preceding in time
   - *(Spatial)* In front of in position or location; ahead of
   - *(Abstract)* In the presence of someone, especially in a formal or official context; facing
   - *(Priority)* In preference to; rather than; with higher importance than
   
   **Synonyms**: prior to (temporal), ahead of (spatial/temporal), in front of (spatial), in the presence of (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: after, following, behind, subsequent to
   
   **When to Use**: Use "before" when indicating time sequence, spatial position ahead of something, appearing in someone's presence (courts, authorities), or expressing preference/priority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use when simultaneity is meant (use "during"); avoid when "by" is more appropriate (deadline contexts); not for causal relationships (use "because of")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: before noon, before the meeting, before long, before the war
   - **Verb + prep**: appear before, come before, go before, stand before
   - **Adjective + prep**: scheduled before, mentioned before
   - **Noun + prep**: time before, day before, moment before, period before
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + fore (front, earlier)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English beforan (be "by" + foran "in front") → Middle English → unchanged temporal/spatial meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we finish this before lunch?
   - I've never been here before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All applications must be submitted before December 31st.
   - The case will be heard before the Supreme Court next month.

1. **Preposition**: before long (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Soon; in a short time from now; after a brief period
   
   **Synonyms**: soon, shortly, in a little while, in the near future, presently
   
   **Antonyms**: much later, eventually, in the distant future
   
   **When to Use**: Indicates something will happen in the near future; commonly used in predictions or expectations; neutral to formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate when specific time is needed; avoid in urgent contexts where "immediately" is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: before long we'll see, before long it became clear
   - **Verb + prep**: happen before long, realize before long, discover before long
   - **Sentence-initial**: Before long, [clause]
   
   **Root Analysis**: before (earlier in time) + long (extended period)
   
   **Etymology**: Idiomatic temporal phrase (15th century) meaning "before a long time passes"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Before long, everyone will know about it.
   - You'll get used to it before long.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before long, the technology became obsolete.
   - The committee will reach a decision before long.

1. **Preposition**: before time (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Earlier than scheduled or expected; ahead of the appointed time; prematurely
   
   **Synonyms**: early, ahead of schedule, prematurely, in advance
   
   **Antonyms**: late, behind schedule, after time, delayed
   
   **When to Use**: Describing arrival or completion earlier than planned; often used in scheduling and project management contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not appropriate when punctuality (exactly on time) is meant; avoid confusion with "beforehand"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: arrive before time, finish before time, complete before time
   - **Verb + prep**: come before time, deliver before time
   - **Adverbial use**: The project was completed before time.
   
   **Root Analysis**: before (earlier than) + time (appointed moment)
   
   **Etymology**: Scheduling terminology (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We got there before time, so we waited outside.
   - If you finish before time, you can leave early.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The construction was completed before time and under budget.
   - Arrivals before time will be accommodated in the waiting area.

1. **Preposition**: the day before (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* The previous day; the day immediately preceding a particular date or event
   
   **Synonyms**: the previous day, the prior day, yesterday (context-dependent)
   
   **Antonyms**: the day after, the next day, the following day
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to the day immediately before a specified date or event; common in narratives and scheduling
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to multiple days earlier; be specific if ambiguity exists
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: the day before yesterday, the day before the wedding, the day before Christmas
   - **Verb + prep**: happened the day before, arrived the day before
   - **Temporal reference**: I saw him the day before.
   
   **Root Analysis**: the (definite article) + day + before (preceding)
   
   **Etymology**: Common temporal reference phrase in English
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I told you about this the day before!
   - She called me the day before her trip.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The contract was signed the day before the deadline.
   - Participants must register the day before the conference begins.

1. **Preposition**: just before (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Immediately preceding; moments or a very short time before an event
   - Emphasizes proximity in time; very recent past relative to reference point
   
   **Synonyms**: immediately before, right before, moments before, shortly before
   
   **Antonyms**: just after, immediately after, right after
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing the very short time interval before an event; creates dramatic effect in narratives; shows close temporal connection
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When longer time periods are meant; avoid if specific duration is needed
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: just before midnight, just before the deadline, just before leaving
   - **Verb + prep**: happened just before, arrived just before, finished just before
   - **Temporal emphasis**: It was just before noon.
   
   **Root Analysis**: just (exactly, immediately) + before (earlier than)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic temporal construction (Modern English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I saw him just before he left.
   - This happened just before you arrived.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system failure occurred just before the scheduled maintenance window.
   - Data was collected just before the intervention began.

1. **Preposition**: right before (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* Immediately preceding in time; with no interval between events
   - *(Spatial)* Directly in front of; in the immediate forward position
   
   **Synonyms**: immediately before, just before, directly before, moments before
   
   **Antonyms**: right after, immediately after, directly behind
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing immediate temporal or spatial proximity; common in casual and narrative contexts; stresses absence of gap
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In highly formal academic writing (prefer "immediately before"); when distance exists
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: right before the event, right before me, right before your eyes
   - **Verb + prep**: happened right before, stood right before
   - **Emphasis**: It was right before midnight.
   
   **Root Analysis**: right (exactly, directly) + before (earlier than/in front of)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic construction combining directional "right" with "before"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He said it right before he left.
   - The store is right before the intersection.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The announcement was made right before the market opened.
   - The specimen was examined right before preservation.

1. **Preposition**: shortly before (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* A short time before; not long before; relatively recently preceding an event
   
   **Synonyms**: a little before, not long before, a short time before, recently before
   
   **Antonyms**: shortly after, a little after, not long after
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating recent past relative to reference point; more formal than "just before"; appropriate in all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When immediate proximity is meant (use "just before"); avoid if specific duration is needed
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: shortly before midnight, shortly before departure, shortly before noon
   - **Verb + prep**: arrived shortly before, happened shortly before, died shortly before
   - **Temporal adverbial**: This occurred shortly before the crisis.
   
   **Root Analysis**: shortly (brief time) + before (earlier than)
   
   **Etymology**: Formal temporal construction (Modern English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He left shortly before you got here.
   - I ate shortly before the meeting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The minister resigned shortly before the election.
   - Symptoms appeared shortly before the diagnosis.

   **When to Use**: Emphasizing significant time gap between events; common in historical contexts and narratives

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Preposition**: never before (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* At no previous time; for the first time ever; without precedent
   - Emphasizes novelty or unprecedented nature
   
   **Synonyms**: not ever before, for the first time, unprecedented, without precedent
   
   **Antonyms**: many times before, previously, often before
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing unprecedented nature or first occurrence; common in dramatic statements and historical significance
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exaggeration should be avoided; not for casual frequency statements
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: never before seen, never before witnessed, never before experienced
   - **Sentence-initial**: Never before has [subject] [verb]
   - **Emphatic inversion**: Never before had we seen such conditions.
   
   **Root Analysis**: never (at no time) + before (in the past)
   
   **Etymology**: Emphatic negation construction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've never been here before.
   - We've never done this before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Never before has the organization faced such challenges.
   - The data reveals patterns never before observed in this population.

1. **Preposition**: as before (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Manner/Comparison)* In the same way as previously; unchanged from the previous state; continuing as earlier
   
   **Synonyms**: as previously, as earlier, the same as before, unchanged
   
   **Antonyms**: differently than before, changed from before, unlike before
   
   **When to Use**: Indicating continuity or lack of change; comparing current state with previous state
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When change has occurred; avoid if comparison is not intended
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: continue as before, proceed as before, remain as before
   - **Verb + prep**: function as before, operate as before, work as before
   - **Comparative construction**: Things are as before.
   
   **Root Analysis**: as (in the manner of) + before (previous time)
   
   **Etymology**: Comparative temporal construction
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's just keep things as before.
   - Everything looks as before.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Operations will continue as before during the transition period.
   - The policy remains as before, with minor amendments.

1. **Preposition**: before one's time (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal/Abstract)* Before someone was born or before they were involved; in an earlier era
   - Too advanced or progressive for the current period; ahead of contemporary understanding
   
   **Synonyms**: in an earlier era, before one's birth, ahead of one's time (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: during one's time, in one's era, contemporary
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to events/people from before someone's experience; describing ideas too advanced for their era
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When contemporaneous events are meant; distinguish between senses carefully
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: before my time, before your time, before our time
   - **Idiomatic use**: ahead of their time (progressive ideas)
   - **Historical reference**: That was before my time.
   
   **Root Analysis**: before (earlier than) + one's + time (era/period)
   
   **Etymology**: Idiomatic temporal reference phrase
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That movie came out before my time.
   - Sorry, that's before my time here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The architect's designs were before their time, anticipating future trends.
   - These events occurred before my tenure at the institution.

1. **Preposition**: up before (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract/Legal)* Appearing for judgment, review, or consideration by an authority; brought to face
   
   **Synonyms**: appearing before, brought before, facing, presenting to
   
   **Antonyms**: dismissed from, excused from
   
   **When to Use**: Legal contexts, formal proceedings, disciplinary situations; indicates formal presentation to authority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual meetings; not for informal encounters
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: up before the judge, up before the committee, up before the board
   - **Verb + prep**: come up before, brought up before, go up before
   - **Legal context**: The case is up before the court.
   
   **Root Analysis**: up (to a position) + before (in the presence of)
   
   **Etymology**: Legal terminology combining directional "up" with formal "before"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He's up before the principal tomorrow.
   - When are you up before the review board?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant will be up before the magistrate on Monday.
   - The proposal is up before the executive committee next week.
