1. **Noun**: precedent (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An earlier event, action, or legal decision that is regarded as an example or guide for similar circumstances
   - An established practice or standard that serves as a model for future cases
   
   **Synonyms**: example, model, pattern, standard, guideline, prototype
   
   **Antonyms**: novelty, innovation, first instance, unprecedented case
   
   **When to Use**: Legal contexts, policy discussions, decision-making processes; formal and professional registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual situations; when describing something truly new (use "first" or "new")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - set a precedent
   - establish a precedent
   - legal precedent
   - follow precedent
   - break with precedent
   - without precedent
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + ced (go) + -ent (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praecedere (to go before) → Old French → English legal terminology (15th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We can't do that; it would set a bad precedent.
   - There's no precedent for this kind of situation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The ruling establishes an important legal precedent for future cases.
   - This decision breaks with established precedent in administrative law.

1. **Noun**: predecessor (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who held a position or job before the current holder; previous occupant
   - Something that came before another thing and was replaced or succeeded by it
   
   **Synonyms**: forerunner, precursor, antecedent, previous holder, former occupant
   
   **Antonyms**: successor, replacement, heir, follower
   
   **When to Use**: Professional contexts, organizational history, technological evolution; formal and neutral registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing contemporaries or successors; distinguish from "ancestor" (family lineage)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - immediate predecessor
   - illustrious predecessor
   - predecessor in office
   - unlike their predecessor
   - build on predecessor's work
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + de- (away) + cess (go) + -or (agent noun)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praedecessor (previous holder of office) → Late Latin → English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The new manager is very different from her predecessor.
   - This phone is way better than its predecessor.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The current administration has adopted policies diverging from those of its predecessor.
   - This model demonstrates significant improvements over its predecessor.

1. **Noun**: antecedent (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thing or event that existed before or logically precedes another; a precursor
   - *(Grammar)* A word, phrase, or clause referred to by a pronoun
   - *(Plural: antecedents)* A person's ancestors, origins, or past history
   
   **Synonyms**: precursor, forerunner, predecessor, prior event, previous circumstance
   
   **Antonyms**: consequent, result, outcome, successor, descendant
   
   **When to Use**: Formal academic contexts, grammar instruction, historical analysis, causal discussions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation (prefer "what came before"); very technical in grammar sense
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - historical antecedents
   - antecedent conditions
   - antecedent events
   - pronoun and its antecedent
   - trace antecedents
   
   **Root Analysis**: ante- (before) + ced (go) + -ent (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin antecedere (to go before) → Middle English → formal temporal/causal noun
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rare in casual use)
   - What are the antecedents of this problem?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Historical antecedents of the current crisis can be traced to policy failures.
   - The pronoun "it" must clearly refer to its antecedent in the sentence.

1. **Noun**: forerunner (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that precedes and indicates the approach of something else; harbinger
   - An earlier version or precursor that paves the way for later developments
   
   **Synonyms**: precursor, predecessor, harbinger, herald, advance sign, pioneer
   
   **Antonyms**: successor, follower, result, outcome
   
   **When to Use**: Describing early versions, advance signs, pioneers; historical and technological contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing exact contemporaries or successors
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - forerunner of modern
   - forerunner to
   - early forerunner
   - direct forerunner
   - considered a forerunner
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before) + runner (one who runs)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English: fore + runner → one who runs ahead/before → precursor
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That device was the forerunner of modern smartphones.
   - Dark clouds are the forerunner of rain.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The Analytical Engine is considered a forerunner of modern computers.
   - Early symptoms may serve as forerunners of more serious conditions.

1. **Noun**: precursor (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person or thing that comes before another of the same kind; forerunner
   - A substance from which another substance is formed, especially in biological or chemical processes
   
   **Synonyms**: forerunner, predecessor, harbinger, herald, antecedent, prototype
   
   **Antonyms**: successor, result, product, outcome, derivative
   
   **When to Use**: Scientific contexts (chemistry, biology), historical evolution, technological development; formal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation; distinguish from "prerequisite" (requirement rather than temporal predecessor)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - precursor to
   - chemical precursor
   - direct precursor
   - evolutionary precursor
   - early precursor
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + curs (run) + -or (agent noun)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praecursor (forerunner) → English via French (16th century) → scientific terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Silent films were the precursor to modern movies.
   - That feeling is often a precursor to illness.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The compound serves as a biochemical precursor in the synthesis pathway.
   - Medieval manuscripts are precursors to modern printed books.

1. **Noun**: priority (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The fact or condition of being regarded or treated as more important; precedence
   - Something regarded as more important than others; a thing that is given special attention
   - The right to take precedence or proceed before others; temporal precedence
   
   **Synonyms**: precedence, importance, urgency, preference, primacy, preeminence
   
   **Antonyms**: low priority, unimportance, secondary concern, afterthought
   
   **When to Use**: Planning, time management, decision-making, organizational contexts; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When equal importance is meant; distinguish from "preference" (personal choice)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - top priority
   - high priority
   - give priority to
   - take priority
   - priority list
   - order of priority
   
   **Root Analysis**: prior (earlier/more important) + -ity (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prior (former, superior) → prioritas → English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My family is my top priority.
   - This project takes priority over everything else.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Strategic planning requires establishing clear priorities.
   - Budget allocations reflect organizational priorities.

1. **Noun**: advance (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Forward movement or progress; development or improvement
   - Money paid before it is due; payment made ahead of time
   - *(In advance)* Beforehand; ahead of time; before an event occurs
   
   **Synonyms**: progress, progression, advancement, development, forward movement, prepayment
   
   **Antonyms**: retreat, regression, setback, delay, payment afterward
   
   **When to Use**: Progress contexts, payment arrangements, military contexts, planning; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When regression or retreat is meant; distinguish noun from verb
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in advance
   - advance payment
   - advance notice
   - advance warning
   - technological advance
   - make advances
   
   **Root Analysis**: ad- (to) + vance (from Latin vadere "to go")
   
   **Etymology**: Old French avancer (move forward) → English noun (13th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can I get an advance on my salary?
   - Let me know in advance if you can't make it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Recent advances in technology have transformed communication.
   - Payment in advance is required to secure the reservation.

1. **Noun**: preparation (C/U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The action or process of making something ready for use or consideration; getting ready
   - Something done to get ready for an event or undertaking; preparatory measures
   - A substance specially prepared or manufactured; a mixture
   
   **Synonyms**: readiness, arrangement, groundwork, planning, provision, setup
   
   **Antonyms**: unpreparedness, spontaneity, improvisation, aftermath
   
   **When to Use**: Planning contexts, event organization, academic study, manufacturing; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing completed actions or final products (unless referring to manufactured preparations)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - in preparation for
   - make preparations
   - advance preparation
   - careful preparation
   - lack of preparation
   - preparation time
   
   **Root Analysis**: prepare (make ready) + -ation (noun suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praeparare (make ready beforehand) + -ation → English (14th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to start preparation for the party.
   - The team's preparation was really thorough.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Adequate preparation is essential for successful project implementation.
   - Preparations for the conference began six months in advance.

1. **Noun**: prerequisite (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A thing required as a prior condition for something else to happen or be done; requirement
   - Something that must be completed or achieved before something else can occur
   
   **Synonyms**: requirement, precondition, necessity, essential, condition, qualification
   
   **Antonyms**: optional element, non-essential, consequence, result
   
   **When to Use**: Educational contexts (course requirements), procedural steps, qualifications; formal and neutral registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing optional elements; distinguish from "consequence" or "result"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prerequisite for
   - prerequisite course
   - meet prerequisites
   - essential prerequisite
   - prerequisite knowledge
   - fulfill prerequisites
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + requisite (required)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prae- (before) + requisitus (required) → English academic/procedural terminology (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Good communication is a prerequisite for any relationship.
   - You need to take Calculus I first; it's a prerequisite.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Completion of introductory courses is a prerequisite for enrollment in advanced seminars.
   - Stakeholder agreement is a prerequisite for project approval.

1. **Noun**: precondition (C)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A condition that must be fulfilled before something else can happen or be done; prerequisite
   - A circumstance or state that must exist beforehand
   
   **Synonyms**: prerequisite, requirement, condition, stipulation, necessity, essential
   
   **Antonyms**: consequence, result, outcome, non-essential
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts, negotiations, procedural requirements, causal relationships
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing results or outcomes; avoid confusion with "preconception" (assumption)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - precondition for
   - essential precondition
   - meet preconditions
   - establish preconditions
   - precondition of success
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + condition (circumstance/requirement)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prae- (before) + condition → English formal terminology (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Trust is a precondition for any good friendship.
   - Meeting the preconditions took longer than expected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Economic stability is a precondition for sustainable development.
   - Fulfillment of technical preconditions is mandatory before system deployment.

1. **Noun**: forethought (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Careful thought or planning done in advance; consideration of future needs or circumstances
   - The action of thinking beforehand; anticipatory planning
   
   **Synonyms**: planning, foresight, anticipation, preparation, prudence, premeditation
   
   **Antonyms**: spontaneity, improvisation, thoughtlessness, hindsight, afterthought
   
   **When to Use**: Describing careful planning, strategic thinking, prudent preparation; formal and neutral registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing spontaneous or reactive decisions; distinguish from "afterthought"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - with forethought
   - lack of forethought
   - careful forethought
   - require forethought
   - without forethought
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before) + thought (mental consideration)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English fore + thought → Middle English → planning/anticipation noun
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A little forethought could have prevented this mess.
   - That shows real forethought on your part.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Successful implementation requires considerable forethought and planning.
   - The design demonstrates remarkable forethought regarding future expansion needs.

1. **Noun**: foresight (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The ability to predict or plan for future events and needs; vision
   - Careful consideration of probable consequences; prudent anticipation
   
   **Synonyms**: vision, prescience, anticipation, far-sightedness, prudence, forethought
   
   **Antonyms**: hindsight, shortsightedness, improvidence, lack of planning
   
   **When to Use**: Describing visionary planning, strategic thinking, anticipation of future needs; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing past analysis (use "hindsight"); when luck rather than planning is involved
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - have the foresight to
   - lack foresight
   - with foresight
   - strategic foresight
   - remarkable foresight
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore (before) + sight (vision/perception)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English fore + sight → ability to see ahead → anticipatory planning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She had the foresight to save money before the crisis.
   - That decision shows real foresight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Strategic foresight enables organizations to anticipate market disruptions.
   - The architect's foresight in designing flexible spaces proved invaluable.
