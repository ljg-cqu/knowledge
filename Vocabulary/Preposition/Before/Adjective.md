1. **Adjective**: beforehand (non-gradable, predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Arranged, prepared, or done in advance of an event or action; prior to the main event
   - Existing or occurring at an earlier time; preliminary
   
   **Synonyms**: in advance, previously, ahead of time, pre-arranged, preparatory
   
   **Antonyms**: afterward, subsequently, later, post-event
   
   **When to Use**: Describing preparation, planning, or arrangements made in advance; scheduling and organizational contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not typically used attributively (✗ "beforehand preparations"); use as adverb instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - know beforehand
   - arrange beforehand
   - prepare beforehand
   - inform beforehand
   - plan beforehand
   
   **Root Analysis**: before (earlier) + hand (archaic "time/position")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English (14th century) from "before" + "hand" (meaning "in advance") → adverbial/predicative adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I wish you'd told me beforehand!
   - Everything was set up beforehand.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants must register beforehand to secure a place.
   - All materials were distributed beforehand to allow adequate review time.

1. **Adjective**: before-mentioned (non-gradable, attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Previously mentioned or cited in an earlier part of a text or discussion
   - Referred to in a preceding statement or document
   
   **Synonyms**: aforementioned, previously mentioned, above-mentioned, aforesaid, foregoing
   
   **Antonyms**: following, subsequent, later-mentioned, forthcoming
   
   **When to Use**: Formal written communication, legal documents, academic papers when referring back to previously discussed items
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; sounds overly formal; less common than "aforementioned" in modern usage
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the before-mentioned individual
   - before-mentioned factors
   - before-mentioned conditions
   - before-mentioned requirements
   - as per before-mentioned guidelines
   
   **Root Analysis**: before (earlier in text) + mentioned (past participle of "mention")
   
   **Etymology**: Legal and documentary language (17th century) → formal reference terminology
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used casually; more natural: "the person I mentioned" or "what I said earlier")
   - The before-mentioned restaurant was excellent.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The before-mentioned criteria must be satisfied before proceeding.
   - Applicants meeting the before-mentioned qualifications should apply immediately.

1. **Adjective**: before-the-fact (non-gradable, attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Occurring or existing before an event, especially before a crime or wrongdoing; preventive or preparatory
   - Relating to knowledge, planning, or involvement prior to an action
   
   **Synonyms**: preemptive, preventive, prior, anticipatory, preparatory
   
   **Antonyms**: after-the-fact, retrospective, post-event, reactive
   
   **When to Use**: Legal contexts (accessory before the fact), planning contexts, preventive measures; emphasizes temporal priority
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing responses or reactions (use "after-the-fact"); avoid in casual contexts
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - before-the-fact knowledge
   - before-the-fact planning
   - before-the-fact involvement
   - accessory before the fact (legal term)
   - before-the-fact preparation
   
   **Root Analysis**: before + the + fact (event/occurrence)
   
   **Etymology**: Legal terminology (18th century) distinguishing prior involvement from subsequent
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I had before-the-fact knowledge about the surprise party.
   - This requires before-the-fact planning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The defendant was charged as an accessory before the fact.
   - Before-the-fact risk assessment is essential for project success.

1. **Adjective**: pre-existing (gradable, attributive/predicative)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Existing before a particular time, condition, or event; already present prior to a reference point
   - Preceding in time or existence; antecedent
   
   **Synonyms**: previous, prior, antecedent, pre-established, earlier
   
   **Antonyms**: new, subsequent, later, post-existing, emerging
   
   **When to Use**: Medical contexts (pre-existing conditions), legal contracts, insurance, describing prior states or conditions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When simultaneous occurrence is meant; avoid redundancy with "previous"
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pre-existing condition
   - pre-existing relationship
   - pre-existing agreement
   - pre-existing circumstances
   - pre-existing damage
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + existing (present participle of "exist")
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prefix "prae-" + English "existing" → medical and legal terminology (19th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you have any pre-existing medical conditions?
   - There was some pre-existing damage to the car.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The insurance policy excludes coverage for pre-existing conditions.
   - Pre-existing relationships between parties may constitute conflicts of interest.

1. **Adjective**: prior (non-gradable, attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Existing or occurring before in time, order, or importance; preceding; earlier
   - Having precedence or priority; more important or urgent
   
   **Synonyms**: previous, earlier, preceding, antecedent, former
   
   **Antonyms**: subsequent, later, following, posterior, succeeding
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts indicating temporal or logical precedence; scheduling, legal documents, academic writing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very casual conversation (prefer "earlier" or "before"); when exact simultaneity is meant
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - prior experience
   - prior knowledge
   - prior approval
   - prior engagement
   - prior commitment
   - without prior notice
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin "prior" (former, superior)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prior (former, first of two) → English (15th century) via French → formal temporal adjective
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Do you have any prior experience with this?
   - Sorry, I have a prior commitment.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Prior approval is required before proceeding with implementation.
   - Candidates must demonstrate prior knowledge of advanced methodologies.

1. **Adjective**: previous (non-gradable, attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Existing or occurring before the present time or before something else in time or order
   - Immediately preceding in time or sequence
   
   **Synonyms**: earlier, prior, preceding, former, past
   
   **Antonyms**: next, following, subsequent, later, succeeding
   
   **When to Use**: General temporal references, comparing current and past states, indicating sequence; all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact simultaneity is meant; distinguish from "prior" (more formal)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - previous experience
   - previous occasion
   - previous chapter
   - previous owner
   - previous year
   - on a previous occasion
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + via (way) + -ous (adjectival suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin praevius (going before) → French précédent → English "previous" (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Have we met on a previous occasion?
   - This is way better than the previous version.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Results were compared with data from the previous fiscal year.
   - The previous administration implemented significant policy changes.

1. **Adjective**: preliminary (gradable, attributive/predicative)

   - Coming before the main event, action, or matter; introductory or preparatory
   - Not final; subject to revision or confirmation; tentative
   
   **Synonyms**: preparatory, introductory, initial, provisional, tentative, opening
   
   **Antonyms**: final, conclusive, definitive, ultimate, concluding

   **Common Phrases**: 
   - preliminary results
   - preliminary investigation
   - preliminary findings
   - preliminary agreement
   - preliminary stage
   - preliminary hearing
   
   **Root Analysis**: pre- (before) + limin (threshold) + -ary (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin prae- (before) + limen (threshold) → French préliminaire → English (17th century)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These are just preliminary results, nothing final yet.
   - We had a preliminary chat about the project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Preliminary findings suggest a significant correlation between variables.
   - The preliminary hearing is scheduled for next Tuesday.

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Precedent conditions must be examined to understand current outcomes.
   - Analysis of precedent circumstances reveals patterns of behavior.

1. **Adjective**: foregoing (non-gradable, attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Previously mentioned or stated; going before in place or time; preceding
   - Just mentioned; above-stated (in written texts)
   
   **Synonyms**: preceding, previous, aforementioned, above-mentioned, prior
   
   **Antonyms**: following, subsequent, forthcoming, later
   
   **When to Use**: Formal written documents, legal texts, academic papers when referring to previously stated information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual conversation; distinguish from "forgoing" (giving up); sounds archaic in modern speech
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - the foregoing discussion
   - the foregoing analysis
   - the foregoing paragraphs
   - in light of the foregoing
   - based on the foregoing
   
   **Root Analysis**: fore- (before) + going (present participle of "go")
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English (14th century) fore + going → formal reference term
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - (Rarely used casually; more natural: "what I just said" or "the earlier discussion")
   - Based on the foregoing, we should proceed carefully.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The foregoing analysis demonstrates significant methodological limitations.
   - In consideration of the foregoing, the committee recommends approval.
