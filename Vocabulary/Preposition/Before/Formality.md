1. **Original Expression**: I've done this before  
   **Context**: Expressing previous experience with a task or activity
   
   **Core Intent**: Indicate familiarity or prior experience with something
   
   **Casual**: I've done this before  
   *Characteristics*: Simple past perfect, conversational, direct  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, informal work settings, everyday situations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: I have previous experience with this  
   *Characteristics*: More professional vocabulary ("previous experience"), neutral tone  
   *Use when*: Job interviews, workplace discussions, polite professional contexts
   
   **Formal**: I possess prior experience in this area  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "possess," technical term "prior experience," abstract noun "area"  
   *Use when*: Formal applications, academic settings, official documents, presentations
   
   **Humorous**: Been there, done that, got the t-shirt  
   *Characteristics*: Idiomatic expression, playful exaggeration, cultural reference  
   *Use when*: Casual settings with good rapport, lightening mood, showing confidence  
   *Avoid when*: Formal settings, could sound dismissive, talking to superiors
   
   **Lively**: Oh yeah, I've totally been through this before!  
   *Characteristics*: Intensifiers ("totally"), exclamation, enthusiastic tone  
   *Use when*: Encouraging others, sharing enthusiasm, building rapport
   
   **Empathetic**: I understand because I've been through this before myself  
   *Characteristics*: Explicit empathy statement, includes "myself" for connection, acknowledges shared experience  
   *Use when*: Supporting someone, difficult situations, mentoring, counseling
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status/formal settings require "possess prior experience"; peers allow casual forms
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers/professionals need semi-formal; close relationships allow casual/humorous
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious professional contexts need formal; light social contexts allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures value humility over direct experience claims
   - *Medium*: Written applications need formal; spoken conversation allows casual
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using humorous form in serious situations (sounds dismissive or arrogant)
   - Using "possess" in casual speech (sounds pretentious)
   - Claiming experience without evidence in formal contexts (needs substantiation)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some cultures, directly stating experience may seem boastful; prefer indirect forms or wait to be asked
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Job interview → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal or formal ("I have previous experience...")
   - **Situation 2**: Friend asking for help → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("I've done this before")
   - **Situation 3**: Reassuring nervous colleague → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("I understand because I've been through this...")

   **Casual**: We gotta finish this before the deadline  
   *Characteristics*: Contraction "gotta" (have to), direct, urgent tone  
   *Use when*: Team members at same level, familiar workplace, time pressure
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We need to complete this before the deadline  
   *Characteristics*: Formal verb "complete" instead of "finish," clear and direct  
   *Use when*: Standard workplace communication, team meetings, project discussions
   
   **Formal**: It is imperative that we complete this prior to the deadline  
   *Characteristics*: Impersonal construction "it is imperative," formal "prior to," passive undertone  
   *Use when*: Official communications, senior management, written reports, high-stakes situations
   
   **Humorous**: We better hustle or we're toast!  
   *Characteristics*: Slang "hustle" and "toast," playful urgency, informal metaphor  
   *Use when*: Lightening tense situations, motivating peers with humor, good team rapport  
   *Avoid when*: Formal meetings, superiors may not appreciate casualness, actual crisis
   
   **Lively**: Come on team, let's power through and beat that deadline!  
   *Characteristics*: Motivational language, energetic verbs "power through" and "beat," inclusive "team"  
   *Use when*: Motivating team, building energy, sports/competition metaphors appropriate
   
   **Empathetic**: I know it's tight, but let's work together to finish this before the deadline  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges difficulty ("it's tight"), collaborative "work together," supportive tone  
   *Use when*: Leading stressed team, acknowledging challenges, building solidarity
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Speaking to superiors requires formal; peers allow casual/lively
   - *Social Distance*: Professional acquaintances need semi-formal; close team allows casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Critical deadlines with consequences need formal/semi-formal; routine tasks allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Hierarchical cultures require formal to superiors; flat structures allow casual
   - *Medium*: Email/written needs semi-formal or formal; in-person allows wider range
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using humorous form when deadline has serious consequences (undermines urgency)
   - Using formal form with peers in casual setting (creates distance)
   - Using "gotta" in written professional communication (too casual)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Direct urgency expressions vary by culture; some prefer indirect suggestions over direct statements
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Email to senior management → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("It is imperative that we complete...")
   - **Situation 2**: Chat with project team → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal or lively ("Let's complete this..." or "Let's power through...")
   - **Situation 3**: Team under stress → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("I know it's tight, but let's work together...")

   *Characteristics*: Simple present perfect, direct question, conversational  
   *Use when*: Social gatherings, informal events, peers, ambiguous recognition
   
   **Semi-Formal**: Have we been introduced? / I believe we may have met previously  
   *Characteristics*: Polite indirection ("I believe"), passive "been introduced," hedged "may have"  
   *Use when*: Professional networking, conferences, business events, showing respect
   
   **Formal**: I believe we may have been acquainted previously  
   *Characteristics*: Formal "acquainted," hedged with "I believe" and "may have," sophisticated vocabulary  
   *Use when*: Very formal events, dignitaries, academic conferences, written correspondence
   
   **Humorous**: Your face looks familiar—have we committed crimes together?  
   *Characteristics*: Absurd scenario, playful exaggeration, icebreaker humor  
   *Use when*: Casual social settings, showing personality, appropriate humor context  
   *Avoid when*: Professional contexts, could offend, actual awkward situations
   
   **Lively**: Hey! Your face totally rings a bell! Where do I know you from?  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic greeting, colloquial "rings a bell," energetic punctuation  
   *Use when*: Friendly casual contexts, showing warmth, social gatherings
   
   **Empathetic**: I'm sorry if I should remember you—have we met before?  
   *Characteristics*: Apologetic opening, acknowledges potential failure to remember, polite question  
   *Use when*: When other person clearly recognizes you, showing consideration, avoiding awkwardness
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status individuals require formal/semi-formal; peers allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: First encounters need semi-formal; familiar contexts allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Professional networking needs semi-formal; social events allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures value formal introductions; Western contexts more casual
   - *Medium*: Face-to-face allows full range; written follow-up needs semi-formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using humorous form with someone who clearly doesn't appreciate humor (awkward)
   - Not using empathetic form when person obviously knows you (seems rude)
   - Over-formality in clearly casual contexts (creates distance)
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Memory for faces varies culturally in importance; some cultures more forgiving of forgetting
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Professional conference → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("Have we been introduced?")
   - **Situation 2**: Casual party → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual or lively ("Have we met before?" or "Your face rings a bell!")
   - **Situation 3**: Person clearly recognizes you → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("I'm sorry if I should remember you...")
