1. **Functional Expression**: Let's start with a light warm-up. (function: giving instructions / starting session)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Signals the beginning of an exercise session and directs people to begin with easy movement.
   - *Formality*: neutral/informal
   - *Directness*: direct but friendly
   
   **Synonyms**: We’ll begin with a gentle warm-up., First, we’ll warm up a bit., Let’s ease in with some light movement.
   
   **Antonyms**: Jump straight into the hardest set. (opposite effect: no preparation)
   
   **When to Use**: At the start of a class, training session, or home workout when guiding others through the first phase.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the group has already warmed up independently; in very formal medical contexts, rephrase more precisely.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let’s start with a gentle warm-up.
   - We’ll begin today with some light mobility work.
   - First, let’s get the body moving with a short warm-up.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Let’s" includes the speaker and creates a sense of shared effort; tone is encouraging rather than commanding.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay! / Sounds good.
   - Do we need any equipment for the warm-up?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let’s start with a light warm-up—just march in place and roll your shoulders.
   - Alright team, we’ll start with a light warm-up so nobody shocks their muscles.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We will begin the session with a brief warm-up to prepare the major muscle groups.
   - The instructor will start with light warm-up activities to reduce injury risk.

1. **Functional Expression**: If anything hurts, please stop and tell me. (function: giving safety instructions / inviting feedback)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Sets a safety rule and explicitly encourages participants to report pain.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct, with polite request
   
   **Synonyms**: If you feel any pain, let me know immediately., If something doesn’t feel right, please stop., If this bothers your joints, don’t push through it.
   
   **Antonyms**: No pain, no gain—keep going no matter what. (dangerous, opposite guidance)
   
   **When to Use**: At the start of group classes, personal training sessions, or rehab exercises.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid softening this too much; safety instructions should stay clear. In large online videos, you may add "consult your doctor" instead of "tell me."
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - If you experience sharp pain, please stop and let me know.
   - If this feels wrong for your body, stop and raise your hand.
   - Don’t push through pain—stop and talk to me.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Balances authority and care; helps prevent people from hiding discomfort out of pride.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, I’ll tell you if it hurts.
   - My knee feels tight—is that okay, or should I stop?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If anything hurts, just stop and tell me right away.
   - Don’t be a hero—if your back complains, stop and talk to me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If participants experience any pain during the exercise, they should stop immediately and inform the instructor.
   - Please discontinue the movement and notify the therapist if you feel sharp or unusual pain.

1. **Functional Expression**: You might want to slow down a little. (function: softening correction / giving feedback)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Gently suggests that someone reduce intensity or speed without sounding harsh.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect/hedged
   
   **Synonyms**: It could help to ease the pace., Try taking it a bit easier., Consider slowing down slightly.
   
   **Antonyms**: Go as hard as you can right now., Don’t slow down at all. (opposite directive)
   
   **When to Use**: Coaching someone who is pushing too hard, losing form, or returning after a break.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when urgent, non-negotiable safety action is needed—then use a clear command ("Stop.").
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - You might want to take shorter steps.
   - You might want to lower the weight for this set.
   - You might want to give yourself an extra rest day.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: The modal "might" softens the suggestion and respects autonomy, which is useful with adults and peers.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, I’ll slow down a bit.
   - Does this pace look better?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You’re breathing really hard—you might want to slow down a little.
   - You just came back from injury; you might want to skip the last round today.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given your current symptoms, you might want to reduce the running volume this week.
   - Early in rehabilitation, patients might want to slow down and focus on controlled movement rather than intensity.

1. **Functional Expression**: Great job—let’s take a short break. (function: encouraging + managing pace)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Combines praise with a clear instruction to rest briefly.
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: direct, wrapped in encouragement
   
   **Synonyms**: Well done, now we’ll rest for a moment., Nice work—grab some water., That was solid; let’s pause for a bit.
   
   **Antonyms**: That wasn’t enough—keep going with no break. (discouraging, opposite pacing)
   
   **When to Use**: Between sets or intervals to reinforce effort and control session pacing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid mechanical overuse; vary praise. In very formal clinical settings, tone may shift to "You may rest now."
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Great job—grab some water and we’ll start the next round.
   - Nice work; let’s take a short breather.
   - Excellent effort—rest for 30 seconds.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Positive feedback increases motivation and makes rest feel earned rather than like failure.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks, I needed that break.
   - That was tough—I’m glad we’re resting.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Great job—let’s take a short break and shake out the legs.
   - Awesome work, everyone; quick break, then we’ll do one more round.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - You have completed the set; great job—now let’s take a brief rest period.
   - After this block, participants will receive positive feedback and a short break.
