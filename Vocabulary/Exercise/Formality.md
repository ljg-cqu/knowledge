1. **Original Expression**: You need to exercise more.  
   **Context**: Giving feedback about someone’s activity level or health.
   
   **Core Intent**: To encourage a person to increase how often or how much they move for health or fitness.
   
   **Casual**: You really should work out more.  
   *Characteristics*: Direct, mild pressure word ("should"), everyday verb ("work out").  
   *Use when*: Talking with close friends or family in informal settings.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: It would be good for your health to be more active.  
   *Characteristics*: Softer modal ("would be"), health-focused framing, less personal criticism.  
   *Use when*: Doctors, trainers, or colleagues give general advice without sounding judgmental.
   
   **Formal**: Increasing your level of regular physical activity is strongly recommended to improve your health outcomes.  
   *Characteristics*: Impersonal tone, formal vocabulary ("level of regular physical activity," "health outcomes"), no contractions.  
   *Use when*: Medical reports, written recommendations, official guidelines.
   
   **Humorous**: Your sneakers are starting to feel abandoned—we should fix that.  
   *Characteristics*: Personification ("sneakers feel abandoned"), playful tone, indirect nudge.  
   *Use when*: Friendly coaching, low-stakes settings, social media posts.  
   *Avoid when*: Serious medical contexts or with people who are sensitive about weight or health.
   
   **Lively**: Let’s get you moving regularly so you feel stronger, lighter, and more energized.  
   *Characteristics*: Positive, future-focused, concrete benefits, inclusive "let’s."  
   *Use when*: Motivational talks, group classes, onboarding new clients.
   
   **Empathetic**: I know it can be hard to find time and energy, but adding a bit more movement into your week could really help you feel better.  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges difficulty, focuses on how the person feels, gentle suggestion.  
   *Use when*: One-on-one coaching, health conversations with loved ones, sensitive situations.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: With doctors or official guidelines → Formal; between peers → Casual/Lively.
   - *Social Distance*: Greater distance → Semi-Formal or Empathetic; close relationships allow Casual or Humorous.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Serious risk factors → Formal/Empathetic; general wellness → Lively or Semi-Formal.
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures view direct health advice as intrusive; soften with Empathetic framing.
   - *Medium*: Written medical notes → Formal; group classes and newsletters → Lively.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Sounding accusatory ("You’re too lazy") instead of focusing on behavior.
   - Pushing too hard without asking about barriers.
   - Ignoring emotional or cultural reasons someone may avoid exercise.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some contexts, weight and fitness are very sensitive topics; prioritize respect, consent, and autonomy when using any variant of this message.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Clinic summary letter → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Increasing your level of regular physical activity is strongly recommended...").
   - **Situation 2**: Trainer talking to new client → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively/Empathetic ("Let’s get you moving regularly..." / "I know it can be hard...").
   - **Situation 3**: Friend conversation → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Humorous (depending on trust).

1. **Original Expression**: I don’t have time to work out.  
   **Context**: Explaining why someone is not exercising.
   
   **Core Intent**: To express that a busy schedule is the main barrier to exercise.
   
   **Casual**: I’m just too busy to work out.  
   *Characteristics*: Direct, colloquial ("too busy"), slight exaggeration sometimes.  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, venting about schedule.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: My current schedule makes it difficult to fit in regular exercise.  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral tone, focuses on schedule rather than personal failure.  
   *Use when*: Talking with a coach, manager, or healthcare provider.
   
   **Formal**: Due to existing commitments, I am currently unable to allocate consistent time for structured physical activity.  
   *Characteristics*: Impersonal, nominalized phrases ("existing commitments," "structured physical activity"), no blame.  
   *Use when*: Written explanations, formal emails, program intake forms.
   
   **Humorous**: My calendar does more cardio than I do right now.  
   *Characteristics*: Metaphor, self-deprecating humor, light tone.  
   *Use when*: Light conversations, social media, friendly coaching where humor is safe.  
   *Avoid when*: With people experiencing guilt or shame about inactivity.
   
   **Lively**: My days are packed, and by the time I stop, I feel like I’ve already run a marathon.  
   *Characteristics*: Vivid metaphor, emotional emphasis, relatable description.  
   *Use when*: Group discussions, workshops exploring barriers to exercise.
   
   **Empathetic**: It really feels like there isn’t any time left in the day, and that makes it hard to even think about working out.  
   *Characteristics*: Validates feelings, shifts focus from blame to shared understanding.  
   *Use when*: Coaching conversations, therapy contexts, supportive discussions.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: With healthcare professionals → Semi-Formal/Formal; with peers → Casual/Lively.
   - *Social Distance*: More distance → avoid heavy self-deprecation; choose Semi-Formal or Empathetic.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: When health is at risk → move from Humorous to Empathetic and solution-focused.
   - *Cultural Context*: In some cultures, admitting lack of time may signal status; respond carefully.
   - *Medium*: Intake forms → Formal; coaching calls → Semi-Formal/Empathetic.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Responding with judgment ("You just need to make time") instead of exploring constraints.
   - Ignoring structural barriers like shift work, caregiving, or unsafe environments.
   - Using guilt as motivation, which often backfires.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Busy schedules can be tied to work identity and family duty; solutions should respect these values.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Health questionnaire → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Due to existing commitments...").
   - **Situation 2**: Trainer-client chat → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal/Empathetic ("My current schedule makes it difficult..." / "It really feels like there isn’t any time...").
   - **Situation 3**: Joking with a close friend → **Appropriate Variant**: Humorous ("My calendar does more cardio than I do...").
