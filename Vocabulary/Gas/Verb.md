# Gas Vocabulary - Verbs

1. **Verb**: emit
   **Meaning**: 
   - To produce and discharge (something, especially gas or radiation) from a source.
   - To send out or give off (light, sound, smell, or gas).
   
   **Synonyms**: release, discharge, give off, expel, produce
   
   **Antonyms**: absorb, take in, capture, sequester
   
   **When to Use**: Environmental science, physics, chemistry, discussions about pollution, radiation, or any process that releases substances.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing intake or absorption processes; avoid for intentional controlled releases where "release" is more appropriate.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - emit carbon dioxide
   - emit greenhouse gases
   - emit radiation
   - emit pollutants
   - emit heat
   
   **Root Analysis**: e- (out) + mit (send)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin emittere (to send out) → English emit (1600s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Cars emit a lot of exhaust fumes in heavy traffic.
   - The factory emits tons of CO2 every day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Industrial facilities that emit more than 25,000 tons of CO2 annually must report their emissions.
   - The decay process emits methane gas as organic matter decomposes.

1. **Verb**: compress
   **Meaning**: 
   - To reduce the volume of (something) by applying pressure; to squeeze or press together.
   - To reduce in size or force into less space by mechanical means.
   
   **Synonyms**: squeeze, condense, compact, press, constrict
   
   **Antonyms**: expand, decompress, stretch, enlarge
   
   **When to Use**: Engineering, physics, gas storage and transportation, mechanics, computing (data compression).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing expansion or stretching processes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - compress gas
   - compress air
   - highly compressed
   - compress data
   - compress the mixture
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + press (push)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin comprimere (to press together) → Old French compresser → English compress (1300s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The pump compresses the air before it goes into the tank.
   - You can compress natural gas to make it easier to transport.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The compressor unit compresses natural gas to 3,600 psi for pipeline transmission.
   - When gases are compressed adiabatically, their temperature increases proportionally.

1. **Verb**: expand
   **Meaning**: 
   - To increase in volume, size, or extent; to become larger or spread out.
   - (Of gases) To increase in volume when pressure decreases or temperature increases.
   
   **Synonyms**: grow, enlarge, swell, dilate, inflate
   
   **Antonyms**: compress, contract, shrink, condense
   
   **When to Use**: Physics, thermodynamics, gas behavior discussions, general growth or enlargement contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing compression or reduction processes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - expand rapidly
   - expand when heated
   - gas expands
   - expand to fill
   - thermal expansion
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out) + pand (spread)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin expandere (to spread out) → English expand (1400s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Gases expand when you heat them up.
   - The balloon expanded as we filled it with helium.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to Charles's Law, gases expand proportionally to absolute temperature at constant pressure.
   - The compressed gas expands rapidly upon release, causing a significant temperature drop.

1. **Verb**: ignite
   **Meaning**: 
   - To catch fire or cause to catch fire; to start burning.
   - To cause (a fuel mixture) to burn or explode, especially in an engine.
   
   **Synonyms**: light, kindle, inflame, spark, set fire to
   
   **Antonyms**: extinguish, quench, put out, douse
   
   **When to Use**: Fire safety, chemistry, combustion discussions, engine operation, emergency situations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For non-combustion chemical reactions or when fire prevention is the topic.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - ignite the fuel
   - ignite a spark
   - easily ignited
   - ignite combustion
   - ignition point
   
   **Root Analysis**: ig- (in) + nite (from ignis, fire)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin ignire (to set on fire, from ignis = fire) → English ignite (1600s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't smoke near the gas station—the fumes could ignite!
   - A single spark can ignite a gas leak.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The fuel-air mixture ignites when the spark plug generates an electrical discharge.
   - Natural gas will not ignite spontaneously below its autoignition temperature of 580°C.

1. **Verb**: diffuse
   **Meaning**: 
   - To spread over a wide area or between spaces; to become dispersed.
   - (Of gases or liquids) To intermingle by molecular motion, moving from areas of high concentration to low concentration.
   
   **Synonyms**: spread, disperse, disseminate, scatter, distribute
   
   **Antonyms**: concentrate, collect, gather, accumulate
   
   **When to Use**: Chemistry, physics, biology (membrane transport), gas behavior, spreading of substances or information.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing focused or concentrated distribution.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - diffuse through
   - diffuse rapidly
   - diffuse evenly
   - diffusion rate
   - diffuse across
   
   **Root Analysis**: dif- (apart) + fuse (pour)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin diffusus (poured out) → English diffuse (1400s as adjective, 1800s as verb in scientific sense)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The gas smell diffused throughout the whole house pretty quickly.
   - Open the windows so the fumes can diffuse.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Gas molecules diffuse from regions of high partial pressure to regions of low partial pressure.
   - Fick's laws describe how quickly substances diffuse through media.

1. **Verb**: pressurize
   **Meaning**: 
   - To increase the pressure within (a container or system) above normal atmospheric pressure.
   - To maintain normal atmospheric pressure in (an aircraft cabin or other enclosed space) at high altitude.
   
   **Synonyms**: compress, inflate, pump up, charge
   
   **Antonyms**: depressurize, decompress, vent
   
   **When to Use**: Aviation, gas storage, engineering systems, diving, industrial processes requiring controlled pressure.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing pressure reduction or venting processes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - pressurize the cabin
   - pressurize the tank
   - highly pressurized
   - pressurized gas
   - pressurize the system
   
   **Root Analysis**: pressure + -ize (verb suffix meaning "to make")
   
   **Etymology**: pressure (from Latin pressura) + -ize → English pressurize (1900s, aviation era)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They pressurize airplane cabins so passengers can breathe normally.
   - Make sure to pressurize the tank before you start welding.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The vessel must be pressurized to 150 bar before the gas transfer operation begins.
   - Aircraft cabins are typically pressurized to an equivalent altitude of 6,000-8,000 feet.

1. **Verb**: leak
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a container or covering) To allow gas, liquid, or light to pass through an unintended hole or crack.
   - To escape or allow to escape through such an opening.
   
   **Synonyms**: seep, escape, discharge, ooze, drip
   
   **Antonyms**: seal, contain, hold
   
   **When to Use**: Safety contexts, maintenance discussions, plumbing, gas fitting, quality control, emergency situations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For intentional releases or controlled discharges.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - gas is leaking
   - leak slowly
   - leak detection
   - prevent leaks
   - leak out
   
   **Root Analysis**: leak (from Old Norse leka)
   
   **Etymology**: Old Norse leka (to drip) → Middle English leken → Modern English leak (1400s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The pipe is leaking gas—we need to call someone right now.
   - I think the tank is leaking because the pressure keeps dropping.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If the system is leaking, evacuate the area and contact emergency services immediately.
   - The joint was leaking at a rate of approximately 2 cubic feet per hour.

1. **Verb**: vent
   **Meaning**: 
   - To provide an opening for gas, air, or liquid to escape from a confined space.
   - To release or discharge (gas or pressure) through an opening.
   
   **Synonyms**: release, discharge, expel, emit, exhaust
   
   **Antonyms**: seal, contain, trap, pressurize
   
   **When to Use**: Safety procedures, engineering systems, pressure relief, gas handling, HVAC discussions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing pressurization or containment processes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - vent the gas
   - vent to atmosphere
   - vent safely
   - vent pressure
   - venting system
   
   **Root Analysis**: vent (from Latin ventus, wind)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin ventus (wind) → French vent → English vent (1300s as noun, 1500s as verb)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure to vent the gas outside, not into the building.
   - The pressure relief valve will vent automatically if it gets too high.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Emergency procedures require operators to vent excess pressure to the flare system.
   - The storage tank is equipped with pressure relief devices that vent to a safe location.

1. **Verb**: liquify (also liquefy)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make or become liquid; to convert from a solid or gaseous state to liquid form.
   - To cool or compress gas to the point where it becomes liquid.
   
   **Synonyms**: melt (from solid), condense (from gas), liquidize
   
   **Antonyms**: solidify, freeze, vaporize, gasify
   
   **When to Use**: Chemistry, industrial gas processing, cryogenics, discussions about phase changes, LNG production.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing solidification or vaporization processes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - liquify natural gas
   - liquify under pressure
   - liquified petroleum gas
   - liquification process
   - liquify by cooling
   
   **Root Analysis**: liqui (liquid) + -fy (make)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin liquefacere (to make liquid) → French liquéfier → English liquefy (1400s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They liquify the natural gas to make it easier to ship overseas.
   - At really cold temperatures, you can liquify almost any gas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Natural gas is liquified at approximately -162°C for transportation in cryogenic tankers.
   - The liquification plant processes 5 million tonnes of LNG annually.

1. **Verb**: disperse
   **Meaning**: 
   - To distribute or spread over a wide area.
   - (Of gas or particles) To cause to separate and move apart in different directions.
   
   **Synonyms**: scatter, spread, disseminate, diffuse, distribute
   
   **Antonyms**: gather, collect, concentrate, accumulate
   
   **When to Use**: Gas behavior, pollution dispersal, emergency response, chemistry, environmental science.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing concentration or gathering processes.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - disperse quickly
   - disperse into the atmosphere
   - disperse evenly
   - gas disperses
   - dispersion pattern
   
   **Root Analysis**: dis- (apart) + perse (scatter)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin dispersus (scattered) → English disperse (1300s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The gas dispersed pretty fast once we opened all the windows.
   - Wind helps disperse pollution so it doesn't build up in one place.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Released gases disperse according to atmospheric conditions, wind speed, and molecular weight.
   - Emergency protocols include modeling how toxic gases will disperse under various meteorological scenarios.
