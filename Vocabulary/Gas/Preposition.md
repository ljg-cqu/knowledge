# Gas Vocabulary - Prepositions

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Expressing movement or direction to a point within an enclosed space or container.
   - *(Abstract)* Expressing transformation or change of state.
   
   **Synonyms**: inside (in context of location), to the interior of
   
   **Antonyms**: out of, from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing gas movement into containers, phase changes, entry into spaces, transformation processes.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing exit or removal from spaces.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into the atmosphere, into the tank, into liquid form
   - **Verb + prep**: release into, compress into, transform into, dissolve into
   - **Adjective + prep**: (rare with "into")
   - **Noun + prep**: entry into, transition into
   
   **Root Analysis**: in + to (combining two prepositions)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English in (in) + to (to) → into (900s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The gas leaked into the basement overnight.
   - When you cool it down, the gas turns into a liquid.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Carbon dioxide is released into the atmosphere through combustion processes.
   - Under cryogenic conditions, natural gas condenses into its liquid phase.

1. **Preposition**: from (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating the point in space at which a journey, motion, or action starts; indicating source or origin.
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the source or origin of something.
   
   **Synonyms**: out of (in some contexts), away from
   
   **Antonyms**: to, toward, into
   
   **When to Use**: Describing gas source, origin, emission source, starting point of movement or change.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing destination or endpoint.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from the source, from combustion, from the atmosphere
   - **Verb + prep**: emit from, escape from, come from, originate from, extract from
   - **Adjective + prep**: different from, separate from
   - **Noun + prep**: emissions from, gas from, release from
   
   **Root Analysis**: from (Old English fram)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English fram (forward, from) → Middle English from (1100s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The smell was coming from a gas leak in the kitchen.
   - Where does the carbon dioxide from cars go?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Methane emissions from livestock operations account for 32% of anthropogenic methane.
   - Natural gas is extracted from underground reservoirs through drilling operations.

1. **Preposition**: at (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Expressing location or position at a specific point.
   - *(Temporal)* Expressing a particular point in time.
   - *(Abstract)* Expressing a specific condition, temperature, or pressure.
   
   **Synonyms**: (context-dependent: in, on for location)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from
   
   **When to Use**: Specifying exact conditions (temperature, pressure), specific locations, particular states.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general areas (use "in") or surfaces (use "on").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at room temperature, at high pressure, at the point, at equilibrium
   - **Verb + prep**: occur at, exist at, operate at, maintain at
   - **Adjective + prep**: present at, stable at, volatile at
   - **Noun + prep**: gas at [condition], reaction at [temperature]
   
   **Root Analysis**: at (Old English æt)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English æt (at, near) → Modern English at
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level.
   - The gas is stored at really high pressure.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Methane exists as a gas at standard temperature and pressure.
   - The reaction proceeds optimally at 350°C and 20 bar pressure.

1. **Preposition**: under (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Below or beneath something.
   - *(Abstract)* Subject to or affected by (a condition, process, or influence); experiencing specific conditions.
   
   **Synonyms**: beneath (spatial), subject to (abstract), experiencing
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, free from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing conditions of pressure, temperature, control; regulatory compliance; specific circumstances.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing location above something or freedom from conditions.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: under pressure, under high temperature, under control, under regulation
   - **Verb + prep**: store under, operate under, function under, test under
   - **Adjective + prep**: stable under, safe under
   - **Noun + prep**: gas under pressure, operation under [conditions]
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English under (beneath, among) → Modern English under
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The tank keeps the gas under pressure so more fits inside.
   - Under normal conditions, it's perfectly safe.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Compressed natural gas is stored under pressures ranging from 200-250 bar.
   - The facility operates under strict environmental regulations enforced by the EPA.

1. **Preposition**: through (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving in one side and out the other side of an opening, channel, or location.
   - *(Abstract)* By means of; by the agency or instrumentality of.
   
   **Synonyms**: via, across, by way of, by means of
   
   **Antonyms**: around, avoiding
   
   **When to Use**: Describing gas flow, pathways, diffusion, passage, means of transmission or action.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing movement around or avoidance of something.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: through the pipe, through diffusion, through the membrane
   - **Verb + prep**: flow through, pass through, diffuse through, move through, travel through
   - **Adjective + prep**: permeable through
   - **Noun + prep**: passage through, flow through
   
   **Root Analysis**: through (Old English thurh)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English thurh (from one end to the other) → Modern English through
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The gas flows through the pipes to your house.
   - You can smell it because the molecules pass through the air.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Natural gas is transported through an extensive pipeline network spanning thousands of kilometers.
   - Gas molecules diffuse through the membrane at a rate proportional to the concentration gradient.

1. **Preposition**: in (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Expressing location or position within an enclosed space or container.
   - *(Temporal)* During a period of time.
   - *(Abstract)* Existing within a particular state, form, or condition.
   
   **Synonyms**: within, inside, contained by
   
   **Antonyms**: out, outside, beyond
   
   **When to Use**: Describing gas location, state, form, containment, time periods, conditions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For surfaces (use "on") or specific points (use "at").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in the atmosphere, in gaseous form, in the tank, in equilibrium
   - **Verb + prep**: exist in, store in, contain in, trap in, dissolve in
   - **Adjective + prep**: present in, abundant in, soluble in, rich in
   - **Noun + prep**: gas in [location], concentration in [medium]
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English in (in, into) → Modern English in
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's too much carbon dioxide in the atmosphere now.
   - The gas is stored in special high-pressure tanks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Oxygen comprises approximately 21% of gases present in Earth's atmosphere.
   - Natural gas exists in both conventional and unconventional reservoirs.

1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Accompanied by; having as a feature or characteristic.
   - Using (an instrument, tool, or method).
   - In the same direction as.
   
   **Synonyms**: accompanied by, using, having, containing
   
   **Antonyms**: without, lacking
   
   **When to Use**: Describing gas properties, instrumentation, accompanying conditions, methods, characteristics.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing absence or lack of something (use "without").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with high pressure, with caution, with oxygen, with a detector
   - **Verb + prep**: react with, combine with, measure with, detect with, mix with
   - **Adjective + prep**: saturated with, filled with, compatible with
   - **Noun + prep**: gas with [property], mixture with [component]
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wiþ (against, toward, with) → Modern English with
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Be careful with flammable gases around open flames.
   - The gas mixture with oxygen can explode.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Methane reacts with oxygen during combustion to produce carbon dioxide and water.
   - Facilities must monitor emissions with EPA-approved continuous monitoring systems.

1. **Preposition**: by (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the means, method, or agent of an action.
   - Through the agency or action of.
   - *(Spatial)* Near or beside (less common in gas contexts).
   
   **Synonyms**: through, via, by means of, using
   
   **Antonyms**: (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing methods, processes, causation, agency, magnitude of change.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the spatial "near" meaning might cause confusion.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by combustion, by pressure, by heating, by volume
   - **Verb + prep**: produced by, caused by, measured by, reduced by, increased by
   - **Adjective + prep**: affected by, influenced by
   - **Noun + prep**: emissions by [source], reduction by [amount]
   
   **Root Analysis**: by (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English bī (near, by) → Modern English by
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The gas is compressed by the pump to fit more in the tank.
   - Emissions have increased by 20% over the last decade.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Carbon dioxide is produced by the complete combustion of hydrocarbon fuels.
   - Atmospheric methane concentrations have risen by 150% since pre-industrial times.

1. **Preposition**: to (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Expressing direction or movement toward a point, place, or position.
   - *(Abstract)* Expressing a point reached or a condition attained; indicating change.
   - Expressing a relationship of proportion or comparison.
   
   **Synonyms**: toward, into (for some contexts), until
   
   **Antonyms**: from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing direction, destination, limits, proportions, ratios, changes of state.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing origin or starting point (use "from").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: to the atmosphere, to liquid state, to the limit
   - **Verb + prep**: expose to, subject to, heat to, cool to, reduce to, convert to
   - **Adjective + prep**: sensitive to, resistant to, proportional to, subject to
   - **Noun + prep**: exposure to, conversion to, ratio to
   
   **Root Analysis**: to (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English tō (toward, in the direction of) → Modern English to
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The gas rises to the ceiling because it's lighter than air.
   - Heat it to about 200 degrees to get the reaction going.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sample was heated to 500°C under an inert nitrogen atmosphere.
   - The compression ratio is defined as the ratio of maximum to minimum volume.

1. **Preposition**: of (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Expressing the relationship between a part and a whole.
   - Expressing the relationship between a general category and specific examples.
   - Used to indicate material, composition, or constituent parts.
   
   **Synonyms**: (context-dependent: composed of, consisting of, from)
   
   **Antonyms**: (no direct antonyms)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing composition, quantity, types, categories, measurements, origins.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: (Universally applicable preposition with few restrictions)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: volume of gas, concentration of CO2, type of fuel, amount of pressure
   - **Verb + prep**: consist of, composed of, made of
   - **Adjective + prep**: full of, capable of, characteristic of
   - **Noun + prep**: emission of [gas], production of [substance], release of [gas]
   
   **Root Analysis**: of (Old English)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English of (from, away from) → Modern English of
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - A lot of gases come out of car exhausts.
   - The tank holds about 50 liters of compressed natural gas.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The atmosphere is composed of nitrogen (78%), oxygen (21%), and trace amounts of other gases.
   - Emissions of greenhouse gases must be reduced by 50% to meet climate targets.
