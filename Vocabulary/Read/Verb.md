1. **Verb**: read
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T/I)* To look at and understand written or printed words.
   - *(T)* To study and think carefully about a text.
   - *(T)* To interpret or understand the meaning of something, such as data, a situation, or a facial expression.
   
   **Synonyms**: peruse, study, go through, examine, interpret (sense 3)
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, skim over (for careful reading), misread (sense 3)
   
   **When to Use**: In almost any context where someone is processing written language—books, articles, emails, messages—or when interpreting information like graphs or signals.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it when you specifically mean "pronounce" (use "pronounce" or "sound out") or "summarize"; those actions go beyond simply reading.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - read a book
   - read an article
   - read carefully
   - read aloud
   - read between the lines
   
   **Root Analysis**: read (to interpret written symbols) with no modern prefix/suffix
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English 'rædan' (to advise, interpret, read). Its meaning shifted from general interpretation to specifically understanding written language.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like to read in bed before I fall asleep.
   - Did you read my message, or did it get buried in your inbox?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were asked to read the passages silently and answer comprehension questions.
   - The graph can be read as evidence of a steady increase in literacy rates.

1. **Verb**: skim
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T/I)* To read something quickly in order to get the main ideas rather than every detail.
   
   **Synonyms**: glance through, browse, scan (overlapping), run your eye over
   
   **Antonyms**: read carefully, study in depth, scrutinize
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about efficient reading strategies for getting the gist of long texts, checking overall structure, or previewing material.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very formal academic writing where more precise phrases like "conduct a detailed analysis" or "read closely" are required; also do not use when you actually read every word slowly.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - skim a text
   - skim the article
   - skim through the chapter
   - skim for the main idea
   
   **Root Analysis**: From a verb meaning to move lightly over a surface; no clear prefix/suffix breakdown in modern English
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from Old Norse 'skima' (to glance). The reading sense developed by analogy with passing lightly over the surface of something.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I just skimmed the report to see if anything urgent was in there.
   - You can skim the first chapter and then slow down when it gets interesting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students were instructed to skim the article first to identify headings and topic sentences.
   - Skimming is an effective strategy when readers need a quick overview before close reading.

1. **Verb**: scan
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T/I)* To look through a text quickly in order to find a specific piece of information.
   - *(T)* To pass light or a device over something to produce a digital copy.
   
   **Synonyms**: search, look through, run your eyes over, browse for
   
   **Antonyms**: dwell on, read slowly, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: Describing focused reading for details such as dates, names, or keywords, or talking about using a scanner or app to make a digital copy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use it if you mean "analyze deeply"; scanning is fast and selective, not thorough.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - scan the text for keywords
   - scan for specific information
   - scan a document
   - scan the page
   
   **Root Analysis**: scan- (to examine) with no productive modern affixes
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'scandere' (to climb), later "to scan verse" (read rhythmically); the modern sense "look over quickly" developed in the 19th–20th centuries.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I scanned the email for dates and totally missed the attachment.
   - Just scan the chapter for the part about eye movement during reading.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were asked to scan the texts for specific target words.
   - Readers often scan headings and subheadings before deciding what to read in depth.

1. **Verb**: highlight
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T)* To mark important words or parts of a text with color or emphasis so that they stand out.
   - *(T)* To emphasize or draw attention to something.
   
   **Synonyms**: mark, emphasize, underline, pick out, draw attention to
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, downplay, obscure
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about study techniques, note-taking, and digital reading tools that let you mark key points.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it literally when no visual marking is involved in reading; in spoken-only contexts, "emphasize" or "stress" may be better.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - highlight key points
   - highlight a sentence
   - highlight in yellow
   - highlight while reading
   
   **Root Analysis**: high (elevated, important) + light (illumination) → highlight (to bring into prominence)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound from 'high' + 'light', originally meaning a bright spot in a painting; later used metaphorically for emphasis and as a verb in reading and editing contexts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like to highlight anything that sounds important when I read textbooks.
   - Don't highlight the whole page or it stops being useful.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students were encouraged to highlight unfamiliar vocabulary for later review.
   - Excessive highlighting can reduce the effectiveness of this strategy by failing to distinguish truly critical information.

1. **Verb**: annotate
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(T/I)* To add brief notes, comments, or explanations to a text while reading.
   
   **Synonyms**: comment on, make notes on, mark up, gloss
   
   **Antonyms**: leave unmarked, erase notes, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: Describing active reading strategies, academic study habits, editing, and collaborative reading tools.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in casual contexts where "jot down a note" or "write in the margin" is clearer; it is more common in academic and professional settings.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - annotate a text
   - annotate in the margins
   - digitally annotate
   - heavily annotated copy
   
   **Root Analysis**: an- (toward) + -not (mark, note) + -ate (verb suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'annotare' (to note down), from 'ad-' (to) + 'notare' (to mark). Adopted into English scholarly language to describe adding notes to texts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like to annotate my books with questions and little stars next to good quotes.
   - You can annotate PDFs on your tablet instead of printing them out.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Students were instructed to annotate the article, noting main claims and supporting evidence.
   - Annotated texts can make complex academic readings more accessible to learners.
