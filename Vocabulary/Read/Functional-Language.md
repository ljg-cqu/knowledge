1. **Functional Expression**: According to the text, ... (function: reporting information from a reading)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce ideas or facts that come directly from a written source rather than from the speaker.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect (attributes information to the text, not to the speaker)
   
   **Synonyms**: The text says that..., The article states that..., In the passage, it says that..., As the author points out,...
   
   **Antonyms**: In my opinion..., I think..., From my point of view... (express personal view instead of reporting)
   
   **When to Use**: In reading comprehension, academic discussion, essays, and presentations when you want to show that you are summarizing or quoting from the text.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when expressing your own interpretation or disagreement; then you should switch to markers of opinion ("I argue that...").
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - According to the article, ...
   - According to the author, ...
   - According to this chapter, ...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression distances the speaker from the claim and shows respect for source-based reading. It is useful for separating what the text says from what the reader thinks.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - So according to the text, ... but do you agree?
   - According to the text, ..., whereas in practice we often see...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to the text, people remember more when they take notes by hand.
   - According to this article, reading before bed helps you sleep better.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to the text, extensive reading can significantly improve vocabulary knowledge.
   - According to the author, critical reading requires questioning both the text and its context.

1. **Functional Expression**: What do you think of this article? (function: asking for an opinion about a text)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asks someone to share their personal evaluation or reaction to a reading.
   - *Formality*: neutral (can be shifted toward formal or casual by tone and context)
   - *Directness*: direct question
   
   **Synonyms**: How do you feel about this article?, What's your opinion of this text?, How do you see this argument?, How does this reading strike you?
   
   **Antonyms**: Do you understand this article? (checks comprehension rather than opinion), Can you summarize this text? (requests summary, not evaluation)
   
   **When to Use**: In seminars, reading circles, book clubs, and discussions where the goal is to exchange views about what has been read.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the other person has not finished the reading, or when a detailed analysis is required instead of a general impression.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What do you think of this chapter?
   - What do you think of the author's argument?
   - How do you feel about this reading?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Invites collaboration and opens space for multiple perspectives. The tone can be made gentler with softeners ("kind of", "a bit").
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I think it's..., because...
   - Honestly, I found it...
   - On the whole, I would say...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - So, what do you think of this article? Boring or interesting?
   - What do you think of the story we had to read for homework?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before we move on, what do you think of this article's main contribution to the field?
   - What do you think of the author's use of evidence in this section of the text?

1. **Functional Expression**: Could you summarize what you've just read? (function: requesting a summary)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely asks someone to give a shorter version of the main ideas from a text.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct but softened by the modal "could"
   
   **Synonyms**: Can you sum up the main points?, Could you give me a quick summary?, Can you briefly explain the text?, Could you tell us the key ideas?
   
   **Antonyms**: Could you read the whole passage aloud? (requests full reading, not summary), Could you quote it exactly? (requests exact words, not shorter version)
   
   **When to Use**: In group work, teacher-student interactions, and meetings where one person shares the gist of a reading with others.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when the person has not had time to read or when you need detailed analysis rather than a short overview.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you summarize the main idea?
   - Can you quickly sum up this section?
   - Would you mind summarizing the chapter for us?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows respect by using polite modals. It frames summarizing as a useful reading skill and a contribution to the group.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, it's mainly about...
   - In short, the author argues that...
   - Basically, the text says that...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could you summarize what you've just read? I zoned out for a second.
   - Can you quickly sum up that paragraph? I didn't quite catch it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you summarize what you've just read so the rest of the group can follow the discussion?
   - Before we move on, would you mind summarizing this section for the class?

1. **Functional Expression**: From what I've read, ... (function: hedging and referencing reading as a source)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces an opinion or conclusion based on the speaker's reading, while signaling that it may be incomplete.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect, with epistemic hedging
   
   **Synonyms**: Based on what I've read..., As far as I can tell from the reading..., From my reading of the text..., According to what I've seen in the literature...
   
   **Antonyms**: I know for certain that..., It is definitely the case that..., There is no doubt that... (strong certainty instead of hedging)
   
   **When to Use**: When contributing ideas in discussions or writing and you want to show they come from reading but may not cover all sources.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in situations where strong claims and clear evidence are required (e.g., final conclusions in a research paper) unless followed by solid support.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - From what I've read so far, ...
   - Based on what I've read, ...
   - From my reading of this chapter, ...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: This expression shows intellectual humility and invites correction or additional information from others.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That matches what I've read too.
   - From what I've read, it's slightly different: ...
   - Actually, some studies suggest the opposite.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - From what I've read, reading a little every day is better than reading a lot once a week.
   - From what I've read, audio books still count as reading for your brain.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - From what I've read, extensive reading programs can have a strong effect on learners' vocabulary growth.
   - From my reading of the current literature, there is still limited research on digital reading habits in older adults.
