1. **Noun**: reading (C/U, pl: readings)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The activity of looking at and understanding written or printed words.
   - *(Sense 2)* A particular interpretation of a text, situation, or piece of data.
   
   **Synonyms**: perusal, study, interpretation (sense 2), examination, analysis (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: writing, speaking, misreading (sense 2), misunderstanding (sense 2)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about the activity or habit of reading books, articles, or documents, and in academic or professional contexts when describing how someone understands a text or set of numbers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it when you specifically mean "literacy" (ability to read and write) or "performance" (test results); use more precise nouns in those cases.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - close reading
   - reading comprehension
   - light reading
   - bedtime reading
   - required reading
   
   **Root Analysis**: read (to interpret written symbols) + -ing (noun-forming suffix indicating activity or result)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English 'ræding' (interpretation, reading) related to 'rædan' (to advise, interpret, read). Over time it came to mean both the activity of reading and a particular interpretation of something.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I did some light reading before bed and fell asleep with the book on my face.
   - My weekend plan is just reading, coffee, and more reading.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Careful reading of the report reveals several inconsistencies in the data.
   - This chapter offers a new reading of the novel's final scene, emphasizing its political implications.

1. **Noun**: reader (C, pl: readers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who reads books, articles, or other written texts.
   - *(Sense 2)* A device or program used to display or process digital text (for example, an e‑book reader or PDF reader).
   
   **Synonyms**: book lover, audience member, user (sense 2), device (sense 2)
   
   **Antonyms**: non-reader, illiterate person, author (contrasting role)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing people who read for pleasure or study, referring to target audiences for texts, or naming tools that display digital documents.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use it when you want to emphasize someone's role as a writer or researcher; in those cases choose "author," "writer," or "researcher."
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - avid reader
   - target reader
   - general reader
   - e-book reader
   - screen reader
   
   **Root Analysis**: read (to interpret written symbols) + -er (person or thing that performs an action)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English 'rædere' (interpreter, counselor, reader), formed from 'rædan' (to read). The technological sense for software and devices developed in the late 20th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm not a fast reader, but I really enjoy getting lost in a good story.
   - Many readers now prefer using an e-book reader instead of carrying heavy paperbacks.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The author assumes that readers have a basic understanding of research methods.
   - Screen readers enable blind and visually impaired users to access digital texts.

1. **Noun**: text (C/U, pl: texts)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The written words of a book, article, message, or any piece of writing.
   - *(In academic fields)* Any object that can be "read" and interpreted, such as a film, image, or advertisement.
   
   **Synonyms**: passage, written material, document, written work, content
   
   **Antonyms**: speech, silence, image (when contrasted with verbal text)
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to the words on a page or screen, or in academic contexts when analyzing books, articles, or other cultural artifacts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in very casual contexts where "message" or "note" sounds more natural, or when you specifically mean a phone "text message"—in that case, make the meaning clear from context.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - analyze the text
   - literary text
   - academic text
   - source text
   - text message
   
   **Root Analysis**: text- (Latin root meaning woven, written) with no modern prefix/suffix in English
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'textus' (that which is woven, a written work), from 'texere' (to weave). Entered English in the late Middle Ages to refer to written material.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The text on this website is too small for me to read comfortably.
   - Our teacher asked us to underline the key ideas in the text.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The text foregrounds questions of power and identity through its use of first-person narration.
   - Students are encouraged to quote directly from the primary text to support their arguments.

1. **Noun**: paragraph (C, pl: paragraphs)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A distinct section of a piece of writing, usually dealing with a single theme and starting on a new line.
   
   **Synonyms**: section, passage, chunk of text, block of writing
   
   **Antonyms**: continuous text (without breaks), heading (different element)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about how writing is organized, giving instructions on structuring essays, or referring to specific parts of a text for close reading.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Do not use it to describe chapters or whole sections of a book; those are larger units. Avoid using it for very short items like single words or headings.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - topic sentence of a paragraph
   - introductory paragraph
   - concluding paragraph
   - long paragraph
   - break this paragraph up
   
   **Root Analysis**: para- (beside) + graph (writing)
   
   **Etymology**: Greek → from Greek 'paragraphos' (short stroke marking a change of sense), from 'para-' (beside) + 'graphein' (to write). In English it came to mean a distinct block of written text.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This paragraph is way too long—no one wants to read a wall of text.
   - Try adding a new paragraph when you change ideas so your writing is easier to follow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Each paragraph in the introduction serves a specific rhetorical function.
   - Clear paragraph structure helps readers track the development of the argument.

1. **Noun**: comprehension (U, pl: —)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The ability to understand and make sense of what you read or hear.
   - *(In education)* The level to which a learner grasps the meaning, details, and implications of a text.
   
   **Synonyms**: understanding, grasp, interpretation, insight
   
   **Antonyms**: confusion, misunderstanding, misinterpretation, ignorance
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about how well someone understands what they are reading, especially in language learning, testing, and reading strategies.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid using it where a simpler word like "understanding" fits better in everyday conversation, or when you mean sympathy or emotional understanding (use "empathy").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reading comprehension
   - test comprehension
   - check comprehension
   - comprehension questions
   - improve comprehension
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + prehend (to grasp, seize) + -sion (noun-forming suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'comprehensio' (a seizing, understanding), from 'comprehendere' (to grasp, seize). In English it developed the sense of mental understanding, especially in reading and listening.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can read fast, but my comprehension drops if I'm tired.
   - The teacher asked a few questions to check our comprehension of the story.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Reading comprehension is strongly influenced by background knowledge and vocabulary size.
   - The study found a significant gap between decoding skills and comprehension in some learners.
