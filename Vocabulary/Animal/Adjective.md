1. **Adjective**: wild (wilder, wildest)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Living in a natural state, not domesticated or tamed by humans; existing in nature without human intervention. (Gradable, both attributive and predicative)
   - (Of places) Uninhabited and uncultivated, characterized by natural vegetation and wildlife.
   
   **Synonyms**: untamed, feral, natural, savage, undomesticated
   
   **Antonyms**: domesticated, tame, captive, civilized
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals in their natural habitats, contrasting with domesticated animals, conservation contexts, discussing natural ecosystems.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For domesticated animals that have become feral (prefer "feral"), or when referring to aggressive behavior in pets (use "aggressive" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wild animals
   - wild population
   - in the wild
   - wild habitat
   - return to the wild
   
   **Root Analysis**: Root word from Old English, no clear morphological breakdown
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from Old English 'wilde' meaning "in the natural state, untamed." Related to Germanic roots meaning "self-willed." Used consistently since Old English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Wild animals are usually more dangerous than pets because they haven't been around humans.
   - It's illegal to keep wild birds as pets in many places.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Wild populations exhibit greater genetic diversity than their captive counterparts.
   - The reintroduction of captive-bred individuals to wild populations requires careful genetic management.

1. **Adjective**: domesticated
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Bred and raised to live with and be useful to humans, having undergone genetic and behavioral changes through selective breeding over generations. (Non-gradable, attributive and predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: tamed, cultivated, domestic, bred
   
   **Antonyms**: wild, untamed, feral, savage
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals that have been selectively bred by humans, discussing agricultural animals, pet species, evolutionary changes under human influence.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For wild animals kept temporarily in captivity (use "captive" instead), or for wild animals that are merely tamed (use "tamed").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - domesticated animals
   - domesticated species
   - domesticated breeds
   - fully domesticated
   - domesticated livestock
   
   **Root Analysis**: domestic (from Latin domus = house) + -ate (having) + -ed (past participle)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'domesticus' meaning "belonging to the household," from 'domus' (house). Extended to animal breeding in the 17th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Dogs and cats are domesticated animals that have lived with humans for thousands of years.
   - Cows are domesticated animals bred for milk and meat.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Domesticated animals exhibit neotenic traits, retaining juvenile characteristics into adulthood.
   - The domestication process has resulted in significant morphological and behavioral divergence from wild progenitors.

1. **Adjective**: endangered
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of species) At serious risk of extinction due to low population numbers, habitat loss, or other threatening factors. (Gradable, primarily attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: threatened, at risk, imperiled, vulnerable
   
   **Antonyms**: abundant, thriving, common, stable (population)
   
   **When to Use**: Conservation biology, discussing species at risk, environmental protection contexts, official classification systems (IUCN categories).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For species already extinct (use "extinct"), or for species with stable populations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - endangered species
   - critically endangered
   - endangered status
   - endangered population
   - protect endangered animals
   
   **Root Analysis**: en- (cause to be) + danger + -ed (state)
   
   **Etymology**: French + English → from French 'danger' (peril) + English prefix and suffix. Applied to species conservation in the mid-20th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Giant pandas are endangered animals because their bamboo forests are disappearing.
   - Many whale species are endangered due to hunting and ocean pollution.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The species has been classified as endangered under IUCN criteria due to a 70% population decline.
   - Habitat fragmentation represents the primary threat to endangered megafauna populations.

1. **Adjective**: nocturnal
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Active primarily during the night rather than during the day. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   - Characterized by or adapted for nighttime activity.
   
   **Synonyms**: night-active, nighttime, night-dwelling
   
   **Antonyms**: diurnal (day-active), crepuscular (dawn/dusk-active)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animal activity patterns, discussing behavioral ecology, explaining adaptations for low-light conditions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals active during dawn/dusk (use "crepuscular"), or day-active animals (use "diurnal").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nocturnal animals
   - nocturnal behavior
   - nocturnal hunter
   - nocturnal species
   - nocturnal habits
   
   **Root Analysis**: noct- (night) + -urn (belonging to) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'nocturnalis' meaning "belonging to the night," from 'nox, noctis' (night). Applied to animal behavior patterns in scientific literature since the 17th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Owls are nocturnal birds that hunt at night when most other birds are sleeping.
   - Bats are nocturnal—you'll see them flying around at dusk and through the night.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Nocturnal species have evolved specialized sensory adaptations including enhanced olfaction and audition.
   - The transition to nocturnal behavior likely evolved as a predator-avoidance strategy.

1. **Adjective**: diurnal
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Active primarily during daylight hours rather than at night. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   - Functioning or occurring during the day.
   
   **Synonyms**: day-active, daytime
   
   **Antonyms**: nocturnal (night-active), crepuscular (dawn/dusk-active)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animal activity patterns, behavioral ecology, discussing circadian rhythms, comparing with nocturnal species.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals active at dawn/dusk (use "crepuscular"), or for animals active day and night (use "cathemeral").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - diurnal animals
   - diurnal species
   - diurnal activity
   - diurnal behavior
   - diurnal pattern
   
   **Root Analysis**: di- (day, variant of dies) + -urn (belonging to) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'diurnalis' meaning "daily, of the day," from 'dies' (day). Applied systematically to animal behavior in the 18th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Most birds are diurnal—they're active during the day and sleep at night.
   - Squirrels are diurnal animals, so you'll see them running around in daylight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Diurnal primates possess trichromatic color vision, an adaptation for diurnal foraging.
   - The shift from nocturnal to diurnal activity patterns correlates with body size increases in mammalian evolution.

1. **Adjective**: carnivorous
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Feeding primarily on meat or flesh; adapted to a diet of animal tissue. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   - Having the characteristics or requirements of a meat-eating organism.
   
   **Synonyms**: meat-eating, predatory, flesh-eating
   
   **Antonyms**: herbivorous (plant-eating), omnivorous (eating both)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing dietary adaptations, discussing feeding strategies, food web positions, evolutionary specializations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that eat both plants and meat (use "omnivorous"), or exclusively plant-eaters (use "herbivorous").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - carnivorous animals
   - carnivorous diet
   - carnivorous species
   - carnivorous mammals
   - obligate carnivorous
   
   **Root Analysis**: carni- (flesh) + -vor (devour) + -ous (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'carnivorus' meaning "flesh-eating," from 'caro, carnis' (flesh) + 'vorare' (to devour). Entered biological terminology in the 19th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Lions are carnivorous animals—they only eat meat from other animals.
   - Carnivorous dinosaurs like T-Rex had sharp teeth for tearing flesh.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Carnivorous species exhibit dental and digestive morphologies optimized for processing animal tissue.
   - Obligate carnivorous mammals lack the enzymatic capacity to efficiently metabolize plant carbohydrates.

1. **Adjective**: herbivorous
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Feeding exclusively or primarily on plants; adapted to a diet of vegetation. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: plant-eating, vegetarian (informal)
   
   **Antonyms**: carnivorous (meat-eating), omnivorous (eating both)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing dietary adaptations, discussing animal feeding ecology, explaining digestive specializations, food web contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that eat both plants and animals (use "omnivorous"), or exclusively meat-eaters (use "carnivorous").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - herbivorous animals
   - herbivorous diet
   - herbivorous species
   - herbivorous mammals
   - strictly herbivorous
   
   **Root Analysis**: herbi- (plant, herb) + -vor (devour) + -ous (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'herbivorus' meaning "plant-eating," from 'herba' (grass, herb) + 'vorare' (to devour). Coined in scientific contexts in the 19th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Elephants are herbivorous—they eat tons of grass, leaves, and bark every day.
   - Rabbits and deer are herbivorous animals that only eat plants.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Herbivorous ungulates possess specialized multi-chambered stomachs for cellulose fermentation.
   - Herbivorous insects exert significant selective pressure on plant chemical defense evolution.

1. **Adjective**: territorial
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Defending or maintaining a territory against others of the same species; exhibiting territorial behavior. (Gradable, primarily predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: possessive of territory, defensive, space-defending
   
   **Antonyms**: non-territorial, communal, social (in shared spaces)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animal behavior patterns, discussing space use and defense, mating systems, aggression contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that share space freely (use "social" or "communal"), or when describing human political territories.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - territorial behavior
   - highly territorial
   - territorial animals
   - territorial disputes
   - territorial boundaries
   
   **Root Analysis**: terri- (land) + -tory (place) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'territorium' (land around a town) + -al. Applied to animal behavior systematically in the early 20th century by ethologists.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Male birds can be very territorial during breeding season, chasing away other males.
   - My cat is territorial and doesn't like other cats in our yard.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Territorial aggression intensity correlates with resource value and population density.
   - Territorial species invest energy in advertisement displays and boundary maintenance.

1. **Adjective**: migratory
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Characterized by seasonal or periodic movement between different regions or habitats. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   - Relating to or exhibiting migration patterns.
   
   **Synonyms**: migrating, traveling seasonally, nomadic (broader sense)
   
   **Antonyms**: resident, sedentary, non-migratory, stationary
   
   **When to Use**: Describing seasonal animal movements, bird studies, discussing long-distance travelers, conservation planning for mobile species.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that stay in one location year-round (use "resident" or "sedentary"), or random wandering (use "nomadic").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - migratory birds
   - migratory species
   - migratory patterns
   - migratory routes
   - migratory behavior
   
   **Root Analysis**: migr- (move, wander) + -atory (relating to, characterized by)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'migratorius' from 'migrare' (to move from place to place). Applied to animal movements in the 18th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Many migratory birds fly thousands of miles south for the winter.
   - Monarch butterflies are migratory insects that travel from Canada to Mexico.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Migratory species face unique conservation challenges due to dependence on multiple spatially separated habitats.
   - Climate change has altered the phenology of migratory arrivals relative to resource availability.

1. **Adjective**: venomous
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Capable of injecting venom through a bite, sting, or other mechanism; possessing specialized venom-delivery systems. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: poisonous (often confused, but technically different), toxic
   
   **Antonyms**: non-venomous, harmless
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals that inject toxins (snakes, spiders, scorpions), discussing defense mechanisms, safety contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that are toxic when eaten but don't inject venom (use "poisonous" instead), or harmless species.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - venomous snakes
   - venomous bite
   - highly venomous
   - venomous species
   - venomous animals
   
   **Root Analysis**: venom (from Latin) + -ous (full of, characterized by)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'venenum' (poison) + -ous. Distinction between venomous (injected) and poisonous (ingested) clarified in modern biology.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Black widow spiders are venomous—their bite can make you really sick.
   - Not all snakes are venomous; many are completely harmless to humans.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Venomous animals have evolved complex toxin cocktails targeting prey nervous systems.
   - The distinction between venomous and poisonous organisms relates to toxin delivery mechanism rather than toxicity.

1. **Adjective**: aquatic
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Living in or closely associated with water; adapted for life in aquatic environments. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: water-dwelling, marine (for saltwater), freshwater (specific)
   
   **Antonyms**: terrestrial (land-dwelling), aerial (air-dwelling)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals that live in water, discussing habitat types, ecological classifications, adaptations for water.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that only visit water occasionally (use "semi-aquatic"), or exclusively land animals.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - aquatic animals
   - aquatic species
   - aquatic environment
   - fully aquatic
   - aquatic habitat
   
   **Root Analysis**: aqua- (water) + -tic (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'aquaticus' meaning "pertaining to water," from 'aqua' (water). Used in biological classification since the 17th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Fish are aquatic animals that can't survive out of water.
   - Whales are aquatic mammals that live their entire lives in the ocean.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Aquatic vertebrates exhibit convergent evolution in streamlined body morphology.
   - Physiological adaptations of aquatic mammals include enhanced oxygen storage capacity and bradycardia.

1. **Adjective**: terrestrial
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Living on or relating to the land rather than water or air; adapted for life on solid ground. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: land-dwelling, ground-living, land-based
   
   **Antonyms**: aquatic (water-dwelling), arboreal (tree-dwelling), aerial (air-dwelling)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals that live on land, ecological classifications, discussing habitat preferences, comparing with aquatic species.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that live primarily in trees (use "arboreal"), or in water (use "aquatic").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - terrestrial animals
   - terrestrial species
   - terrestrial habitat
   - terrestrial mammals
   - terrestrial life
   
   **Root Analysis**: terrestr- (earth, land) + -ial (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'terrestris' meaning "of the earth," from 'terra' (earth, land). Used in biological contexts since the 17th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Lions and elephants are terrestrial animals that live on land.
   - Most reptiles are terrestrial, though some like sea turtles live in water.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The transition from aquatic to terrestrial life required significant physiological and morphological innovations.
   - Terrestrial ecosystems support lower biomass per unit area than aquatic systems.

1. **Adjective**: arboreal
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Living in or adapted to life in trees; spending most time in tree canopies. (Non-gradable, primarily attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: tree-dwelling, tree-living, canopy-dwelling
   
   **Antonyms**: terrestrial (ground-dwelling), aquatic (water-dwelling), fossorial (burrowing)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals that live in trees, discussing forest ecology, explaining adaptations for climbing and living in canopies.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that only climb trees occasionally but live primarily on ground (use "scansorial"), or ground-dwellers.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - arboreal animals
   - arboreal species
   - arboreal habitat
   - arboreal lifestyle
   - arboreal primates
   
   **Root Analysis**: arbor- (tree) + -eal (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'arboreus' meaning "pertaining to trees," from 'arbor' (tree). Applied to animal ecology in the 19th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Monkeys are arboreal animals that spend most of their lives in trees.
   - Sloths are arboreal mammals that rarely come down to the ground.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Arboreal locomotion has selected for prehensile appendages and enhanced stereoscopic vision.
   - Arboreal species exhibit specialized morphological adaptations including elongated limbs and curved claws.

1. **Adjective**: social
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Living in organized groups or colonies with cooperative behavior; exhibiting complex social interactions. (Gradable, primarily attributive or predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: gregarious, communal, group-living, colonial
   
   **Antonyms**: solitary, isolated, lone, asocial
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals that live in groups, discussing cooperative behavior, animal societies, group dynamics.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that live and hunt alone (use "solitary"), or temporary aggregations without social structure.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - social animals
   - highly social
   - social behavior
   - social species
   - social structure
   
   **Root Analysis**: soci- (companion, ally) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'socialis' meaning "of companionship," from 'socius' (companion). Applied to animal behavior in the 19th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Wolves are social animals that hunt together in packs.
   - Bees are highly social insects that work together in colonies.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Social species exhibit complex communication systems and division of labor.
   - The evolution of eusociality requires specific ecological conditions favoring cooperation over independent reproduction.

1. **Adjective**: solitary
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of animals) Living or spending most of its time alone rather than in groups; not exhibiting social behavior. (Non-gradable, primarily predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: lone, isolated, non-social, independent
   
   **Antonyms**: social, gregarious, communal, group-living
   
   **When to Use**: Describing animals that live independently, discussing asocial behavior, contrasting with social species, mating systems.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For animals that live in groups (use "social"), or animals that form temporary breeding pairs (specify "socially monogamous").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - solitary animals
   - solitary lifestyle
   - solitary behavior
   - solitary species
   - live solitary lives
   
   **Root Analysis**: soli- (alone) + -tary (characterized by)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'solitarius' meaning "alone, lonely," from 'solus' (alone). Applied to animal behavior patterns in scientific literature.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Tigers are solitary animals that prefer to hunt and live alone.
   - Most cats are solitary, unlike dogs which prefer company.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Solitary carnivores typically establish large territories to secure adequate prey resources.
   - The solitary lifestyle minimizes intraspecific competition while potentially increasing predation risk.

1. **Adjective**: extinct
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of species) No longer in existence; having died out completely with no living members remaining. (Non-gradable, primarily predicative)
   
   **Synonyms**: died out, vanished, gone, eradicated
   
   **Antonyms**: extant (still existing), surviving, thriving
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing species that have completely disappeared, paleontology contexts, conservation failures, historical biodiversity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For species still surviving but declining (use "endangered" or "threatened"), or local disappearances (use "extirpated").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - extinct species
   - become extinct
   - gone extinct
   - extinct animals
   - mass extinction
   
   **Root Analysis**: ex- (out, thoroughly) + tinct (extinguished)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from Latin 'extinctus' (extinguished), past participle of 'extinguere' (to quench, wipe out). Applied to species loss in the 19th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Dinosaurs became extinct millions of years ago.
   - The dodo bird is extinct—the last one died over 300 years ago.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Species that have become extinct cannot be recovered, representing irreversible biodiversity loss.
   - Background extinction rates are significantly lower than current anthropogenically-driven extinction rates.
