1. **Original Expression**: This animal eats other animals.  
   **Context**: Describing dietary behavior of a predator
   
   **Core Intent**: To communicate that a species feeds on other animal species
   
   **Casual**: This animal eats other animals.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary ("eat"), direct construction, present tense  
   *Use when*: Talking to children, casual conversation, general audience, everyday discussion
   
   **Semi-Formal**: This animal preys on other species.  
   *Characteristics*: More specific verb ("preys on"), neutral professional tone, appropriate for general education  
   *Use when*: Educational settings, nature documentaries, general wildlife discussions, museum exhibits
   
   **Formal**: This species exhibits carnivorous behavior, consuming other fauna as its primary nutritional source.  
   *Characteristics*: Technical vocabulary ("exhibits," "fauna," "nutritional source"), complex sentence structure, passive constructions possible  
   *Use when*: Academic papers, scientific journals, research presentations, formal reports
   
   **Humorous**: This critter's basically a four-legged food processor for other critters!  
   *Characteristics*: Playful metaphor ("food processor"), informal vocabulary ("critter"), exclamation mark  
   *Use when*: Casual conversation with friends, entertaining children, light-hearted nature discussions  
   *Avoid when*: Academic settings, serious conservation discussions, professional contexts
   
   **Lively**: This fierce predator hunts down and devours its prey!  
   *Characteristics*: Dramatic verbs ("hunts down," "devours"), emphatic punctuation, vivid imagery  
   *Use when*: Nature documentaries with dramatic narration, engaging children, storytelling contexts
   
   **Empathetic**: This animal needs to hunt to survive—it's just following its natural instincts to feed itself and its family.  
   *Characteristics*: Explaining motivation ("needs to," "survive"), acknowledging natural behavior, compassionate framing  
   *Use when*: Discussing predation with those uncomfortable with nature's reality, educational contexts addressing emotions, family discussions
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher formality for academic/professional audiences; casual for peers
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers warrant semi-formal; close relationships allow casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Scientific research requires formal; general interest allows casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Western academic contexts prefer formal; popular media prefers engaging tones
   - *Medium*: Academic publications demand formal; social media allows casual/humorous
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using overly technical language with general audiences ("The organism exhibits obligate carnivory") - alienates non-specialists
   - Being too casual in academic writing ("This dude eats meat") - undermines credibility
   - Anthropomorphizing in scientific contexts ("The predator wants to eat") - lacks objectivity
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some cultures are more sensitive to predation discussions; emphasizing natural balance and empathetic framing helps. Japanese educational contexts often emphasize harmony in nature; Western contexts may emphasize competition.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Explaining to 5-year-old → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual or Empathetic ("This animal eats other animals to stay alive")
   - **Situation 2**: Writing biology research paper → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("The species exhibits carnivorous feeding behavior")
   - **Situation 3**: Nature documentary narration → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively ("The fierce hunter stalks its prey through the grasslands!")
   - **Situation 4**: Museum exhibit text → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-Formal ("This predator preys primarily on small mammals")
