1. **Adjective**: competitive (comp: more competitive, sup: most competitive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Describing an athlete, team, or match where the level of play is strong and the outcome is uncertain. (Gradable, attributive and predicative)
   - Having a strong desire to win or perform better than others.
   
   **Synonyms**: ambitious, driven, high-level
   
   **Antonyms**: uncompetitive, casual, one-sided
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about tight matches, strong leagues, or players who always want to win and push themselves.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For purely recreational activity where results don’t matter (use "casual" or "just for fun" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - competitive match
   - highly competitive league
   - competitive athlete
   - competitive environment
   
   **Root Analysis**: compet(e) (strive) + -itive (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *competere* (to strive together) via French and English; adjective form "competitive" became common in business and sports in the 19th–20th centuries.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She’s super competitive and hates losing, even in friendly games.
   - The match was really competitive—nobody could relax until the final whistle.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A highly competitive domestic league can improve national team performance.
   - Scholarship programs often target the most competitive youth athletes in each region.

1. **Adjective**: defensive
   **Meaning**: 
   - Focused on preventing the opponent from scoring rather than taking risks in attack. (Gradable, attributive and predicative)
   - Related to defending in tactical systems, positions, or behaviors.
   
   **Synonyms**: cautious, conservative, protective
   
   **Antonyms**: attacking, aggressive, expansive
   
   **When to Use**: Describing playing styles, tactics, formations, or players who prioritize stopping the opposition.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For non-sport emotional reactions (use "defensive" in psychological sense but clarify context).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - defensive tactics
   - defensive mindset
   - defensive formation
   - defensive duties
   
   **Root Analysis**: defens(e) (protect) + -ive (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *defendere* (to ward off), via Old French into English. Tactical sports sense ("focused on preventing goals") became standard with the development of modern team games.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They were way too defensive and never really tried to score.
   - He’s great in a defensive role but struggles in attack.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Defensive strategies often sacrifice possession and territory in exchange for compactness.
   - The team adopted a more defensive shape after taking the lead.

1. **Adjective**: attacking
   **Meaning**: 
   - Focused on creating chances, scoring, and taking risks going forward. (Gradable, attributive)
   - Relating to players or positions whose main role is to score or set up goals.
   
   **Synonyms**: offensive, forward-thinking, adventurous
   
   **Antonyms**: defensive, conservative, cautious
   
   **When to Use**: Describing styles of play, game plans, or players who prioritize creating chances.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In non-sport contexts where "attacking" may suggest aggression toward people in a non-metaphorical sense—clarify with context.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - attacking football
   - attacking options
   - attacking mindset
   - attacking formation
   
   **Root Analysis**: attack (to assault, go forward) + -ing (participle used adjectivally)
   
   **Etymology**: French/Latin → from French *attaquer*, from Old Italian *attaccare* (fasten to, attack). Sports sense "offensive, forward" is widespread in modern commentary.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Fans love watching attacking football with lots of chances.
   - We switched to a more attacking setup after half-time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Attacking systems typically commit more players ahead of the ball during possession phases.
   - The data show that attacking substitutions increased expected goals but also defensive vulnerability.

1. **Adjective**: professional
   **Meaning**: 
   - Playing sport as a paid occupation rather than as a hobby. (Non-gradable in core sense, attributive and predicative)
   - Showing high standards of preparation, behaviour, and discipline that match elite expectations.
   
   **Synonyms**: paid, elite, disciplined
   
   **Antonyms**: amateur, recreational, casual
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about full-time athletes, paid leagues, or behaviour that meets high performance standards.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For youth or community sport where players are unpaid (use "amateur" or "grassroots").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - professional athlete
   - turn professional
   - professional contract
   - professional attitude
   
   **Root Analysis**: profess (declare publicly) + -ion (act) + -al (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *profiteri* (declare publicly a vocation). In sport, "professional" came to contrast with "amateur" as payment and commercialisation increased.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He finally signed his first professional contract with a big club.
   - Even in local games, she’s really professional about her preparation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The transition from amateur to professional status brings significant changes in training load and public expectations.
   - Professional leagues often impose strict codes of conduct on registered athletes.

1. **Adjective**: intense (comp: more intense, sup: most intense)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Involving very high physical or mental effort, often with fast pace and strong emotions. (Gradable, attributive and predicative)
   - Describing matches, training sessions, or rivalries that demand maximum focus and energy.
   
   **Synonyms**: demanding, high-pressure, high-octane
   
   **Antonyms**: relaxed, easy, low-key
   
   **When to Use**: Characterizing high-stakes games, close rivalries, or tough training blocks.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simple, light exercise or friendly games with no pressure.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - intense match
   - intense rivalry
   - intense training session
   - emotionally intense finish
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (toward) + tens(e) (stretch) + -e
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from *intensus* (stretched, strained), from *intendere* (to stretch toward). Applied broadly in English, and now frequently used in sports media for high-pressure contexts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That was such an intense game—I was nervous the whole time.
   - Pre-season has been really intense this year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Intense competition schedules can increase injury risk without adequate recovery strategies.
   - The most intense phases of play often coincide with spikes in heart rate and perceived exertion.
