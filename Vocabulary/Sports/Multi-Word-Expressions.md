1. **Expression**: home advantage (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The benefit a team is believed to gain when playing in its own stadium, with familiar conditions and local supporters.
   - *Fixedness*: medium/strong (very common pairing in sports writing).
   
   **Synonyms**: home-field advantage, home-court advantage, home support
   
   **Antonyms**: away disadvantage, hostile environment
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing performance differences between home and away matches, crowd influence, and travel effects.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For neutral venues like finals at a shared stadium (use "neutral ground" instead).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - strong home advantage
   - lose home advantage
   - make the most of home advantage
   - statistical home advantage
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Home" and "advantage" naturally combine to express the widely observed edge enjoyed by teams in their own environment, making this a core term in sports analytics.
   
   **Root Analysis**: home (place where a team is based) + advantage (benefit, edge)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We really need to use our home advantage in the second leg.
   - Their home advantage is huge—the stadium is always packed and loud.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Statistical models consistently confirm a measurable home advantage across most professional leagues.
   - Travel fatigue and crowd support are often cited as key drivers of home advantage.

1. **Expression**: penalty shoot-out (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A method of deciding a winner when a knockout match ends in a draw, where players take turns shooting from the penalty spot.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (set term in football and similar sports).
   
   **Synonyms**: shoot-out, penalty shoot, spot-kick contest
   
   **Antonyms**: golden goal (sudden-death extra time), replay
   
   **When to Use**: Describing tie-break procedures after extra time in football, hockey, or similar sports with penalty systems.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For penalties during ordinary play; use "penalty" or "penalty kick" instead.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - win on penalties
   - dramatic penalty shoot-out
   - go to a penalty shoot-out
   - penalty shoot-out defeat
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Penalty" specifies the type of kick, while "shoot-out" highlights the one‑by‑one, sudden-death feel, forming the standard label for this format.
   
   **Root Analysis**: penalty (punishment kick) + shoot-out (series of shots to decide a contest)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The final went to a penalty shoot-out and my nerves were shot.
   - We always seem to lose in penalty shoot-outs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A penalty shoot-out introduces high psychological pressure and often appears only weakly related to overall team dominance.
   - Coaches sometimes select penalty takers based on training data and stress tolerance for the shoot-out.

1. **Expression**: man of the match (type: fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The player judged to have given the best overall performance in a particular match.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (set phrase in football and many team sports, with gender-neutral alternatives increasingly used).
   
   **Synonyms**: player of the match, MVP (Most Valuable Player)
   
   **Antonyms**: poor performer, weakest link
   
   **When to Use**: After a game, in awards, commentary, or fan discussions about standout individual performances.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In contexts aiming for gender-neutral language; use "player of the match" there.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - win man of the match
   - voted man of the match
   - clear man of the match
   - unofficial man of the match
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Man" historically referred to any player, and "of the match" specifies the time frame; the phrase became embedded in broadcasting and award ceremonies.
   
   **Root Analysis**: man (person, player) + of + the match (specific game)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our keeper was definitely man of the match—he saved everything.
   - She put in a man-of-the-match performance in midfield.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Broadcasters often announce a man‑of‑the‑match award sponsored by commercial partners.
   - Advanced metrics sometimes disagree with traditional man‑of‑the‑match selections driven by narrative moments.

1. **Expression**: season opener (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The first official match or event of a new season.
   - *Fixedness*: medium/strong; widely used in schedules and previews.
   
   **Synonyms**: opening game, first fixture, curtain-raiser
   
   **Antonyms**: season finale, last game, decider
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about the first competitive game after pre-season, or the first race/round in a new campaign.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For friendly matches or pre‑season games (use "pre-season friendly" instead).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - home season opener
   - season opener at home/away
   - televised season opener
   - high-profile season opener
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Season" sets the overall competition period, and "opener" (from "open") identifies the starting fixture, a natural pairing in sports calendars.
   
   **Root Analysis**: season (competitive year) + opener (first event)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I’ve already got tickets for the season opener.
   - The season opener is always full of surprises.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Attendance at the season opener is often boosted by marketing campaigns and fan optimism.
   - Coaches may field conservative line‑ups in the season opener while assessing player fitness.

1. **Expression**: go the distance (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal/Boxing: To complete the full scheduled number of rounds without being knocked out.
   - Figurative/Sport: To last until the end of a match, season, or challenge without giving up; to maintain performance throughout.
   - *Fixedness*: strong idiom, especially in combat sports.
   
   **Synonyms**: last the full distance, see it through, stay the course
   
   **Antonyms**: fade late, drop out, retire early
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about endurance in fights, long seasons, comebacks, or tough training plans.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For short, explosive actions with no endurance element.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - go the full distance
   - can he go the distance?
   - struggle to go the distance
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Go" signals movement or continuation, while "distance" stands for the complete scheduled length of a contest; together they capture the idea of enduring to the very end.
   
   **Root Analysis**: go (move, continue) + the distance (full length of contest)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Both fighters were exhausted but managed to go the distance.
   - Our squad might not be deep enough to go the distance this season.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Athletes with superior aerobic capacity are more likely to go the distance in high-intensity tournaments.
   - Depth in key positions enables teams to go the distance in multiple competitions simultaneously.
