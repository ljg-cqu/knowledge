1. **Noun**: match (C, pl: matches)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A game or contest between two individuals or teams in which they compete under agreed rules.
   - *(In sport coverage)* A specific fixture or event, often with an official result and statistics.
   
   **Synonyms**: game, contest, fixture
   
   **Antonyms**: training session, practice, friendly (informal, non-competitive context)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about a specific sporting contest, schedules, results, previews, or reports across almost any sport.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to a whole season, league, or informal play with no clear start/end or score (use "season," "campaign," or "pickup game" instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - league match
   - home match
   - away match
   - crucial match
   - match day
   
   **Root Analysis**: match (root: equal, peer) + Ø (no productive prefix/suffix in modern form)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English/Old Norse → from Old English *mæcca* (companion, mate) and later Old French *mache* (opponent, equal). Sports sense of "contest between opponents" developed in Early Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Are you watching the match tonight, or just checking the score later?
   - That was the best football match I’ve seen in years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The match was decided by a single defensive error in the closing minutes.
   - Ticket revenue from each home match significantly influences the club’s operating budget.

1. **Noun**: tournament (C, pl: tournaments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A series of matches played over a short period in which teams or players compete to determine an overall winner.
   - A structured competition with a bracket, groups, or round-robin format, often with trophies, ranking points, or prize money.
   
   **Synonyms**: competition, championship, event
   
   **Antonyms**: one-off match, exhibition game, regular-season fixture
   
   **When to Use**: Describing multi-day events like the World Cup, Grand Slams, playoffs, or youth weekend competitions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For a single game (use "match"), or for an entire league season spread over many months (use "league" or "season").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - knockout tournament
   - youth tournament
   - major tournament
   - tournament schedule
   - host a tournament
   
   **Root Analysis**: tour- (turn, round) + -nament (action, event)
   
   **Etymology**: Old French → from *tournoiement* (a jousting contest where knights turned and charged), from *tourner* (to turn). Meaning broadened to "organized sporting competition" in modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our team is playing in a basketball tournament this weekend.
   - She won her first professional tennis tournament last year.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Tournament formats influence competitive balance and recovery time between fixtures.
   - The club uses pre-season tournaments to test tactical systems and evaluate younger players.

1. **Noun**: league (C, pl: leagues)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An organized system in which teams play one another over a season, usually earning points for wins and draws.
   - *(By extension)* The group of teams participating in that system, often divided into levels or divisions.
   
   **Synonyms**: division, competition, association
   
   **Antonyms**: knockout cup, one-off final, friendly schedule
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about season-long competitions, standings, promotion and relegation, or long-term performance.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For short events like weekend cups or international tournaments with a clear end; avoid in purely informal settings with no structure.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - top league
   - league table
   - league title
   - league position
   - get promoted/relegated from a league
   
   **Root Analysis**: league (root: alliance) + Ø
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from Old French *ligue*, from Italian *lega*, from Latin *ligare* (to bind). Originally meant a political alliance; later extended to "group of clubs bound by shared competition rules."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - His dream is to play in the top league in Europe.
   - We’re just trying to avoid relegation from the league this season.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - League structures often employ promotion and relegation to maintain competitive intensity.
   - Financial disparities between clubs can significantly distort competitive balance within a league.

1. **Noun**: referee (C, pl: referees)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The official responsible for enforcing the rules in a match and making decisions on fouls, scores, and discipline.
   - *(In some sports)* Any neutral official who oversees play on the field, court, or ring.
   
   **Synonyms**: official, umpire, match official
   
   **Antonyms**: player, coach, fan, participant
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to the person in charge of decisions on the field, especially in football, rugby, basketball, boxing, and many team sports.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In sports where "umpire" is the standard term (e.g., tennis, baseball, cricket) unless you are generalizing to all officials.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - referee a match
   - the referee blew the whistle
   - question the referee’s decision
   - assistant referee
   - video assistant referee (VAR)
   
   **Root Analysis**: refer (to consult, appeal to) + -ee (person who receives an action)
   
   **Etymology**: Early Modern English → from "referee" meaning "one referred to for a decision" in disputes; later specialized to sports officials who decide on rule interpretations during play.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The referee gave a penalty in the last minute and everyone went crazy.
   - Fans thought the referee was too harsh with the yellow cards.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Referee bias, whether perceived or real, can influence athletes’ behavior and crowd reactions.
   - Elite referees undergo regular fitness tests and decision-making assessments throughout the season.

1. **Noun**: championship (C, pl: championships)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The highest-level contest or series of contests that determines the best team or player in a category, league, or season.
   - The state or title of being the recognized winner at the top level.
   
   **Synonyms**: title, final series, crown
   
   **Antonyms**: relegation battle, early-round exit, friendly match
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the ultimate prize in a sport—league titles, world championships, national championships, or end-of-season finals.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For minor trophies, local cups without elite status, or single ordinary matches.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - win the championship
   - defend the championship
   - championship game/final
   - championship run
   - world championship
   
   **Root Analysis**: champion (winner) + -ship (state, status)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from Old French *champion* (warrior, combatant) + English suffix *-ship*. Extended from personal status ("being a champion") to the contests that decide that status.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They finally won the championship after losing in the final last year.
   - She has three world championships to her name.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Championship victories often generate significant commercial value through sponsorships and media rights.
   - The pressure of a championship final can alter athletes’ typical decision-making patterns.

1. **Noun**: underdog (C, pl: underdogs)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A team or player that is expected to lose because they are considered weaker or less experienced than the opponent.
   - A competitor with lower odds of winning who may attract neutral support.
   
   **Synonyms**: outsider, long shot, lower seed
   
   **Antonyms**: favorite, top seed, front-runner
   
   **When to Use**: Previewing matches, discussing surprising results, storytelling around upsets and emotional support for weaker teams.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For evenly balanced contests, or when talking about long-term league performance rather than a single matchup.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - root for the underdog
   - classic underdog story
   - underdog victory
   - underdog mentality
   - underdog against a giant
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (beneath) + dog (metaphorical, one down in a fight)
   
   **Etymology**: 19th‑century English → originally from dog-fighting and mechanical saw pits, where the "underdog" worked below. Figurative sports sense "weaker side" became common in modern sports journalism.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Everyone was cheering for the underdog when they reached the final.
   - It felt amazing to win as total underdogs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Underdog teams often adopt conservative, counter-attacking strategies against technically superior opponents.
   - Media narratives frequently romanticize underdog success, reinforcing fan identification with smaller clubs.

1. **Noun**: stamina (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The physical and mental ability to sustain effort over a long period without significant loss of performance.
   - In sports science, endurance capacity supported by cardiovascular fitness and muscular efficiency.
   
   **Synonyms**: endurance, staying power, resilience
   
   **Antonyms**: fatigue, exhaustion, burnout
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing long-distance events, high-intensity schedules, late-game performance, or training goals.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For short explosive power (use "strength" or "power"), or technical skill (use "technique" or "ability").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - build stamina
   - lack of stamina
   - improve stamina
   - test a player’s stamina
   - mental stamina
   
   **Root Analysis**: stamina (root: staying power) + Ø
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → from plural of *stamen* (thread, foundation), used figuratively in English for "vital force"; meaning "ability to endure" became common in the 18th–19th centuries and is now central in sports contexts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You need serious stamina to finish a marathon.
   - My stamina really drops in the last ten minutes of the match.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - High aerobic stamina allows athletes to maintain intensity deep into competition.
   - Coaches tailor conditioning programs to match the specific stamina demands of each position.
