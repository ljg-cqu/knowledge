1. **Original Expression**: We won the match.  
   **Context**: Describing the outcome of a game.
   
   **Core Intent**: To state that your team was successful in a specific contest.
   
   **Casual**: We won the game.  
   *Characteristics*: Simple vocabulary ("won," "game"), direct, everyday phrasing.  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, teammates, on social media, or in casual fan conversations.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We secured a win in today’s match.  
   *Characteristics*: Slightly more formal verb ("secured"), explicit reference to "today’s match," neutral tone.  
   *Use when*: Post‑match interviews, club newsletters, school reports, internal updates.
   
   **Formal**: Our team emerged victorious in the fixture, achieving a positive result.  
   *Characteristics*: Elevated verbs ("emerged victorious"), more abstract noun phrase ("positive result"), impersonal tone.  
   *Use when*: Written reports, formal summaries, official season reviews.
   
   **Humorous**: We actually remembered how to score—and somehow won the whole thing!  
   *Characteristics*: Self‑deprecating humour ("remembered how to score"), exaggeration ("the whole thing"), informal tone.  
   *Use when*: Joking with teammates, light‑hearted social posts, friendly group chats.  
   *Avoid when*: Serious debriefs, professional communications, high‑stakes contexts.
   
   **Lively**: We battled from start to finish and came away with a fantastic win!  
   *Characteristics*: Energetic verbs ("battled," "came away"), strong positive adjective ("fantastic"), exclamation mark.  
   *Use when*: Motivational speeches, fan engagement, club media posts aiming to excite supporters.
   
   **Empathetic**: We worked hard for each other today and earned a win that everyone in the team can be proud of.  
   *Characteristics*: Emphasis on effort, togetherness, and shared pride ("for each other," "everyone in the team"), warm tone.  
   *Use when*: Supporting teammates emotionally, acknowledging effort after a tough period, community‑oriented messages.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Formal language for official reports; casual for peer‑to‑peer conversations.
   - *Social Distance*: Semi‑formal for public statements; casual for close friends and teammates.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: More formal when the match is part of a major competition; lively/empathetic for celebrations.
   - *Medium*: Written reports lean formal/semi‑formal; spoken and social media allow casual, humorous, and lively registers.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Over‑celebrating in professional contexts ("We smashed them!")—can be seen as disrespectful.
   - Being too dry in fan communication ("We obtained three points")—fails to engage supporters.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some sporting cultures value humble celebration; others encourage more expressive, emotional language. Adjust how strongly you praise the win depending on local norms.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Club season report → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal or Semi‑Formal.
   - **Situation 2**: Interview on local TV → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi‑Formal/Lively.
   - **Situation 3**: Message in team group chat → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual or Humorous.
   - **Situation 4**: Speech to young players and parents → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic/Lively.

1. **Original Expression**: We lost the match.  
   **Context**: Describing a defeat while managing emotions and expectations.
   
   **Core Intent**: To acknowledge a negative result while potentially framing it constructively.
   
   **Casual**: We lost today.  
   *Characteristics*: Short, direct, emotionally open; may sound flat or disappointed.  
   *Use when*: Talking honestly with friends or teammates, immediate reactions.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: We suffered a defeat today but learned a lot from the game.  
   *Characteristics*: Balanced phrasing ("suffered a defeat" + "learned a lot"), acknowledges result and growth, neutral professional tone.  
   *Use when*: Post‑match interviews, coach feedback, club statements.
   
   **Formal**: The team was unable to secure a positive result in today’s fixture, highlighting areas for further improvement.  
   *Characteristics*: Impersonal, process‑focused language; avoids emotional wording; focuses on implications.  
   *Use when*: Written evaluations, match reports, organizational reviews.
   
   **Humorous**: The scoreline wasn’t our friend today—but at least our fitness levels got a great workout.  
   *Characteristics*: Light tone, reframing loss with a playful twist; avoids harsh blame.  
   *Use when*: Informal chats, low‑stakes matches, friendly tournaments.  
   *Avoid when*: High‑stakes defeats, emotional dressing‑room moments.
   
   **Lively**: We fought hard but came up short in the final minutes.  
   *Characteristics*: Dynamic verbs ("fought," "came up short"), focuses on effort and drama, not just the result.  
   *Use when*: Communicating with fans, summarizing close games, motivating players despite loss.
   
   **Empathetic**: It’s a tough result to take, but everyone gave everything, and we’ll support each other as we bounce back.  
   *Characteristics*: Validates emotions ("tough result"), emphasizes effort and mutual support, looks forward positively.  
   *Use when*: Speaking to young athletes, addressing fans after a disappointing loss, team meetings.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Coaches and club officials often prefer semi‑formal/formal when speaking publicly; casual for inside the team.
   - *Social Distance*: The greater the distance (media, fans), the more careful and less emotional the wording.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Major finals often need empathetic but measured language; small friendly losses can accept humour.
   - *Medium*: Written statements should avoid jokes; spoken dressing‑room talks can be more emotional.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Blaming individuals publicly ("We lost because our keeper was terrible")—damages trust.
   - Being too casual after a painful loss ("No big deal")—can feel disrespectful to effort.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some cultures, public self‑criticism after defeat is expected; in others, focus stays on effort and collective responsibility. Adjust tone accordingly.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Press conference after a derby defeat → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi‑Formal/Empathetic.
   - **Situation 2**: Talk with youth players after their first loss → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic.
   - **Situation 3**: Tweet after a friendly → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Humorous.
   - **Situation 4**: Official season review document → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal.

1. **Original Expression**: The team is under pressure to win the title.  
   **Context**: Describing expectations and stress around performance.
   
   **Core Intent**: To express that strong external or internal expectations are pushing the team to achieve a specific goal.
   
   **Casual**: Everyone expects us to win the league this year.  
   *Characteristics*: Everyday vocabulary ("everyone expects"), direct statement of pressure, informal tone.  
   *Use when*: Conversations among fans or players, podcasts, casual previews.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: There’s a lot of pressure on the team to win the championship this season.  
   *Characteristics*: Clear mention of "pressure," neutral phrasing, accessible vocabulary.  
   *Use when*: Media discussions, expert panels, club communications.
   
   **Formal**: The squad faces significant expectations to secure the championship, creating substantial psychological pressure.  
   *Characteristics*: Technical phrasing ("significant expectations," "psychological pressure"), more analytical tone.  
   *Use when*: Sports psychology reports, analytical articles, academic discussions.
   
   **Humorous**: No pressure at all—just the whole season, the fans, and the club’s history resting on our shoulders.  
   *Characteristics*: Irony ("no pressure at all"), exaggeration for comic effect, informal.  
   *Use when*: Lightening the mood, friendly interviews, informal team talks.  
   *Avoid when*: Players are genuinely overwhelmed or struggling with anxiety.
   
   **Lively**: The race for the title is intense, and every match feels like a final for this team.  
   *Characteristics*: Dramatic metaphors ("race," "feels like a final"), energetic tone.  
   *Use when*: Commentaries, build‑up pieces, fan engagement content.
   
   **Empathetic**: The players are dealing with huge expectations, so they need support, patience, and understanding from everyone around them.  
   *Characteristics*: Focus on emotional impact, calls for supportive behaviour, softening judgement.  
   *Use when*: Addressing fans, parents, club communities, or youth environments.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Analysts and psychologists use semi‑formal/formal; teammates may speak casually among themselves.
   - *Social Distance*: The more public the audience, the more careful and neutral the wording about pressure.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Title races and relegation battles require sensitive, empathetic framing.
   - *Medium*: Academic writing stresses "psychological pressure"; fan media often prefers lively metaphors.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Romanticizing unhealthy pressure ("Real champions love being crushed by expectations")—can normalise burnout.
   - Ignoring mental health impacts when discussing pressure.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some cultures expect athletes to openly embrace pressure; others encourage balance and mental well‑being. Phrasing can signal whether pressure is seen as positive challenge or harmful burden.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Sports psychology report → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal.
   - **Situation 2**: TV studio discussion → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi‑Formal/Lively.
   - **Situation 3**: Pre‑match team talk → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic/Lively.
   - **Situation 4**: Fan podcast → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual/Humorous.

1. **Original Expression**: He got injured during the game.  
   **Context**: Describing an injury in a sensitive and accurate way.
   
   **Core Intent**: To communicate that a player suffered a physical problem while competing.
   
   **Casual**: He picked up an injury in the game.  
   *Characteristics*: Common sports phrase ("picked up an injury"), softens impact slightly, everyday tone.  
   *Use when*: Talking with friends, fan discussions, basic commentary.
   
   **Semi-Formal**: He suffered an injury during the match and had to come off.  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral description ("suffered an injury"), mentions consequence ("had to come off"), professional tone.  
   *Use when*: Match reports, coach interviews, club updates.
   
   **Formal**: He sustained an injury during the fixture and will undergo further medical assessment.  
   *Characteristics*: Clinical verb ("sustained"), references medical process, impersonal tone.  
   *Use when*: Official medical statements, formal reports, press releases.
   
   **Humorous**: He seems to have injured his pride more than anything else.  
   *Characteristics*: Downplays physical harm, focuses on ego, obviously only suitable for minor knocks.  
   *Use when*: Light knocks, friendly atmosphere, clear that the player is fine.  
   *Avoid when*: Serious injuries, head injuries, long‑term issues.
   
   **Lively**: In the middle of a fierce challenge, he went down awkwardly and couldn’t continue.  
   *Characteristics*: Vivid description ("fierce challenge," "went down awkwardly"), paints a picture, emotionally engaging.  
   *Use when*: Commentary, written match narratives, documentaries.
   
   **Empathetic**: He was injured during the game, and our first priority now is his health and recovery.  
   *Characteristics*: Puts health above results, caring tone, acknowledges human side.  
   *Use when*: Addressing teammates, fans, families, or youth players.
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Medical staff and officials use formal wording; teammates use casual/empathetic language.
   - *Social Distance*: Public statements are usually semi‑formal/formal; internal team chats are more personal.
   - *Gravity of Situation*: The more serious the injury, the more careful and formal the language.
   - *Medium*: Live commentary can be lively but should avoid jokes when injury is unclear.
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Joking about potentially serious injuries.
   - Speculating publicly about diagnoses without confirmation.
   
   **Cultural Notes**: In some sports cultures, playing through pain is praised; in others, health protection is strongly emphasized. Choice of words can reflect or challenge those norms.
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Official club statement → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal.
   - **Situation 2**: Coach talking to youth team → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic.
   - **Situation 3**: TV commentator → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi‑Formal/Lively.
   - **Situation 4**: Fans chatting online → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual.
