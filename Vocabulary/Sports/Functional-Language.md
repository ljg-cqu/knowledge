1. **Functional Expression**: Great game today. (function: praising/acknowledging effort)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to congratulate someone on their overall performance in a match, focusing on effort and quality rather than just the result.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct praise
   
   **Synonyms**: Well played., You had a great game., That was an excellent performance., You were outstanding today.
   
   **Antonyms**: That was disappointing., You didn’t play well., I’m not happy with that performance.
   
   **When to Use**: After matches with teammates, opponents, or students to recognise effort and build rapport; in coaching, fan interactions, or peer feedback.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Immediately after a painful loss where the person is clearly upset and not ready to talk; when critical, specific feedback is required instead of general praise.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Great game today, everyone.
   - You had a great game out there.
   - Really great game from you in the second half.
   - That was a great game to watch.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Praises performance without going into technical detail; supports motivation and team culture; appropriate even when the result was not ideal if the effort was strong.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks, I appreciate it.
   - Yeah, we worked hard today.
   - I’m happy with how we played.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Great game today—you ran nonstop.
   - Hey, great game! That assist was unreal.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Great game today. Your decision-making under pressure was noticeably improved.
   - Great game this afternoon; your positioning between the lines was excellent.

1. **Functional Expression**: Come on, you’ve got this. (function: encouraging/motivating)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Encourages a player or team to keep going, expressing belief in their ability to succeed in the next action or phase of play.
   - *Formality*: informal
   - *Directness*: direct encouragement
   
   **Synonyms**: Keep going!, You can do this., Stay with it., Don’t give up now., Believe in yourself.
   
   **Antonyms**: You’ll never manage this., Just give up., There’s no point.
   
   **When to Use**: During tough phases of training or matches, before crucial plays (penalties, free throws, serves), or when someone looks discouraged or tired.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal team talks or analysis sessions where more specific language is expected; when the player needs concrete tactical instructions instead of emotional support.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Come on, you can do it.
   - Come on, let’s go!
   - You’ve got this—trust your training.
   - You’ve got this, just relax and hit your shot.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Relies on emotional support rather than technical detail; works best when relationship is close and tone is positive, not sarcastic; often used by teammates, coaches, and fans.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, I’ll give it everything.
   - Thanks, that helps.
   - I’ll focus and do my best.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - One more sprint—come on, you’ve got this.
   - Come on, you’ve got this! Just hit it like you do in practice.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Immediately before a key attempt, the coach added quietly, "You’ve got this—trust your routine."
   - Teammates often use short encouragements such as "Come on, you’ve got this" to regulate group morale.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let’s stick to the game plan. (function: aligning/redirecting strategy)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Reminds the team to follow the agreed tactics instead of reacting emotionally or chaotically to the current score or pressure.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect directive framed as shared decision ("let’s").
   
   **Synonyms**: Stay with the plan., Let’s keep to what we trained., Let’s play our football., Let’s keep our shape.
   
   **Antonyms**: Forget the plan., Just do whatever you want., Ignore the tactics.
   
   **When to Use**: When the team is under pressure, has conceded a goal, or is tempted to abandon structure and rush forward without thinking; in time‑outs or on‑field leadership moments.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: If the plan is clearly failing and genuine tactical change is needed; when higher authority (head coach) has already changed the strategy.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Stick to the game plan, please.
   - Remember the plan and trust it.
   - Let’s get back to our game plan.
   - Let’s trust the plan and stay calm.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Encourages discipline while preserving players’ sense of shared control; good for captains and coaches managing emotions on the field; often paired with brief, concrete reminders ("stay compact," "press together").
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Got it, we’ll calm down.
   - Okay, let’s reset and follow it.
   - Right, let’s get back to what we trained.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re panicking—let’s stick to the game plan and the chances will come.
   - Guys, stick to the game plan, don’t just chase the ball.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - At half‑time the coach urged the players to stick to the game plan rather than chasing the match individually.
   - Leaders on the pitch frequently use phrases like "Let’s stick to the game plan" to stabilise team behaviour.

1. **Functional Expression**: What went well, and what didn’t? (function: prompting reflection/feedback)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Invites players to analyse performance by identifying positives and areas for improvement; encourages balanced reflection instead of pure criticism.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect request for self‑evaluation.
   
   **Synonyms**: Let’s talk about what worked and what didn’t., How do you feel about that performance?, What should we keep and what should we change?, What did you like and what needs work?
   
   **Antonyms**: That was just bad., We don’t need to talk about it., There’s nothing to learn here.
   
   **When to Use**: In post‑match or post‑training debriefs, one‑to‑one coaching sessions, or leadership meetings; when building a reflective, learning‑focused culture.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Immediately after an emotionally painful defeat when players are too upset to think clearly—allow some cooling time first; when a quick decision is required and there is no time for discussion.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What went well today, and what do we need to fix?
   - What worked and what didn’t out there?
   - What should we keep doing, and what should we change?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Encourages balanced thinking (strengths + weaknesses) and gives players a voice; supports growth mindset rather than blame; useful for building trust and autonomy.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Our pressing went well, but our finishing didn’t.
   - I felt strong physically, but my decision‑making wasn’t great.
   - Communication was better, but our shape broke down late on.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Okay, team, what went well and what didn’t? Be honest.
   - From your side, what went well and what didn’t in that half?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analyst began the session with, "What went well, and what didn’t?" to elicit player‑led reflections.
   - Structured debriefs often start with prompts such as "What went well and what didn’t?" before introducing video clips.

1. **Functional Expression**: Take a deep breath and focus. (function: calming/regaining concentration)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Helps a player manage nerves or frustration by pausing and redirecting attention to the next action.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct instruction softened by supportive tone.
   
   **Synonyms**: Slow down and reset., Calm down and concentrate., Center yourself and think about the next play., Let’s pause and refocus.
   
   **Antonyms**: Hurry up!, Just ignore your nerves., Keep panicking., Don’t think—just do something.
   
   **When to Use**: Before penalties, free throws, serves, or any high‑pressure action; after a mistake that has rattled a player; during time‑outs or short breaks in play.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the main issue is tactical misunderstanding rather than nerves; when the player is injured or clearly needs physical, not psychological, support.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Take a breath and focus on the next play.
   - Breathe, reset, and focus.
   - Take a deep breath, block everything out, and trust your technique.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Combines a simple physical action (breathing) with a mental instruction (focus); signals empathy and support instead of criticism; often used quietly, one‑to‑one, in close physical proximity.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Okay, I’ll reset.
   - Right, I’ll focus on the next one.
   - Thanks, I needed that.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Take a deep breath and focus—it’s just another shot.
   - Hey, breathe and focus. Forget the last mistake.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The sports psychologist reminded the athlete to "take a deep breath and focus" before each attempt.
   - Coaches often instruct players to "take a deep breath and focus" as a simple in‑game regulation strategy.
