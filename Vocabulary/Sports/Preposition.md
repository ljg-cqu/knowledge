1. **Preposition**: against (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial/Relational)* In opposition to another team or player in a contest.
   - *(Abstract)* Versus; competing with; facing as an opponent.
   
   **Synonyms**: versus (vs), opposite, in opposition to
   
   **Antonyms**: with (on the same side as), for (representing)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about fixtures ("play against"), matchups, or individual duels within a game.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For cooperation with teammates (use "with" or "alongside") or for representing a team (use "for").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: against their rivals, against the top seed
   - **Verb + prep**: play against, defend against, compete against, score against
   - **Adjective + prep**: strong against, weak against
   - **Noun + prep**: match against, record against
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (toward) + -gainst (opposite)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → from *ongegn* (toward, opposite), later "again" + "-st". In sports, now the standard preposition for naming opponents.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re playing against the league leaders tomorrow.
   - He always raises his game against big clubs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The team has struggled against high-pressing opponents.
   - Their defensive structure is particularly effective against counter‑attacking sides.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Representing a team, club, or country as a player or coach.
   - *(Purpose)* With the aim of achieving a particular result or statistic (goals, points, records).
   
   **Synonyms**: on behalf of, in the service of
   
   **Antonyms**: against (as opponent), without (lacking purpose)
   
   **When to Use**: Saying who a player represents ("play for"), or what they are trying to achieve ("play for a win").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For opposition (use "against"), or precise locations ("at" the stadium, "in" the league).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for the club, for the national team, for the win
   - **Verb + prep**: play for, sign for, cheer for, fight for
   - **Noun + prep**: support for the team, race for promotion
   
   **Root Analysis**: for (reason, purpose) + Ø
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → *for* (before, on account of). The sense of "on behalf of" and "in favour of" underlies sports uses like "play for" and "cheer for."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She plays for the national team now.
   - We’re really going for the win today, not just a draw.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Athletes selected for their country often face increased media scrutiny.
   - Clubs competing for promotion tend to invest heavily in experienced players.

1. **Preposition**: over (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Throughout a period of time, especially a season or career, when assessing performance.
   - *(Relational)* Better than in a comparison: victory over a particular opponent.
   
   **Synonyms**: across (time), during, above (in ranking context)
   
   **Antonyms**: under (below in ranking), behind
   
   **When to Use**: Describing performance over seasons, changes over time, or wins over named opponents.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For exact locations on a surface (use "on"), or movement through space ("across" or "through").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: over the season, over the years, over three sets
   - **Verb + prep**: win over, improve over, decline over
   - **Noun + prep**: control over the game, dominance over opponents
   
   **Root Analysis**: over (above, beyond) + Ø
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → *ofer* (above, across). Sports uses extend the idea of "across time" or "in control above others."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our fitness has improved a lot over the season.
   - They earned a famous win over the defending champions.
   
   **Examples [Formal**]: 
   - Performance metrics were tracked over a ten‑month competitive period.
   - The club has established clear superiority over local rivals in recent years.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* To the inside of a goal, court, or scoring area.
   - *(Abstract)* Moving from one state or phase into another (e.g., from defence into attack).
   
   **Synonyms**: inside, within (spatial), toward (change of state)
   
   **Antonyms**: out of, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the ball going into the net, players moving into space, or transitions into different phases of play.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For static positions already inside an area (use "in"), or surface contact ("on").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into the box, into space, into the net
   - **Verb + prep**: cross into, move into, break into, get into position
   - **Noun + prep**: transition into attack, change into top gear
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (inside) + to (direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English compound *into*, combining "in" + directional "to"; in sports it often marks decisive movements or scoring actions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He smashed the ball into the top corner.
   - The winger made a great run into the box.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Midfielders are encouraged to move into advanced half‑spaces when possession is secure.
   - Quick transition into attack can exploit disorganized defensive structures.

1. **Preposition**: throughout (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* During the whole of a period, such as a match, season, or career.
   - *(Spatial)* In every part of an area, stadium, or organization.
   
   **Synonyms**: all through, across, during the whole of
   
   **Antonyms**: briefly, occasionally (temporal), in one area only (spatial)
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing consistency or distribution: effort throughout a game, injuries throughout a squad, support throughout the stadium.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For single moments (use "during" or "at"), or isolated areas.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: throughout the match, throughout the season, throughout the stadium
   - **Verb + prep**: perform throughout, struggle throughout, dominate throughout
   - **Noun + prep**: consistency throughout the year, support throughout the club
   
   **Root Analysis**: through (across) + out (completely)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English → from "through" + "out," originally meaning "from one side to the other completely." Sports contexts extend this to time and performance.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The crowd was loud throughout the whole match.
   - She played consistently well throughout the tournament.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The team maintained high pressing intensity throughout the ninety minutes.
   - Injury problems persisted throughout the campaign, limiting rotation options.
