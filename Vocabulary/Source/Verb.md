# Source - Verbs

1. **Verb**: source
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To obtain something from a particular place, person, organization, or system, especially materials, data, or information.
   - (Transitive) To identify and document where information, evidence, or content originally came from.
   
   **Synonyms**: obtain, procure, acquire, get from, draw from
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, discard, detach from, disconnect from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing how you get data, materials, quotes, or evidence from specific places or providers, especially in research, journalism, and procurement.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For casual "getting" that does not care about origin (prefer "get" or "buy"), or when you emphasize *creating* information (prefer "produce," "generate").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - source data from
   - source materials from
   - responsibly source
   - locally source
   - independently source
   
   **Root Analysis**: From the noun "source" (origin, starting point) used as a verb meaning "to obtain from a source."
   
   **Etymology**: Verbal use developed in 20th-century business English ("to source components"), later expanded to data and information ("to source data from multiple systems").
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to source better data before we trust these charts.
   - They sourced most of the information from government reports and public APIs.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study sourced its primary data from national administrative records and two longitudinal surveys.
   - To reduce bias, we sourced independent expert opinions from three unrelated organizations.

1. **Verb**: cite
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To mention a source formally in writing or speech as evidence or support for a statement.
   - (Transitive) To refer to a specific document, study, or authority by name.
   
   **Synonyms**: reference, quote, mention, attribute to, point to
   
   **Antonyms**: omit, ignore, withhold, plagiarize (by not citing)
   
   **When to Use**: In academic, professional, or journalistic contexts when you explicitly point to the work that supports a claim.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very informal conversation where people rarely list formal sources, or when you physically summon someone (legal "cite" is different).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cite a source
   - correctly cite
   - cite the evidence
   - widely cited
   - cite in your paper
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *citare* (to call, to summon) â†’ "to call" a source into the discussion.
   
   **Etymology**: Originally a legal term meaning "to summon"; the scholarly sense of "to reference as an authority" is recorded from the 17th century onward.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If you use that statistic, make sure you cite where it came from.
   - She cited three different studies to back up her argument.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Authors must cite all data sources and prior work that substantially influenced their analysis.
   - The report explicitly cites its methodological guidelines in an extensive reference section.

1. **Verb**: reference
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To mention or point to a source, document, or piece of information so that others can find it.
   - (Transitive) To include citations or links that show where information originated.
   
   **Synonyms**: refer to, allude to, point to, mention, cite
   
   **Antonyms**: detach, isolate, omit, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about adding links, footnotes, or bibliographic entries that connect a statement to its underlying sources.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you mean *consult* a source (prefer "look up" or "check"), or when you talk about general background without any specific pointer.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reference the source
   - explicitly reference
   - reference in the appendix
   - properly referenced
   - cross-reference
   
   **Root Analysis**: From noun "reference" (act of referring) â†’ verb "to reference" (to provide a reference).
   
   **Etymology**: Verb use became common in 20th-century technical and academic English, especially in documentation and standards.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Please reference the original article instead of just copying the chart.
   - The slides reference several external reports if you want more detail.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All externally obtained datasets are referenced in the data availability statement.
   - The policy brief carefully references both primary legislation and secondary guidance documents.

1. **Verb**: attribute
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To say or show that a statement, idea, or piece of information comes from a particular person or source.
   - (Transitive) To credit authorship or responsibility to the correct origin.
   
   **Synonyms**: credit, ascribe, assign, trace to, pin on
   
   **Antonyms**: misattribute, detach, anonymize, obscure
   
   **When to Use**: Describing who said or wrote something, who provided data, or which source is responsible for a claim.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you talk about *causes* in a technical sense ("attributable risk" is specialized), or when you simply mention a source without assigning responsibility.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - attribute a quote to
   - correctly attribute
   - attribute the claim to
   - attribute the data to
   - misattribute a statement
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *ad-* (to) + *tribuere* (to assign, to give) â†’ "to assign to."
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 15th century meaning "to regard as caused by"; the media and research sense of crediting a statement to a source is a natural extension.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Always attribute quotes to the person who actually said them.
   - The article wrongly attributed that comment to the CEO instead of the spokesperson.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Findings are explicitly attributed to their original authors in the literature review.
   - The report attributes the revenue estimates to a confidential internal source.

1. **Verb**: verify
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To check that a piece of information, data point, or claim is accurate, often by consulting independent or primary sources.
   - (Transitive) To confirm the authenticity or reliability of a source.
   
   **Synonyms**: confirm, validate, check, cross-check, authenticate
   
   **Antonyms**: assume, accept blindly, overlook, ignore
   
   **When to Use**: When you describe the process of checking whether sources and statements are true, consistent, and well supported.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you simply *read* or *collect* sources without evaluating them, or when casual guessing is acceptable.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - verify a source
   - verify the data
   - independently verify
   - verify claims
   - verify authenticity
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *verus* (true) + *facere* (to make) â†’ *verificare* (to prove true).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 15th century meaning "to prove to be true," now widely used in research, auditing, and fact-checking.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Before you share that screenshot, verify that itâ€™s real.
   - We still need to verify the numbers with the original source.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All key variables were verified against independent administrative sources where possible.
   - Journalists are expected to verify claims from anonymous sources before publication.

1. **Verb**: corroborate
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To support or strengthen a statement by providing additional, independent evidence or sources that agree with it.
   
   **Synonyms**: confirm, support, back up, substantiate, reinforce
   
   **Antonyms**: contradict, undermine, refute, discredit
   
   **When to Use**: When multiple sources align to support the same conclusion, especially in investigative work, research, and audits.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When there is only a single, unverified source, or when evidence clearly goes in the opposite direction.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - corroborate a story
   - corroborate a claim
   - corroborate with documents
   - independently corroborate
   - corroborating evidence
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *cor-* (together) + *roborare* (to strengthen) from *robur* (strength).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 15th century with legal and evidential usesâ€”"to make more certain by additional proof."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Two other witnesses corroborated what the original source said.
   - We found documents that corroborate the numbers in the spreadsheet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Interview data corroborate the patterns observed in administrative records.
   - Independent laboratories were engaged to corroborate the original experimental results.

1. **Verb**: cross-check
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To compare information from one source with information from other, independent sources to see if they agree.
   
   **Synonyms**: double-check, compare, validate across, triangulate, verify
   
   **Antonyms**: accept uncritically, overlook, ignore inconsistencies
   
   **When to Use**: Describing careful verification routines where you do not rely on a single source, especially in data analysis and fact-checking.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When there is only one available source, or when quick, low-stakes decisions do not justify extra effort.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - cross-check the data
   - cross-check against
   - cross-check multiple sources
   - cross-check manually
   - automatically cross-check
   
   **Root Analysis**: "cross" (across, intersecting) + "check" (to examine).
   
   **Etymology**: Industrial and aviation safety jargon in the 20th century; widely adopted in research and journalism to mean "verify across sources."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Letâ€™s cross-check those dates with the original emails.
   - I always cross-check social media rumors with at least one reputable outlet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Survey responses were cross-checked against administrative records to detect inconsistencies.
   - We cross-check model outputs with external benchmarks derived from independent data sources.

1. **Verb**: curate

   **Synonyms**: select, organize, assemble, compile, edit

   **When to Use**: When emphasizing thoughtful selection and organization of sources for a reading list, dataset collection, or knowledge base.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For raw scraping or indiscriminate collection of material, or when content is generated from scratch rather than gathered.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - curate sources
   - curated dataset
   - carefully curated
   - curate a reading list

   **Root Analysis**: Latin *curare* (to care for) â†’ *curator* (guardian) â†’ "curate" (to organize with care).

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The research team curated a benchmark corpus from multiple public data sources with strict inclusion criteria.
   - Curated source lists help new analysts navigate a fragmented information landscape.

1. **Verb**: misquote
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To quote a source incorrectly by changing the words, leaving out crucial context, or attributing a statement to the wrong person.
   
   **Synonyms**: distort, quote inaccurately, misrepresent, garble
   
   **Antonyms**: quote accurately, represent faithfully, reproduce correctly
   
   **When to Use**: Describing errors or misconduct when someone reports what a source said in a misleading or incorrect way.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the problem is choosing poor sources (prefer "misjudge" or "mis-evaluate"), or when you refer to paraphrasing rather than quoting.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - misquote a source
   - badly misquoted
   - repeatedly misquoted
   - feel misquoted
   - misquote out of context
   
   **Root Analysis**: "mis-" (wrongly) + "quote" (to repeat someones words).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 17th century; the media sense of misrepresenting sources is now central.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The article misquoted her and made it sound harsher than what she actually said.
   - If you misquote your sources, people will stop trusting your work.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The transcript reveals that the speaker was misquoted in several key passages.
   - Researchers must avoid selectively quoting or misquoting sources in ways that distort the original meaning.
