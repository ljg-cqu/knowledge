# Source - Adverbs

1. **Adverb**: directly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a way that involves no intermediaries; from the original source without passing through other channels.
   - *Position*: Usually mid-position (before main verb) or end-position.
   
   **Synonyms**: straight, immediately, first-hand, without intermediaries
   
   **Antonyms**: indirectly, second-hand, via intermediaries
   
   **When to Use**: Describing information or data obtained straight from the original source, or communication without middle layers.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For physical direction only ("walk directly ahead") unless context is clearly about information flow.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - directly from the source
   - accessed directly
   - cite directly
   - quote directly
   - obtained directly
   
   **Root Analysis**: direct (straight, without deviation) + -ly (adverb suffix).
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *directus* (straight, right); adverbial form used since Middle English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Whenever I can, I read the research directly instead of relying on summaries.
   - We got the numbers directly from the official database.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - All identifiers were obtained directly from administrative registers under data-sharing agreements.
   - The report quotes directly from primary documents archived in the national library.

1. **Adverb**: independently (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Without influence or control from the original parties; using separate methods or sources.
   - *Position*: Usually mid-position or end-position.
   
   **Synonyms**: separately, autonomously, on ones own, without coordination
   
   **Antonyms**: jointly, collaboratively, dependently, under direction
   
   **When to Use**: When verification, replication, or confirmation is done using different, unconnected sources or teams.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For personal life situations unrelated to evidence or research, unless context makes it clear.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - independently verify
   - independently confirm
   - independently collected
   - analyzed independently
   - independently replicated
   
   **Root Analysis**: independent (not depending on others) + -ly.
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *independens* (not hanging from) via French; adverb widely used in scientific and journalistic contexts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Two teams independently came to the same conclusion using different data.
   - We tried to independently check what the article claimed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The findings were independently replicated by three research groups using distinct data sources.
   - Key statistics were independently verified against external administrative datasets.

1. **Adverb**: allegedly (type: sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - According to what has been claimed but not yet proven or officially confirmed; used to distance the speaker from the truth of a statement.
   - *Position*: Usually front-position (Allegedly, ...), or mid-position after the subject.
   
   **Synonyms**: reportedly, supposedly, apparently (in some uses)
   
   **Antonyms**: demonstrably, conclusively, unquestionably
   
   **When to Use**: Reporting what sources say when the facts are disputed, under investigation, or lacking solid evidence.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For well-established facts, or when you intend to assert something strongly.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - allegedly leaked
   - allegedly from
   - allegedly said
   - allegedly based on
   - allegedly occurred
   
   **Root Analysis**: alleged (claimed, asserted) + -ly.
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *alegier* (to justify, to clear); legal English developed "allegedly" for unproven accusations.
   
   **Examples [Casual**]: 
   - The email was allegedly from an insider, but nobody has confirmed it yet.
   - Several documents allegedly show that the data were changed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report describes events that allegedly occurred between 2018 and 2020 according to multiple unnamed sources.
   - Allegedly leaked records were not included until their authenticity could be evaluated.

1. **Adverb**: reportedly (type: sentence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - According to reports from other sources; used to signal that the information comes from prior reporting rather than direct observation.
   - *Position*: Typically front-position or after the subject.
   
   **Synonyms**: according to reports, by some accounts, it is reported that
   
   **Antonyms**: personally, directly, unquestionably
   
   **When to Use**: Summarizing what news outlets or other intermediaries have said, especially when you have not checked the primary sources.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you are the primary source or when an official statement has already confirmed the facts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reportedly from
   - reportedly based on
   - reportedly said
   - reportedly leaked
   - reportedly came from
   
   **Root Analysis**: report (to give an account) + -edly.
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 17th century; very common in journalism to indicate second-hand information.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The data reportedly came from an internal dashboard that isnt public.
   - Several outlets reportedly used the same anonymous source.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The incident reportedly originated from a misconfigured data export according to local media.
   - The memo, reportedly leaked last week, has not yet been authenticated.

1. **Adverb**: originally (type: time)
   **Meaning**: 
   - At first; in the beginning; from the first source or version.
   - *Position*: Flexibleâ€”front, mid, or end-position.
   
   **Synonyms**: at first, initially, in the beginning, in its original form
   
   **Antonyms**: finally, ultimately, eventually, later
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about how sources or texts have changed from their first form, or where material first appeared.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you are only describing the present state without any contrast to the past.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - originally published
   - originally from
   - originally appeared
   - originally written
   - originally collected
   
   **Root Analysis**: original (first form) + -ly.
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *origo* (beginning) via French; adverb used in English since the 15th century.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The story was originally published in a small local paper.
   - The dataset was originally collected for a different project.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The tables were originally derived from administrative sources before being harmonized.
   - These results were originally reported in a conference proceeding and later expanded in a journal article.

1. **Adverb**: explicitly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - In a clear and detailed way that leaves no room for confusion about the source or evidence.
   - *Position*: Usually mid-position or end-position.
   
   **Synonyms**: clearly, expressly, plainly, unambiguously
   
   **Antonyms**: implicitly, vaguely, obliquely, indirectly
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing that sources, limitations, or assumptions are clearly named rather than hinted at.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When subtlety or anonymity is required, or when source details cannot be disclosed.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - explicitly cite
   - explicitly state
   - explicitly attribute
   - explicitly acknowledge
   - explicitly documented
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *explicitus* (unfolded, clear) + -ly.
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 17th century; common in academic and legal writing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The post doesnt explicitly say where the numbers came from.
   - Always explicitly mention your main data sources.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The methodology section explicitly describes all external data sources and access procedures.
   - Assumptions about source reliability are explicitly acknowledged in the limitations.

1. **Adverb**: anonymously (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Without revealing a name or identity; in a way that keeps the source unknown to the audience.
   - *Position*: Usually end-position or front-position for emphasis.
   
   **Synonyms**: without attribution, unnamed, confidentially, off the record (overlaps)
   
   **Antonyms**: openly, on the record, publicly, by name
   
   **When to Use**: Describing how people share information, provide feedback, or act as sources while protecting their identity.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the source is known and named, or when anonymity would violate ethical or legal requirements.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - speak anonymously
   - comment anonymously
   - submit anonymously
   - reported anonymously
   - shared anonymously
   
   **Root Analysis**: anonymous (without name) + -ly.
   
   **Etymology**: From Greek *an-* (without) + *onyma* (name); adverb used widely in media and research ethics discussions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Several employees spoke anonymously about the issue.
   - You can fill out the survey anonymously if you prefer.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants could respond anonymously to reduce social desirability bias.
   - Sources who requested to speak anonymously were granted confidentiality under agreed conditions.

1. **Adverb**: indirectly (type: manner)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not straight from the original source but through one or more intermediaries; in a roundabout way.
   - *Position*: Usually mid-position or end-position.
   
   **Synonyms**: second-hand, via intermediaries, through others, obliquely
   
   **Antonyms**: directly, first-hand, immediately
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining that information comes from summaries, retellings, or reprocessed datasets rather than from primary sources.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the pathway from source to statement is clear and direct.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - indirectly from
   - indirectly reported
   - indirectly based on
   - indirectly influenced by
   - heard indirectly
   
   **Root Analysis**: indirect (not straight, not direct) + -ly.
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 16th century; often used in logical and causal contexts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We only heard about the policy change indirectly through social media.
   - The chart is indirectly based on census data that was cleaned by another team.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many indicators rely indirectly on self-reported information from earlier surveys.
   - The estimate is indirectly derived from multiple secondary sources rather than primary measurement.
