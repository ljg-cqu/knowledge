# Source - Prepositions

1. **Preposition**: from (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract/Source)* Indicating the origin or starting point of information, data, or materials.
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating the place where something begins or is taken.
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating the starting point in time for a record, series, or source.
   
   **Synonyms**: out of (contextual), originating in, coming out of
   
   **Antonyms**: to, into, toward
   
   **When to Use**: Describing where information, datasets, quotes, or resources originate: "data from surveys," "quote from the report."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you describe destination only (prefer "to"), or when you need a more specific relationship ("according to," "based on").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from the source, from official records, from multiple datasets, from interviews
   - **Verb + prep**: sourced from, derived from, quoted from, adapted from
   - **Adjective + prep**: different from, separate from, independent from
   - **Noun + prep**: data from X, evidence from Y, feedback from users
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *fram* (forward, away from).
   
   **Etymology**: A very old Germanic preposition; the sense of "origin of information" is a natural extension.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Most of our numbers come from last years annual report.
   - This quote is from the original interview, not the summary.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis uses microdata from three independent administrative sources.
   - All statistics are calculated from publicly available datasets released by the national bureau.

1. **Preposition**: according to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* As stated by; in agreement with the authority or information of a particular source.
   
   **Synonyms**: as reported by, as stated in, in the view of, per (more formal)
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, against, despite
   
   **When to Use**: Introducing what a particular person, document, dataset, or outlet says; a standard way to attribute information.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you are expressing your own conclusion (prefer "in our view"), or when attribution is unnecessary.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: according to the report, according to the data, according to experts
   - **Verb + prep**: report according to, group according to
   - Often placed at the beginning of a clause to foreground the source.
   
   **Root Analysis**: according (in agreement with) + to.
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *acorder* (to agree); "according to" has long been used to introduce authorities.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to this article, the dataset has serious gaps.
   - According to the original source, the quote has been shortened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to official statistics, unemployment fell by two percentage points.
   - According to the primary sources examined, earlier estimates were overstated.

1. **Preposition**: based on (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Using something as the main source, foundation, or starting point for an analysis, statement, or decision.
   
   **Synonyms**: grounded in, derived from, built on, drawing on
   
   **Antonyms**: independent of, unrelated to, ignoring
   
   **When to Use**: Explaining which data, documents, or theories you used as the foundation for your conclusions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the connection is weak or merely inspirational (prefer "inspired by" or "informed by"), or when there is no clear underlying source.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: based on data from, based on interviews with, based on the report
   - **Verb + prep**: estimate based on, decide based on, classify based on
   - Often follows past participles: computed based on, designed based on
   
   **Root Analysis**: base (foundation) + -ed (having) + on.
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *basis* (foundation); the phrasal preposition developed in modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - These graphs are based on last years sales data.
   - The article seems to be based on just one questionable source.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Estimates are based on survey microdata combined with administrative tax records.
   - The recommendations are based on systematic reviews of high-quality primary sources.

1. **Preposition**: via (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* By means of; through the route or channel of a particular medium or intermediary.
   - *(Spatial)* By way of; going through a certain place.
   
   **Synonyms**: through, by way of, through the channel of, using
   
   **Antonyms**: directly (in contrast), without
   
   **When to Use**: Describing how information or data travels or is accessed, especially through APIs, platforms, or intermediaries.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very informal speech where "through" sounds more natural, or when the route is irrelevant.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: via an API, via email, via a third party, via scraping
   - **Verb + prep**: access via, share via, route via
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *via* (road, way).
   
   **Etymology**: Borrowed directly from Latin into English; widely used in technical writing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We pulled the data via their public API.
   - I got the document via a link someone sent me.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Usage metrics were collected via instrumented client applications.
   - External data were obtained via secure file transfer from partner institutions.

1. **Preposition**: through (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* By the means, instrument, or process of; using a particular method or intermediary source.
   - *(Spatial)* Moving in one side and out the other of something.
   
   **Synonyms**: by, by means of, using, via
   
   **Antonyms**: without, around (avoiding), outside
   
   **When to Use**: Describing how you gained access to sources ("through a contact," "through a partner"), or how information was filtered.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the process is trivial or identical to "from" without any intermediate step.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: through interviews, through an intermediary, through a data broker
   - **Verb + prep**: obtain through, verify through, learn through
   - **Noun + prep**: access through partners, insight through logs
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *thurh* (from side to side).
   
   **Etymology**: Ancient Germanic preposition; the sense of "by means of" is long established.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We learned about the issue through a friend who works there.
   - Most of our sources came through a single contact.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Sensitive documents were accessed through a secure archival interface.
   - Additional context was obtained through follow-up interviews with key informants.

1. **Preposition**: by (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Agency)* Indicating who created, provided, or reported a source.
   - *(Proximity/Attribution)* Indicating attachment to a particular author or outlet.
   
   **Synonyms**: authored by, reported by, produced by, prepared by
   
   **Antonyms**: anonymous (no "by" line), unattributed
   
   **When to Use**: When naming the author or provider of information: "a report by the ministry," "data by the research team."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you emphasize origin place (prefer "from"), or when the actor is unknown.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by the author, by the researcher, by the agency
   - **Verb + prep**: compiled by, written by, collected by
   - **Noun + prep**: report by X, analysis by Y
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English *bi* (near, beside, by means of).
   
   **Etymology**: Very old; the "byline" in journalism literally names who the story is "by."
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This chart is by the same team that did last years study.
   - The leak was first reported by a small local outlet.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The working paper by Smith et al. provides the primary empirical evidence.
   - All figures are reproduced from materials prepared by the statistics office.

1. **Preposition**: out of (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Using something as the basis or source; derived from a larger pool.
   - *(Ratio)* Indicating how many items in a group have a certain property.
   
   **Synonyms**: from, taken from, drawn from
   
   **Antonyms**: into (movement toward), within (inside)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing how many cases, quotes, or data points you selected from a larger set, or where you drew examples from.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For simple origin without a sense of subset (prefer "from"), or for physical motion.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: out of the sample, out of all sources, out of the dataset
   - **Verb + prep**: select out of, choose out of, sample out of
   
   **Root Analysis**: out (away from) + of.
   
   **Etymology**: A long-standing English phrasal preposition used for ratio and selection.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We only used ten articles out of the hundreds we found.
   - Three out of five sources disagreed with the original claim.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Five out of twelve interviews were excluded due to incomplete source information.
   - Only a subset of records out of the full administrative database was accessible for analysis.

1. **Preposition**: per (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* According to; in line with the authority or instructions of a source, often formal.
   
   **Synonyms**: according to, as stated in, in accordance with
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, against, despite
   
   **When to Use**: In formal writing or documentation when referencing guidelines, contracts, or official sources: "per the agreement," "per the data."
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech (prefer "according to"), or when you want to avoid legalistic tone.
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: per the report, per the contract, per the dataset, per the source
   - Often placed before a noun phrase representing the source.
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *per* (through, by means of), borrowed directly.
   
   **Etymology**: Used in legal and formal English to reference clauses or authorities.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Per the website, the data will be updated monthly.
   - Per the email we got yesterday, the source has been corrected.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Per the original protocol, all source data must be retained for at least ten years.
   - Per the published specification, the implementation must cite upstream sources in release notes.
