# Source - Adjectives

1. **Adjective**: primary
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source) Providing first-hand, original evidence such as raw data, eyewitness accounts, original documents, or direct observations. (Non-gradable in this sense, mainly attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: original, first-hand, direct, foundational
   
   **Antonyms**: secondary, tertiary, derivative, second-hand
   
   **When to Use**: Describing sources that are closest to the event or phenomenon itself, such as official records, experimental data, or interviews.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For summaries, commentaries, or textbooks that interpret earlier work (prefer "secondary").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - primary source
   - primary data
   - primary evidence
   - primary documents
   - primary research
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *primarius* (of the first rank) from *primus* (first).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 15th century; the research sense of "primary sources" became standard in history and social sciences.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Whenever possible, go back to the primary source instead of relying on summaries.
   - Interview transcripts and raw survey files are primary data.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis is based primarily on primary sources collected through structured interviews.
   - Distinguishing primary from secondary sources is crucial for historical method.

1. **Adjective**: secondary
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source) Based on, summarizing, or interpreting primary sources rather than providing original evidence. (Non-gradable, attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: derivative, interpretive, second-hand, synthesized
   
   **Antonyms**: primary, original, first-hand
   
   **When to Use**: Describing textbooks, review papers, meta-analyses, and news stories that rely on earlier work.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For direct data, originals, or unmediated accounts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - secondary source
   - secondary literature
   - secondary analysis
   - secondary reporting
   - largely secondary
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *secundarius* (following, second) from *secundus* (second).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 15th century; the research meaning of "secondary source" is standard in academic writing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That article is a secondary source; it just explains someone elses study.
   - For the exam, we read both primary documents and secondary commentary.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The literature review synthesizes secondary sources to frame the research problem.
   - Reliance on secondary reporting without checking primary sources can propagate errors.

1. **Adjective**: reliable
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source) Consistently providing accurate, verifiable, and unbiased information. (Gradable: more/less reliable)
   
   **Synonyms**: trustworthy, dependable, credible, consistent
   
   **Antonyms**: unreliable, untrustworthy, flaky, inconsistent
   
   **When to Use**: Evaluating whether you can safely base decisions or arguments on a particular person, outlet, dataset, or document.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For sources whose quality you have not evaluated, or when you want to talk specifically about *expertise* (prefer "authoritative").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reliable source
   - highly reliable
   - not very reliable
   - reliable dataset
   - reliable evidence
   
   **Root Analysis**: "rely" (to depend on) + "-able" (capable of) â†’ "reliable" (able to be depended on).
   
   **Etymology**: Formed in the 19th century; now widely used in quality assessment of both people and information.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That blog isnt a very reliable source; it often gets basic facts wrong.
   - Government statistics are usually more reliable than random social media posts.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study prioritizes reliable sources with transparent data collection procedures.
   - Policy decisions should be grounded in reliable, peer-reviewed evidence.

1. **Adjective**: credible
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source or claim) Believable and convincing because it is supported by evidence, expertise, or past accuracy. (Gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: believable, convincing, trustworthy, plausible
   
   **Antonyms**: dubious, questionable, implausible, incredible (not believable)
   
   **When to Use**: Assessing whether a source deserves to be taken seriously, especially when evidence is incomplete.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For purely factual correctness already proven (prefer "accurate"), or for peoples moral character (prefer "honest" or "ethical").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - credible source
   - highly credible
   - not credible
   - credible explanation
   - credible allegation
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *credere* (to believe) â†’ *credibilis* (worthy of belief).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 16th century; central in evaluating witness testimony and journalistic sources.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Is that really a credible source, or just some random account?
   - The story sounds wild, but it came from a very credible journalist.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Only allegations supported by credible sources were included in the report.
   - Source credibility is influenced by expertise, track record, and transparency.

1. **Adjective**: biased
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source) Showing systematic preference for one side, view, or group in a way that distorts information. (Gradable, predicative or attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: one-sided, partial, skewed, slanted, prejudiced
   
   **Antonyms**: unbiased, balanced, neutral, objective
   
   **When to Use**: Describing sources that consistently favor certain narratives, omit opposing evidence, or misrepresent facts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For casual preferences that do not affect information quality, or when a clear value stance is openly signaled in opinion pieces.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - biased source
   - heavily biased
   - politically biased
   - biased coverage
   - biased sampling
   
   **Root Analysis**: From French *biais* (slant, diagonal line) â†’ metaphor for "slant" in perspective.
   
   **Etymology**: Originally a term in geometry and bowling; later used metaphorically for mental "slant" or prejudice.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That outlet is pretty biased, so I always check other sources too.
   - The sample is biased because it only includes people who answered an online survey.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Biased sources can systematically distort estimates if not properly adjusted for.
   - The review notes that media coverage of the event was heavily biased toward official narratives.

1. **Adjective**: anonymous
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source) Not named or identified; the person providing information chooses to remain unknown. (Non-gradable, mainly attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: unnamed, unidentified, confidential, undisclosed
   
   **Antonyms**: named, on-the-record, identified, transparent
   
   **When to Use**: Describing sources who share information only under conditions that their identity will not be revealed.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For general privacy without journalism or research context (prefer "private"), or when the person is actually known to the audience.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anonymous source
   - speak anonymously
   - remain anonymous
   - anonymous tip
   - anonymous whistleblower
   
   **Root Analysis**: Greek *an-* (without) + *onyma* (name).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 17th century; widely used in journalism and whistleblowing contexts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The article quotes an anonymous source inside the company.
   - Anonymous sources can be useful, but theyre also harder to trust.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Statements from anonymous sources were included only when corroborated by independent documentation.
   - The study allows participants to remain anonymous to protect their privacy.

1. **Adjective**: independent
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source) Not controlled, funded, or strongly influenced by the parties involved in the issue being discussed. (Gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: impartial, neutral, external, arms-length
   
   **Antonyms**: affiliated, dependent, conflicted, captured
   
   **When to Use**: Highlighting that information comes from organizations or experts with no direct stake in the outcome.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For purely personal autonomy (prefer "self-reliant") or political independence of countries.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - independent source
   - independent reviewer
   - independent verification
   - independent audit
   - independent expert
   
   **Root Analysis**: "in-" (not) + "dependent" (relying on).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 17th century; widely used in governance, auditing, and research ethics.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we find an independent source that doesnt have money in this project?
   - It sounds better when independent experts back up the companys claims.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Independent sources were used to validate self-reported outcomes.
   - The committee commissioned an independent review to avoid conflicts of interest.

1. **Adjective**: authoritative
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source) Widely recognized as accurate and dependable because of expertise, reputation, or official status. (Gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: respected, definitive, expert, canonical, reputable
   
   **Antonyms**: dubious, fringe, unofficial, questionable
   
   **When to Use**: Referring to sources that set standards, summarize best evidence, or are treated as benchmarks (e.g., major reference works, leading journals, official statistics).
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For sources that are merely popular or loud but not well supported by evidence.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - authoritative source
   - authoritative report
   - authoritative guide
   - authoritative review
   - widely authoritative
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *auctor* (author, originator) â†’ *auctoritas* (authority) â†’ "authoritative" (having authority).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 16th century; strongly associated with expertise and institutional backing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - For medical questions, I try to stick to authoritative sources like official guidelines.
   - That book is considered the authoritative text on research methods.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis relies on authoritative meta-analyses published in top-tier journals.
   - Authoritative statistics from national agencies were used as baseline population figures.

1. **Adjective**: traceable
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source or datum) Able to be followed back through clear documentation to its origin. (Gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: trackable, auditable, documented, transparent
   
   **Antonyms**: untraceable, opaque, undocumented, anonymous (for data)
   
   **When to Use**: Emphasizing that you can see exactly where data or claims originate and how they have been transformed.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For physical tracking of objects (prefer "trackable" or "locatable") unless the context is clearly information-centric.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - traceable source
   - fully traceable
   - traceable data
   - traceable pipeline
   - traceable decision
   
   **Root Analysis**: "trace" (to follow a path) + "-able" (capable of).
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 17th century; widely used in quality control, supply chains, and now data governance.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I like using traceable data so I know exactly where each number came from.
   - Screenshots from social media arent very traceable as sources.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Traceable data pipelines improve reproducibility and accountability in empirical research.
   - All figures in the report are traceable back to named primary sources.

1. **Adjective**: verifiable
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Of a source or claim) Able to be checked and confirmed as true or accurate by independent examination. (Gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: checkable, testable, confirmable, falsifiable
   
   **Antonyms**: unfalsifiable, untestable, unverifiable, purely anecdotal
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about claims and data that can be examined by others using accessible sources or methods.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For opinions, values, or confidential statements that cannot realistically be checked.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - verifiable source
   - verifiable evidence
   - independently verifiable
   - not verifiable
   - verifiable fact
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *verus* (true) + *facere* (to make) â†’ *verificare* (to make true) + "-able".
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 17th century; central to scientific and journalistic standards.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - If it isnt verifiable, Im not comfortable using it as a source.
   - Rumors from anonymous chats are rarely verifiable.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Only verifiable statements were included in the main findings section.
   - The journal requires that all data underlying the results be verifiable by independent reviewers.
