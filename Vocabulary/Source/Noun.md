# Source - Nouns

1. **Noun**: source (C/U, pl: sources)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The place, person, document, dataset, or system that something comes from, especially information, ideas, or data.
   - *(Abstract)* The origin or starting point of a process, influence, or phenomenon.
   
   **Synonyms**: origin, starting point, supplier, provider, root, wellspring
   
   **Antonyms**: outcome, result, endpoint, receiver, sink
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about where information, data, funding, materials, or energy come from, especially in research, journalism, analysis, and system design.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need to emphasize the *cause* of an event rather than where it comes from (prefer "cause"), or when you refer to a *location* only (prefer "place," "site," or "location").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - primary source
   - secondary source
   - data source
   - reliable source
   - source of funding
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *surgere* (to rise) → Old French *sourse* (a rising, a fountain) → English "source" (origin, starting point)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *source* meaning "a spring or fountain," originally referring to water rising from the ground. Over time it generalized to mean the origin of anything, including information, ideas, and resources.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Always check the source before you share something online.
   - This report lists all the data sources at the end so you can dig deeper.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study identifies three main data sources: administrative records, survey responses, and sensor logs.
   - Decisions should be based on verifiable sources rather than unsubstantiated claims.

1. **Noun**: origin (C/U, pl: origins)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The point in time, place, or situation where something begins or is created.
   - *(Abstract)* The background, cause, or underlying source from which something develops.
   
   **Synonyms**: beginning, root, starting point, source, provenance
   
   **Antonyms**: outcome, result, endpoint, destination
   
   **When to Use**: Describing where a piece of information, a dataset, a tradition, or a problem *comes from* in time, space, or context.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to talk about the *path* or process (prefer "trajectory," "development," or "evolution"), or the *author* specifically (prefer "author," "creator," or "inventor").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - trace the origin
   - unknown origin
   - common origin
   - origin story
   - country of origin
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *oriri* (to rise, to appear) → *origo* (beginning, source) → English "origin"
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *origo* meaning "beginning, source, birth," used in English since the 14th century to talk about where things, people, or ideas come from.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We spent hours trying to figure out the origin of that strange rumor.
   - The origin of this dataset is unclear, so I don't fully trust the numbers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The origin of the outbreak was traced to a single contaminated water source.
   - Understanding the origin of a research claim is essential for assessing its credibility.

1. **Noun**: provenance (C/U, pl: provenances)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The documented history of where something came from and how it has been handled or transmitted over time, especially for data, artworks, and artifacts.
   - *(Data/Information)* A detailed record of the sources, transformations, and ownership of data or evidence.
   
   **Synonyms**: origin, traceability, pedigree, history, lineage
   
   **Antonyms**: opacity, uncertainty, anonymity, unknown origin
   
   **When to Use**: When you need to emphasize *traceable history* and *chain of custody* for information, datasets, models, or physical items.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very casual speech where simpler words like "background" or "history" are enough, or when you only care about the immediate source, not the full chain.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - data provenance
   - provenance metadata
   - verify provenance
   - unclear provenance
   - provenance record
   
   **Root Analysis**: French *provenir* (to come forth, arise) from Latin *provenire* (*pro-* forward + *venire* to come)
   
   **Etymology**: From French *provenance* meaning "place of origin, source," first used in art and antiques, later adopted in computer science for tracking the history of data and computations.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Before we trust this chart, we should check the provenance of the numbers.
   - The painting's provenance shows who owned it over the last hundred years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Robust data provenance is critical for reproducible research and auditability.
   - The model card documents the dataset's provenance, including its collection methods and known limitations.

1. **Noun**: citation (C, pl: citations)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A formal reference to a source of information, showing where a claim, idea, or quotation comes from.
   - *(Academic)* A standardized entry in a bibliography or reference list that identifies the original work.
   
   **Synonyms**: reference, attribution, footnote, endnote, source mention
   
   **Antonyms**: omission, plagiarism, unattributed claim
   
   **When to Use**: In academic, professional, or journalistic contexts where you must show exactly which source supports a statement.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very informal conversations where people rarely list formal sources, or when the context is not about documented evidence.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - add a citation
   - missing citation
   - citation style
   - citation needed
   - in-text citation
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *citare* (to summon, to call) → Medieval Latin *citatio* → English "citation"
   
   **Etymology**: Originally meant a legal summons or formal call to appear; the scholarly sense of "reference to an authority" developed in the 17th–18th centuries.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That Wikipedia paragraph has a "citation needed" tag because no source is listed.
   - My professor keeps telling me to add more citations to back up my arguments.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Every empirical claim in the introduction should be supported by at least one citation.
   - The article follows APA style for in-text citations and reference formatting.

1. **Noun**: reference (C/U, pl: references)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A mention of a source that supports a statement, especially in academic writing.
   - *(Countable)* A specific item in a bibliography, reference list, or footnote.
   
   **Synonyms**: citation, source, bibliographic entry, pointer
   
   **Antonyms**: guess, unsupported claim, hearsay
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about lists of sources at the end of a paper, or any explicit pointer that allows others to find the original material.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need the verb "refer to" (action), or when speaking about *personal* references in hiring contexts (prefer "referee" or "recommendation").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - list of references
   - key references
   - reference list
   - cross-reference
   - reference section
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *referre* (to carry back, to relate) → *referentia* (act of referring) → English "reference"
   
   **Etymology**: Entered English in the 17th century meaning "act of referring" and later "source referred to." The academic sense of "bibliographic entry" is now standard.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Check the references at the end of the book if you want to read the original studies.
   - I always keep a separate file with all my references when I write a paper.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reference list includes all works cited in the body of the article.
   - Key references on this topic emphasize the importance of triangulating multiple independent sources.

1. **Noun**: dataset (C, pl: datasets)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A structured collection of data points, often assembled from one or more sources for analysis, modeling, or reporting.
   - *(Practical)* The file, table, or repository that contains this organized data.
   
   **Synonyms**: data collection, data corpus, data table, data resource
   
   **Antonyms**: single observation, anecdote, raw stream (when not yet organized)
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the concrete bundle of data you obtain from a source—such as a survey, API export, log file, or public repository.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When talking about a *live* data stream (prefer "feed" or "stream"), or when the content is unstructured and not yet organized.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - training dataset
   - source dataset
   - benchmark dataset
   - dataset documentation
   - curated dataset
   
   **Root Analysis**: "data" (plural of Latin *datum*, given) + "set" (group of items considered as a whole)
   
   **Etymology**: "Dataset" emerged in the mid-20th century in statistics and computing to describe structured data collections; widely adopted in data science and machine learning.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We downloaded a public dataset of news articles to test our classifier.
   - Before training the model, clean up the source dataset and remove duplicates.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The study relies on three independent datasets sourced from government registries and commercial providers.
   - Transparent documentation of each dataset's source, coverage, and limitations is critical for responsible use.

1. **Noun**: repository (C, pl: repositories)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A place where data, documents, code, or artifacts are stored and managed, often with version control and access controls.
   - *(Information)* A central location that aggregates multiple sources into a single, organized collection.
   
   **Synonyms**: archive, store, hub, database, codebase, registry
   
   **Antonyms**: scattered files, ad-hoc storage, local copy (single file)
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about where you *keep* or *publish* source materials: code repositories, data repositories, institutional repositories, or open-access archives.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For short-lived, informal storage (prefer "folder" or "file"), or when there is no organizing or sharing function.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - source repository
   - data repository
   - central repository
   - public repository
   - institutional repository
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin *reponere* (to put back) → *repositorium* (place where things are stored) → English "repository"
   
   **Etymology**: In English since the 15th century for storage places; in computing it now refers to code and data stores, especially those hosting source materials for collaboration.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - All the source code lives in a shared repository on GitHub.
   - The university maintains a data repository where researchers can upload their datasets.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project deposits its primary data in a public repository with persistent identifiers and rich metadata.
   - Version-controlled repositories help maintain a clear history of changes to both source code and source data.

1. **Noun**: outlet (C, pl: outlets)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Media) A news organization, platform, or channel that publishes information from sources to the public.
   - *(Extended)* Any organization or platform that acts as a visible surface for deeper source networks (e.g., research outlets, media outlets).
   
   **Synonyms**: publication, channel, platform, news organization, media house
   
   **Antonyms**: source, insider, background contact, private note
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to distinguish between the *public-facing publisher* and the *underlying sources* that provide information.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you refer directly to the person providing confidential information (prefer "source"), or to the original research itself (prefer "study" or "paper").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - news outlet
   - media outlet
   - local outlet
   - major outlet
   - independent outlet
   
   **Root Analysis**: "out" (toward the outside) + "let" (small opening)
   
   **Etymology**: Originally meant a physical opening for something to flow out; later extended metaphorically to emotional or creative expression, and then to media channels that allow information to "flow out" to the public.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Several outlets reported the story, but they all relied on the same original source.
   - I try to follow at least one international news outlet for a different perspective.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis compares how different outlets framed the same set of primary sources.
   - Relying on a single outlet can hide biases in how underlying sources are selected and interpreted.

1. **Noun**: leak (C, pl: leaks)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The unauthorized or unexpected release of confidential information from an internal source to the public or to external parties.
   - *(By extension)* The information or document that has been released in this way.
   
   **Synonyms**: disclosure, revelation, unauthorized release, spill, leak of information
   
   **Antonyms**: secrecy, containment, non-disclosure, confidentiality
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about situations where someone inside an organization passes information to journalists, investigators, or the public without official permission.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the information is published openly and officially (prefer "announcement" or "release"), or when you refer to physical leaks (water, gas) rather than informational ones.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - anonymous leak
   - document leak
   - major leak
   - leak investigation
   - leak to the press
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old Norse *leka* (to drip) → Old English *leccan* (to leak) → metaphorical use for information
   
   **Etymology**: Originally described physical fluids escaping through small holes; by the early 20th century it was used metaphorically for confidential information escaping through hidden "holes" in organizations.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The story came from a leak inside the company.
   - After the leak, everyone started asking who had shared the documents.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The report relies on internal emails obtained through an unauthorized leak.
   - Journalists must verify leaked materials against independent sources before publishing.
