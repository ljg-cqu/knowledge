# Source - Functional Language

1. **Functional Expression**: Where did you get this (from)? (function: checking sources)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A question used to ask about the origin of information, data, or a claim; it gently challenges someone to reveal or justify their source.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: Whats your source for this?, Where does this come from?, Where did you find this?
   
   **Antonyms**: I wont ask where this came from., Im not interested in the source.
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to understand or evaluate the reliability of what someone just said or shared.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal writing (prefer "Could you indicate the source of this information?"), or when the person clearly cannot reveal their source.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Where did that come from?
   - Whats your source for this?
   - Where did you find this information?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Can sound skeptical or challenging depending on tone; useful for encouraging evidence-based discussion; often used to push back against rumors or unsupported claims.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Its from... [name source]
   - I saw it in... [article/report]
   - I dont actually remember where I got it.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thats a strong claimâ€”where did you get this from?
   - This chart looks suspicious. Where did you find it?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Before we proceed, may I ask where you got this figure from?
   - The committee asked repeatedly, "Where did you get this data from, and how was it collected?"

1. **Functional Expression**: Do you have a reference for that? (function: requesting evidence)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to ask someone to provide a concrete sourceâ€”such as a paper, report, or datasetâ€”for a claim.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect-polite
   
   **Synonyms**: Could you share a source for that?, Is there a paper or report I can look at?, Can you point me to the original?
   
   **Antonyms**: I dont need any references., Lets just trust this without checking.
   
   **When to Use**: In meetings, peer review, or collaboration when you want to follow up on a claim and read the underlying material.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In highly confrontational contexts where it may sound dismissive; consider softer variants if the power distance is large.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Can you share a reference for that?
   - Is there a reference I can read?
   - Do you happen to have a reference?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Signals intellectual seriousness without outright rejecting the claim; helps move conversation from opinion to documented evidence.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, Ill send you the paper.
   - Its in [journal/report]â€”Ill share the link.
   - Not yet; Ill look for one.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thats interestingâ€”do you have a reference for that?
   - Before I repeat this, Id like to see a source. Do you have a reference?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reviewer wrote, "Do you have a reference for this assertion?"
   - In academic writing, it is standard to ask, "Do you have a reference for that?" before accepting strong claims.

1. **Functional Expression**: According to [source], ... (function: attributing information)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A framing device used to attribute a statement or fact to a particular source before presenting it.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (shifts responsibility to the named source)
   
   **Synonyms**: As reported by..., As stated in..., As [source] notes...
   
   **Antonyms**: In my opinion..., I think..., I guess...
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to make clear that a claim comes from someone else rather than from your own authority.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you are presenting your own conclusions or when the source cannot be safely named.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - According to the data...
   - According to the report...
   - According to our sources...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Helpful for clear attribution; can also signal distance or skepticism depending on intonation ("according to them...").
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That makes sense, given the source.
   - Thats surprising, especially coming from that source.
   - Other sources contradict what youre citing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to this article, the numbers were updated last week.
   - According to our data, most users come from just three countries.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to the primary data source, coverage rates exceed 90%.
   - According to multiple independent sources, the earlier estimate was overstated.

1. **Functional Expression**: I havent verified this yet. (function: hedging reliability)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A disclaimer that you are sharing information you have not personally checked against sources.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct admission of uncertainty
   
   **Synonyms**: I havent checked this., I havent confirmed this., I havent seen the original source.
   
   **Antonyms**: I have verified this., Ive checked this against the original data.
   
   **When to Use**: When you want to pass along a tentative claim while being honest about its current status.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In final reports or formal publications, where unverified information usually should not appear.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I havent checked the original yet.
   - I havent seen the primary source myself.
   - This hasnt been independently verified.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Shows intellectual humility and protects trust; invites others to treat the claim cautiously and to seek the source themselves.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks for flagging thatâ€”it needs verification.
   - Lets verify it before we use it.
   - Do you know where we could verify it?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I heard this on a podcast, but I havent verified this yet.
   - I havent seen the data myself, so take this with a grain of salt.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - At this stage, I must say I havent verified this yet against primary sources.
   - The memo clearly marked several figures as "not yet verified."

1. **Functional Expression**: My source says that... (function: reporting from a source)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to introduce information that comes from a particular contact or insider rather than from public documents.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: A contact told me that..., One of my sources reports that..., I was told that...
   
   **Antonyms**: I saw for myself that..., The public report says that...
   
   **When to Use**: When passing along information that depends heavily on trust in a person you are not naming.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal writing where anonymous sourcing is discouraged, or when the information can be supported by public documents instead.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - One of my sources says that...
   - A reliable source has told me that...
   - People close to the project say that...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Relies on your credibility; listeners will assess both your judgment and the unnamed sources reliability.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - How confident are you in that source?
   - Can we get a second source to confirm this?
   - Is there any documentation to back that up?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My source says that the numbers in the press release were rounded up.
   - One of my sources says that the dataset will be made public next month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - A confidential source says that internal audits raised similar concerns last year.
   - Multiple sources say that the original data were more complete than what was later published.
