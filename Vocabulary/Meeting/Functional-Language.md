1. **Functional Expression**: Could we schedule a meeting to discuss...? (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite request to arrange a meeting about a specific topic.
   - *Formality*: formal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Can we set up a meeting to...?, Would it be possible to meet about...?, I'd like to schedule time to discuss...
   
   **Antonyms**: We won't be meeting about..., No meeting necessary for...
   
   **When to Use**: In professional email or verbal requests; appropriate when initiating meeting requests with colleagues or clients.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for very casual teams; avoid when immediate urgent meeting is needed (be more direct).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could we meet to discuss...?
   - Could we set up a time to talk about...?
   - Would you be available to meet about...?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses modal "could" for polite indirectness; respects recipient's autonomy by framing as question rather than directive; negative politeness strategy.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, when works for you?
   - Yes, I'm available next week.
   - I'm quite busy, but let me check my calendar.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could we schedule a meeting to discuss the website redesign?
   - Could we meet sometime this week to go over the budget?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could we schedule a meeting to discuss the quarterly performance metrics?
   - Could we arrange a time to discuss the strategic implications of this proposal?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'd like to add something to that point (function: contributing/turn-taking)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to contribute additional information or perspective to an ongoing discussion.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite)
   
   **Synonyms**: Can I add to that?, I have something to add, If I may add something, Building on that...
   
   **Antonyms**: I disagree completely, That's not relevant, Let's move on
   
   **When to Use**: During meeting discussions when wanting to contribute; appropriate for collaborative, respectful dialogue.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use if completely changing topic; avoid if interrupting important point (wait for pause).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd like to build on that
   - Can I add something here?
   - If I could add to what [name] said
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I'd like" softens the contribution; acknowledges current speaker's point while adding own perspective; positive politeness strategy.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Of course, go ahead
   - Sure, what would you add?
   - Please do
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'd like to add something to that point about timelines.
   - Can I add something? I think we're missing the client perspective.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'd like to add something to that point regarding regulatory compliance.
   - If I may, I'd like to contribute an additional consideration to this discussion.

1. **Functional Expression**: Just to clarify, are you saying that...? (function: clarifying/checking understanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A request to confirm understanding of what someone has said; checking comprehension or asking for elaboration.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (polite questioning)
   
   **Synonyms**: Do you mean...?, If I understand correctly..., So you're suggesting that...?, Am I right in thinking...?
   
   **Antonyms**: That's confusing, I don't understand, What do you mean?
   
   **When to Use**: When needing clarification in meetings; essential for ensuring accurate understanding before decisions.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid overusing or it suggests inattention; don't use confrontationally.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just to make sure I understand...
   - Let me check if I've got this right...
   - So what you're saying is...?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Just to clarify" frames as your need for clarity rather than speaker's lack of clarity; face-saving for both parties; reduces potential embarrassment.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, exactly
   - Not quite, what I meant was...
   - Let me rephrase that
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just to clarify, are you saying we should postpone the launch?
   - So just to make sure—we're moving the deadline to Friday?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Just to clarify, are you suggesting that we revise the procurement process?
   - If I may seek clarification, are you proposing a complete restructuring of the department?

1. **Functional Expression**: I see your point, but/however... (function: disagreeing politely)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Acknowledging someone's perspective before presenting a different view; diplomatic disagreement.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I understand what you're saying, but..., That's a valid point, however..., I appreciate that perspective, but...
   
   **Antonyms**: I completely agree, You're absolutely right, That's exactly what I think
   
   **When to Use**: When disagreeing in meetings; essential for maintaining positive relationships while expressing different views.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use if you don't actually see their point; avoid if agreement is feigned and disagreement is harsh.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I understand what you're saying, but...
   - That's a fair point, however...
   - I take your point, but...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Two-part structure: first validates speaker (positive face), then introduces disagreement (negative face); classic face-saving disagreement strategy.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - What are your concerns specifically?
   - I'd be interested to hear your perspective
   - Let's discuss the alternative
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I see your point, but I'm worried about the timeline.
   - I get what you're saying, but have we considered the cost?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I see your point regarding efficiency gains, but we must also consider risk mitigation.
   - While I appreciate that perspective, I believe we need to examine the regulatory implications more carefully.

1. **Functional Expression**: Would you mind if we moved on to...? (function: suggesting/turn-taking)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite request to change topics or proceed to the next agenda item.
   - *Formality*: formal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Can we move on to...?, Should we transition to...?, Shall we discuss...?, Let's move to the next item
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's stay on this topic, We need to finish this first
   
   **When to Use**: When facilitating meetings or managing time; appropriate for moving discussions forward diplomatically.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use if current topic needs more discussion; avoid if cutting people off rudely.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would it be okay to move on to...?
   - Can we transition to...?
   - Shall we move to the next point?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Frames as request respecting group's autonomy; "would you mind" is highly polite form acknowledging potential imposition; negative politeness.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, let's move on
   - Actually, I have one more point about this
   - Yes, we should keep moving
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Would you mind if we moved on to the budget discussion?
   - Can we move on to the next item? We're running short on time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Would you mind if we transitioned to the next agenda item?
   - In the interest of time, shall we proceed to the strategic planning discussion?

1. **Functional Expression**: I'm afraid I have to disagree (function: disagreeing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A formal, polite way to express disagreement; softens the disagreement with apologetic framing.
   - *Formality*: formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I'm not sure I agree, I have a different view, I must respectfully disagree, I see things differently
   
   **Antonyms**: I completely agree, I'm with you on this, Exactly right
   
   **When to Use**: In formal meetings when disagreeing; appropriate when maintaining professionalism while expressing opposition.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Can sound overly formal in casual settings; avoid if you're not actually afraid/sorry (can seem insincere).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'm afraid I don't agree
   - I'm afraid I must disagree
   - I'm afraid I see things differently
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I'm afraid" softens disagreement by expressing regret; hedging device that maintains face for both parties; very British English strategy.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I'd like to hear your perspective
   - What are your concerns?
   - Let's discuss our different viewpoints
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm afraid I have to disagree about the timeline.
   - I'm afraid I don't see it that way.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I'm afraid I must disagree with the proposed approach to risk management.
   - While I respect your position, I'm afraid I have to disagree with the conclusion.

1. **Functional Expression**: If I may interrupt/If I could just jump in... (function: interrupting politely)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to interrupt an ongoing discussion or speaker to add urgent or relevant information.
   - *Formality*: formal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Sorry to interrupt, but..., Can I just add something quickly?, May I interject?
   
   **Antonyms**: *[Interrupting rudely without preamble]*, Let me finish first
   
   **When to Use**: When needing to interrupt in meetings; appropriate when information is time-sensitive or highly relevant.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't overuse or you'll seem domineering; avoid if information can wait; inappropriate if constantly interrupting.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Sorry to interrupt, but...
   - Can I just jump in here?
   - If I could interject briefly...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Acknowledges interruption as potential imposition; "if I may/could" requests permission; mitigates face-threat of interruption.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Of course, go ahead
   - Can it wait a moment?
   - Sure, what is it?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Sorry to interrupt, but we just got an urgent email from the client.
   - Can I just jump in? This relates to what you're saying.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - If I may interrupt, there's a critical timing consideration we should address.
   - Pardon the interruption, but I believe this information is immediately relevant.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's table this for now (function: suggesting postponement)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A suggestion to postpone discussion of a topic to a later time (US usage).
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct (but softened with "let's")
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's postpone this, Let's come back to this later, Can we revisit this?, Let's park this for now
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's decide now, We need to resolve this today, Let's finish this discussion
   
   **When to Use**: When managing meeting time; appropriate when topic needs more information or time isn't available.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Note US vs UK meaning difference; avoid if urgent decision needed; don't use to avoid difficult discussions repeatedly.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's park this for now
   - Can we come back to this?
   - Let's revisit this later
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Let's" creates collaborative framing; softens directive by including speaker in action; manages meeting flow without assigning blame.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreed, let's move on
   - Can we at least decide on [X] first?
   - When should we revisit this?
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's table this for now and focus on the immediate priorities.
   - Can we park this discussion and come back to it next week?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Given time constraints, I propose we table this matter until additional data is available.
   - The committee recommends tabling this agenda item pending legal review.

1. **Functional Expression**: To summarize/In summary... (function: summarizing)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introducing a brief overview or recap of main points; signaling transition to summary.
   - *Formality*: formal/neutral
   - *Directness*: direct
   
   **Synonyms**: To sum up, In conclusion, To recap, Let me summarize, The key points are...
   
   **Antonyms**: To elaborate, In more detail, Let me expand on that
   
   **When to Use**: When concluding discussions or presentations; essential for reinforcing key points and ensuring understanding.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use if you haven't actually covered the content yet; avoid if summary is premature.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let me summarize
   - To sum up
   - So in summary
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Discourse marker signaling shift to conclusion; helps audience process and retain information; manages expectations about remaining content.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That captures it well
   - I think you missed [X]
   - Yes, those are the main points
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - To summarize, we're launching in Q2 with a limited feature set.
   - So in summary, everyone needs to submit their reports by Friday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - To summarize the committee's findings, three strategic priorities have emerged.
   - In summary, the proposal demonstrates fiscal viability and strategic alignment.

1. **Functional Expression**: Let's take this offline (function: suggesting private discussion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A suggestion to discuss a topic outside the current meeting, usually privately or in a smaller group.
   - *Formality*: informal/neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect (suggests rather than directs)
   
   **Synonyms**: Let's discuss this separately, Can we talk about this later?, Let's follow up on this one-on-one
   
   **Antonyms**: Let's discuss this now, This needs to be addressed in this meeting
   
   **When to Use**: When topic is too detailed for group meeting or involves only subset of attendees; managing meeting time.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use to avoid difficult group discussions that should be public; can seem evasive if overused.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Let's discuss this offline
   - Can we take this offline?
   - Let's follow up on this separately
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Offline" is tech-derived metaphor (not connected to network); manages meeting efficiency; can also manage conflict by deferring.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Sure, let's connect after
   - Okay, I'll follow up with you
   - Actually, I think the group needs to hear this
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's take this offline—it's getting into too much detail.
   - Can we talk about this offline? It doesn't affect everyone here.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I suggest we take this matter offline and reconvene with a specific recommendation.
   - This operational detail should be addressed offline by the relevant parties.

   **Common Variations**:

   **Pragmatic Notes**: Creates explicit commitment to future action; manages immediate time constraints; shows respect by promising attention to concern.
