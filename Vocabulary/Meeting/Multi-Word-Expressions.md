1. **Expression**: call a meeting (type: collocation - verb + noun)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To arrange or organize a meeting; to formally convene people for a discussion or decision-making session.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very common collocation in meeting contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: convene a meeting, schedule a meeting, arrange a meeting, organize a meeting
   
   **Antonyms**: cancel a meeting, postpone a meeting, adjourn a meeting
   
   **When to Use**: In professional contexts when organizing meetings; appropriate for formal and informal business communication.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when meeting is already scheduled (use "schedule" instead); less common for very informal gatherings.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - call an emergency meeting
   - call a special meeting
   - call a team meeting
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Call" emphasizes the summoning or convening action, commonly paired with "meeting" in organizational contexts due to high frequency of co-occurrence.
   
   **Root Analysis**: call (Old English - ceallian) + meeting (Old English - meting)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you call a meeting with the design team for next week?
   - The boss called an urgent meeting about the deadline.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The board chair will call a special meeting to address the matter.
   - Management has called an all-hands meeting to announce organizational changes.

1. **Expression**: on the agenda (type: fixed phrase - prepositional)

   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed expression in meeting contexts)
   
   **Synonyms**: scheduled for discussion, up for discussion, to be discussed, on the schedule

   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid when referring to personal plans unrelated to meetings; ensure clarity about which agenda is referenced.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - on today's agenda
   - next on the agenda
   - high on the agenda
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: The preposition "on" naturally pairs with "agenda" (a list or plan), creating a fixed expression for meeting planning contexts.
   
   **Root Analysis**: on (preposition) + agenda (Latin - things to be done)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What's on the agenda for today's meeting?
   - Is the budget discussion on the agenda or should I add it?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The proposal is on the agenda for next week's board meeting.
   - Three strategic initiatives are on the agenda for quarterly review.

1. **Expression**: off the record (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Not for official documentation or public disclosure; said in confidence without being formally recorded.
   - *Literal meaning*: Not written in the record
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: Spoken confidentially without official attribution
   - *Fixedness*: strong (established idiom)
   
   **Synonyms**: confidentially, unofficially, in confidence, privately, between you and me
   
   **Antonyms**: on the record, officially, publicly, for attribution
   
   **When to Use**: When sharing information confidentially; appropriate for sensitive discussions not meant for official documentation.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in fully documented formal proceedings; ensure all parties understand the confidential nature; can create legal/ethical issues if misused.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - this is off the record
   - strictly off the record
   - keep this off the record
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Origin from journalism and official proceedings where "the record" refers to official documentation; "off" indicates exclusion from this documentation.
   
   **Root Analysis**: off (not on) + record (Latin - recordari, to remember)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Off the record, I think the project is going to be delayed.
   - Can we talk off the record for a minute?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The executive spoke off the record about preliminary negotiations.
   - *"Typically informal; use 'confidentially' in formal contexts"*

1. **Expression**: table a discussion (type: collocation - verb + noun)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *US meaning*: To postpone or set aside a topic for later consideration.
   - *UK meaning*: To bring a topic forward for immediate discussion.
   - *Fixedness*: strong (but note regional variation in meaning)
   
   **Synonyms**: postpone (US), defer (US), bring forward (UK), raise for discussion (UK)
   
   **Antonyms**: discuss immediately (US), postpone (UK)
   
   **When to Use**: When managing meeting agendas; CRITICAL: verify regional usage to avoid confusion.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Avoid in international meetings without clarification due to opposite meanings; specify intention clearly.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - table the matter
   - table this for now
   - table the proposal
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Table" refers to placing on/off the meeting table; usage evolved differently in US (remove from table) vs UK (place on table).
   
   **Root Analysis**: table (meeting surface) + discussion
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let's table this discussion until we have more data. (US: postpone)
   - Can we table this topic for today's meeting? (UK: discuss now)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The committee voted to table the motion until the next session. (US: postpone)
   - The member tabled a motion for immediate consideration. (UK: bring forward)
