1. **Noun**: parenting (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The activity, process, and responsibility of raising and caring for a child from infancy to adulthood.
   - The style or approach a mother, father, or caregiver uses to guide a child’s behavior, learning, and emotional growth.
   
   **Synonyms**: child-rearing, child-raising, bringing up children, upbringing (process), caregiving (for children)
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, abandonment, hands-off approach
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about the overall role, work, and approach of raising children, especially in discussions of family life, psychology, or advice ("gentle parenting," "modern parenting").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For legal status or biology (use "motherhood", "fatherhood", "parenthood"), or when referring to a single act like changing a diaper (use a specific verb instead).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - modern parenting
   - conscious parenting
   - positive parenting
   - parenting style
   - parenting advice
   
   **Root Analysis**: parent (mother or father/caregiver) + -ing (activity, process)
   
   **Etymology**: From "parent" (Middle English, from Old French, from Latin *parent-, parens* meaning "father or mother") + English verbal noun suffix "-ing" to describe the ongoing process of raising children.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Parenting is so much harder than I imagined, but it’s also incredibly rewarding.
   - We follow gentle parenting at home, so we try not to yell at the kids.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Contemporary research emphasizes that responsive, consistent parenting strongly predicts secure attachment.
   - Public policies increasingly aim to support effective parenting through parental leave, childcare subsidies, and education programs.

1. **Noun**: caregiver (C, pl: caregivers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who regularly looks after a child, especially meeting their daily physical and emotional needs.
   - In psychology and social work, any primary adult responsible for a child’s safety, attachment, and development (not limited to biological parents).
   
   **Synonyms**: guardian, carer, primary carer, parent figure, nanny (for paid role)
   
   **Antonyms**: dependent, child, care recipient
   
   **When to Use**: When focusing on the caring role rather than legal or biological status, especially in blended families, foster care, or professional childcare settings.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When legal relationship matters more than daily care (use "legal guardian"), or when referring specifically to a mother or father (use "mother", "father", or "parent").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - primary caregiver
   - consistent caregiver
   - caregiver stress
   - caregiver support
   - caregiver–child relationship
   
   **Root Analysis**: care (to look after, be concerned about) + giver (person who provides)
   
   **Etymology**: Formed in modern English from "care" + "giver" in the 19th–20th century, later expanded in psychology and social policy to include non-parental adults caring for children or dependent adults.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - In our family, my grandma is the main caregiver while we’re at work.
   - Kids do better when they have at least one stable caregiver they can rely on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Secure attachment typically develops when a caregiver responds sensitively and consistently to an infant’s needs.
   - Caregiver burnout is common among parents who lack social support or access to affordable childcare.

1. **Noun**: toddler (C, pl: toddlers)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A very young child who has just learned to walk, usually between about one and three years old.
   - Informally, a child in the early stage of independence marked by intense curiosity and frequent emotional swings.
   
   **Synonyms**: young child, little one, two-year-old, preschooler (overlapping, slightly older)
   
   **Antonyms**: newborn, infant, teenager, adult
   
   **When to Use**: Describing children in the early walking and language-explosion stage, especially when talking about behavior like tantrums, potty training, or early independence.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For babies who cannot walk yet (use "infant" or "baby") or for children already in school (use "child" or "school-age child").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - toddler years
   - toddler tantrum
   - toddler bed
   - toddler stage
   - toddler development
   
   **Root Analysis**: toddle (to walk with short, unsteady steps) + -er (person doing the action)
   
   **Etymology**: From English verb "toddle" (19th century) describing the unsteady walk of a small child, plus the agent suffix "-er" to mean "one who toddles" (a young child just learning to walk).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Our toddler gets into everything the second we look away.
   - Parenting a toddler means chasing them around all day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - During the toddler period, children rapidly expand their vocabulary and experiment with autonomy.
   - Many parenting programs focus on helping caregivers manage typical toddler behaviors such as tantrums and limit testing.

1. **Noun**: tantrum (C, pl: tantrums)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A sudden, intense outburst of anger or frustration in a child, often with crying, screaming, or physical agitation.
   - A loss of emotional control, usually in young children, triggered by unmet needs, limits, or overstimulation.
   
   **Synonyms**: meltdown, outburst, fit, temper tantrum
   
   **Antonyms**: calm response, self-control, composed reaction
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about emotional storms typical of toddlers and preschoolers when they hear "no", are tired, hungry, or overwhelmed.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For serious mental health crises or violent episodes (use more precise clinical terms), or when describing ordinary disagreement without loss of control.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - throw a tantrum
   - toddler tantrum
   - public tantrum
   - manage a tantrum
   - full-blown tantrum
   
   **Root Analysis**: Root noun with no clear modern prefix/suffix; related historically to "tantrums" meaning bursts of ill temper.
   
   **Etymology**: Origin uncertain; recorded in English in the early 18th century meaning "fit of petulance or rage," probably from dialect or expressive origin imitating stamping or flailing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My son had a huge tantrum in the supermarket when I said we couldn’t buy more candy.
   - Staying calm during a tantrum is tough, but it really helps.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Tantrums in early childhood are often normal expressions of frustration and developing autonomy.
   - Parenting interventions frequently teach caregivers to recognize triggers and respond calmly to children’s tantrums.

1. **Noun**: attachment (C/U, pl: attachments)
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Psychology, U) The deep, lasting emotional bond that forms between a child and their primary caregiver.
   - (C/U) A particular emotional connection to a specific person, object, or routine, often providing comfort and security.
   
   **Synonyms**: emotional bond, connection, closeness, secure bond
   
   **Antonyms**: detachment, emotional distance, insecurity, separation
   
   **When to Use**: In parenting and child-development contexts, especially when discussing "secure attachment," "attachment styles," and the effects of caregiver responsiveness.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For mechanical or technical fittings (e.g., "email attachment")—in those cases, context usually makes the meaning different.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - secure attachment
   - attachment figure
   - attachment style
   - parent–child attachment
   - attachment theory
   
   **Root Analysis**: attach (to fasten, to connect) + -ment (result, state, or process)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *atachier* (to fasten, fix) and English suffix "-ment"; in the 20th century, psychologists such as John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth adopted "attachment" to describe emotional bonds between children and caregivers.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Babies build attachment when parents pick them up and comfort them.
   - My daughter has a strong attachment to her stuffed bunny at bedtime.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Secure attachment is associated with better emotion regulation and social competence in later childhood.
   - Disruptions in early attachment relationships can increase the risk of behavioral and emotional difficulties.

1. **Noun**: screen time (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The amount of time a child spends using devices with screens, such as phones, tablets, televisions, or computers.
   - By extension, a category of daily activity involving digital media that parents may limit or structure.
   
   **Synonyms**: device time, media time, time on screens, digital time
   
   **Antonyms**: offline time, outdoor play, screen-free time
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing how long children use digital devices, setting household rules, or referring to expert recommendations about healthy technology use.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing the content itself (use "cartoons", "games", "videos") or specific school-related computer use (say "online class" or "homework on the computer").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - limit screen time
   - excessive screen time
   - screen-time rules
   - screen-time guidelines
   - screen-free time
   
   **Root Analysis**: screen (display surface on a device) + time (measured period of activity)
   
   **Etymology**: Modern compound that became common in the late 20th and early 21st centuries as televisions, computers, and smartphones entered family life; widely used in parenting advice, health guidelines, and media research.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re trying to cut down the kids’ screen time during the week.
   - I let them have extra screen time on rainy days.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many parenting guidelines recommend setting clear limits on recreational screen time, especially before bedtime.
   - Studies suggest that unstructured outdoor play can buffer some of the negative effects of excessive screen time.
