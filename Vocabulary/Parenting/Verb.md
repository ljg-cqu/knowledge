1. **Verb**: nurture
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To care for and encourage the growth, development, and well-being of a child.
   - (Transitive) To support someone’s emotional, social, or intellectual growth over time through attention and guidance.
   
   **Synonyms**: care for, raise, foster, support, nourish (figurative)
   
   **Antonyms**: neglect, ignore, mistreat, stifle
   
   **When to Use**: Describing the long-term, supportive side of parenting—meeting needs, giving love, and helping children’s personalities and abilities grow.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For short, practical tasks like changing a diaper (use "change" or "feed"), or for harsh control-focused actions (use "discipline" or "punish").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - nurture a child
   - nurture emotional growth
   - nurture curiosity
   - nurture a secure bond
   - nurture independence
   
   **Root Analysis**: nurt- (to nourish, to feed) + -ure (action, process, result)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *nourrir* / *nourriture* (to feed, nourishment), from Latin *nutrire* (to suckle, nourish). English "nurture" shifted from literal feeding to broader emotional and developmental care.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We try to nurture our kids’ interests instead of pushing them into activities we like.
   - Reading together every night is one way to nurture their love of books.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Responsive caregiving helps nurture secure attachment and healthy self-esteem.
   - Early childhood programs aim to nurture both cognitive skills and emotional resilience.

1. **Verb**: discipline
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To teach a child to follow rules and develop self-control, often by setting limits and applying fair consequences.
   - (Transitive) To correct behavior in a structured, consistent way rather than through anger or humiliation.
   
   **Synonyms**: train, guide, correct, set limits for, enforce rules with
   
   **Antonyms**: indulge, spoil, ignore misbehavior, let slide
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about teaching right and wrong, household rules, and consistent consequences ("to discipline a child calmly and fairly").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the focus is harsh punishment or physical harm (use "punish", "hit", or "spank" if you need that precise meaning), or when describing general care (use "raise" or "nurture").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - discipline a child
   - discipline with respect
   - discipline consistently
   - use positive discipline
   - struggle to discipline
   
   **Root Analysis**: discip- (pupil, learner) + -line (related to teaching/order)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *disciplina* (instruction, training), related to *discipulus* (pupil). Earlier emphasis was on instruction; in parenting, "discipline" now often contrasts "teaching and guiding" with purely punitive approaches.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re trying to discipline the kids without yelling all the time.
   - It’s hard to discipline fairly when you’re exhausted.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Effective parents discipline by combining clear expectations with empathy and predictable consequences.
   - Many experts distinguish between punitive punishment and positive discipline that focuses on teaching skills.

1. **Verb**: scold
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To speak angrily to a child because they have done something wrong or dangerous.
   - (Transitive) To criticize sharply using a raised or irritated voice.
   
   **Synonyms**: tell off, reprimand, rebuke, lecture, snap at
   
   **Antonyms**: praise, encourage, comfort, reassure
   
   **When to Use**: Describing moments when a parent reacts with anger or frustration, especially to unsafe or repeated misbehavior.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For calm teaching or gentle correction (use "explain", "remind", or "guide"), or when talking about formal discipline in schools (use "discipline" or "sanction").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - scold a child
   - scold harshly
   - gently scold
   - scold in public
   - constantly scold
   
   **Root Analysis**: Root verb of Germanic origin with no clear modern prefix/suffix; related to older words for "nagging" or "abusing with words".
   
   **Etymology**: From Middle English *scolden* (to quarrel, scold), likely from Old Norse *skald* (mocking poem) or similar Germanic roots; historically associated with nagging or abusive speech.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tried not to scold her, but she ran into the street without looking.
   - He tends to scold the kids instead of explaining what they did wrong.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Frequent harsh scolding is linked to higher levels of anxiety and aggression in children.
   - Parenting programs often encourage caregivers to replace scolding with clear, calm instructions.

1. **Verb**: praise
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To express approval or admiration of a child’s behavior, effort, or qualities.
   - (Transitive) To say positive things to encourage a child to repeat helpful or kind behavior.
   
   **Synonyms**: compliment, commend, applaud, celebrate, give credit
   
   **Antonyms**: criticize, blame, belittle, put down
   
   **When to Use**: When parents talk about reinforcing good behavior, motivation, or self-esteem, especially "praising effort" rather than only results.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For neutral feedback (use "comment on"), or for intense admiration directed at famous people or gods (use in broader sense, but clarify context).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - praise a child
   - praise effort
   - offer specific praise
   - praise in public
   - overpraise a child
   
   **Root Analysis**: Root noun/verb "praise" with no productive modern affixes; related to words for "value" and "price".
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *preisier* (to prize, value) and Latin *pretiare* (to value), from *pretium* (price). Meaning shifted from valuing something highly to speaking approvingly about someone.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We try to praise the kids when they share their toys.
   - Don’t just yell when they’re loud—remember to praise them when they play nicely too.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research suggests that parents should praise children’s strategies and persistence rather than fixed traits like intelligence.
   - Balanced, sincere praise can strengthen parent–child relationships and support intrinsic motivation.

1. **Verb**: reassure
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To say or do something that makes a child feel less worried, scared, or insecure.
   - (Transitive) To restore confidence or a sense of safety, especially during big changes or stressful events.
   
   **Synonyms**: comfort, soothe, calm, encourage, put at ease
   
   **Antonyms**: alarm, frighten, unsettle, discourage
   
   **When to Use**: Describing emotional support—parents calming a child at bedtime, before school, during illness, or after a conflict.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When giving detailed factual explanations without emotional focus (use "inform" or "explain"), or when physically soothing (use "hug", "hold", or "cuddle").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reassure a child
   - reassure them that they are safe
   - reassure before bedtime
   - constantly reassure
   - words to reassure
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again) + assure (to make certain, to give confidence)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *rassurer* (to restore confidence) and Latin *re-* (again) + *securus* (safe). In parenting, it highlights repeated emotional support in response to children’s fears.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I reassured my daughter that monsters aren’t real and stayed until she fell asleep.
   - Before his first day at kindergarten, we reassured him that the teacher would help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Parents can reassure anxious children by acknowledging their feelings and offering clear, age-appropriate information.
   - Consistent routines often reassure young children by making daily life more predictable.

1. **Verb**: supervise
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To watch a child’s activities to keep them safe and guide their behavior.
   - (Transitive) To be responsible for what children are doing, especially in situations with possible risks (playgrounds, internet use, cooking, busy streets).
   
   **Synonyms**: watch over, monitor, oversee, keep an eye on, look after
   
   **Antonyms**: leave unsupervised, ignore, abandon, overlook
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about safety and responsibility—"supervising play", "supervising screen time", or "supervising homework".
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For casual visiting or friendly time (use "hang out with"), or for deep emotional work (use "support", "counsel").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - supervise children
   - closely supervise
   - adequately supervise
   - supervise screen time
   - supervise outdoor play
   
   **Root Analysis**: super- (over, above) + -vise (to see)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *supervidere* (to oversee), from *super-* (over) + *videre* (to see). In parenting, it refers to the duty to watch over children’s safety and activities.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You still need to supervise them in the pool, even if they can swim a little.
   - I can cook if you supervise the kids in the living room.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Young children require close supervision around water, traffic, and hazardous household items.
   - Guidelines advise parents to supervise online activities and discuss digital safety with their children.
