1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the company of someone; together in the same place or activity.
   - *(Temporal)* At the same time as someone else’s action or state.
   - *(Abstract)* Using something as a tool or method; having a particular quality, attitude, or condition.
   
   **Synonyms**: alongside (spatial), together with (spatial), using (instrumental), having (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: without, alone, lacking
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about doing things together with children or other caregivers, using particular tools or attitudes in parenting ("parenting with patience", "discipline with love").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For opposition or conflict (use "against"), or for origins and sources (use "from").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with the kids, with my son, with both parents, with love, with patience, with clear rules
   - **Verb + prep**: play with, talk with, stay with, argue with, connect with, bond with
   - **Adjective + prep**: patient with, gentle with, strict with, honest with, frustrated with
   - **Noun + prep**: time with family, conflict with teenagers, relationship with your child
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple preposition, historically related to ideas of association and presence.
   
   **Etymology**: Old English origin, from forms meaning "against, toward, together with"; over time it developed the modern sense of accompaniment and association.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I love spending time with the kids after work.
   - Try to stay calm when you talk with her about her grades.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Children who regularly engage in shared activities with caregivers often develop stronger attachment bonds.
   - Setting clear expectations with children works best when combined with warmth and respect.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* During a period of time.
   - *(Purpose)* In order to achieve something; with the aim of.
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating benefit, target, or reason.
   
   **Synonyms**: during (temporal), in order to (purpose), on behalf of (benefit), because of (reason in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: against (opposition), without (lack of purpose/benefit)
   
   **When to Use**: Setting rules or routines ("for an hour"), explaining reasons ("for your safety"), or talking about who something is meant to help ("support for parents").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For locations (use "at" or "in"), or to describe what something is made of (use "of").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for an hour, for bedtime, for school, for your own good, for your safety
   - **Verb + prep**: care for, provide for, ask for, hope for, prepare for
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready for, responsible for, good for, important for
   - **Noun + prep**: rules for homework, consequences for hitting, support for new parents
   
   **Root Analysis**: Simple preposition historically linked to ideas of purpose and benefit.
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English forms meaning "before, on account of, for the sake of"; in modern parenting language it often signals purpose and benefit ("for your future").
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - You can play for twenty minutes and then it’s time for bed.
   - I set these rules for your safety, not to punish you.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Consistent routines are important for children’s sense of security.
   - Parenting courses can provide valuable support for caregivers under stress.

1. **Preposition**: without (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Not having something present or available.
   - *(Temporal)* During a period when something does not happen.
   - *(Abstract)* Lacking a quality, resource, or behavior.
   
   **Synonyms**: lacking, minus, free of, in the absence of
   
   **Antonyms**: with, including, accompanied by
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about parenting choices that avoid certain behaviors or tools ("without shouting", "without screens"), or describing children’s experiences when support is missing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For separation between two objects or people (use "between"), or to express opposition (use "against").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: without shouting, without punishment, without supervision, without sleep, without support
   - **Verb + prep**: grow up without, manage without, cope without, parent without help
   - **Adjective + prep**: comfortable without, anxious without, lost without
   - **Noun + prep**: life without parents, boundaries without yelling, rules without punishment
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (together) + out (outside, lacking), forming the opposite of "with".
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *wiðutan* meaning "outside, beyond", later generalized to mean "not having" or "lacking".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We’re trying to handle conflicts without yelling.
   - The baby won’t sleep without her favorite blanket.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Many programs teach parents how to set limits without using physical punishment.
   - Growing up without a stable caregiver can increase the risk of emotional difficulties.

1. **Preposition**: between (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the space that separates two or more people or things.
   - *(Temporal)* In the period separating two points in time.
   - *(Abstract)* Involving a relationship, comparison, or distribution among two or more people or aspects.
   
   **Synonyms**: among (for more than two), in the middle of, separating
   
   **Antonyms**: alone, within (inside one thing), outside
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about relationships or conflicts between family members ("between siblings"), sharing responsibilities ("between parents"), or splitting time ("between two homes").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When focusing on the inside of a single thing (use "within" or "inside"), or for choosing from a larger group in a general sense (often use "among").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: between you and me, between parents and children, between siblings, between home and school
   - **Verb + prep**: choose between, move between, split time between, communicate between
   - **Adjective + prep**: fair between, shared between, balanced between
   - **Noun + prep**: agreement between parents, conflict between brothers, balance between work and family
   
   **Root Analysis**: be- (by) + tween (two), related to "two".
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *betwēonum* or *betweonum* meaning "in the space separating two"; now extended to emotional and practical relationships in parenting and family life.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We try to split chores fairly between the kids.
   - There’s a lot of tension between him and his teenage son right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Clear communication between caregivers and children reduces misunderstandings about rules.
   - Joint custody arrangements require coordination between households.

1. **Preposition**: around (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In many places across an area; surrounding a place.
   - *(Temporal)* Approximately at a time.
   - *(Abstract)* Loosely connected to a topic or activity; centered on.
   
   **Synonyms**: about (approximate), all over, surrounding, roughly (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly (temporal), away from, outside
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about general routines and parenting life ("around bedtime", "chores around the house"), or activities involving children in many parts of the home.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For precise locations (use "at" or "in front of"), or exact times (use "at" with a clock time).
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: around the house, around the table, around bedtime, around age three, around meal times
   - **Verb + prep**: run around, play around, help around, be around, organize around
   - **Adjective + prep**: flexible around, clear around expectations, strict around screens
   - **Noun + prep**: rules around screen time, routines around homework, support around parenting
   
   **Root Analysis**: a- (on) + round (circular, surrounding).
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French and Middle English forms meaning "in a circle, on every side"; now commonly used for approximate times and diffuse locations in everyday parenting talk.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The kids leave their toys all around the house.
   - We usually start the bedtime routine around eight o’clock.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Family routines around mealtimes and bedtime can strongly influence children’s behavior.
   - Many parenting guidelines recommend setting clear expectations around digital device use.
