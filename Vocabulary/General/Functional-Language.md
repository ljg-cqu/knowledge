1. **Functional Expression**: Could you please [do something]? (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite way to ask someone to do something, focusing on their ability and willingness rather than giving an order.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal (depending on tone and context)
   - *Directness*: indirect (question form instead of direct imperative)
   
   **Synonyms**: Would you mind [doing]...?, Would it be possible to...?, Can you [do something]?, Id appreciate it if you could...
   
   **Antonyms**: Dont do that., Please stop..., Im not asking you to...
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Everyday polite requests at work, in emails, in service situations, and in study contexts.
   - When you want to sound respectful but still clear.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - When the situation requires a clear instruction (then use a direct imperative with "please").
   - When talking to small children who may not understand complex structures.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Could you please send me the file?
   - Could you possibly explain that again?
   - Could you help me with this?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Adding "please" softens the request; adding "possibly" or "perhaps" makes it even more gentle.
   - Intonation strongly affects how polite it sounds; rising tone at the end signals a question.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Positive: Sure, Id be happy to. / Of course. / No problem.
   - Negative/limited: Im afraid I cant right now. / I can, but later today.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Could you please grab me a coffee when you go?
   - Could you send me the link again? I lost it.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Could you please review the draft by Friday?
   - Could you possibly share the updated schedule with the whole team?

1. **Functional Expression**: I think (that)... (function: expressing opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces a personal opinion or belief in a soft, everyday way.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct, but relatively soft
   
   **Synonyms**: In my opinion..., I believe..., It seems to me that..., From my point of view...
   
   **Antonyms**: I know that..., Im certain that..., Theres no doubt that...
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Sharing views in conversation, meetings, and discussions when certainty is not absolute.
   - Good default opener for comments.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - In formal writing where hedging needs to be more precise (use "It appears that", "The evidence suggests that").
   - When your role requires clear decisions rather than personal opinions.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I think we should...
   - I think its a good idea.
   - I dont think thats necessary.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Softens statements and invites discussion rather than closing it.
   - Overuse can make you sound uncertain; balance with stronger phrases when needed.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Agreement: I think so too. / I agree.
   - Disagreement: I see it differently. / Im not sure I agree.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think we should order food before it gets too late.
   - I dont think this app is very user‑friendly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - I think we need more data before making a final decision.
   - I think this approach aligns well with our long‑term goals.

1. **Functional Expression**: From my point of view, ... (function: expressing opinion/stance)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Signals that what follows is your personal perspective, not an absolute truth.
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect, clearly framed as personal
   
   **Synonyms**: In my view..., From my perspective..., As I see it..., Personally, I think...
   
   **Antonyms**: Objectively..., It is generally agreed that..., The general view is that...
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - In discussions where multiple perspectives are welcome.
   - When you want to respect other views and avoid sounding dogmatic.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - In contexts where you are presenting shared decisions rather than personal opinions.
   - In very casual talk where "I think" is more natural.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - From my point of view, this is a good compromise.
   - From my perspective, the main problem is time.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Helps maintain good relationships by clearly marking your statement as your own view.
   - Useful when disagreeing or raising concerns.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thats a fair point. / I see where youre coming from.
   - From my point of view, ... (offering another perspective)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - From my point of view, working from home has more pros than cons.
   - From my perspective, its better to be honest about delays.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - From my point of view, the current timeline is optimistic.
   - From my perspective, the key risk is communication rather than technology.

1. **Functional Expression**: Just to clarify, ... (function: clarifying/checking understanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces a question or restatement to make sure everyone has the same understanding.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect but clear
   
   **Synonyms**: So, if I understand correctly..., Let me make sure Ive got this right..., To clarify...
   
   **Antonyms**: Never mind., Forget it., It doesnt matter. (ending clarification)
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Meetings, calls, and emails when details matter and misunderstandings would be costly.
   - When giving or receiving instructions, decisions, or feedback.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - For very small details that dont affect the outcome.
   - If it might sound like youre questioning someones competence in a sensitive moment (soften further).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just to clarify, youre suggesting that...
   - Just to clarify, the deadline is next Friday, right?
   - To clarify, our main goal is...
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Signals that your goal is understanding, not criticism.
   - Tone should be calm and curious, not sharp.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yes, thats correct. / Exactly.
   - Not quitedwhat I meant was...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Just to clarify, are we meeting online or in person?
   - Just to clarify, you need this today or tomorrow?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Just to clarify, does this change apply to all projects or only new ones?
   - To clarify, our priority is to improve response time, not to add new features.

1. **Functional Expression**: To be honest, ... (function: hedging/stance marking)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Signals that you are about to give a personal, sometimes uncomfortable or direct opinion.
   - *Formality*: informal to semi‑formal
   - *Directness*: can be very direct after the softening phrase
   
   **Synonyms**: Honestly..., Frankly..., If Im honest..., To be frank...
   
   **Antonyms**: Politely speaking..., Officially..., In theory... (distancing phrases)
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - When you want to show sincerity and openness, especially about your real feelings.
   - Often before slightly negative feedback or disagreement.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - With people who may feel hurt by frankness.
   - In very formal writing or when representing an official position.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - To be honest, Im not sure this is working.
   - Honestly, I find the interface confusing.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Can suggest that you were not fully honest before, so overuse may sound strange.
   - Tone should be gentle when giving criticism.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Thanks for being honest. / I appreciate your honesty.
   - I feel the same way. / I see it differently, but thanks for sharing.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - To be honest, Im too tired to go out tonight.
   - Honestly, I dont really like this design.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - To be honest, Im concerned about the current level of risk.
   - Frankly, we need clearer priorities before we move forward.

1. **Functional Expression**: What do you think? (function: inviting opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Asks someone for their opinion, encouraging participation and shared decision‑making.
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: direct question, but friendly
   
   **Synonyms**: How do you see it?, Whats your view?, How do you feel about this?, Any thoughts?
   
   **Antonyms**: Ive decided., This is not up for discussion., Thats final.
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - After sharing an idea or proposal and you want feedback.
   - In teamwork, leadership, or teaching to involve others.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - When there is no real choice (forced decisions); it may feel fake.
   - In very formal announcements where discussion is not expected.
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - What do you think about this?
   - How do you feel about it?
   - Any thoughts on this?
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - Shows respect and openness; good for building trust.
   - Can reduce power distance when used by leaders.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I think its a good idea because...
   - Im not sure. Maybe we could...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Im thinking of changing jobs. What do you think?
   - We could meet earlier, what do you think?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Here are three options; what do you think works best for the team?
   - Id like to hear your view. What do you think about this approach?

1. **Functional Expression**: The reason is that... / The reason why... is that... (function: explaining causation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Introduces an explanation for a decision, result, or behavior.
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: direct explanation
   
   **Synonyms**: This is because..., The explanation is that..., This happens because...
   
   **Antonyms**: The result is..., The consequence is... (effect, not cause)
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - When answering "why" questions.
   - When giving clear reasons in teaching, meetings, or writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - In very formal writing where shorter forms like "because" are preferred.
   - When the cause is not known (avoid guessing as fact).
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - The reason is that we dont have enough data.
   - The reason why this is difficult is that expectations are not clear.
   - This is because we started late.
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: 
   - "The reason is because..." is common in speech but considered redundant in strict grammar.
   - Emphasizes logic and transparency.
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Ah, I see. / That makes sense.
   - So thats why...
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The reason Im late is that the bus broke down.
   - The reason why Im worried is that were running out of time.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reason this approach is effective is that it reduces unnecessary steps.
   - The reason for the delay is that several approvals are still pending.
