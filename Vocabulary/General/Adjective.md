1. **Adjective**: basic (comp: more basic, sup: most basic)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Forming the simplest or most important part of something; necessary and not advanced. (gradable, usually attributive)
   - *(Informal)* Simple and not very interesting or special.
   
   **Synonyms**: simple, fundamental, essential, elementary, primary
   
   **Antonyms**: advanced, complex, sophisticated
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about simple levels, essential skills, or not‑advanced versions of something in everyday and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal writing where "fundamental" or "elementary" may sound better; avoid using the informal meaning ("boring, unoriginal") in professional contexts.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - basic ideas
   - basic skills
   - basic knowledge
   - basic level
   - basic needs
   
   **Root Analysis**: base (foundation) + -ic (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: From French *basique*, from "base" meaning foundation. Used in chemistry and more generally as "fundamental" in English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I only have a basic understanding of coding.
   - Lets start with some basic exercises.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The course covers basic concepts in statistics.
   - Access to clean water is a basic human need.

1. **Adjective**: major (comp: more major, sup: most major)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Very large or important when compared to others of the same kind; serious or significant. (gradable, attributive)
   
   **Synonyms**: significant, important, key, serious, substantial
   
   **Antonyms**: minor, small, insignificant
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral, common word for size or importance in news, reports, and conversations.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When a softer word like "noticeable" or "moderate" is better; avoid overusing it for very small things.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - major change
   - major problem
   - major decision
   - major factor
   - major advantage
   
   **Root Analysis**: maj- (greater) + -or (comparative)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *maior* (greater), comparative of *magnus* (great). English borrowed it for "greater in importance".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Losing that file was a major mistake.
   - Moving to another country is a major life decision.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Cost is a major factor in the final decision.
   - The study identifies three major trends in consumer behavior.

1. **Adjective**: significant (comp: more significant, sup: most significant)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Large or important enough to have an effect or to be noticed. (gradable)
   - *(Statistical)* Unlikely to have happened by chance.
   
   **Synonyms**: important, meaningful, considerable, notable
   
   **Antonyms**: insignificant, negligible, minor
   
   **When to Use**: Formal and neutral contexts for results, changes, differences, or events that matter.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For very small differences; in everyday very casual talk where "big" or "important" sounds more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - significant change
   - significant difference
   - statistically significant
   - significant impact
   - significant amount
   
   **Root Analysis**: sign (mark) + -ific (making) + -ant (doing)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *significans* (showing, meaning), from *significare* (to indicate). In English, used for "meaningful" and later for "statistically meaningful".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Theres been a significant improvement in my sleep.
   - The weather made a significant difference to the trip.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The results show a significant reduction in error rates.
   - No statistically significant difference was found between the two groups.

1. **Adjective**: typical (comp: more typical, sup: most typical)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Having the usual qualities or features of a particular type of person or thing. (gradable)
   - *(Often disapproving)* Behaving in a way that is expected, especially when annoying.
   
   **Synonyms**: usual, normal, characteristic, representative
   
   **Antonyms**: unusual, atypical, exceptional
   
   **When to Use**: Describing common patterns, behaviors, or examples in everyday and academic contexts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need to emphasize rarity or exception; avoid using the disapproving tone in sensitive situations.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - typical example
   - typical behavior
   - typical day
   - typical pattern
   - typical situation
   
   **Root Analysis**: typ- (model, type) + -ical (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: From Late Latin *typicus* (of a type), from Greek *typos* (model, impression). In English, used for "characteristic".
   
   **Examples [Casual**]: 
   - On a typical day I get up around seven.
   - Thats such a typical comment from him.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The chart shows a typical pattern of growth over time.
   - This case is typical of many small organizations.

1. **Adjective**: common (comp: more common, sup: most common)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Happening often or existing in large numbers; usual. (gradable)
   - Shared by two or more people or things.
   
   **Synonyms**: frequent, usual, widespread, ordinary, shared
   
   **Antonyms**: rare, unusual, uncommon
   
   **When to Use**: Very frequent in everyday talk and writing to describe frequency or shared features.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the tone might sound negative ("common" can imply "low" in some contexts, especially in British English); use "ordinary" or "everyday" if you want a softer feel.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - common problem
   - common mistake
   - in common
   - common sense
   - common practice
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + mon (share) from Latin *communis*
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *communis* (shared by all), through Old French *comun*. In English, used for both "shared" and "frequent".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Its quite common to feel nervous before a big presentation.
   - We have a lot in common.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This is a common feature of many modern cities.
   - Common mistakes are listed in the appendix.

1. **Adjective**: practical (comp: more practical, sup: most practical)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Relating to real situations rather than ideas or theories; useful and sensible. (gradable)
   - Good at organizing things and dealing with problems.
   
   **Synonyms**: realistic, useful, hands‑on, pragmatic, workable
   
   **Antonyms**: theoretical, impractical, unrealistic
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about real‑world use, everyday solutions, or people who are good at dealing with reality.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to emphasize creativity or imagination more than usefulness.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - practical advice
   - practical solution
   - practical skills
   - practical reasons
   - practical experience
   
   **Root Analysis**: pract- (do, act) + -ical (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: From Late Latin *practicus*, from Greek *praktikos* (concerned with action), from *prattein* (to do).
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thats a very practical way to save time.
   - Shes a practical person who just gets things done.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The workshop focuses on practical applications of the theory.
   - We need a practical solution that can be implemented this quarter.

1. **Adjective**: efficient (comp: more efficient, sup: most efficient)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Working well and quickly without wasting time, energy, or resources. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: effective (in some contexts), productive, streamlined, economical
   
   **Antonyms**: inefficient, wasteful, slow
   
   **When to Use**: Describing people, systems, tools, or processes that do more with less.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you only mean "successful" (use "effective"); when the focus is on quality rather than speed or resources.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - efficient system
   - energy‑efficient
   - time‑efficient
   - highly efficient
   - efficient use of resources
   
   **Root Analysis**: ef- (out) + fic (do, make) + -ient (being)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *efficiens* (producing, effective), from *efficere* (to bring about). In English, it focuses on output vs. input.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This new app is much more efficient than the old one.
   - She has a very efficient morning routine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new process is more efficient in terms of both time and cost.
   - Efficient resource use is a key goal of the project.

1. **Adjective**: effective (comp: more effective, sup: most effective)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Producing the result that was wanted or intended. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: successful, powerful, efficient (in some contexts), impactful
   
   **Antonyms**: ineffective, weak, unsuccessful
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about results, solutions, tools, or actions that actually work.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you only mean "quick" or "cheap" (use "efficient"); avoid using without describing "effective at what".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - effective method
   - highly effective
   - effective communication
   - effective solution
   - effective way to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ef- (out) + fect (do, make) + -ive (having the quality of)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *effectivus* (productive), from *efficere* (to bring about). In English, strongly linked to achieving results.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Thats a really effective way to remember new words.
   - The medicine was very effective.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Regular feedback is an effective tool for improving performance.
   - The campaign was effective in raising public awareness.

1. **Adjective**: complex (comp: more complex, sup: most complex)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Consisting of many different and connected parts; not easy to understand or deal with. (gradable)
   
   **Synonyms**: complicated, detailed, multi‑layered, sophisticated
   
   **Antonyms**: simple, straightforward, basic
   
   **When to Use**: Describing systems, problems, emotions, or ideas with many parts.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the difficulty is mainly emotional ("hard" or "tough" might be better); avoid exaggerating small difficulties.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - complex system
   - complex problem
   - complex relationship
   - complex process
   - highly complex
   
   **Root Analysis**: com- (together) + plex (plait, fold) from Latin *complexus*
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *complexus* (embracing, entwined). In English, used for things with many connected parts.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The instructions are a bit complex for beginners.
   - People are complex; its not always easy to know what they feel.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The project involves a complex set of dependencies.
   - Human behavior is influenced by complex social and psychological factors.

1. **Adjective**: flexible (comp: more flexible, sup: most flexible)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Able to change or be changed easily according to the situation. (gradable)
   - *(Physical)* Able to bend without breaking.
   
   **Synonyms**: adaptable, adjustable, open‑minded, elastic
   
   **Antonyms**: rigid, inflexible, fixed
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about people, plans, schedules, or systems that can adjust.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you need a stronger word like "unstable" or "uncertain"; avoid confusing physical flexibility with mental or organizational flexibility.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - flexible schedule
   - flexible approach
   - flexible working
   - be flexible
   - flexible rules
   
   **Root Analysis**: flex (bend) + -ible (able to)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *flexibilis* (that can be bent), from *flectere* (to bend). Extended to mental and organizational uses in English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My boss is pretty flexible about working from home.
   - We need a more flexible plan in case things change.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Flexible policies can respond more quickly to changing conditions.
   - The system architecture is designed to be flexible and extensible.
