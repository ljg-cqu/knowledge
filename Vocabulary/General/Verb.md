1. **Verb**: handle
   **Meaning**: 
   - To deal with a situation, person, or problem, especially in a practical or effective way. (usually transitive)
   - *(Extended)* To touch, hold, or move something with your hands. (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: deal with, manage, cope with, take care of, address
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, avoid, mishandle
   
   **When to Use**: Everyday and professional contexts when you want a neutral, flexible verb for managing work, people, or difficulties.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal academic writing where a more specific verb ("process", "treat", "address") may be preferred; when you literally mean "lift" or "carry".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - handle a problem
   - handle pressure
   - handle a request
   - handle complaints
   - handle a situation
   
   **Root Analysis**: hand (noun) + -le (verb‑forming suffix)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *handlian* (to touch, manage), based on "hand". The sense "deal with" became common in Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Dont worry, Ill handle it.
   - She handles stress much better than I do.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The support team handles all technical inquiries.
   - Sensitive information must be handled with great care.

1. **Verb**: improve
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make something better, or to become better over time. (transitive and intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: enhance, develop, upgrade, refine, strengthen
   
   **Antonyms**: worsen, decline, deteriorate
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about progress in skills, systems, results, or quality in almost any context.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the change is only about size or number (use "increase" or "decrease"); when you need a more precise verb like "optimize" or "correct".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - improve quality
   - improve performance
   - improve your skills
   - significantly improve
   - room to improve
   
   **Root Analysis**: im- (in) + prove (profit, benefit) from Old French *emprover*
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *emprover* (to use to profit), from Latin *probare* (to test, approve). Sense shifted to "make better" in English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Im trying to improve my speaking skills.
   - Things should improve once we get more help.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new design significantly improves user experience.
   - Training programs aim to improve overall productivity.

1. **Verb**: manage
   **Meaning**: 
   - To be in charge of something and make decisions about it. (transitive)
   - To succeed in dealing with or controlling something, especially a difficult situation. (transitive/intransitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: oversee, supervise, run, control, handle
   
   **Antonyms**: mismanage, neglect, fail (to manage)
   
   **When to Use**: Work, study, and life contexts for running projects, teams, time, money, or personal energy.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you only mean "organize" a small task; in very casual speech where "deal with" might sound more natural.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - manage a team
   - manage time
   - manage expectations
   - manage stress
   - manage resources
   
   **Root Analysis**: man- (hand) from Latin *manus* + -age (action)
   
   **Etymology**: From Italian *maneggiare* (to handle), from Latin *manus* (hand). Entered English via French *manager* as "to direct, control".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Im still learning how to manage my time better.
   - She manages a small team of designers.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The department manages several high‑priority projects.
   - Leaders must manage risk while pursuing growth.

1. **Verb**: increase
   **Meaning**: 
   - To become larger in number, amount, level, or degree; to make something larger in this way. (intransitive and transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: rise, grow, go up, boost, raise
   
   **Antonyms**: decrease, reduce, decline, drop
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about numbers, prices, speed, volume, intensity, or frequency in any register.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When you want to describe improvement in quality (use "improve"); avoid doubling with "increase up" (just "increase").
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - increase by X percent
   - increase in demand
   - gradually increase
   - significantly increase
   - increase productivity
   
   **Root Analysis**: in- (in, into) + crease (grow) from Latin *crescere*
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *increscere* (to grow), via Old French *encroistre*. The spelling stabilized as "increase" in Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The price of coffee has increased again.
   - I need to increase how much water I drink every day.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Traffic to the website increased by 30% last quarter.
   - The company aims to increase efficiency across all departments.

1. **Verb**: reduce
   **Meaning**: 
   - To make something smaller or less in size, amount, or degree. (transitive)
   - *(Extended)* To simplify or bring something down to a more basic form.
   
   **Synonyms**: decrease, cut, lower, shrink, lessen
   
   **Antonyms**: increase, raise, expand
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about costs, risks, waste, stress, noise, or any quantity you want to lower.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When the change is positive improvement rather than just making something smaller; avoid vague use without stating "reduce what".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reduce costs
   - reduce waste
   - reduce risk
   - reduce stress
   - significantly reduce
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + duce (lead) from Latin *ducere*
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *reducere* (to lead back), via Old French *reduire*. Abstract sense "make smaller" developed in English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Im trying to reduce how much sugar I eat.
   - Turning off lights helps reduce our electricity bill.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The new process should reduce errors by at least 20%.
   - We must reduce unnecessary complexity in the system.

1. **Verb**: consider
   **Meaning**: 
   - To think carefully about something before making a decision. (transitive)
   - *(Extended)* To have a particular opinion about someone or something. (transitive, often passive)
   
   **Synonyms**: think about, reflect on, examine, weigh, regard
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, overlook, dismiss
   
   **When to Use**: Neutral, slightly formal verb for decision‑making, planning, and evaluation in both speech and writing.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For quick, casual thoughts ("think about" is more natural); when you really mean "look at" physically.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - carefully consider
   - consider the options
   - consider doing something
   - be considered (as)
   - consider the impact
   
   **Root Analysis**: con- (together) + sider (star) from Latin *considerare*
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *considerare* (to look at the stars, examine carefully). In English, it keeps the sense of "think about seriously".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Ill consider your suggestion and let you know.
   - Have you considered moving closer to work?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - We must consider both short‑term and long‑term effects.
   - The proposal will be considered by the review committee.

1. **Verb**: assume
   **Meaning**: 
   - To accept something as true without proof, often temporarily or for the sake of argument. (transitive)
   - *(Extended)* To take on a role, responsibility, or appearance. (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: suppose, presume, take for granted, accept
   
   **Antonyms**: verify, question, doubt
   
   **When to Use**: Logic, discussion, planning, and everyday talk when you talk about ideas you are treating as true.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When information is already confirmed; avoid overusing it when you mean "believe" or "know".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - assume that...
   - safely assume
   - assume responsibility
   - assume a role
   - assume the worst/best
   
   **Root Analysis**: as- (to) + sume (take) from Latin *sumere*
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *assumere* (to take up, adopt), via Old French *assumer*. Abstract sense "suppose to be true" is common in Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I just assumed you knew about the meeting.
   - Dont assume the worst before you ask.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - For this model, we assume that demand remains stable.
   - The manager will assume full responsibility for the results.

1. **Verb**: rely on
   **Meaning**: 
   - To depend on someone or something for help, support, or to do what is expected. (phrasal verb, transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: depend on, count on, trust, lean on
   
   **Antonyms**: distrust, avoid, do without
   
   **When to Use**: Talking about trust, support, systems, and people you depend on in life or work.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In very formal academic writing where "depend on" may sound more neutral; avoid "rely in" (wrong preposition).
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - rely on data
   - rely on support
   - rely heavily on
   - cant rely on
   - rely on experience
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + ly (bind) from Old French *relier* + on (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old French *relier* (to link, bind back), from Latin *religare*. The sense "depend on" developed in English with the preposition "on".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I know I can rely on you to tell me the truth.
   - We cant rely on good luck forever.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis relies on data from multiple independent sources.
   - Many communities still rely on public transport as their main option.

1. **Verb**: focus on
   **Meaning**: 
   - To give most of your attention or effort to one particular person, activity, or thing. (phrasal verb)
   
   **Synonyms**: concentrate on, pay attention to, center on, highlight
   
   **Antonyms**: ignore, neglect, distract from
   
   **When to Use**: Study, work, and everyday situations when you talk about attention and priorities.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When literally adjusting a camera or lens ("focus" alone is often enough); avoid mixing with "on" + object that is not something you can attend to.
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - focus on the main point
   - focus on results
   - focus on details
   - focus on the present
   - focus on solutions
   
   **Root Analysis**: focus (hearth, center) + on (preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *focus* (fireplace, center). Sense "center of activity" extended to mental attention in English; combined with "on" for the phrasal verb.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to focus on my exam this week.
   - Lets focus on what we can change, not what we cant.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The workshop will focus on practical communication skills.
   - Our strategy focuses on long‑term value rather than quick wins.

1. **Verb**: represent
   **Meaning**: 
   - To speak or act officially for someone else. (transitive)
   - To be a sign or symbol of something; to show or describe something. (transitive)
   
   **Synonyms**: stand for, symbolize, speak for, depict, express
   
   **Antonyms**: misrepresent, distort, conceal
   
   **When to Use**: In politics, business, art, data, and everyday speech when you talk about speaking for others or what something stands for.
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For physical visiting ("go to") or informal "be"; avoid confusing with "present".
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - represent a company
   - represent a group
   - represent an idea
   - represent a change
   - accurately represent
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (again) + present (to place before)
   
   **Etymology**: From Latin *repraesentare* (to present again, show), via Old French *representer*. In English, it covers both "act for others" and "symbolize".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Shell represent our team at the meeting.
   - For me, this song represents a really important time in my life.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - These numbers represent a significant improvement over last year.
   - The committee includes members who represent different departments.
