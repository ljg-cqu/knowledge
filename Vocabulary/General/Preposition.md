1. **Preposition**: in (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Inside or within the limits of a space, area, or container.  
     *Example meaning*: inside a room, city, or country.
   - *(Temporal)* During a period of time or after a certain time.  
     *Example meaning*: in the morning, in 2025, in two hours.
   - *(Abstract)* Involved in or part of a state, condition, or situation.  
     *Example meaning*: in trouble, in love, in detail.
   
   **Synonyms**: inside (spatial), within (formal, spatial/abstract), during (temporal, in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: out of, outside, beyond
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Spatial: to show something is inside boundaries ("in the box", "in the building").
   - Temporal: with longer time periods (months, years, seasons) and future time after a period ("in July", "in three days").
   - Abstract: with states and conditions ("in a hurry", "in danger", "in general").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - For surfaces (use "on" → "on the table").
   - For specific times like clock times or short points (use "at" → "at 7:30").
   - For days and dates (use "on" → "on Monday", "on May 5th").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in fact, in general, in detail, in public, in private
   - **Verb + prep**: believe in, take part in, succeed in, result in
   - **Adjective + prep**: interested in, rich in, strong in, experienced in
   - **Noun + prep**: increase in, change in, interest in, rise in
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "in" (inside) as a basic spatial preposition
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *in* (in, into), related to German *in* and Latin *in*. One of the oldest and most basic English prepositions.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The keys are in my bag.
   - Ill call you in a few minutes.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - There has been a steady increase in demand over the last decade.
   - The results are discussed in detail in the next section.

1. **Preposition**: on (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Touching and supported by a surface.  
     *Example meaning*: on the table, on the wall.
   - *(Temporal)* On a particular day or date.  
     *Example meaning*: on Monday, on 5 May.
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning or about a topic; using a device or system.  
     *Example meaning*: on this topic, on the phone, on the internet.
   
   **Synonyms**: upon (more formal/literary in some spatial uses), about (for topics, informal)
   
   **Antonyms**: off, under, beneath
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - For surfaces ("on the desk", "on the floor").
   - For specific days/dates ("on Friday", "on my birthday").
   - For devices and platforms ("on TV", "on Zoom", "on social media").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - For being inside spaces (use "in" → "in the room").
   - For months/years (use "in" → "in May", "in 2024").
   - For times of day without "day" (often use "in" or "at" → "in the morning", "at night").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on purpose, on time, on average, on the one hand
   - **Verb + prep**: depend on, focus on, rely on, work on
   - **Adjective + prep**: based on, keen on, hard on (strict with)
   - **Noun + prep**: impact on, effect on, focus on
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "on" (on, in, into) overlapping with "in" historically
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *on* (on, in, into), related to German *an*. Over time, English separated many uses from "in".
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The book is on the table.
   - Were meeting on Friday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This report focuses on three main questions.
   - The policy has a direct impact on customer satisfaction.

1. **Preposition**: at (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* At a specific point or location.  
     *Example meaning*: at the door, at the station.
   - *(Temporal)* At a specific clock time or fixed point in time.  
     *Example meaning*: at 7 oclock, at noon.
   - *(Abstract)* In a particular state, level, or activity.  
     *Example meaning*: at risk, at work, at peace.
   
   **Synonyms**: by (near a place, in some contexts), around (approximate position/time)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, far from (spatial), before/after (temporal)
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - For exact places/points ("at the bus stop", "at home").
   - For precise times ("at 9:30", "at midnight").
   - For activities/states ("at work", "at rest", "at war").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - For larger areas (use "in" → "in the city").
   - For days/dates (use "on" → "on Monday").
   - For months/years (use "in").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at least, at most, at first, at last
   - **Verb + prep**: look at, arrive at, succeed at, excel at
   - **Adjective + prep**: good at, bad at, surprised at, angry at
   - **Noun + prep**: attempt at, chance at, shot at
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "æt" indicating point or near‑ness
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *æt* (at, near, by), related to Old Norse *at*.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Ill meet you at the station.
   - The meeting starts at nine.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The system is currently operating at full capacity.
   - Participants were asked to respond at regular intervals.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract: purpose)* With the aim of doing or getting something.  
     *Example meaning*: for work, for fun.
   - *(Temporal)* For a length of time.  
     *Example meaning*: for two years, for a while.
   - *(Abstract: benefit)* Intended to help or be used by someone or something.  
     *Example meaning*: for you, for beginners.
   
   **Synonyms**: in order to (purpose, with verb), during (some time‑period contexts), on behalf of (in some benefit contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: against (opposite purpose), during (as contrast in some grammar explanations)
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Showing purpose ("for practice", "for safety").
   - Indicating duration with present perfect/simple past ("for three months").
   - Showing who receives something ("for my friend").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - Before a verb in base form when "to" is required ("to study", not *"for study"* as a verb form).
   - For exact time when something started (use "since" → "since 2019").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for example, for instance, for now, for sure
   - **Verb + prep**: apply for, ask for, wait for, care for
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready for, suitable for, responsible for, famous for
   - **Noun + prep**: reason for, demand for, need for
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English "for" (in front of, on account of)
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *for* (before, on account of), related to German *für*. Very flexible in Modern English.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This gift is for you.
   - Ive lived here for six years.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Thank you for your attention.
   - There is strong demand for clear, practical guidance.

1. **Preposition**: about (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* On the subject of; concerning.  
     *Example meaning*: talk about, think about.
   - *(Approximate)* Approximately or nearly.  
     *Example meaning*: about ten minutes, about 100 people.
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning (more formal), around (for numbers, approximate)
   
   **Antonyms**: exactly (for numbers), unrelated to (for topics)
   
   **When to Use**: 
   - Introducing topics of thought, speech, or writing ("a book about time management").
   - For approximate numbers or amounts ("about ten", "about half").
   
   **When NOT to Use**: 
   - In very formal writing, prefer "regarding" or "concerning" for topic meaning.
   - For movement around something (use "around" or "round" → "walk around the park").
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: about to (be about to do), just about, all about
   - **Verb + prep**: talk about, think about, worry about, care about
   - **Adjective + prep**: sure about, worried about, excited about
   - **Noun + prep**: information about, questions about, discussion about
   
   **Root Analysis**: a‑ (on) + bout (around, near) from Old English construction
   
   **Etymology**: From Old English *onbūtan* (on the outside of, around). Topic meaning developed over time.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - What are you thinking about?
   - There were about twenty people at the party.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - This report is about improving everyday communication skills.
   - We received several questions about the new procedure.
