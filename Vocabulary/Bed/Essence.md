1. Q: What is the page-level essence (purpose, scope, and organizing structure) of vocabulary for "Bed"?
   A:
   - **Purpose**: Provide an 80/20 set of English words and expressions for talking about beds, bedroom furniture, bedding, and sleep-related routines so learners can describe their real lives and common situations naturally.
   - **Scope**: Core everyday and professional terms about beds (bed, mattress, pillow, sheet, blanket, bedding, bedroom), bedtime routines, sleep quality, and simple sleep problems, including their most important collocations and patterns. Excludes detailed medical sleep-disorder terminology, deep neuroscience of sleep, and idioms whose meaning is not clearly connected to literal bed or sleep.
   - **Central Questions**:
     - If I can only learn 20–40 words and phrases about beds and sleep, which ones matter most for everyday communication?
     - How do I describe my sleeping setup, bedtime routine, and sleep quality clearly in English?
     - How do I adjust my bed-and-sleep vocabulary between casual conversation, hospitality settings, and basic medical or wellbeing contexts?
     - How do I avoid common learner mistakes with prepositions, collocations, and register around "bed" and "sleep"?
   - **Organizing Dimensions**:
     - **Part of speech & function**: nouns (bed, mattress, pillow), verbs and expressions (go to bed, fall asleep, wake up), adjectives/adverbs (comfortable, restless, sleep soundly), multi-word expressions (stay up late, take a nap).
     - **Context**: home and family, travel and hospitality, health and wellbeing, work and study routines.
     - **Register & tone**: neutral/core, informal/slang, formal/professional, child-directed.
     - **Time focus**: before bed (bedtime routine), during sleep (in bed), after sleep (waking up, getting out of bed).
   - **Minimal Glossary**:
     - Term: **bed** — The basic piece of furniture used for sleeping.
     - Term: **mattress** — The padded support you lie on, placed on a bed frame.
     - Term: **pillow** — A soft cushion that supports your head while you sleep.
     - Term: **bedding** — All the covers and linens on a bed (sheets, blankets, pillowcases, comforter/duvet).
     - Term: **bedtime** — The usual time when someone goes to bed to sleep.
     - Term: **sleep in** — To stay in bed later than your usual wake-up time.
     - Term: **stay up late** — To go to bed much later than your normal bedtime.
   - **Wiki Neighborhood**:
     - **Upstream / Parent**: Daily routines; Home and rooms vocabulary; Health and wellbeing (sleep and rest).
     - **Siblings**: Living room and furniture; Bathroom and personal care; Morning routine; Evening routine; Travel and accommodation basics.
     - **Downstream**: Hotel and accommodation conversations; Doctor or therapist visits about sleep; Productivity and energy discussions; Parenting routines and bedtime rules.
   - **Decision-Critical Metadata**:
     - **Decision Criticality**: Blocks / Roles / Action / Quantified
     - **Primary Stakeholders / Roles**: English learners (A2–B2); ESL/EFL teachers; Exam candidates preparing speaking and writing tasks that include daily routines or health.
     - **Time Sensitivity**: Evergreen

1. Q: Essence card – Core bed objects and bedding
   A:
   - **Label**: Core bed objects and bedding
   - **Core Idea**: Master the small set of high-frequency nouns that name the bed itself and its main parts (bed, mattress, pillow, sheet, blanket, comforter/duvet, bedding, bedroom).
   - **Why It Matters**: These words let you describe your sleeping setup, ask for or buy the right items, and understand basic instructions in homes, hotels, dormitories, or hospitals.
   - **Type**: concept
   - **Dimensions**: part of speech = nouns; context = home, travel, medical
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - bed
     - mattress
     - bedding

1. Q: Essence card – Sleep actions and routines
   A:
   - **Label**: Sleep actions and routines
   - **Core Idea**: Focus on high-frequency verbs and expressions for going to bed, sleeping, and getting up (go to bed, fall asleep, sleep, wake up, get out of bed, sleep in, stay up late, take a nap).
   - **Why It Matters**: These patterns let you explain your daily routine, talk about good or bad habits, and follow or give basic instructions about when to sleep or get up.
   - **Type**: pattern
   - **Dimensions**: part of speech = verbs and multi-word expressions; time focus = before, during, and after sleep
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - go to bed
     - stay up late
     - take a nap

1. Q: Essence card – Sleep quality and comfort adjectives
   A:
   - **Label**: Sleep quality and comfort adjectives
   - **Core Idea**: Learn adjectives that describe the bed and the sleep you get from it (comfortable, uncomfortable, soft, firm, restful, restless, tired, sleepy, exhausted, wide awake).
   - **Why It Matters**: These words help you explain whether your bed works for you, describe problems to a doctor, give feedback to hosts, or review a hotel or sleep product.
   - **Type**: concept
   - **Dimensions**: part of speech = adjectives; context = health and wellbeing, product and service reviews
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - comfortable
     - restless
     - exhausted

1. Q: Essence card – Formal vs casual talk about bed and sleep
   A:
   - **Label**: Formal vs casual talk about bed and sleep
   - **Core Idea**: Distinguish neutral phrases (go to bed, go to sleep) from informal or slang ones (crash, crash on the sofa, hit the sack) and more formal ones (turn in, retire for the night).
   - **Why It Matters**: Choosing the right register keeps you polite and appropriate with teachers, doctors, or hotel staff while still sounding natural and relaxed with friends and family.
   - **Type**: constraint
   - **Dimensions**: register and tone = neutral / informal / formal; context = social vs professional situations
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - crash (go to bed very quickly, usually because you are very tired)
     - hit the sack
     - retire for the night

1. Q: Essence card – Basic sleep problems and needs
   A:
   - **Label**: Basic sleep problems and needs
   - **Core Idea**: Use common phrases for talking about not getting enough good sleep (have trouble sleeping, can't fall asleep, wake up in the night, sleep badly, lack of sleep).
   - **Why It Matters**: These expressions are essential for everyday complaints, medical visits, and explaining how your sleep affects your energy, mood, and performance.
   - **Type**: concept
   - **Dimensions**: context = health and wellbeing; time focus = during and after sleep
   - **Essential Terms**:
     - have trouble sleeping
     - lack of sleep
     - sleep badly
