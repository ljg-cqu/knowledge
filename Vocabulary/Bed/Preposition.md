1. **Preposition**: in (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Positioned within the confines or boundaries of something; enclosed by
   - *(Temporal)* During a period of time
   - *(Abstract)* In a state or condition
   
   **Synonyms**: inside (spatial), within, during (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: out (of), outside, beyond
   
   **When to Use**: Use "in bed" (not "on bed") to indicate the state of being under covers for sleeping or resting; universal usage
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "on the bed" (on top of covers/surface)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: in bed, in the bedroom, in sleep
   - **Verb + prep**: lie in bed, stay in bed, get in bed
   - **Adjective + prep**: comfortable in bed
   - **Noun + prep**: time in bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (Old English inn, simple preposition)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English in → unchanged meaning (oldest English preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm staying in bed all morning.
   - Are you still in bed?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients should remain in bed during the recovery period.
   - Time spent in bed does not necessarily correlate with sleep duration.

1. **Preposition**: on (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Positioned at the surface or top of something; supported by
   - *(Temporal)* At a specific time or date
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning or about
   
   **Synonyms**: upon, atop, over (spatial)
   
   **Antonyms**: off, under, beneath
   
   **When to Use**: Use "on the bed" to indicate position on top of the bed surface (not under covers); sitting or lying on top
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use for being under covers sleeping (use "in bed")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on the bed, on the mattress, on the pillow
   - **Verb + prep**: sit on the bed, put on the bed, place on the bed
   - **Adjective + prep**: placed on the bed
   - **Noun + prep**: books on the bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: on (Old English on, an)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English on → unchanged meaning (fundamental spatial preposition)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't sit on the bed with your dirty clothes!
   - I left my phone on the bed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Personal items should not be placed on the bed in clinical settings.
   - The study examined objects commonly left on the bed surface.

1. **Preposition**: under (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Positioned below or beneath something; covered by
   - *(Abstract)* Subject to or affected by
   
   **Synonyms**: beneath, below, underneath
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, on top of
   
   **When to Use**: "Under the covers/blankets" indicates position beneath bed linens; common in all contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "underneath the bed" (below the bed frame)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: under the covers, under the blanket, under the bed
   - **Verb + prep**: get under the covers, hide under the bed, sleep under the blanket
   - **Adjective + prep**: warm under the covers
   - **Noun + prep**: position under the covers
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (Old English under)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English under → Proto-Germanic *under → unchanged meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Get under the covers before you catch a cold!
   - I found my book under the bed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Children often seek security by positioning themselves under the covers.
   - Items stored under the bed can accumulate dust and allergens.

1. **Preposition**: to (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating direction or destination toward something
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the receiver or target of an action
   - *(Temporal)* Until a particular time
   
   **Synonyms**: toward (spatial), until (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: "Go to bed" is the standard expression for the action of going to sleep; idiomatic and fixed
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Not used with "bedroom" in the same way ("go to the bedroom" not "go to bedroom")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: to bed, to sleep, to the bedroom
   - **Verb + prep**: go to bed, take to bed, send to bed
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready to bed (rare)
   - **Noun + prep**: time to bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: to (Old English tō)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English tō → directional/goal preposition → unchanged
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm going to bed early tonight.
   - Time to go to bed, kids!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants were instructed to go to bed at their usual time.
   - The recommendation is to go to bed and wake at consistent times.

1. **Preposition**: from (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating origin, source, or starting point
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating starting time
   
   **Synonyms**: out of (in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: to, toward, into
   
   **When to Use**: "Get out of bed" or "get from bed" (less common) indicates leaving bed; "from" shows origin point
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Get from bed" is less idiomatic than "get out of bed"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from bed, from the bedroom, from sleep
   - **Verb + prep**: rise from bed, emerge from bed, call from bed
   - **Adjective + prep**: fresh from bed
   - **Noun + prep**: movement from bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: from (Old English fram, from)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English fram → indicating source/origin → unchanged
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I just rolled from bed and came straight here.
   - Can you call me from bed? I don't want to get up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients should rise from bed gradually to prevent orthostatic hypotension.
   - The transition from bed to standing requires careful coordination.

1. **Preposition**: out of (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving from inside to outside of something; leaving an enclosed space
   
   **Synonyms**: from inside, away from
   
   **Antonyms**: into, inside
   
   **When to Use**: "Get out of bed" is the standard idiomatic expression for leaving bed; fixed collocation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: "Get off bed" is grammatically incorrect; must use "out of"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: out of bed, out of the bedroom
   - **Verb + prep**: get out of bed, climb out of bed, roll out of bed
   - **Adjective + prep**: unable to get out of bed
   - **Noun + prep**: difficulty getting out of bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: out (Old English ūt) + of (Old English of)
   
   **Etymology**: out + of → compound preposition (Middle English)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I can't get out of bed this morning!
   - Get out of bed right now!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients experiencing difficulty getting out of bed should request assistance.
   - The ability to get out of bed independently is a key mobility indicator.

1. **Preposition**: into (type: compound)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving from outside to inside something; entering
   - *(Abstract)* Transitioning to a state or condition
   
   **Synonyms**: in, inside, to the inside of
   
   **Antonyms**: out of, from, away from
   
   **When to Use**: "Get into bed" indicates the action of entering bed for sleeping; standard usage
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Less commonly "go into bed" (prefer "get into bed" or "go to bed")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: into bed, into the bedroom, into sleep
   - **Verb + prep**: get into bed, climb into bed, hop into bed, slip into bed
   - **Adjective + prep**: ready to get into bed
   - **Noun + prep**: movement into bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: in (Old English inn) + to (Old English tō)
   
   **Etymology**: in + to → compound indicating entry (Old English intō)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm so tired, I just want to get into bed.
   - She climbed into bed with a good book.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Subjects were instructed to get into bed at the designated time.
   - The process of getting into bed should be part of a consistent routine.

1. **Preposition**: for (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Purpose)* Intended to be used by or benefit someone/something
   - *(Duration)* Throughout the continuance of a time period
   - *(Benefit)* In favor of or to the advantage of
   
   **Synonyms**: during (duration), intended for (purpose)
   
   **Antonyms**: against (in some abstract contexts)
   
   **When to Use**: "Bed for two people," "sleep for eight hours," "ready for bed"; indicating purpose, duration, or preparation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context-dependent; ensure clarity about intended meaning
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: for sleeping, for comfort, for rest
   - **Verb + prep**: prepare for bed, ready for bed, shop for beds
   - **Adjective + prep**: suitable for bed, ready for bed
   - **Noun + prep**: bed for guests, time for bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: for (Old English for)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English for → indicating purpose/benefit → unchanged
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This bed is too small for two people.
   - I slept for ten hours straight!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The mattress is designed for optimal spinal support.
   - Participants slept for a duration of eight hours.

1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Accompaniment)* In the company of; alongside
   - *(Instrument)* Using or by means of
   - *(Characteristic)* Having as a feature or quality
   
   **Synonyms**: alongside (accompaniment), having (characteristic), using (instrument)
   
   **Antonyms**: without, lacking
   
   **When to Use**: "Bed with storage," "sleep with a partner," "bed with memory foam"; indicates features, accompaniment, or characteristics
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ensure clarity about relationship being described
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with pillows, with blankets, with storage
   - **Verb + prep**: sleep with, share a bed with, cover with blankets
   - **Adjective + prep**: equipped with, comfortable with
   - **Noun + prep**: bed with headboard, mattress with springs
   
   **Root Analysis**: with (Old English wið)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wið (against, toward) → evolved to indicate accompaniment
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I love beds with built-in storage.
   - Do you sleep with a lot of pillows?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The bed frame with integrated storage maximizes space efficiency.
   - Participants who sleep with partners reported different sleep patterns.

1. **Preposition**: at (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating specific location or position
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating specific point in time
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating state or activity
   
   **Synonyms**: in (some contexts), by (spatial proximity)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, distant from
   
   **When to Use**: "At bedtime" (specific time), "at the side of the bed" (specific location); precise point reference
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "at bed" for being in bed (use "in bed"); "at" requires more specific reference
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at bedtime, at the edge of the bed, at rest
   - **Verb + prep**: arrive at bedtime, sit at the side of bed
   - **Adjective + prep**: present at bedtime
   - **Noun + prep**: position at the bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: at (Old English æt)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English æt → indicating precise location/time → unchanged
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I always read at bedtime.
   - Sit at the edge of the bed and put your shoes on.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Medication should be administered at bedtime.
   - Positioning at the side of the bed facilitates mobility assistance.
