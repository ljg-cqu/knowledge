1. **Functional Expression**: "Would you mind making the bed?" (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A polite indirect request asking someone to tidy the bed; minimizes imposition
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Could you make the bed?, Would it be possible for you to make the bed?, Can you please make the bed?
   
   **Antonyms**: Make the bed (direct command), You need to make the bed (obligation statement)
   
   **When to Use**: When asking household members, guests, or children to make their bed; appropriate for maintaining politeness in domestic settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When urgent action is needed; overly polite for very close family members; too indirect for professional housekeeping instructions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Would you mind making up the bed? (more formal)
   - Mind making the bed? (more casual)
   - Could you make the bed, please? (slightly less formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Uses negative politeness strategy to minimize face threat; "would you mind" softens the request by framing it as checking willingness
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Not at all / Sure, no problem (accepting)
   - I'm a bit busy right now, can I do it later? (declining/delaying)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Would you mind making the bed before you leave?"
   - "Hey, would you mind making the bed? We have guests coming."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Would you mind making the bed before checkout?"
   - "I would appreciate it if you wouldn't mind making the bed each morning."

1. **Functional Expression**: "I was wondering if you could help me choose a mattress" (function: requesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An indirect, tentative request for assistance; softens request by expressing it as past contemplation
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Could you help me pick a mattress?, Would you be able to help me select a mattress?, Can you assist me with choosing a mattress?
   
   **Antonyms**: Help me choose a mattress (direct command), I need you to help me with a mattress (direct obligation)
   
   **When to Use**: When seeking advice from sales staff, friends with expertise, or family members; appropriate for respectful requests
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When confident and direct preference is more appropriate; may sound overly tentative in casual relationships
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I was wondering if you might help me... (even more tentative)
   - I'm wondering if you could help me... (present tense, slightly more direct)
   - Could you help me choose a mattress? (more direct alternative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Past progressive "was wondering" adds tentative politeness; "if you could" presents option rather than demand
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Of course! What are you looking for? (accepting enthusiastically)
   - I'd be happy to help (accepting formally)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I was wondering if you could help me choose a mattress—I'm totally lost."
   - "Hey, I was wondering if you could give me advice on mattresses?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I was wondering if you could assist me in selecting an appropriate mattress for my needs."
   - "I was wondering if you could provide guidance regarding mattress selection."

1. **Functional Expression**: "Why don't we look at mattresses this weekend?" (function: suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A gentle suggestion framed as a question; invites agreement while minimizing directiveness
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: How about looking at mattresses this weekend?, Shall we look at mattresses?, Let's go mattress shopping
   
   **Antonyms**: We're looking at mattresses this weekend (statement of fact), We should look at mattresses (obligation)
   
   **When to Use**: When proposing shared activities to partners, family, or roommates; maintaining collaborative decision-making
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When definite plans already exist; may sound uncommitted in contexts requiring firm decisions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Why don't we go mattress shopping? (more specific activity)
   - How about we check out some beds? (alternative phrasing)
   - We could look at mattresses... (even softer suggestion)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Negative question form softens suggestion; implies mutual benefit and shared decision
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - That sounds good / Sure, let's do it (agreeing)
   - I'm not sure I'm free, how about next weekend? (negotiating)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Why don't we look at mattresses this weekend? Ours is getting old."
   - "Why don't we hit some furniture stores and check out beds?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Why don't we schedule time to evaluate mattress options?"
   - "Perhaps we could examine bedroom furniture options this weekend?"

1. **Functional Expression**: "I think we need a new bed" (function: expressing opinion)
   **Meaning**: 
   - States personal assessment while hedging with "I think" to allow for disagreement
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: In my opinion, we need a new bed; It seems to me we need a new bed; I believe we should get a new bed
   
   **Antonyms**: We need a new bed (direct statement), We definitely need a new bed (emphatic certainty)
   
   **When to Use**: When discussing household purchases with partners or family; opening negotiation about expenses
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When stating facts rather than opinions; may sound tentative when certainty is appropriate
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I think it's time for a new bed (temporal framing)
   - I feel like we need a new bed (emotional emphasis)
   - Don't you think we need a new bed? (seeking agreement)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I think" hedges opinion, inviting discussion rather than imposing decision; negative politeness strategy
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I agree / Yeah, I think so too (agreeing)
   - Really? I think ours is fine (disagreeing)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I think we need a new bed—this one's falling apart."
   - "I think it's time to replace our bed, don't you?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I believe we need to invest in a new bed for health reasons."
   - "It is my assessment that a replacement bed is necessary."

1. **Functional Expression**: "Would you prefer a firm or soft mattress?" (function: clarifying/asking preferences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Polite question seeking information about preferences to aid decision-making
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (but polite)
   
   **Synonyms**: Do you like firm or soft mattresses?, What type of mattress do you prefer?, Which do you like better—firm or soft?
   
   **Antonyms**: You should get a firm mattress (directive statement)
   
   **When to Use**: Sales contexts, shopping with partners, consulting about preferences; appropriate when genuine choice exists
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When only one option available; may sound condescending if preferences are already known
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Do you prefer firm or soft? (less formal)
   - Which would you prefer—firm or soft? (slightly more formal)
   - What's your preference regarding firmness? (more formal)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Presents options as equally valid; shows respect for personal preferences; facilitates collaborative decision-making
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - I prefer firm / I'd say firm (stating preference)
   - I'm not sure, what do you recommend? (deferring to expertise)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Would you prefer a firm or soft mattress? I'm flexible either way."
   - "So, firm or soft—what's your thing?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Would you prefer a firm or soft mattress for optimal support?"
   - "May I inquire whether you have a preference for firmness level?"

1. **Functional Expression**: "I'd love to, but I need to go to bed" (function: refusing/declining)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Polite refusal that acknowledges invitation positively before declining; provides reason
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: I wish I could, but I have to sleep; I would, but I'm exhausted; Thanks, but I need to turn in
   
   **Antonyms**: No, I'm going to bed (direct refusal), Sure, I'll stay (accepting)
   
   **When to Use**: Declining social invitations due to tiredness; maintaining relationships while setting boundaries
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In urgent situations; may sound like weak excuse if overused
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - I'd really like to, but I should get to bed (more tentative)
   - I'd love to, but I'm exhausted (emphasizes condition)
   - Wish I could, but bedtime calls (more casual)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "I'd love to" provides positive face to requester; "but" signals refusal; "need to" justifies decision; classic positive politeness before refusal pattern
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - No problem, sleep well! (accepting graciously)
   - Oh come on, just a little longer! (gentle pressure)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "I'd love to keep hanging out, but I really need to go to bed."
   - "I'd love to, but I'm dead tired—gotta hit the hay."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "I would very much enjoy continuing, but I must retire for the evening."
   - "While I'd be delighted to stay, I'm afraid I need to get some rest."

1. **Functional Expression**: "Just to clarify, is this a king-size or queen-size bed?" (function: clarifying/checking understanding)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Polite request for clarification; signals potential misunderstanding and seeks correction
   - *Formality*: neutral to formal
   - *Directness*: direct (but polite)
   
   **Synonyms**: Can you confirm if this is king or queen?, Let me check—is this king or queen?, Which size is this—king or queen?
   
   **Antonyms**: This is king-size (asserting rather than checking)
   
   **When to Use**: Shopping for furniture, confirming orders, ensuring accuracy in important decisions
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When information is clearly labeled; may sound overly cautious in casual contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Just to be clear, is this king or queen? (slightly less formal)
   - Can I clarify—is this king-size? (seeking confirmation)
   - To make sure I understand, this is queen-size? (confirming comprehension)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Just to clarify" softens potential face threat of implying confusion; shows careful attention while allowing for correction
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - This is queen-size (providing clarification)
   - Good question—it's actually king-size (appreciating clarification request)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Just to clarify, is this bed gonna fit in our room?"
   - "Wait, just to clarify—you said queen, right?"
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "Just to clarify, is the listed bed size king or queen?"
   - "If I may clarify, the dimensions suggest a queen-size configuration?"

1. **Functional Expression**: "Sorry to interrupt, but I think the bed goes against that wall" (function: interrupting/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Politely interrupts ongoing activity to offer suggestion; apologizes for interruption while contributing
   - *Formality*: informal to neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Excuse me, but maybe the bed should go there; If I may interrupt, the bed might fit better there; Hold on—what about that wall?
   
   **Antonyms**: The bed goes there (direct statement without apology)
   
   **When to Use**: When others are working on furniture arrangement; contributing to collaborative tasks; maintaining politeness while asserting opinion
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When not actually interrupting; may be too apologetic with close family
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - Sorry to butt in, but... (more casual)
   - Excuse the interruption, but... (more formal)
   - If I may, I think the bed... (more tentative)
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: "Sorry to interrupt" acknowledges face threat of interruption; "I think" hedges suggestion to minimize imposition
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Oh yeah, good idea! (accepting suggestion)
   - Maybe, but I think it works here (politely disagreeing)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "Sorry to interrupt, but shouldn't the bed face the window?"
   - "Sorry guys, but I think the bed looks better over there."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "My apologies for interrupting, but I believe the bed placement would be optimal against that wall."
   - "Excuse me for interjecting, but perhaps the bed should be positioned differently."

1. **Functional Expression**: "It might be time to replace the mattress" (function: hedging/suggesting)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Tentative suggestion that softens potentially sensitive topic; uses "might" to allow disagreement
   - *Formality*: neutral
   - *Directness*: indirect
   
   **Synonyms**: Maybe we should replace the mattress; Perhaps it's time for a new mattress; I think we might need a new mattress
   
   **Antonyms**: We need to replace the mattress now (direct statement), The mattress must be replaced (obligation)
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing expenses with partners; raising potentially contentious domestic issues; opening negotiation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In urgent safety situations; may sound uncommitted when decisiveness is needed
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - It could be time to replace... (even more tentative)
   - Maybe it's time to replace... (slightly less hedged)
   - I suppose we should replace... (hedging with "suppose")
   
   **Pragmatic Notes**: Double hedging with "might be" creates space for disagreement; softens potentially expensive suggestion; negative politeness strategy
   
   **Response Patterns**: 
   - Yeah, you might be right (tentative agreement)
   - You think? It seems fine to me (questioning the suggestion)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - "It might be time to replace the mattress—I keep waking up with back pain."
   - "I think it might be time for a new bed, honestly."
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - "It may be appropriate to consider replacing the mattress at this juncture."
   - "It appears it might be time to invest in a replacement sleeping surface."
