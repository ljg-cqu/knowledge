1. **Expression**: make the bed (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To arrange and tidy the bedding (sheets, blankets, pillows) neatly after sleeping
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: tidy the bed, fix up the bed, arrange the bedding, straighten the bed
   
   **Antonyms**: unmake the bed, mess up the bed, leave the bed unmade
   
   **When to Use**: Daily household contexts, parenting instructions, hospitality training; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to constructing a bed frame (use "assemble" or "build" instead)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - make up the bed (more formal, hospitality context)
   - make your bed
   - get the bed made
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Make" conventionally pairs with "bed" for tidying; historical usage established this partnership over "do" or "fix"
   
   **Root Analysis**: make (Old English macian) + bed (Old English bedd)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't forget to make your bed before school!
   - I never make the bed on weekends.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Housekeeping staff are trained to make beds according to hotel standards.
   - Children should learn to make their beds as part of daily routine development.

1. **Expression**: hit the hay (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To go to bed; to go to sleep
   - *Literal meaning*: To strike hay (agricultural reference)
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: To go to sleep for the night
   - *Fixedness*: strong (highly fixed expression)
   
   **Synonyms**: go to bed, turn in, hit the sack, call it a night, crash
   
   **Antonyms**: wake up, get up, rise, stay up
   
   **When to Use**: Informal conversations among friends and family; casual social settings; adds colorful, friendly tone
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal or professional contexts; written business communication; with people unfamiliar with English idioms
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - time to hit the hay
   - gonna hit the hay
   - hitting the hay early tonight
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Originated from times when mattresses were filled with hay; "hitting" referred to flattening hay to create sleeping surface; first known use: early 20th century American English
   
   **Root Analysis**: hit (Old English hyttan "to meet with") + hay (Old English hīeg "cut grass")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm exhausted—time to hit the hay.
   - Let's hit the hay early tonight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *"Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts"*

1. **Expression**: toss and turn (type: idiom/binomial)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To move around restlessly in bed, unable to sleep comfortably
   - *Literal meaning*: To throw and rotate
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: To experience restless, uncomfortable sleep
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed word order: always "toss and turn," never "turn and toss")
   
   **Synonyms**: sleep restlessly, lie awake, have a restless night, thrash about
   
   **Antonyms**: sleep soundly, rest peacefully, sleep like a log
   
   **When to Use**: Describing poor sleep quality, discomfort in bed, anxiety-related sleep problems; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing normal sleeping movements; not suitable for clinical sleep disorder descriptions
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - tossing and turning all night
   - tossed and turned
   - kept tossing and turning
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Binomial expression with coordinated verbs; alliterative quality makes it memorable; describes two types of restless movement; established usage since 14th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: toss (Middle English tossen) + and + turn (Old English turnian from Latin)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tossed and turned all night worrying about the exam.
   - This mattress makes me toss and turn constantly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants reported tossing and turning throughout the night during the stress condition.
   - Sleep quality declined as subjects tossed and turned due to environmental factors.

1. **Expression**: sleep like a log (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To sleep very deeply and soundly without waking
   - *Literal meaning*: To sleep in the manner of a piece of wood
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: To sleep deeply and motionlessly
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed comparison)
   
   **Synonyms**: sleep soundly, sleep deeply, be out like a light, sleep like a baby
   
   **Antonyms**: toss and turn, sleep fitfully, lie awake, sleep lightly
   
   **When to Use**: Describing excellent sleep quality; casual to neutral registers; emphasizes deep, uninterrupted sleep
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Formal medical contexts; when precision about sleep stages is required
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - slept like a log
   - sleeping like a log
   - sleep like a baby (alternative with similar meaning)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Logs are immobile, heavy, and don't move—metaphor for motionless, deep sleep; origin uncertain but used since at least 18th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: sleep (Old English slǣpan) + like + log (Middle English logge)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I slept like a log on that new mattress.
   - After that long hike, I'll sleep like a log tonight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - *"Typically informal; avoid in formal contexts"*

1. **Expression**: under the covers (type: collocation/prepositional phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Positioned beneath the bed sheets and blankets; in bed with bedding over oneself
   - *Fixedness*: medium (preferred collocation)
   
   **Synonyms**: under the blankets, beneath the covers, in bed, under the sheets
   
   **Antonyms**: on top of the covers, above the blankets, outside the bed
   
   **When to Use**: Describing position in bed, reading in bed, sleeping arrangements; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specifically distinguishing sheets from blankets (be more precise)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get under the covers
   - hide under the covers
   - snuggle under the covers
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Covers" is collective term for sheets, blankets, and duvets; "under" indicates position beneath these layers; natural and conventional pairing
   
   **Root Analysis**: under (Old English) + the + covers (Old French covrir "to cover")
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I love snuggling under the covers with a good book.
   - Get under the covers before you get cold!
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Children often seek comfort by retreating under the covers.
   - The study examined whether subjects preferred sleeping under or on top of covers.

1. **Expression**: call it a night (type: idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To decide to stop an activity and go to bed; to end the evening
   - *Literal meaning*: To name/declare it nighttime
   - *Figurative/Idiomatic meaning*: To conclude activities for the evening
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed expression; "call it a day" for daytime equivalent)
   
   **Synonyms**: turn in, call it quits, wrap it up, head to bed
   
   **Antonyms**: keep going, pull an all-nighter, stay up, continue on
   
   **When to Use**: Social gatherings, work sessions, evening activities; suggests mutual agreement to end; neutral to informal register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Very formal settings; when you're alone (sounds odd without group context)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - let's call it a night
   - should we call it a night?
   - ready to call it a night
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: "Call it" means to declare or name; metaphorically declaring the evening concluded; parallels "call it a day" for daytime; mid-20th century usage
   
   **Root Analysis**: call (Old Norse kalla) + it + night (Old English niht)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - It's getting late—let's call it a night.
   - I'm exhausted, shall we call it a night?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Perhaps we should call it a night and reconvene tomorrow.
   - Given the late hour, the committee decided to call it a night.

1. **Expression**: king-size bed (type: compound noun/collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A bed of extra-large dimensions, typically 76 x 80 inches (193 x 203 cm)
   - *Fixedness*: strong (standard industry terminology)
   
   **Synonyms**: king bed, extra-large bed
   
   **Antonyms**: twin bed, single bed, queen-size bed
   
   **When to Use**: Furniture shopping, real estate listings, hotel descriptions, discussing bed sizes; standard terminology across registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When exact custom dimensions needed; regional variations may differ
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - California king (longer, narrower variation)
   - king-sized bed
   - standard king
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Commercial naming convention using royal hierarchy; "king" denotes largest standard size; marketing terminology established mid-20th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: king (Old English cyning) + size + bed (Old English bedd)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We upgraded to a king-size bed and love the extra space.
   - Do you have king-size sheets in stock?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The master suite accommodates a king-size bed with ample surrounding space.
   - Hotel rooms designated as suites feature king-size bedding.

1. **Expression**: beauty sleep (type: compound noun)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Sleep considered important for maintaining one's appearance and health; often humorous reference to needed rest
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed compound with idiomatic quality)
   
   **Synonyms**: restorative sleep, needed rest, good night's sleep
   
   **Antonyms**: sleep deprivation, sleepless night, insomnia
   
   **When to Use**: Light-hearted references to needing sleep; joking about appearance; emphasizing importance of rest; casual to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Serious medical discussions; may sound vain in certain contexts
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - get my beauty sleep
   - need my beauty sleep
   - beauty rest (alternative term)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Based on belief that sleep improves appearance; "beauty" modifies "sleep" to emphasize restorative, appearance-enhancing qualities; popularized early 20th century
   
   **Root Analysis**: beauty (Old French beauté) + sleep (Old English slǣp)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need my beauty sleep—goodnight!
   - Don't disturb me, I'm getting my beauty sleep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Research supports the concept of "beauty sleep" showing that sleep deprivation affects facial appearance.
   - The notion of beauty sleep has scientific basis in cellular restoration during sleep.
