1. **Verb**: sleep
   **Meaning**: 
   - To rest in a state of reduced consciousness, typically lying down with eyes closed and body relaxed
   - *(Sense 2)* To provide sleeping accommodation for (transitive: "the bed sleeps two")
   
   **Synonyms**: slumber, rest, doze, snooze, nap
   
   **Antonyms**: wake, wake up, stay awake, remain conscious
   
   **When to Use**: Universal term for the act of sleeping; suitable for all contexts from casual to medical
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing light rest while awake (use "rest" or "relax")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sleep soundly
   - sleep through the night
   - sleep like a log
   
   **Root Analysis**: sleep from Old English slǣpan
   
   **Etymology**: Old English slǣpan → Proto-Germanic slepan → unchanged meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I slept really well last night.
   - Can't wait to sleep in this new bed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants slept for eight hours during each trial period.
   - Adequate sleep is essential for cognitive restoration.

1. **Verb**: lie
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To be in or assume a horizontal or resting position on a surface
   - *(Sense 2)* To be positioned or situated
   
   **Synonyms**: recline, rest, stretch out, repose
   
   **Antonyms**: stand, sit, rise, get up
   
   **When to Use**: Describing body position in bed; note irregular past tense (lay, lain); suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't confuse with "lay" (transitive verb meaning "to place"); common learner error
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - lie down
   - lie in bed
   - lie flat
   
   **Root Analysis**: lie from Old English licgan
   
   **Etymology**: Old English licgan → Proto-Germanic legjan → irregular verb pattern preserved
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'm just going to lie down for a bit.
   - I lay in bed all morning yesterday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients should lie flat on their backs during the examination.
   - Subjects were instructed to lie in a supine position.

1. **Verb**: rest
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To relax and cease activity in order to recover strength or ease fatigue
   - (Transitive) To allow to be inactive or to support something in a position
   
   **Synonyms**: relax, repose, take a break, recuperate, unwind
   
   **Antonyms**: work, exert, strain, tire oneself
   
   **When to Use**: Describing relaxation with or without actual sleep; suitable for medical, casual, and formal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When specifically referring to sleep (though often used interchangeably in casual speech)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get some rest
   - rest your head
   - bed rest
   
   **Root Analysis**: rest from Old English rest, ræst
   
   **Etymology**: Old English rest → Proto-Germanic rasto → unchanged meaning
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to rest for a while on the bed.
   - Just rest your head on the pillow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients are advised to rest in bed following the procedure.
   - Adequate rest is crucial for tissue repair and recovery.

1. **Verb**: make
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) In "make the bed"—to arrange and tidy bedding neatly
   - *(General sense)* To create, construct, or arrange something
   
   **Synonyms**: tidy (the bed), arrange (bedding), fix up (casual), prepare
   
   **Antonyms**: unmake, mess up, disarrange
   
   **When to Use**: "Make the bed" is fixed collocation for tidying bedding; universal usage across all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Don't use "do the bed" (incorrect in English); "make" is the only correct verb
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - make the bed
   - make up the bed (more formal, hospitality)
   - make your bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: make from Old English macian
   
   **Etymology**: Old English macian → Proto-Germanic makōną → "to fit, construct"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't forget to make your bed before school!
   - I always make the bed right after I get up.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Hotel staff make beds according to standardized protocols.
   - Children who learn to make their beds develop organizational skills.

1. **Verb**: tuck
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To push or fold the edges of bedding under the mattress or cushions for neatness and security
   - *(Sense 2)* To cover someone snugly with bedding ("tuck someone in")
   
   **Synonyms**: fold in, secure, wrap snugly
   
   **Antonyms**: pull out, loosen, untuck
   
   **When to Use**: Bed-making contexts, parenting situations, hospitality training; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Ensure context makes bed-related meaning clear
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - tuck in the sheets
   - tuck someone into bed
   - tuck the blanket
   
   **Root Analysis**: tuck from Middle English tukken
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English tukken → possibly Germanic origin → "to pull sharply"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can you tuck the kids into bed tonight?
   - I like to tuck the sheets in really tight.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Properly tucked bedding prevents displacement during sleep.
   - Caregivers should tuck patients in securely for comfort and safety.

1. **Verb**: fluff
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To shake or pat something (pillows, comforters) to make it softer, fuller, or more evenly distributed
   
   **Synonyms**: plump, shake up, pat, puff up
   
   **Antonyms**: flatten, compress, pack down
   
   **When to Use**: Bed-making, housekeeping, preparing bedding; casual to neutral register
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for very formal contexts; use "adjust" or "arrange" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - fluff the pillows
   - fluff up the comforter
   - fluff the bedding
   
   **Root Analysis**: fluff (imitative origin, suggesting light, soft movement)
   
   **Etymology**: Possibly from Flemish fluff "loose, light material" → verb form (18c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Let me fluff your pillow for you.
   - I always fluff the comforter before making the bed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Housekeeping staff should fluff pillows as part of daily room preparation.
   - Regular fluffing maintains the loft and comfort of down bedding.

1. **Verb**: recline
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To lean or lie back in a relaxed position; to assume a position between sitting and lying
   
   **Synonyms**: lean back, lie back, lounge, stretch out
   
   **Antonyms**: sit upright, stand, straighten
   
   **When to Use**: Describing adjustable bed positions, relaxation, formal or neutral contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for very casual conversation; "lie down" is simpler alternative
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - recline on the bed
   - recline the bed
   - fully reclined position
   
   **Root Analysis**: recline from Latin re- (back) + clinare (to lean)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reclinare → Old French recliner → English recline (15c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I love to recline on this bed and watch TV.
   - The bed reclines so you can read comfortably.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Patients should recline at a 30-degree angle during treatment.
   - Adjustable beds allow users to recline to their preferred position.

1. **Verb**: toss
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive/Transitive) To move restlessly from side to side, especially in bed; to throw or flip something
   - *(In "toss and turn")* To move restlessly due to inability to sleep
   
   **Synonyms**: thrash, turn, flip, throw (different context)
   
   **Antonyms**: lie still, remain motionless, rest peacefully
   
   **When to Use**: Describing restless sleep or throwing objects onto bed; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context must clarify whether meaning is sleep-related or general throwing
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - toss and turn
   - toss the pillow
   - toss in bed
   
   **Root Analysis**: toss (Middle English tossen, origin uncertain)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English tossen → possibly Scandinavian origin → "to throw, fling"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I tossed and turned all night.
   - Just toss your bag on the bed.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Participants who tossed and turned frequently reported lower sleep quality.
   - Restless subjects tossed repeatedly during observation periods.

1. **Verb**: snuggle
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To settle into a warm, comfortable position; to nestle closely against someone or something
   
   **Synonyms**: cuddle, nestle, curl up, nuzzle
   
   **Antonyms**: withdraw, separate, move away
   
   **When to Use**: Describing cozy, comfortable positioning in bed; casual, affectionate contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too informal for professional or formal contexts; use "settle" or "position" instead
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - snuggle up in bed
   - snuggle under the covers
   - snuggle into the pillow
   
   **Root Analysis**: snuggle (frequentative form related to "snug")
   
   **Etymology**: Related to snug (Scandinavian origin) + -le (frequentative suffix) → 17c.
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I love to snuggle into bed with a good book.
   - The kids snuggled under the blankets.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Children who snuggle with comfort objects report feeling more secure.
   - The behavior of snuggling into bedding indicates thermal comfort-seeking.

1. **Verb**: doze
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To sleep lightly or briefly; to be in a light sleep or drowsy state
   
   **Synonyms**: nap, snooze, drowse, catnap
   
   **Antonyms**: be alert, stay awake, remain conscious
   
   **When to Use**: Describing light sleep in bed or elsewhere; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When describing deep, intentional sleep (use "sleep" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - doze off
   - doze in bed
   - doze peacefully
   
   **Root Analysis**: doze (origin uncertain, possibly Scandinavian)
   
   **Etymology**: Possibly Old Norse dūsa "to doze" → English doze (17c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I dozed off reading in bed.
   - He's just dozing, not really asleep.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Subjects frequently dozed during the afternoon rest period.
   - Light dozing differs physiologically from consolidated sleep.

1. **Verb**: retire
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Intransitive) To go to bed; to withdraw to a private or quiet place for rest (formal usage)
   - *(Sense 2)* To stop working permanently (different context)
   
   **Synonyms**: go to bed, turn in, withdraw for rest
   
   **Antonyms**: rise, wake, get up, emerge
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts, polite company, literary usage; very formal alternative to "go to bed"
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Too formal for casual everyday conversation; may sound pretentious with friends
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - retire for the evening
   - retire to bed
   - retire early
   
   **Root Analysis**: retire from Latin re- (back) + tirer (to draw)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin retirare → Old French retirer → English retire (16c., bed-related usage)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I think I'll retire early tonight. (sounds formal even in casual context)
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Guests may retire to their rooms at their convenience.
   - The speaker retired for the evening after the formal dinner.

1. **Verb**: change
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To replace bedding with fresh items; to remove and replace sheets, pillowcases, etc.
   
   **Synonyms**: replace, switch out, swap, refresh
   
   **Antonyms**: keep, maintain, leave unchanged
   
   **When to Use**: Household chores, hospitality contexts, hygiene discussions; suitable for all registers
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Context must make clear that bedding is being replaced
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - change the sheets
   - change the bedding
   - change the pillowcase
   
   **Root Analysis**: change from Latin cambiare (to exchange)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin cambiare → Old French changier → English change (13c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I need to change the sheets on the guest bed.
   - How often do you change your bedding?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Hotel policy requires staff to change bedding between guests.
   - Healthcare facilities should change bed linens daily for infection control.
