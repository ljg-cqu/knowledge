1. **Original Expression**: "Time to hit the hay"  
   **Context**: Informal expression for going to bed
   
   **Core Intent**: Announcing or suggesting that it's time to go to sleep
   
   **Casual**: "Time to hit the hay" / "I'm gonna crash"  
   *Characteristics*: Idiomatic, slangy, playful vocabulary  
   *Use when*: With friends, family, casual social settings, relaxed mood
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "I'm going to turn in" / "Time for bed"  
   *Characteristics*: Direct, clear, neutral tone, standard vocabulary  
   *Use when*: With colleagues, acquaintances, general social situations
   
   **Formal**: "I shall retire for the evening" / "I'm going to retire now"  
   *Characteristics*: Sophisticated vocabulary, complete sentence structure, polite distancing  
   *Use when*: Formal gatherings, professional events, with authority figures
   
   **Humorous**: "Time to commune with my pillow" / "Off to count sheep"  
   *Characteristics*: Playful imagery, creative expressions  
   *Use when*: Building rapport, light-hearted conversations, storytelling  
   *Avoid when*: Serious situations, professional contexts, with strangers
   
   **Lively**: "I'm off to dreamland!" / "Time to snuggle up!"  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic tone, exclamation marks, vivid imagery  
   *Use when*: Expressing anticipation, with children, energetic contexts
   
   **Empathetic**: "You must be exhausted—time to get some rest" / "Go ahead and get the sleep you need"  
   *Characteristics*: Acknowledges tiredness, caring tone, permissive language  
   *Use when*: Someone appears tired, supporting others, caring relationships
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Higher status requires formal ("I shall retire"), equal status allows casual ("hitting the hay")
   - *Social Distance*: Strangers require semi-formal, close relationships allow full casual
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Formal events require formal language, everyday routines allow casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures expect more formal evening farewells
   - *Medium*: Face-to-face allows more casual; written communication may be more formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using "hit the hay" in professional settings—sounds too casual and unprofessional
   - Using "retire" with peers—can sound pretentious or overly formal
   - Assuming all cultures use playful sleep expressions—some prefer straightforward language
   
   **Cultural Notes**: British English may use "turn in" more formally than American English; some Asian cultures prefer indirect expressions about leaving
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: At a business dinner at 10 PM → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("I should head to bed") to avoid disrupting professional tone
   - **Situation 2**: Family movie night with siblings → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("I'm gonna crash") reflects intimacy and comfort
   - **Situation 3**: Hosting foreign diplomats at late reception → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("I shall retire") shows respect and protocol

1. **Original Expression**: "Make the bed"  
   **Context**: Referring to tidying the bed after sleeping
   
   **Core Intent**: Instructing or stating the action of arranging bed linens neatly
   
   **Casual**: "Fix up the bed" / "Tidy up your bed"  
   *Characteristics*: Simple verbs, conversational phrasing  
   *Use when*: Daily home routines, speaking with children or family
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "Make the bed" / "Arrange the bedding"  
   *Characteristics*: Standard phrasal verb, neutral and clear  
   *Use when*: General instructions, household discussions, parenting
   
   **Formal**: "Prepare the bed" / "Arrange the bed linens"  
   *Characteristics*: More precise vocabulary, professional terminology  
   *Use when*: Hospitality training, professional housekeeping, instructional materials
   
   **Humorous**: "Wrestle the sheets into submission" / "Tame the bed beast"  
   *Characteristics*: Exaggerated combat imagery, playful metaphors  
   *Use when*: Making chores fun, talking with children, light-hearted contexts  
   *Avoid when*: Serious household management discussions, professional training
   
   **Lively**: "Let's get that bed looking sharp!" / "Spruce up the bed!"  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic action words, motivational tone  
   *Use when*: Motivating children, creating positive energy around chores
   
   **Empathetic**: "Would you mind making your bed when you get a chance?" / "I know you're tired, but let's make the bed together"  
   *Characteristics*: Polite requests, acknowledgment of effort/feelings  
   *Use when*: Asking tired individuals, teaching children, gentle reminders
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Parents to children use direct; roommates use polite requests
   - *Social Distance*: Family members can be direct; guests require polite phrasing
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Daily routine allows casual; professional hospitality requires formal
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures emphasize bed-making more than others
   - *Medium*: Face-to-face allows tone modulation; checklists require clarity
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using overly formal language at home—"arrange the bed linens" sounds stiff with family
   - Being too direct with guests—"make the bed" may sound demanding
   - Using playful language in professional hospitality—undermines competence
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Some cultures consider bed-making less important; others view it as essential daily discipline
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Parent to child before school → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("Make your bed, please") for clear instruction
   - **Situation 2**: Hotel housekeeping training → **Appropriate Variant**: Formal ("Prepare the bed according to protocol") for professional standards
   - **Situation 3**: Roommate to roommate → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("Could you make your bed when you get a chance?") to maintain harmony

1. **Original Expression**: "I need a new mattress"  
   **Context**: Expressing the need to purchase new bedding
   
   **Core Intent**: Stating that current sleeping surface is inadequate
   
   **Casual**: "I gotta get a new mattress" / "My mattress is done"  
   *Characteristics*: Contracted forms, simple vocabulary, definitive statements  
   *Use when*: Conversations with friends and family, informal shopping discussions
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "I need to buy a new mattress" / "It's time for a new mattress"  
   *Characteristics*: Complete sentences, standard vocabulary, neutral tone  
   *Use when*: Speaking with salespeople, general adult conversations, planning
   
   **Formal**: "I require a replacement mattress" / "I'm in the market for a new sleeping surface"  
   *Characteristics*: Sophisticated vocabulary, precise terminology  
   *Use when*: Professional correspondence, formal retail environments, written inquiries
   
   **Humorous**: "My mattress has gone to mattress heaven" / "I'm sleeping on a medieval torture device"  
   *Characteristics*: Exaggeration, creative metaphors, self-deprecating humor  
   *Use when*: Making conversation entertaining, explaining with humor  
   *Avoid when*: Actually in pain, speaking with medical professionals
   
   **Lively**: "I'm so excited to get a brand new mattress!" / "Mattress shopping time!"  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic tone, positive anticipation, exclamations  
   *Use when*: Shopping with companions, expressing anticipation
   
   **Empathetic**: "My back has been hurting—I really need to get a new mattress" / "I haven't been sleeping well because my mattress is worn out"  
   *Characteristics*: Explains impact, connects need to wellbeing, vulnerable sharing  
   *Use when*: Explaining to partner/family, justifying expense, seeking support
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: With salespeople use semi-formal; with doctor use empathetic to explain health impact
   - *Social Distance*: Friends allow casual; professional retail allows semi-formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Health concerns require empathetic; simple preference allows casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Some cultures discuss purchases openly; others more privately
   - *Medium*: In-store conversations allow casual; email inquiries require semi-formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Being too casual in formal retail—"My mattress is done" may not convey needs clearly
   - Overexplaining to friends—formal language about "replacement mattress" sounds stiff
   - Using humor when discussing pain—may prevent getting proper help
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Discussing personal furniture needs varies; some cultures consider it private financial information
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: In mattress store with salesperson → **Appropriate Variant**: Semi-formal ("I need to buy a new mattress") for clear commercial transaction
   - **Situation 2**: Complaining to friend about back pain → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("My back hurts so much—I really need a new mattress") to express genuine concern
   - **Situation 3**: Casual conversation at work → **Appropriate Variant**: Humorous ("I'm basically sleeping on rocks at this point") for entertainment value

1. **Original Expression**: "Can't get out of bed"  
   **Context**: Expressing difficulty or inability to wake up and leave bed
   
   **Core Intent**: Communicating extreme tiredness, illness, or reluctance to rise
   
   **Casual**: "Can't drag myself out of bed" / "Glued to my bed"  
   *Characteristics*: Figurative expressions, hyperbole, relatable imagery  
   *Use when*: Complaining to friends, social media posts, casual conversations
   
   **Semi-Formal**: "Having trouble getting up this morning" / "Finding it hard to get out of bed"  
   *Characteristics*: Neutral phrasing, understatement, standard vocabulary  
   *Use when*: Explaining lateness at work, general conversations, mild complaints
   
   **Formal**: "Experiencing difficulty rising" / "Unable to mobilize this morning"  
   *Characteristics*: Medical/technical vocabulary, objective description  
   *Use when*: Speaking with healthcare providers, formal absence explanations
   
   **Humorous**: "My bed has me in a death grip" / "Bed has Stockholm syndrome'd me"  
   *Characteristics*: Dramatic exaggeration, pop culture references, absurdist humor  
   *Use when*: Making light of situation, entertaining friends, social media  
   *Avoid when*: Actually ill, speaking with employers, serious contexts
   
   **Lively**: "I'm having the BEST sleep and never want to leave!" / "This bed is a cloud!"  
   *Characteristics*: Enthusiastic tone, positive spin, sensory imagery  
   *Use when*: Expressing enjoyment, vacation/hotel contexts, positive experiences
   
   **Empathetic**: "I'm completely exhausted and just can't get up" / "I'm not feeling well and need to stay in bed"  
   *Characteristics*: Honest about condition, vulnerable, explains genuine need  
   *Use when*: Calling in sick, asking for understanding, genuine health concerns
   
   **Register Selection Guide**:
   - *Power Distance*: Employers require empathetic (explain genuine need); friends allow casual
   - *Social Distance*: Close friends allow humor; supervisors require semi-formal
   - *Gravity of Situation*: Actual illness requires empathetic/formal; simple tiredness allows casual
   - *Cultural Context*: Work cultures vary on acceptable excuses for staying in bed
   - *Medium*: Text to friends allows casual; email to supervisor requires semi-formal
   
   **Common Mistakes**:
   - Using humor when genuinely ill—may make others not take it seriously
   - Being too casual with employers—"glued to bed" sounds unprofessional
   - Using medical language casually—"unable to mobilize" sounds overly dramatic with friends
   
   **Cultural Notes**: Different work cultures have varying expectations about illness disclosure and bed rest
   
   **Context Examples**:
   - **Situation 1**: Texting friend about weekend plans → **Appropriate Variant**: Casual ("Can't drag myself out of bed today") for relatable complaint
   - **Situation 2**: Calling supervisor about absence → **Appropriate Variant**: Empathetic ("I'm not feeling well and need to stay in bed") for legitimate sick leave
   - **Situation 3**: Sharing hotel experience on social media → **Appropriate Variant**: Lively ("This bed is AMAZING—never leaving!") for positive emphasis
