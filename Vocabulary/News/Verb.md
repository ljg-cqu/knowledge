1. **Verb**: report
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To give an account of news events or findings through journalism or official channels
   - *(Sense 2)* (Intransitive) To work as a journalist covering news stories
   
   **Synonyms**: cover, document, announce, relay, communicate
   
   **Antonyms**: conceal, suppress, withhold, censor
   
   **When to Use**: Journalism contexts, news production, formal information sharing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When expressing personal opinions (use "opine" or "comment" instead)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - report on an event
   - report the facts
   - report breaking news
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + port (carry)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reportare (carry back information) → Old French reporter → English report (14c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's been reporting from the field all week.
   - Did they report on the accident yet?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The journalist reported objectively on the diplomatic negotiations.
   - News organizations report events as they unfold to inform the public.

1. **Verb**: publish
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To prepare and issue a newspaper, magazine, book, or digital content for public distribution
   - *(Sense 2)* To make information publicly known through media channels
   
   **Synonyms**: print, issue, release, distribute, circulate
   
   **Antonyms**: suppress, withhold, retract, censor
   
   **When to Use**: Media production, content creation, formal announcement contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For unpublished drafts or private communications
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - publish a story
   - publish online
   - publish findings
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin publicus (public) + -ish (make/cause)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin publicare (make public) → Old French publier → English publish (14c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - When are they publishing your article?
   - The magazine publishes every month.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The newspaper published an investigative exposé on corporate fraud.
   - Academic journals publish peer-reviewed research after rigorous evaluation.

1. **Verb**: retract
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To withdraw or take back a published statement or article, typically due to error or inaccuracy
   - *(Sense 2)* To formally disavow previously published information
   
   **Synonyms**: withdraw, take back, revoke, rescind, recant
   
   **Antonyms**: affirm, confirm, republish, stand by
   
   **When to Use**: Error correction, journalistic accountability, legal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For routine updates or corrections (use "correct" or "update")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - retract a statement
   - retract an article
   - issue a retraction
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + tract (draw/pull)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin retrahere (draw back) → English retract (15c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The paper had to retract that story—it was totally wrong.
   - They retracted their claims after the investigation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The publication formally retracted the article following discovery of fabricated sources.
   - Journalistic integrity requires promptly retracting inaccurate information and acknowledging errors.

1. **Verb**: quote
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To repeat or reproduce exact words spoken or written by someone in a news story
   - *(Sense 2)* To cite someone as an authority or source of information
   
   **Synonyms**: cite, reference, reproduce, echo, attribute
   
   **Antonyms**: paraphrase, summarize, misquote, fabricate
   
   **When to Use**: Direct attribution, accurate representation of sources, formal reporting
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When paraphrasing or summarizing instead of using exact words
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - quote a source
   - quote directly
   - quote verbatim
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin quotare (number/mark sections)
   
   **Etymology**: Medieval Latin quotare (mark chapter numbers) → English quote (16c.) "cite exactly"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Don't quote me on this, but I heard...
   - The article quoted her saying she'd resign.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reporter quoted the official's statement accurately and in full context.
   - Journalists must quote sources precisely to maintain credibility and avoid misrepresentation.

1. **Verb**: censor
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive) To suppress, remove, or restrict news content or information deemed objectionable or sensitive
   - *(Sense 2)* To examine content and remove parts considered inappropriate
   
   **Synonyms**: suppress, redact, expurgate, restrict, ban
   
   **Antonyms**: publish, disclose, reveal, permit
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing restrictions on press freedom, content moderation, authoritarian contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For routine editorial decisions or quality control (use "edit")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - censor the press
   - censor content
   - government censorship
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin censor (Roman official who conducted census)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin censor (Roman magistrate) → English censor (examiner of public morals, 16c.) → modern usage (suppress content)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They tried to censor the article but it got leaked.
   - Some countries heavily censor news media.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Authoritarian regimes frequently censor independent journalism to control information.
   - Press freedom advocates oppose efforts to censor legitimate news reporting.

1. **Verb**: leak
   **Meaning**: 
   - (Transitive/Intransitive) To deliberately disclose confidential or restricted information to journalists or the public
   - *(Sense 2)* To become known through unofficial or unauthorized disclosure
   
   **Synonyms**: disclose, reveal, divulge, expose, release
   
   **Antonyms**: conceal, protect, secure, safeguard
   
   **When to Use**: Investigative journalism, unauthorized information disclosure, whistleblowing contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For official, authorized announcements (use "announce" or "release")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - leak documents
   - leak to the press
   - leaked information
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle Dutch leken (drip/allow to escape)
   
   **Etymology**: Middle Dutch leken → English leak (physical sense, 15c.) → figurative "information escape" (20c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Someone leaked the memo to reporters.
   - The story leaked before the official announcement.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Anonymous officials leaked classified documents revealing government surveillance programs.
   - Organizations investigate how sensitive information leaked to prevent future breaches.

   **Antonyms**: accept uncritically, ignore accuracy, fabricate

   - fact-check the article
   - rigorous fact-checking
   
   **Root Analysis**: fact (truth) + check (verify)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound fact + check → journalism term (mid-20c.) for verification process
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure you fact-check everything before publishing.
   - They fact-check every single claim in their stories.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Professional fact-checkers systematically verify claims using reliable sources and evidence.
   - Rigorous fact-checking processes distinguish credible journalism from misinformation.
