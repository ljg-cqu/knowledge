1. **Adjective**: breaking (gradable, usually attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Describing news that is just happening or has just happened, requiring immediate reporting
   - Used to characterize urgent, current events being reported in real-time
   
   **Synonyms**: urgent, immediate, developing, unfolding, current
   
   **Antonyms**: archived, old, historical, outdated
   
   **When to Use**: Real-time news reporting, urgent announcements, developing situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For planned announcements or past events
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - breaking news
   - breaking story
   - breaking development
   
   **Root Analysis**: break (interrupt) + -ing (ongoing action)
   
   **Etymology**: English break → breaking → journalism usage (late 20c.) "interrupting regular programming"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - There's breaking news about the election!
   - Turn on the TV—breaking story right now.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The network interrupted scheduled programming to deliver breaking news of the earthquake.
   - Breaking developments require immediate verification despite time pressure.

1. **Adjective**: unbiased (non-gradable)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Describing reporting that is fair, impartial, and free from prejudice or favoritism
   - Characterized by objectivity and balanced presentation of facts
   
   **Synonyms**: impartial, objective, neutral, fair, balanced
   
   **Antonyms**: biased, prejudiced, partial, slanted, one-sided
   
   **When to Use**: Describing ideal journalism standards, objective reporting, ethical practices
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For opinion pieces or editorial content (where perspective is expected)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - unbiased reporting
   - unbiased coverage
   - unbiased journalism
   
   **Root Analysis**: un- (not) + bias (prejudice) + -ed (having quality)
   
   **Etymology**: French biais (oblique) → English bias → unbiased (without slant, 17c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I wish we could find some unbiased news these days.
   - She's pretty unbiased in her reporting.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Unbiased journalism requires presenting multiple perspectives without favoring any particular viewpoint.
   - The publication maintains editorial standards ensuring unbiased coverage of controversial issues.

1. **Adjective**: sensational (gradable, usually attributive)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Describing news presentation designed to provoke excitement, shock, or interest, often at the expense of accuracy
   - Characterized by exaggeration or dramatic emphasis to attract attention
   
   **Synonyms**: dramatic, shocking, lurid, melodramatic, exaggerated
   
   **Antonyms**: understated, factual, sober, measured, restrained
   
   **When to Use**: Criticizing tabloid journalism, describing attention-grabbing headlines, discussing media ethics
   
   **When NOT to Use**: To compliment quality journalism (has negative connotation in news context)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - sensational headlines
   - sensational reporting
   - sensational journalism
   
   **Root Analysis**: sensation (strong feeling) + -al (characterized by)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin sensationem → English sensation → sensational (19c.) "causing sensation," journalism usage (critical)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's such a sensational headline—way overblown.
   - They always go for the sensational angle.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Sensational reporting undermines public trust in journalistic institutions.
   - The publication avoided sensational language in favor of factual, measured coverage.
