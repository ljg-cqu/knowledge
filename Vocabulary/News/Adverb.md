1. **Adverb**: allegedly (type: sentence adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Used to indicate that something is claimed to be true but not yet proven, often in reporting accusations or unverified claims
   - Functions to distance the reporter from the truth of the statement while reporting it
   
   **Synonyms**: purportedly, reportedly, supposedly, ostensibly, claimed to be
   
   **Antonyms**: certainly, definitely, provably, confirmedly
   
   **When to Use**: Reporting unproven allegations, legal cases, unverified claims; protects against libel
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For confirmed facts or when attribution is clear through other means
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - allegedly committed
   - allegedly involved
   - allegedly said
   
   **Root Analysis**: allege (claim without proof) + -ed + -ly (adverb marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin allegare (cite as evidence) → allege → allegedly (19c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - He allegedly stole company funds, but nothing's proven yet.
   - The suspect allegedly fled the scene.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The official allegedly accepted bribes from multiple contractors over a five-year period.
   - Journalists use "allegedly" to report accusations while maintaining legal protection and journalistic objectivity.

1. **Adverb**: reportedly (type: sentence adverb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - According to reports or claims, though not independently confirmed
   - Indicates information comes from secondary sources rather than direct observation
   
   **Synonyms**: according to reports, allegedly, supposedly, apparently, by accounts
   
   **Antonyms**: confirmedly, verifiably, certainly, observably
   
   **When to Use**: Citing information from other sources, unconfirmed reports, secondhand information
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For firsthand observation or thoroughly verified information
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reportedly said
   - reportedly planning
   - reportedly occurred
   
   **Root Analysis**: report (give account) + -ed + -ly (adverb marker)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reportare → report → reportedly (19c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - They're reportedly closing down next month.
   - She reportedly quit her job last week.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The company reportedly plans to announce layoffs next quarter.
   - Government officials are reportedly considering new regulatory measures to address the crisis.
