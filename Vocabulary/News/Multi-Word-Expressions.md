1. **Expression**: breaking news (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Urgent, developing news events being reported as they happen, requiring immediate coverage
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: developing story, urgent news, flash news, late-breaking news
   
   **Antonyms**: archived news, old news, historical coverage
   
   **When to Use**: Real-time reporting, urgent announcements, immediate coverage situations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Routine news, planned announcements, historical reporting; overuse dilutes urgency
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - breaking story
   - breaking development
   - late-breaking news
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Breaking" metaphorically represents interrupting normal programming/schedule; "news" requires immediate attention. This collocation has become the standard term for urgent, developing news since the rise of 24-hour news cycles.
   
   **Root Analysis**: break (interrupt) + news (new information)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Turn on the TV—there's breaking news about the earthquake!
   - They interrupted the show for breaking news.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The network provided breaking news coverage of the diplomatic crisis as events unfolded.
   - Breaking news alerts informed viewers of significant developments throughout the evening.

1. **Expression**: press conference (type: collocation)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A formal meeting where officials make announcements and answer questions from assembled journalists
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed partnership)
   
   **Synonyms**: news conference, media briefing, press briefing
   
   **Antonyms**: private meeting, one-on-one interview, off-the-record discussion
   
   **When to Use**: Official announcements, crisis communications, political/corporate statements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Informal media interactions, casual interviews
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - news conference
   - press briefing
   - media conference
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Press" (collective noun for journalists) naturally partners with "conference" (formal meeting). Term emerged in early 20th century as formal mechanism for officials to address multiple journalists simultaneously.
   
   **Root Analysis**: press (journalism collective) + conference (formal meeting)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The mayor's holding a press conference at noon.
   - Did you catch the press conference yesterday?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The chief executive addressed shareholder concerns during the quarterly press conference.
   - Press conferences provide structured opportunities for officials to communicate with media representatives simultaneously.

1. **Expression**: off the record (type: idiom/fixed phrase)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Literal meaning: Away from the written record
   - Figurative/Idiomatic meaning: Information shared confidentially that cannot be published or attributed
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed phrase)
   
   **Synonyms**: confidentially, not for attribution, privately, in confidence
   
   **Antonyms**: on the record, for attribution, publicly stated
   
   **When to Use**: Confidential source discussions, background briefings, establishing trust
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Must be established before information is shared; don't confuse with "on background" (different attribution rules)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - off-the-record (attributive form)
   - go off the record
   - speak off the record
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Metaphor from court reporting—information "off the record" means not officially documented. Originated in legal/governmental contexts (early 20c.), now standard journalism terminology for unpublishable information.
   
   **Root Analysis**: off (away from) + the + record (official documentation)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is totally off the record, okay?
   - She went off the record to explain what really happened.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The official spoke off the record to provide context for understanding the policy decision.
   - Information shared off the record cannot be published or attributed to the source under journalistic ethics.

1. **Expression**: fact-check (type: collocation, compound verb)
   **Meaning**: 
   - To systematically verify the accuracy of claims, statements, or information
   - *Fixedness*: strong (very fixed in journalism context)
   
   **Synonyms**: verify, validate, check accuracy, confirm facts, authenticate
   
   **Antonyms**: accept uncritically, ignore accuracy, fabricate
   
   **When to Use**: Verification processes, journalism standards, combating misinformation
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual verification without systematic methodology
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - fact-checking (gerund/present participle)
   - fact-checker (agent noun)
   - fact check (noun form)
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: "Fact" + "check" forms natural compound emphasizing systematic verification. Term gained prominence with rise of political fact-checking organizations (late 20c.) and misinformation concerns.
   
   **Root Analysis**: fact (verifiable truth) + check (verify/examine)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Make sure you fact-check everything before publishing.
   - They have a whole team that fact-checks stories.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Professional fact-checkers systematically verify claims using documented evidence and reliable sources.
   - Rigorous fact-checking processes distinguish credible journalism from misinformation and propaganda.

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Expression**: yellow journalism (type: collocation/idiom)
   **Meaning**: 
   - Sensationalized, exaggerated, or unethical journalism prioritizing sales over accuracy
   - *Fixedness*: strong (fixed historical term)
   
   **Synonyms**: tabloid journalism, sensationalism, gutter press, scandal sheets
   
   **Antonyms**: responsible journalism, ethical reporting, quality journalism
   
   **When to Use**: Criticizing sensational coverage, historical discussions, media ethics
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Neutral descriptions (use "tabloid" or "sensational"); considered somewhat dated
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - yellow press
   - tabloid journalism
   - sensational journalism
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For idioms*: Term originated from 1890s circulation wars between Hearst and Pulitzer newspapers featuring "The Yellow Kid" comic. Color printing and sensationalism became associated with unethical practices.
   
   **Root Analysis**: yellow (associated with sensationalism) + journalism
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That's just yellow journalism—completely exaggerated.
   - The tabloids engage in classic yellow journalism.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Yellow journalism practices in the late 19th century contributed to public demand for professional ethical standards.
   - Critics characterize the publication's coverage as yellow journalism that prioritizes sensation over accuracy.

1. **Expression**: embargo (type: noun, journalism-specific usage)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An agreement that information can be reported only after a specified time or date
   - *Fixedness*: strong (technical journalism term)
   
   **Synonyms**: publication restriction, release time, hold order
   
   **Antonyms**: immediate release, unrestricted publication
   
   **When to Use**: Controlled information releases, coordinated announcements, press relations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Informal timing requests (use specific language about timing)
   
   **Common Variations**: 
   - under embargo
   - lift the embargo
   - embargo until [time]
   
   **Why This Partnership**: 
   - *For collocations*: Term borrowed from trade contexts (Spanish embargo = prohibition). Journalism usage (20c.) describes temporary prohibition on publishing information provided in advance.
   
   **Root Analysis**: Spanish embargar (seize, prohibit)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This information's under embargo until Friday.
   - When does the embargo lift?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Information released under embargo allows journalists time for verification and analysis before publication.
   - The administration provided advance briefings under strict embargo to coordinate simultaneous coverage.
