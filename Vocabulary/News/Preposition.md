1. **Preposition**: according to (type: phrasal)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Used to cite sources or attribute information in news reporting
   - Indicates the origin of information or claims being reported
   
   **Synonyms**: as stated by, as reported by, per, based on, in the words of
   
   **Antonyms**: contrary to, despite claims by, regardless of
   
   **When to Use**: Attributing information to sources, citing reports, referencing authorities
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For direct quotes (use attribution with quote marks instead)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: according to sources, according to reports, according to officials
   - **Verb + prep**: stated according to, reported according to
   - **Noun + prep**: findings according to, data according to
   
   **Root Analysis**: ac- (to) + cord (heart/agreement) + -ing + to
   
   **Etymology**: Latin accordare (bring to agreement) → Old French acorder → according to (as stated by, 14c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - According to my sources, the deal's almost done.
   - According to the news, they're closing down.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - According to government officials, the policy will take effect next quarter.
   - The investigation proceeded according to established journalistic protocols.

1. **Preposition**: on (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning or about a particular subject or topic
   - *(Temporal)* At the time of; during
   
   **Synonyms**: about (abstract), concerning (abstract), regarding (abstract), at (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: off, away from
   
   **When to Use**: Describing news topics, report subjects, broadcast timing
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "about" would be more natural in formal writing
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: on the record, on background, on the scene
   - **Verb + prep**: report on, comment on, focus on
   - **Noun + prep**: story on, article on, report on
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English on (indicating contact/topic)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English on (unchanged through Middle English to present)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you read that article on climate change?
   - What's the latest news on the election?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The correspondent filed a comprehensive report on diplomatic negotiations.
   - The publication focuses primarily on investigative journalism and political analysis.

1. **Preposition**: from (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating the source, origin, or location of a report or reporter
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating information source or attribution
   
   **Synonyms**: originating from, coming from, out of, sourced from
   
   **Antonyms**: to, toward
   
   **When to Use**: Identifying report origins, source attribution, reporter locations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common learner error: omitting "from" after "according"
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: from the scene, from sources, from our correspondent
   - **Verb + prep**: report from, hear from, learn from
   - **Noun + prep**: reporter from, dispatch from, update from
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English from (indicating origin/source)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English from (away, forth) → indicating source/origin
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're getting reports from the scene right now.
   - I heard from a source that they're resigning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The network received confirmation from multiple independent sources.
   - Correspondents reporting from conflict zones face significant safety challenges.

1. **Preposition**: by (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating authorship or attribution in bylines
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating deadline or time limit
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating means or method
   
   **Synonyms**: written by (authorship), before (temporal), through (method)
   
   **Antonyms**: after (temporal), without (method)
   
   **When to Use**: Author attribution, deadlines, methods of reporting or distribution
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common error: confusion with "until" for ongoing deadlines
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: by [author name], by the deadline, by email
   - **Verb + prep**: written by, reported by, published by
   - **Noun + prep**: article by, story by, investigation by
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English be (near, beside)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English be → by (indicating agent/means/time limit)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This article's by Sarah Johnson—she's great.
   - Can you get this done by tomorrow?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation was conducted by a team of experienced reporters.
   - All submissions must be received by the editorial deadline to ensure publication.

1. **Preposition**: about (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning the subject or topic of news coverage
   - Indicates what news story discusses or covers
   
   **Synonyms**: regarding, concerning, on, relating to, with respect to
   
   **Antonyms**: N/A
   
   **When to Use**: Describing news topics, story subjects, coverage focus
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common error: using "about" instead of "on" after certain verbs (report on, not report about)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: about the incident, about the controversy, about allegations
   - **Verb + prep**: write about, talk about, ask about
   - **Noun + prep**: story about, news about, article about
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English onbutan (on the outside of)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English onbutan → aboute → about (concerning, 13c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you see that story about the mayor?
   - There's been a lot of news about the scandal lately.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The documentary presents a comprehensive account about systemic inequalities.
   - Public discourse about media bias has intensified in recent years.

   **Antonyms**: out, outside, beyond

   - **Noun + prep**: article in, story in, report in

   **Examples [Formal]**:

1. **Preposition**: at (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Indicating specific location where reporting occurs
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating specific time of events or broadcasts
   
   **Synonyms**: in (spatial), on (temporal, in some contexts)
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, distant from
   
   **When to Use**: Specific locations, precise times, event sites
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common error: "at" vs. "in" for publications (in The Times, not at The Times)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: at the scene, at the press conference, at 6 PM
   - **Verb + prep**: report at, arrive at, speak at
   - **Noun + prep**: reporter at, presence at, coverage at
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English æt (near, by, toward)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English æt (indicating specific point in space/time)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I'll be at the conference covering the announcement.
   - The broadcast starts at 7 o'clock.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Reporters at the summit provided real-time updates on the negotiations.
   - The press briefing will occur at the State Department at 2:00 PM Eastern Time.

1. **Preposition**: with (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating collaboration or joint authorship
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating source of information or interview subject
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating association or relationship
   
   **Synonyms**: alongside (collaboration), from (source), involving (association)
   
   **Antonyms**: without, against, separate from
   
   **When to Use**: Co-authors, interview subjects, partnerships, supporting evidence
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common error: overusing "with" for attribution (prefer specific attribution)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: with additional reporting by, with sources, with evidence
   - **Verb + prep**: speak with, collaborate with, interview with
   - **Noun + prep**: interview with, conversation with, partnership with
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English wið (against, toward, with)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English wið → meaning shifted from "against" to "accompanying"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I did the story with Tom from the politics desk.
   - She's interviewing with the CEO tomorrow.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation was conducted with additional reporting by regional correspondents.
   - In an exclusive interview with this publication, the official revealed new information.

1. **Preposition**: through (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Indicating the means or channel of information distribution
   - *(Temporal)* Indicating duration spanning a time period
   
   **Synonyms**: via (means), by means of (means), throughout (temporal)
   
   **Antonyms**: despite, notwithstanding
   
   **When to Use**: Distribution channels, communication methods, time spans
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common error: "through" vs. "throughout" (through = by means of; throughout = during entire period)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: through social media, through sources, through investigation
   - **Verb + prep**: obtained through, distributed through, learned through
   - **Noun + prep**: access through, information through, distribution through
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English thuruh (from one side to the other)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English thuruh → through (by means of, from beginning to end)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We got the information through a confidential source.
   - The news spread through social media instantly.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The documents were obtained through Freedom of Information Act requests.
   - Information dissemination through digital platforms has transformed journalism fundamentally.

1. **Preposition**: against (type: simple)
   **Meaning**:

   - *(Abstract)* Compared with or measured relative to
   
   **Synonyms**: contrary to, opposed to, versus, compared with
   
   **Antonyms**: for, supporting, in favor of
   
   **When to Use**: Describing opposition, contrasts, contradictions, allegations

   - **Verb + prep**: argue against, report against, testify against
   - **Noun + prep**: charges against, case against, action against

   **Examples [Casual]**:

   - The evidence is stacking up against him.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation uncovered substantial evidence against multiple officials.

1. **Preposition**: under (type: simple)

   **Synonyms**: subject to, governed by, during, beneath
   
   **Antonyms**: over, above, free from

   - **Verb + prep**: operate under, work under, publish under
   - **Noun + prep**: conditions under, circumstances under, pressure under

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The company's under investigation right now.
   - This story's under embargo until Friday.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The official is currently under investigation for ethics violations.

1. **Preposition**: off (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Not for attribution or publication (as in "off the record")
   - Indicating information shared confidentially
   
   **Synonyms**: confidentially, privately, not for attribution
   
   **Antonyms**: on (as in "on the record")
   
   **When to Use**: Describing confidential information, background briefings, non-attributable sources
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common error: confusing "off the record" with "on background" (different levels of attribution)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: off the record, off camera, off limits
   - **Verb + prep**: speak off the record, go off the record
   - **Adjective + prep**: off-the-record (when used attributively)
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English of (away, away from)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English of → off → journalism usage "off the record" (20c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is totally off the record, okay?
   - She went off the record to explain the real situation.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The official spoke off the record to provide context for the policy decision.
   - Information shared off the record cannot be published or attributed to the source.

1. **Preposition**: over (type: simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Temporal)* During a span of time or throughout a period
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning or about (especially disputes or controversies)
   
   **Synonyms**: during (temporal), throughout (temporal), about (abstract), concerning (abstract)
   
   **Antonyms**: under, within
   
   **When to Use**: Time periods of coverage, topics of controversy or debate
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Common error: "over" vs. "more than" with numbers (both acceptable, "more than" often preferred)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: over the years, over the weekend, controversy over
   - **Verb + prep**: report over, debate over, argue over
   - **Noun + prep**: coverage over, dispute over, controversy over
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English ofer (above, beyond)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English ofer → extended meanings including temporal and topical senses
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's covered this beat over the past decade.
   - There's a big controversy over those leaked emails.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The correspondent reported on the conflict over a five-year period.
   - Public debate over media ethics has intensified following recent scandals.

   - **Noun + prep**: statement to, response to, letter to
