1. **Noun**: headline (C, pl: headlines)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The title of a news article or story, typically displayed in large type at the top, designed to catch attention and summarize the main point
   - *(Sense 2)* The most important or attention-grabbing news item
   
   **Synonyms**: title, heading, banner, caption, lead
   
   **Antonyms**: footnote, afterthought, minor detail
   
   **When to Use**: Journalism, media discussions, news production contexts; suitable for all formality levels
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to entire article content rather than just the title
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - grab headlines
   - make headlines
   - headline news
   
   **Root Analysis**: head (top/main) + line (written text)
   
   **Etymology**: English compound head + line → journalism term (late 19th century) from printing practice of placing prominent type at head of column
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you see today's headline about the earthquake?
   - That story's gonna make headlines for sure.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The headline accurately captures the essence of the policy announcement.
   - Sensational headlines can undermine journalistic credibility.

1. **Noun**: reporter (C, pl: reporters)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person who investigates and writes or presents news stories for newspapers, magazines, television, or radio
   
   **Synonyms**: journalist, correspondent, newsman/newswoman, press member, newshound
   
   **Antonyms**: source, subject, interviewee
   
   **When to Use**: Professional journalism contexts, media industry discussions, newsroom settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to columnists, editors, or opinion writers (different roles)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - investigative reporter
   - field reporter
   - on-the-scene reporter
   
   **Root Analysis**: re- (back) + port (carry) + -er (agent)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin reportare (to carry back) → Old French reporter → English reporter (one who carries back information, 16c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - My sister works as a reporter for the local paper.
   - The reporter asked some tough questions at the press conference.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The reporter adhered to strict ethical guidelines throughout the investigation.
   - Experienced reporters verify facts through multiple independent sources.

1. **Noun**: coverage (U)
   **Meaning**: 
   - The extent to which a news organization reports on a particular event, topic, or issue
   - *(Sense 2)* The quality and comprehensiveness of news reporting
   
   **Synonyms**: reporting, treatment, analysis, account, broadcast
   
   **Antonyms**: silence, omission, blackout, censorship
   
   **When to Use**: Media analysis, journalism discussions, evaluating news reporting quality and scope
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to insurance protection or physical covering
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - media coverage
   - extensive coverage
   - live coverage
   
   **Root Analysis**: cover (spread over) + -age (action/result)
   
   **Etymology**: English cover → coverage (19c.), extended to journalism (20c.) meaning "extent of reporting"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The coverage of that scandal was everywhere yesterday.
   - Their coverage of local events is pretty good.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The network provided comprehensive coverage of the election results.
   - Balanced coverage requires presenting multiple perspectives on controversial issues.

1. **Noun**: source (C, pl: sources)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A person, document, or organization that provides information to journalists for news stories
   - *(Sense 2)* The origin of information or evidence used in reporting
   
   **Synonyms**: informant, contact, reference, authority, origin
   
   **Antonyms**: recipient, end user, consumer
   
   **When to Use**: Journalism contexts, discussing information verification, research methodology
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to water sources or energy sources (different contexts)
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - reliable source
   - anonymous source
   - confirm sources
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin surgere (to rise) → source (point of origin)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin surgere → Old French source (spring, origin) → English source (14c.), journalism usage (19c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Where'd you hear that? What's your source?
   - They won't reveal their sources for that story.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Journalists must protect confidential sources to maintain trust.
   - Multiple independent sources corroborated the allegations.

1. **Noun**: broadcast (C, pl: broadcasts)

   - A radio or television program or transmission

   **Synonyms**: transmission, program, show, airing, telecast
   
   **Antonyms**: recording, private communication, narrowcast
   
   **When to Use**: Radio/TV news contexts, media production, discussing aired content
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to print journalism or online-only content
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - live broadcast
   - news broadcast
   - broadcast journalism
   
   **Root Analysis**: broad (wide) + cast (throw/spread)
   
   **Etymology**: English broad + cast (agricultural term "scatter seeds widely") → broadcast (radio/TV usage, 1920s)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you catch the broadcast last night?
   - The game's broadcast starts at 7 PM.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The broadcast reached an estimated 12 million viewers.
   - Emergency broadcasts provide critical public safety information.

1. **Noun**: byline (C, pl: bylines)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A line in a newspaper or magazine article identifying the author by name
   - *(Sense 2)* Credit for authorship displayed above or below the article title
   
   **Synonyms**: author credit, writer attribution, signature line
   
   **Antonyms**: anonymous article, unsigned piece
   
   **When to Use**: Journalism contexts, discussing article authorship, newsroom practices
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to article headlines or content
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get a byline
   - byline story
   - share the byline
   
   **Root Analysis**: by (secondary position) + line (text)
   
   **Etymology**: English by + line → journalism term (early 20c.) indicating author identification line
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She finally got her first byline in a major paper!
   - Check the byline to see who wrote this.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The byline indicates collaborative authorship by three investigative reporters.
   - Earning a byline on significant stories enhances a journalist's professional reputation.

1. **Noun**: deadline (C, pl: deadlines)

   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I've got a crazy deadline tomorrow morning.

   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Journalists work under strict deadlines to ensure timely news delivery.
   - The publication maintains rigorous deadline standards for all content submissions.

1. **Noun**: scoop (C, pl: scoops)
   **Meaning**: 
   - An exclusive news story published or broadcast by one news organization before others
   - *(Sense 2)* A significant revelation or piece of information obtained ahead of competitors
   
   **Synonyms**: exclusive, breaking news, revelation, exposé, insider story
   
   **Antonyms**: old news, common knowledge, rehash
   
   **When to Use**: Competitive journalism contexts, discussing exclusive reporting achievements
   
   **When NOT to Use**: In formal academic writing (prefer "exclusive report")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - get a scoop
   - score a scoop
   - scoop the competition
   
   **Root Analysis**: Middle Dutch schope (shovel) → figurative "grab/obtain first"
   
   **Etymology**: Dutch schope (shovel) → English scoop → journalism usage (19c.) "obtain news first"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Did you hear? They got a huge scoop on the merger!
   - She's always chasing the next big scoop.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The investigation represented a significant journalistic scoop for the publication.
   - Competitive pressure to obtain scoops must be balanced against verification standards.

1. **Noun**: press conference (C, pl: press conferences)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A formal meeting where officials, politicians, celebrities, or organizations make announcements and answer questions from journalists
   
   **Synonyms**: news conference, media briefing, press briefing, media event
   
   **Antonyms**: private meeting, off-the-record discussion
   
   **When to Use**: Political, corporate, entertainment news contexts; formal announcement settings
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For informal interviews or one-on-one journalist interactions
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - hold a press conference
   - attend a press conference
   - emergency press conference
   
   **Root Analysis**: press (news media) + conference (formal meeting)
   
   **Etymology**: press (journalism collective noun, 18c.) + conference → compound term (early 20c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The mayor's having a press conference at noon.
   - Were you at the press conference yesterday?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The CEO addressed shareholder concerns during the quarterly press conference.
   - Press conferences provide structured opportunities for media inquiry and public accountability.

1. **Noun**: editorial (C, pl: editorials)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A newspaper or magazine article expressing the opinion of the editor or publisher on a specific issue
   - *(Sense 2)* Opinion content that represents the publication's institutional stance
   
   **Synonyms**: opinion piece, leader (British), commentary, position statement
   
   **Antonyms**: news report, objective article, factual coverage
   
   **When to Use**: Discussing opinion journalism, publication stances, media analysis
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to objective news reporting or feature articles
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - editorial board
   - editorial opinion
   - write an editorial
   
   **Root Analysis**: editor + -ial (relating to)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin edere (publish) → editor → editorial (adjective/noun, 18c.) "relating to editing/editor's opinion"
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The editorial in today's paper was pretty harsh.
   - I usually skip the editorials and go straight to news.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The editorial criticized the government's economic policy decisions.
   - Editorial independence is essential for maintaining journalistic integrity and public trust.

1. **Noun**: wire service (C, pl: wire services)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A news agency that provides news articles and photographs to newspapers, broadcasters, and online media outlets
   
   **Synonyms**: news agency, press agency, news service, syndicate
   
   **Antonyms**: original reporting, in-house journalism
   
   **When to Use**: Media industry discussions, discussing news distribution systems, journalism infrastructure
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to individual news organizations or reporters
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - wire service report
   - subscribe to wire services
   - wire service photo
   
   **Root Analysis**: wire (telegraph) + service (provision)
   
   **Etymology**: telegraph wire → "wire service" (late 19c.) news distribution via telegraph, later extended to modern methods
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - That story came from a wire service, not our reporters.
   - Most papers rely on wire services for international news.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Major wire services like AP and Reuters provide global news coverage to thousands of outlets.
   - Wire service agreements enable smaller publications to access international reporting resources.

1. **Noun**: article (C, pl: articles)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A piece of writing on a specific topic published in a newspaper, magazine, or online platform
   - *(Sense 2)* The main unit of content in news publications
   
   **Synonyms**: story, piece, report, feature, item
   
   **Antonyms**: advertisement, sponsored content (in journalism context)
   
   **When to Use**: General news and publishing contexts; suitable for all formality levels
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to broadcast news segments (use "story" or "segment")
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - news article
   - feature article
   - read an article
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin articulus (small joint/division)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin articulus (small connecting part) → Old French article → English article (written composition, 14c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I read a great article about climate change yesterday.
   - Did you write that article on the city council meeting?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The article presents a comprehensive analysis of economic trends.
   - Academic articles undergo rigorous peer review before publication.

1. **Noun**: column (C, pl: columns)
   **Meaning**: 
   - A regular feature in a newspaper or magazine written by the same person, expressing opinions or commentary
   - *(Sense 2)* A recurring section dedicated to specific topics or perspectives
   
   **Synonyms**: opinion piece, commentary, feature, regular article, op-ed
   
   **Antonyms**: news report, breaking news, factual coverage
   
   **When to Use**: Opinion journalism, regular features, personal perspectives in media
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to objective news reporting
   
   **Common Phrases**: 
   - write a column
   - advice column
   - opinion column
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin columna (pillar) → typographical column → journalism meaning
   
   **Etymology**: Latin columna (supporting pillar) → English column (printed pillar of text, 15c.) → journalism usage (18c.)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Her column on parenting is hilarious!
   - Do you read that sports column regularly?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The columnist's weekly column addresses contemporary political issues.
   - Opinion columns provide diverse perspectives beyond objective news reporting.
