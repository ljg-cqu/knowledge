# Preposition Vocabulary - LinerGDR

1. **Preposition**: across (simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* From one side to the other of something
   - *(Abstract)* Throughout the extent of; in various parts of
   
   **Synonyms**: through, throughout, over
   
   **Antonyms**: within, inside
   
   **When to Use**: Spatial and distributive contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to opposition (use "against")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: across projects, across teams, across domains
   - **Verb + prep**: spread across, distribute across, apply across
   - **Adjective + prep**: consistent across, uniform across
   
   **Root Analysis**: a (on) + cross
   
   **Etymology**: Middle English compound
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - The news spread across the entire office.
   - We need consistency across all departments.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Inconsistent code quality persists across projects despite comprehensive guidelines.
   - The analysis covers adoption patterns across multiple industries.

1. **Preposition**: despite (compound/phrasal, functions like preposition)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Without being affected by; in spite of
   
   **Synonyms**: in spite of, notwithstanding, regardless of
   
   **Antonyms**: because of, due to, owing to
   
   **When to Use**: Contrastive contexts showing unexpected outcomes
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Never use "despite of" (common learner error)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: despite challenges, despite efforts, despite benefits
   - **Common errors to avoid**: "despite of" is incorrect (remove "of")
   
   **Root Analysis**: des (down) + spite
   
   **Etymology**: Old French → despit (scorn)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We went hiking despite the rain.
   - She succeeded despite all the obstacles.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Performance comparable to C occurs despite memory safety overhead.
   - Adoption grows despite the steep learning curve barrier.

1. **Preposition**: within (simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Inside the limits or bounds of
   - *(Temporal)* Before the end of a period; not later than
   
   **Synonyms**: inside, in, during
   
   **Antonyms**: beyond, outside, exceeding
   
   **When to Use**: Spatial and temporal boundary contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When referring to "among" a group (use "among")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: within limits, within scope, within timeframe
   - **Verb + prep**: stay within, operate within, remain within
   - **Noun + prep**: boundaries within, constraints within
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + in
   
   **Etymology**: Old English compound
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We need to finish this within two weeks.
   - Stay within the budget limits.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Developers achieve productivity parity within two to four months.
   - The analysis remains within the scope of publicly available documentation.

1. **Preposition**: through (simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Moving in one side and out the other; across
   - *(Abstract)* By means of; via
   
   **Synonyms**: via, by means of, throughout, across
   
   **Antonyms**: around, avoiding
   
   **When to Use**: Method, process, or spatial traversal contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "finished" is meant (use "finished with")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: through analysis, through implementation, through testing
   - **Verb + prep**: go through, work through, achieve through
   - **Noun + prep**: path through, way through
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English thurh
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → thurh (from one end to the other)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I learned this through trial and error.
   - We need to work through these problems.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Memory safety is achieved through compile-time ownership verification.
   - The document analyzes challenges through 5-Why methodology.

1. **Preposition**: without (simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* In the absence of; lacking
   - Not having or using
   
   **Synonyms**: lacking, minus, absent, -less
   
   **Antonyms**: with, including, possessing
   
   **When to Use**: Negation and absence contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: As conjunction (archaic; use "unless")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: without overhead, without runtime, without garbage collection
   - **Verb + prep**: operate without, function without, achieve without
   - **Adjective + prep**: possible without, feasible without
   
   **Root Analysis**: with + out
   
   **Etymology**: Old English compound
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Can we do this without hiring more people?
   - I can't work without coffee in the morning.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Rust achieves memory safety without garbage collection overhead.
   - Zero-cost abstractions provide high-level features without runtime penalties.

1. **Preposition**: beyond (simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* At or to the further side of
   - *(Abstract)* Outside the scope or limits of
   
   **Synonyms**: past, exceeding, outside, above
   
   **Antonyms**: within, inside, below
   
   **When to Use**: Exceeding boundaries or limitations
   
   **When NOT to Use**: When "behind" (spatial back) is meant
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: beyond scope, beyond capabilities, beyond expectations
   - **Verb + prep**: go beyond, extend beyond, move beyond
   
   **Root Analysis**: be (by) + yond (far)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English compound
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - This is beyond my expertise - I need help.
   - The project went beyond our expectations.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The analysis extends beyond surface symptoms to root causes.
   - Requirements exceed capabilities beyond current ecosystem maturity.

1. **Preposition**: regarding (compound, gerund-based)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* Concerning; about; with respect to
   
   **Synonyms**: concerning, about, with regard to, as to
   
   **Antonyms**: (context-dependent)
   
   **When to Use**: Formal contexts introducing topics
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual contexts (use "about")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: regarding implementation, regarding adoption, regarding performance
   - **Verb + prep**: questions regarding, concerns regarding
   
   **Root Analysis**: regard + -ing
   
   **Etymology**: French → regarder (look at)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - I have questions regarding the new policy.
   - What's the decision regarding the project?
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Developers express concerns regarding compilation performance.
   - Guidelines provide recommendations regarding code organization and style.

1. **Preposition**: via (simple, borrowed)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Abstract)* By way of; through the medium of
   - Traveling through or stopping at
   
   **Synonyms**: through, by means of, using, by way of
   
   **Antonyms**: directly, without intermediary
   
   **When to Use**: Method or route contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Casual speech (can sound formal)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: via FFI, via runtime, via compiler
   - **Verb + prep**: access via, achieve via, integrate via
   
   **Root Analysis**: Latin via (way, road)
   
   **Etymology**: Latin → via (road, way)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - Send it to me via email.
   - We're traveling to Paris via London.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Integration occurs via Foreign Function Interface boundaries.
   - Safety is enforced via compile-time borrow checking.

1. **Preposition**: toward/towards (simple, variant spellings)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* In the direction of
   - *(Abstract)* Contributing to; with the aim of
   
   **Synonyms**: to, in the direction of, for
   
   **Antonyms**: away from, against
   
   **When to Use**: Direction and purpose contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: Both spellings are acceptable (towards is British, toward is American)
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: toward goal, toward solution, toward understanding
   - **Verb + prep**: work toward, move toward, progress toward
   
   **Root Analysis**: Old English to + -ward (direction)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English compound
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - We're working toward a solution.
   - She walked toward the exit.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - The industry trends toward memory-safe languages.
   - Efforts focus toward improving compilation performance.

1. **Preposition**: among (simple)
   **Meaning**: 
   - *(Spatial)* Surrounded by; in the company of
   - *(Abstract)* Being one of or part of a group
   
   **Synonyms**: amongst, amid, between (for two items)
   
   **Antonyms**: apart from, separate from, outside
   
   **When to Use**: Group membership or distribution contexts
   
   **When NOT to Use**: For only two items (use "between")
   
   **Common Patterns**: 
   - **Prepositional phrases**: among developers, among options, among challenges
   - **Verb + prep**: distribute among, divide among, choose among
   - **Common errors to avoid**: Use "among" for 3+ items, "between" for 2
   
   **Root Analysis**: on + mong (crowd)
   
   **Etymology**: Old English → on gemang (in the crowd)
   
   **Examples [Casual]**: 
   - She's popular among her classmates.
   - Divide the work among all team members.
   
   **Examples [Formal]**: 
   - Rust ranks highly among most-loved programming languages.
   - Ownership concepts are among the most challenging for new developers.
