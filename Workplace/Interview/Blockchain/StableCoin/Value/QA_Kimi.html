Executive Summary: The Stablecoin Value Proposition

Core Function: Bridging Traditional Finance and Digital Assets

Stablecoins have emerged as a foundational technology in the digital asset ecosystem, designed to address the inherent volatility of cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and Ethereum. Their primary function is to provide a stable store of value and a reliable medium of exchange by pegging their market value to a stable asset, most commonly a fiat currency such as the U.S. dollar or euro [1].

Instant Settlement

24/7 global transactions without banking delays

Global Reach

Borderless payments and remittances

By combining the price stability of traditional currencies with the technological advantages of blockchain—such as instant settlement, global reach, and programmability—stablecoins serve as a critical bridge between the conventional financial system and the burgeoning world of decentralized finance (DeFi) [1]. This bridging function is not merely a technical convenience; it is a transformative capability that unlocks a wide array of use cases, from everyday payments and cross-border remittances to sophisticated financial instruments in DeFi.

The ability to transact with a digital dollar on a global, 24/7 network without the delays and high costs of traditional banking rails represents a paradigm shift in how value can be moved and managed [2]. The continued evolution and integration into mainstream finance signal a fundamental rethinking of how money itself can be designed and operated in the digital age.

Institutional Momentum

A survey conducted by Fireblocks in March 2025 revealed that 90% of financial institutions are actively integrating stablecoins into their operations [3]. This adoption is not merely experimental; 49% of these institutions are already using stablecoins for live payments, while another 23% are conducting pilot tests.

Traditional banks, in particular, are prioritizing cross-border payments as a key use case, with 58% utilizing stablecoins specifically for international transfers [3].

Major Strategic Moves

  • Stripe's $1.1 billion acquisition of Bridge - Largest Web3 acquisition to date [3]
  • Visa and Worldpay partnerships - Enabling card-based spending and merchant payouts

This institutional embrace is a clear indicator that stablecoins are transitioning from a disruptive technology to a mainstream financial instrument. The sheer scale of this market, dominated by two major players—Tether (USDT) and Circle (USDC)—underscores the immense demand for stable, dollar-denominated digital assets that can operate on blockchain networks.

Primary Use Cases and Value Drivers

Payments and Settlement

Stablecoins are rapidly emerging as a powerful alternative to traditional payment rails, offering significant advantages in speed, cost, and accessibility for both consumer and business transactions. The core value proposition lies in the ability to leverage blockchain technology to settle transactions almost instantaneously, 24/7, without relying on the complex and often slow network of correspondent banks [2].

The Role of APIs in Payment Infrastructure

Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) are the critical enablers that are transforming stablecoins from a niche technology into a scalable and accessible payment infrastructure. By providing a standardized way for different software systems to communicate, APIs allow businesses to integrate stablecoin payment capabilities into their existing platforms without needing to build and maintain complex blockchain infrastructure from scratch [4].

Cross-border remittances represent one of the most compelling and socially impactful use cases for stablecoins. The traditional remittance market is characterized by high fees, slow transaction times, and a lack of transparency, which disproportionately affects migrant workers and their families in developing countries. Stablecoins offer a direct solution to these pain points by enabling near-instant, low-cost, and transparent transfers of value across borders [2] [5].

Decentralized Finance (DeFi)

Stablecoins are the lifeblood of the Decentralized Finance (DeFi) ecosystem, serving as a foundational building block for a wide array of innovative financial protocols and applications. The inherent volatility of most cryptocurrencies makes them unsuitable for many financial applications, which stablecoins solve by providing a stable unit of account and reliable store of value [6].

Taxonomy of Stablecoin Designs

Stablecoins can be broadly categorized into three primary types: fiat-collateralized, crypto-collateralized, and algorithmic. Each category represents a different approach to achieving price stability, balancing trade-offs between decentralization, capital efficiency, and reliance on external systems.

Feature Fiat-Collateralized Crypto-Collateralized Algorithmic
Backing Asset Fiat currency (e.g., USD, EUR) held in off-chain reserves [7] Other cryptocurrencies (e.g., ETH, BTC) held in on-chain smart contracts [8] No external collateral; relies on algorithms and economic incentives [7]
Collateralization 1:1 backing with fiat reserves Over-collateralized (e.g., 150% or higher) to absorb crypto volatility [9] Uncollateralized or partially collateralized
Centralization High. Relies on a central issuer to manage reserves [10] Low. Managed by decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs) and smart contracts [6] Low. Aim for full decentralization through autonomous algorithms
Primary Risk Counterparty risk: issuer default, reserve mismanagement, regulatory action [10] Smart contract risk: code vulnerabilities, oracle manipulation, liquidation cascades [11] Reflexivity risk: "death spirals" due to loss of market confidence [12]
Key Examples USDC (Circle), USDT (Tether), BUSD (Binance/Paxos) [13] DAI (MakerDAO), LUSD (Liquity) [8] TerraUSD (UST) - Collapsed, FRAX (Partially collateralized) [14]

Fiat-Collateralized Stablecoins

Mechanism

For every stablecoin token issued, an equivalent amount of fiat currency is held in reserve by the issuing entity [7]. This 1:1 backing ensures that the stablecoin maintains its peg to the underlying fiat currency.

  • • Cash and cash-equivalent assets
  • • Short-term government securities
  • • Regulated financial institutions

Key Players

Centralization and Counterparty Risk

Despite their stability and widespread use, fiat-collateralized stablecoins are inherently centralized, which introduces significant counterparty risk. Users must trust the issuing company to honestly manage reserves and maintain the 1:1 backing [10].

Crypto-Collateralized Stablecoins

Mechanism: Over-Collateralization

Unlike fiat-collateralized stablecoins, these are backed by other cryptocurrencies like Ethereum (ETH) or Bitcoin (BTC) [8]. Because the collateral itself is volatile, these stablecoins must be over-collateralized to maintain their peg.

150%+
Collateralization Ratio
e.g., $150 worth of ETH to mint $100 of stablecoin

Case Study: DAI and MakerDAO

DAI is the preeminent example of a crypto-collateralized stablecoin, issued by the MakerDAO protocol. Users generate DAI by depositing approved collateral assets into Collateralized Debt Positions (CDPs) [8]. The protocol uses oracles to track collateral prices and automatically liquidates positions if values fall below certain thresholds.

Algorithmic and Seigniorage-Style Stablecoins

Algorithmic stablecoins represent the most experimental and decentralized end of the stablecoin spectrum. Unlike collateralized stablecoins, they rely on algorithms and smart contracts to automatically manage supply in response to changes in demand [7].

Case Study: Terra/LUNA Collapse

The collapse of TerraUSD (UST) and LUNA in May 2022 serves as a stark warning about algorithmic stablecoin risks. Over $40 billion in market capitalization was wiped out in a "death spiral" triggered by loss of market confidence [12].

Future Innovations

Future algorithmic stablecoin development is shifting towards more robust models integrating artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning to enhance stability and responsiveness [15].

Global Regulatory Landscape and Compliance

The regulatory landscape for stablecoins is a rapidly evolving tapestry of international standards, regional frameworks, and national laws. As stablecoins transition from niche crypto-assets to potential pillars of the global financial system, regulators worldwide are grappling with how to balance fostering innovation with mitigating risks.

Anti-Money Laundering and Countering Financing of Terrorism

The FATF "Travel Rule"

A cornerstone of the global AML/CFT framework, the Travel Rule mandates that Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) must collect and share information about the originator and beneficiary of virtual asset transfers exceeding USD 1,000 [17].

  • • Names and account numbers of sender and recipient
  • • Real-time data transmission between VASPs
  • • Enhanced due diligence requirements

RegTech Solutions

A new industry of Regulatory Technology (RegTech) has emerged, offering sophisticated tools for on-chain monitoring and compliance [18].

Blockchain Analytics
  • • Transaction pattern analysis

Compliance Scoring
  • • AML compliance scores

The future of stablecoins is likely to be shaped by the convergence of technological innovation, regulatory development, and evolving market demands.
