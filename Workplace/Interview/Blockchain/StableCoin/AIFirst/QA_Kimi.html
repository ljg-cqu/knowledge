Stablecoin Fundamentals: A Multi-Stakeholder Perspective

Defining Stablecoins

A stablecoin is a cryptocurrency designed to maintain a stable value relative to a specific asset, typically a fiat currency like the U.S. dollar. The primary value proposition is combining the best features of traditional fiat currencies with the advantages of blockchain technology [413] .

Core Value Proposition

  • Reliable medium of exchange
  • Stable unit of account
  • Secure store of value
  • Fast, low-cost transactions

The Three Primary Models

Fiat-Backed (e.g., USDC, USDT)

Collateralized 1:1 by fiat currency reserves. Simple and stable but centralized [83] .

High Stability Centralized

Crypto-Backed (e.g., DAI)

Over-collateralized with cryptocurrencies. More decentralized but capital-inefficient [83] .

Decentralized Complex

Algorithmic (e.g., UST)

No collateral, algorithmically managed supply. Highly experimental with significant risks [144] .

High Risk Capital Efficient

Case Study: The Tether Settlement

The 2021 settlement between Tether and the New York Attorney General was a watershed moment. The investigation concluded that Tether had made false statements about USDT backing and engaged in a cover-up of $850 million in lost funds [127] .

Key Outcomes

  • • $18.5 million fine
  • • Banned from operating in New York
  • • Quarterly reserve reporting requirement
  • • Influenced GENIUS Act transparency mandates

Case Study: The TerraUSD (UST) Collapse

The May 2022 collapse of algorithmic stablecoin TerraUSD (UST) was a seismic event that wiped out $40-42 billion in market value within a week, demonstrating the systemic risks of under-collateralized models [119] [128] .

Regulatory Impact

  • • Prompted global calls for strict regulation
  • • Ended debate on algorithmic stablecoins
  • • Cited by Treasury Secretary in Congress
  • • Solidified full backing consensus
