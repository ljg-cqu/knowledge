   A: Phased transition pattern where new routines slowly replace old patterns through structured rollout with checkpoints—typically 2-week phases. Q: Okay. A: Right. Lets you learn and tweak before going all in.
   
   Q: How's it work?
   A: Break down big behavior changes into testable chunks. Q: Got it. A: Phase 1-2: nail down foundational habit with minimal complexity, like morning wake time consistency. Phase 3-4: add next layer once foundation's solid, like commute optimization. Q: Makes sense. A: Phase 5-6: throw in advanced stuff, like meal prep logistics. Phase 7-8: fine-tune based on performance data. Q: Right. A: Yeah. Each phase has clear success criteria. Failure triggers rollback or tweaking rather than ditching the whole thing. Catches incompatibilities early—family schedule conflicts, workflow mismatches.

8. Q: What's the decision-matrix framework?
   A: Evaluation tool using alternatives (rows) times criteria (columns) with weights and scores (1-10 scale) to figure out weighted totals. Q: Okay. A: Right. Gives you a numbers-based comparison of routine optimization options—commute modes, meal strategies, recovery protocols.
   
   Q: How's it work?
   A: Five steps. Q: Got it. A: Criteria ID: pull out decision-relevant dimensions from context like time efficiency, cost, energy preservation, schedule consistency, weather resilience. Q: Makes sense. A: Weight assignment: split up percentage weights based on what matters—like energy preservation 25% vs weather resilience 15% shows that sustainable performance beats avoiding occasional weather issues. Q: Right. A: Alternative scoring: rate each option 1-10 per criterion based on data or guesses. Weighted calculation: multiply scores by weights, add up to total. Q: Okay. A: Plus sensitivity analysis: test how weight changes mess with rankings, showing solid vs shaky decisions. Matrices force you to spell out trade-offs—Option A wins on criterion X but loses on Y.
   
   Q: Why not just use pros/cons lists?
   A: Good question. Stops cognitive biases that mess up gut decisions: recency bias—overweighting recent stuff, availability bias—overweighting vivid factors like weather vs boring factors like consistency, status quo bias—sticking with current approach. Q: Aha! A: Yeah. Weighted evaluation admits that not all criteria matter the same. Time efficiency 25% matters more than variety satisfaction 15% for commute choice. Q: True. A: Plus lets you look back: if Option C fails, Decision Record shows whether your prediction sucked (scores off) or priorities shifted (weights should change). Key for Phase 1-3 decisions hitting hundreds of hours annually where bad gut calls pile up into massive time/energy losses.
   
   Q: Walk me through a real example.
   A: Sure. Meal decision matrix. Cafeteria scores 6.95 weighted total—strong time efficiency 9/10 times 25% weight equals 2.25, weak nutrition 6/10 times 30% equals 1.80. Q: Okay. A: Restaurant 6.80—strong satisfaction 9/10 times 15% equals 1.35, weak time 5/10 times 25% equals 1.25. Q: Got it. A: Meal Prep 8.70—strong nutrition 9/10 times 30% equals 2.70, strong cost 10/10 times 20% equals 2.00. Q: Wait, so Meal Prep wins? A: Exactly. Without weights, worker might pick Restaurant (highest satisfaction score), but weighted analysis shows Meal Prep maxes out high-priority stuff—nutrition, cost—despite lower variety. Q: Makes sense. A: Yeah. If nutrition weight goes up to 40% (like health diagnosis), Meal Prep advantage grows. If time weight jumps to 35% (like deadline crunch), Cafeteria gets competitive.
