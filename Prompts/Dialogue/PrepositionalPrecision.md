# Prepositional Precision for Natural English

**Type**: Dual-purpose - Supporting reference AND standalone post-check/improvement tool

**Task**: Enhance English content with precise, natural prepositional usage to achieve structural clarity, conciseness, and native-like fluency.

**Definition**: Systematically improve preposition and prepositional phrase usage in existing content, transforming verbose, awkward, or incorrect expressions into clear, concise, and idiomatically natural English.

## Precision Principle

**Critical**: Prepositional precision enhances **clarity and naturalness** without changing **meaning or facts**.

**Rules**:
- **Preserve all semantic content** from the original text
- Never change facts, arguments, or core meaning
- Maintain the logical flow and structure
- Don't add ungrounded information
- Prepositions enhance precision, not substance

**Example**:
```
Before: We need to have a discussion regarding the matter of database optimization.
After:  We need to discuss database optimization.
[Same meaning, more concise with correct preposition usage]
```

## Quick Reference: Top 15 Patterns (80% of Use Cases)

**Most frequent prepositional improvements covering 80% of clarity needs. Apply these first.**

### 1. Spatial Relationships (90% frequency)
**Purpose**: Precise location and position  
**Patterns**: 
- **at** (point): at the door, at the corner, at home
- **on** (surface): on the table, on the wall, on the floor
- **in** (enclosed): in the room, in the box, in the city

**Example**:
```
✗ I'm standing by the entrance
✓ I'm standing at the entrance
```

### 2. Temporal Relationships (85% frequency)
**Purpose**: Precise time expressions  
**Patterns**:
- **at** (specific time): at 3 PM, at midnight, at noon
- **on** (days/dates): on Monday, on July 4th, on Christmas Day
- **in** (longer periods): in 2024, in January, in the morning

**Example**:
```
✗ The meeting starts in Monday
✓ The meeting starts on Monday
```

### 3. Direction & Movement (70% frequency)
**Purpose**: Clear motion and direction  
**Patterns**:
- **to** (destination): go to, move to, travel to
- **toward/towards** (direction): walk toward, head toward
- **into** (entering): go into, step into, dive into
- **from** (origin): come from, originate from, stem from

**Example**:
```
✗ We're going at the office
✓ We're going to the office
```

### 4. Abstract Relationships (75% frequency)
**Purpose**: Conceptual connections  
**Patterns**:
- **about** (concerning): talk about, think about, worry about
- **on** (topic/subject): lecture on, focus on, work on
- **of** (belonging/composition): part of, member of, type of
- **for** (purpose/duration): wait for, prepare for, work for

**Example**:
```
✗ This article discusses regarding AI safety
✓ This article discusses AI safety / is about AI safety
```

### 5. Dependency & Causation (65% frequency)
**Purpose**: Show cause-effect relationships  
**Patterns**:
- **because of** (cause): because of delays, because of weather
- **due to** (formal cause): due to technical issues
- **thanks to** (positive cause): thanks to your help
- **despite / in spite of** (contrast): despite challenges

**Example**:
```
✗ The project failed owing to the reason of poor planning
✓ The project failed due to poor planning
```

### 6. Possession & Association (60% frequency)
**Purpose**: Show ownership and connection  
**Patterns**:
- **of** (possession): the color of the car, the CEO of the company
- **with** (association): agree with, consistent with, familiar with
- **to** (belonging): belongs to, related to, applicable to

**Example**:
```
✗ This belongs for the team
✓ This belongs to the team
```

### 7. Method & Manner (55% frequency)
**Purpose**: Describe how actions are performed  
**Patterns**:
- **by** (method): by train, by email, by hand
- **with** (tool): with a knife, with Python, with care
- **in** (manner): in detail, in general, in secret
- **through** (means): through analysis, through experience

**Example**:
```
✗ Send the file via using email
✓ Send the file by email / via email
```

### 8. Comparison (50% frequency)
**Purpose**: Express similarities and differences  
**Patterns**:
- **to** (compared to): compared to, similar to, equal to
- **with** (compared with): compared with, contrast with
- **from** (different from): differ from, distinguish from
- **like** (resemblance): looks like, sounds like, feels like

**Example**:
```
✗ This differs with the previous version
✓ This differs from the previous version
```

### 9. Purpose & Intention (55% frequency)
**Purpose**: Clarify goals and reasons  
**Patterns**:
- **for** (purpose): designed for, used for, built for
- **to** (infinitive purpose): to learn, to improve, to understand
- **in order to** (formal purpose): in order to achieve

**Example**:
```
✗ This tool is designed to the purpose of testing
✓ This tool is designed for testing
```

### 10. Conciseness (70% frequency)
**Purpose**: Eliminate redundant prepositional phrases  
**Patterns**:
- ~~in terms of~~ → (omit or use specific preposition)
- ~~with regard to~~ → about / regarding
- ~~in the event of~~ → if
- ~~for the purpose of~~ → to / for
- ~~by means of~~ → by / using

**Example**:
```
✗ In terms of performance, the system is slow
✓ The system performs poorly / The system's performance is poor
```

### 11. Idiomatic Verb + Preposition (80% frequency)
**Purpose**: Natural phrasal verb usage  
**Common patterns**:
- **depend on** (not ~~depend of~~)
- **consist of** (not ~~consist in~~)
- **listen to** (not ~~listen~~)
- **look for** (search), **look at** (observe), **look after** (care for)
- **focus on** (not ~~focus at~~)

**Example**:
```
✗ Success depends of many factors
✓ Success depends on many factors
```

### 12. Adjective + Preposition (75% frequency)
**Purpose**: Correct adjective-preposition pairings  
**Common patterns**:
- **good at** (not ~~good in~~)
- **interested in** (not ~~interested about~~)
- **capable of** (not ~~capable to~~)
- **responsible for** (not ~~responsible of~~)
- **familiar with** (not ~~familiar to~~)

**Example**:
```
✗ She's good in mathematics
✓ She's good at mathematics
```

### 13. Noun + Preposition (70% frequency)
**Purpose**: Correct noun-preposition collocations  
**Common patterns**:
- **solution to** (not ~~solution of~~)
- **reason for** (not ~~reason of~~)
- **answer to** (not ~~answer of~~)
- **impact on** (not ~~impact to~~)
- **effect on** (not ~~effect to~~)

✓ What's the solution to this problem?

### 14. Elimination of Redundancy (65% frequency)
**Purpose**: Remove unnecessary prepositions  
**Patterns**:
- ~~Where... at~~ → Where...

✓ Where's the meeting?

### 15. Natural Prepositional Phrases (60% frequency)
**Purpose**: Use idiomatic prepositional expressions  
**Common patterns**:
- **at first** (initially), **at last** (finally)
- **in advance** (beforehand), **in time** (eventually)
- **on time** (punctual), **in time** (before deadline)
- **by accident** (unintentionally), **on purpose** (intentionally)

**Example**:
```
✗ We arrived in the right time
✓ We arrived on time (punctual) / in time (before it was too late)
```

## Standalone Usage: Post-Check & Improvement

**Purpose**: Use this file independently to audit existing content and enhance prepositional precision after initial writing.

**Two modes of operation**:

### Mode 1: Supporting Reference (During Writing)
Keep this as a quick reference while writing to ensure correct preposition usage from the start.

### Mode 2: Standalone Post-Check (After Writing)
Use independently to audit and improve existing content:
- Review completed text for prepositional errors
- Identify verbose or awkward prepositional phrases
- Apply precise, natural alternatives
- Validate clarity and conciseness before finalization

### Review Workflow

**Step 1: Quick Diagnostic Scan**

Run through this checklist to identify issues:
- [ ] **Spatial prepositions unclear?** → Check at/on/in usage for location
- [ ] **Time expressions awkward?** → Check at/on/in for temporal relationships
- [ ] **Verbose phrases?** → Look for "in terms of", "with regard to", "for the purpose of"
- [ ] **Unnatural verb combinations?** → Check phrasal verbs (depend on, consist of, etc.)
- [ ] **Wrong adjective pairings?** → Check good at, interested in, familiar with
- [ ] **Incorrect noun collocations?** → Check solution to, reason for, impact on
- [ ] **Redundant prepositions?** → Check for "where...at", "inside of", "off of"
- [ ] **Non-idiomatic expressions?** → Check prepositional phrases (on time, in time, etc.)

**Step 2: Pattern Selection**

**Priority 1 (Apply First)** - High-frequency corrections (80/20 rule):
- Conciseness: Eliminate verbose prepositional phrases
- Idiomatic Verb + Preposition: Fix common errors
- Adjective + Preposition: Correct pairings
- Spatial/Temporal: Clarify at/on/in usage

**Priority 2 (Add Second)** - Precision improvements:
- Direction & Movement: Clarify to/toward/into/from
- Abstract Relationships: about/on/of/for distinctions
- Comparison: to/with/from for comparisons
- Method & Manner: by/with/in/through

**Priority 3 (Polish)** - Advanced refinement:
- Dependency & Causation: because of/due to/despite
- Natural Prepositional Phrases: Idiomatic expressions
- Noun + Preposition: Collocation accuracy

**Step 3: Targeted Application**

**Replacement strategy**:
- Identify the problematic preposition or phrase
- Determine the intended meaning (spatial, temporal, abstract, etc.)
- Apply the correct preposition from the pattern library
- Read aloud to verify naturalness

**Application principles**:
- Precision over verbosity: Choose the simplest correct form
- Preserve meaning: Never change semantic content
- Maintain register: Keep formality level consistent
- Natural integration: Ensure the result sounds fluent

**Step 4: Conciseness Validation**

Check for opportunities to simplify:
- Replace verbose prepositional phrases with simple prepositions
- Eliminate redundant prepositions
- Use direct constructions where possible
- Maintain clarity while reducing word count

**Step 5: Quality Check**

- ✓ All semantic content preserved?
- ✓ Factual accuracy unchanged?
- ✓ Prepositions grammatically correct?
- ✓ Idiomatic and natural-sounding?
- ✓ More concise than original?
- ✓ Clear structural relationships?

### Common Improvement Scenarios

**Verbose Prepositional Phrases**: Replace "in terms of", "with regard to", "for the purpose of" with concise alternatives

**Wrong Preposition with Verbs**: Fix "depend of" → "depend on", "focus at" → "focus on"

**Wrong Preposition with Adjectives**: Fix "good in" → "good at", "interested about" → "interested in"

**Confused Spatial Prepositions**: Clarify at/on/in based on point/surface/enclosed space

**Confused Temporal Prepositions**: Clarify at/on/in based on specific time/day/period

**Awkward Comparisons**: Fix "differ with" → "differ from", "similar with" → "similar to"

**Redundant Prepositions**: Eliminate "where...at", "inside of", "off of"

## I. Spatial Prepositions

**Purpose**: Precise location and position in physical or abstract space (90% of spatial expressions)

### At - Point/Specific Location

**Usage**: Specific points, addresses, locations, events

**Patterns**:
- **At + specific point**: at the entrance, at the corner, at the intersection
- **At + address**: at 123 Main Street, at Platform 9
- **At + event location**: at the conference, at the party, at the meeting
- **At + institution** (activity focus): at school, at work, at university

**Examples**:
```
✓ We'll meet at the station
✓ She's waiting at the door
✓ The conference is at the Convention Center
✗ We'll meet in the station (unless referring to being inside the building)
```

### On - Surface/Line Contact

**Usage**: Surface contact, lines, days, topics

**Patterns**:
- **On + surface**: on the table, on the wall, on the floor, on the ceiling
- **On + transportation** (surface contact): on the bus, on the train, on the plane
- **On + street**: on Main Street, on the avenue
- **On + electronic medium**: on the website, on the page, on the screen

**Examples**:
```
✓ The book is on the desk
✓ I saw it on the news
✓ They live on Elm Street
✗ The book is in the desk (means inside a drawer)
```

### In - Enclosed Space/Container

**Usage**: Three-dimensional enclosure, larger areas, countries, cities

**Patterns**:
- **In + enclosed space**: in the room, in the box, in the building
- **In + geographic area**: in New York, in China, in Europe, in the world
- **In + medium/environment**: in the water, in the air, in the cloud
- **In + abstract space**: in the document, in the code, in the system

**Examples**:
```
✓ She lives in Tokyo
✓ The data is stored in the database
✓ We work in a competitive environment
✗ She lives at Tokyo (Tokyo is not a point, it's an area)
```

### Advanced Spatial Distinctions

**Between vs Among**:
- **Between** (two items or distinct items): between you and me, between the options
- **Among** (three or more, not distinct): among the crowd, among many choices

**Above vs Over**:
- **Above** (higher position, no contact): above sea level, above average
- **Over** (covering, spanning): over the bridge, over the table (hanging)

**Below vs Under**:
- **Below** (lower position): below zero, below standard
- **Under** (directly beneath, covered): under the table, under water

**Examples**:
```
✓ Choose between Option A and Option B
✓ He's one among many candidates
✓ The temperature is above zero
✓ The bridge goes over the river
✓ Performance is below expectations
✓ The cat is hiding under the couch
```

## II. Temporal Prepositions

**Purpose**: Precise time expressions (85% of temporal expressions)

### At - Specific Time Point

**Usage**: Clock times, precise moments, short periods

**Patterns**:
- **At + clock time**: at 3 PM, at noon, at midnight, at 7:30
- **At + specific moment**: at the moment, at that time, at present
- **At + age**: at 25, at the age of 30
- **At + meals**: at breakfast, at lunch, at dinner
- **At + festivals** (point in time): at Christmas, at Easter

**Examples**:
```
✓ The meeting starts at 9 AM
✓ At the moment, we're analyzing data
✓ She learned Python at 15
✗ The meeting starts in 9 AM
```

### On - Days and Dates

**Usage**: Specific days, dates, special days

**Patterns**:
- **On + day**: on Monday, on weekends, on weekdays
- **On + date**: on July 4th, on the 15th of March
- **On + specific day**: on my birthday, on Christmas Day, on New Year's Eve
- **On + day + part**: on Monday morning, on Tuesday afternoon

**Examples**:
```
✓ The deadline is on Friday
✓ We launched on January 1st, 2024
✓ On Sunday mornings, I study
✗ The deadline is in Friday
```

### In - Longer Periods

**Usage**: Months, years, centuries, seasons, parts of day (except night)

**Patterns**:
- **In + month**: in January, in December
- **In + year**: in 2024, in the 1990s
- **In + season**: in summer, in winter
- **In + part of day**: in the morning, in the afternoon, in the evening
- **In + century**: in the 21st century
- **In + duration** (future): in two weeks, in three days, in a moment

**Examples**:
```
✓ I'll finish in two hours
✓ The project started in March
✓ In the morning, I code
✗ I'll finish on two hours
```

**Special Case - At Night**:
```
✓ We work at night (not ~~in night~~)
```

### Duration and Deadlines

**For vs During vs Since**:
- **For** (duration length): for three hours, for two weeks
- **During** (when it happened): during the meeting, during winter
- **Since** (starting point): since Monday, since 2020

**By vs Until/Till**:
- **By** (deadline, completed before): by Friday, by 5 PM
- **Until/Till** (continuing up to): until Friday, till 5 PM

**Examples**:
```
✓ I've worked here for five years (duration)
✓ I've worked here since 2019 (starting point)
✓ Something happened during the presentation (within that time)
✓ Finish by Friday (deadline)
✓ Work until 6 PM (continue up to)
```

## III. Directional Prepositions

**Purpose**: Clear motion, direction, and transition (70% of movement expressions)

### To - Destination/Target

**Usage**: Movement toward destination, recipient, limit

**Patterns**:
- **To + destination**: go to, move to, travel to, drive to
- **To + person** (recipient): give to, send to, speak to, explain to
- **To + limit**: from 1 to 10, rise to 100

**Examples**:
```
✓ We're going to the office
✓ Send the file to the client
✓ Prices rose from $10 to $15
✗ We're going at the office
```

### Toward/Towards - Direction (Not Arrival)

**Usage**: Movement in a direction without necessarily reaching

**Examples**:
```
✓ Walk toward the exit
✓ Moving toward a solution
✓ Progress toward our goals
```

### Into - Entering/Transformation

**Usage**: Movement from outside to inside, transformation

**Patterns**:
- **Into + container**: go into, step into, put into
- **Into + transformation**: turn into, transform into, translate into

**Examples**:
```
✓ She walked into the room
✓ Translate this into Chinese
✓ The water turned into ice
✗ She walked in the room (can mean "while inside the room")
```

### From - Origin/Source

**Usage**: Starting point, source, separation

**Patterns**:
- **From + origin**: come from, originate from, stem from
- **From + source**: learn from, derive from, quote from
- **From + range start**: from 1 to 10, from Monday to Friday

**Examples**:
```
✓ The team comes from various backgrounds
✓ This insight stems from research
✓ We work from 9 to 5
```

### Through - Passage/Means

**Usage**: Moving within and exiting, means/process

**Patterns**:
- **Through + space**: walk through, pass through, go through
- **Through + means**: through analysis, through experience, learn through practice

**Examples**:
```
✓ Drive through the tunnel
✓ Improve through continuous learning
✓ We discovered this through testing
```

## IV. Abstract Prepositions

**Purpose**: Conceptual relationships and abstract connections (75% of abstract expressions)

### About - Topic/Concerning

**Usage**: Subject matter, approximate amount

**Patterns**:
- **About + topic**: talk about, think about, learn about, worry about
- **About + approximate**: about 50 people, about $100

**Examples**:
```
✓ We discussed about AI ethics → We discussed AI ethics (discuss is transitive)
✓ Let's talk about the project
✓ The meeting is about strategic planning
```

**Note**: Some verbs take direct objects without "about" (discuss, consider, describe)

### On - Subject/Topic (Formal)

**Usage**: Formal topic, focus, dependency

**Patterns**:
- **On + formal topic**: lecture on, presentation on, paper on, focus on
- **On + dependency**: depend on, rely on, based on, contingent on

**Examples**:
```
✓ A lecture on machine learning
✓ Success depends on execution
✓ Focus on the core issue
```

### Of - Belonging/Composition/Characteristic

**Usage**: Possession, part-whole, material, characteristic

**Patterns**:
- **Of + possession**: the CEO of the company, the color of the car
- **Of + composition**: made of, consist of, composed of
- **Of + characteristic**: a person of integrity, a matter of time

**Examples**:
```
✓ The success of the project depends on the team
✓ The system consists of three components
✓ It's a question of priorities
```

### For - Purpose/Beneficiary/Duration

**Usage**: Purpose, intended recipient, duration, reason

**Patterns**:
- **For + purpose**: designed for, used for, meant for
- **For + beneficiary**: work for, do for, buy for
- **For + duration**: for three hours, for two weeks
- **For + reason**: famous for, known for, reason for

**Examples**:
```
✓ This tool is designed for testing
✓ I bought this for you
✓ We waited for two hours
✓ The reason for the delay is...
```

### With - Association/Tool/Manner

**Usage**: Accompaniment, instrument, manner, agreement

**Patterns**:
- **With + accompaniment**: go with, work with, come with
- **With + tool/means**: write with, cut with, build with
- **With + characteristic**: a person with experience, a system with flaws
- **With + agreement**: agree with, consistent with, compatible with

**Examples**:
```
✓ I disagree with your conclusion
✓ Build the feature with React
✓ A developer with strong skills
✓ This aligns with our strategy
```

## VI. Idiomatic Expressions

**Purpose**: Natural prepositional phrases native speakers use (60% of idiomatic usage)

**Note**: For basic temporal prepositions (at/on/in), see Section II. These are idiomatic phrases that extend beyond simple time expressions.

### Time Idioms

**At first** vs **At last** vs **In the end** vs **Finally**
```
✓ At first, I didn't understand (initially)
✓ At last, we found the bug (after long wait)
✓ In the end, we chose React (ultimate outcome)
✓ Finally, the deployment succeeded (last in sequence)
```

**On time** vs **In time**
```
✓ The meeting started on time (punctual, scheduled time)
✓ We arrived in time for the meeting (before deadline/too late)
```

**In advance** vs **Ahead of time**
```
✓ Please notify us in advance
✓ We finished ahead of schedule
```

✓ For the time being, use this workaround (temporary)
✓ At the moment, the system is down (current state)

## VII. Efficiency Patterns

**Purpose**: Eliminate verbosity and avoid common errors (70% of clarity improvements)

### A. Verbose Phrases → Concise Alternatives

**In terms of** → (omit or use specific word)
```
✗ In terms of performance, the system is slow
✓ The system performs poorly
✓ The system's performance is poor
```

**With regard to / In regard to** → about / regarding
```
✗ With regard to your question about databases...
✓ Regarding your database question...
```

**In the event of / In the event that** → if
```
✗ In the event of a failure, the system will retry
✓ If it fails, the system will retry
```

**For the purpose of** → to / for
```
✗ This is designed for the purpose of testing
✓ This is designed for testing
```

**By means of** → by / using / with
```
✗ Authenticate by means of OAuth
✓ Authenticate using OAuth
```

**In order to** → to (when clear)
```
✗ In order to improve performance, we cache data
✓ To improve performance, we cache data
```

**At this point in time** → now / currently
```
✗ At this point in time, the feature is unavailable
✓ Currently, the feature is unavailable
```

**Due to the fact that** → because / since
```
✗ Due to the fact that the API is slow...
✓ Because the API is slow...
```

**In spite of the fact that** → although / despite
```
✗ In spite of the fact that we tested thoroughly...
✓ Although we tested thoroughly...
```

**During the course of** → during
```
✗ During the course of the project...
✓ During the project...
```

**In the process of** → verb + -ing
```
✗ We are in the process of migrating
✓ We're migrating
```

**A large number of** → many
```
✗ A large number of users reported issues
✓ Many users reported issues
```

**In close proximity to** → near
```
✗ The server is in close proximity to the database
✓ The server is near the database
```

### B. Redundant Prepositions → Eliminate

**Where... at** → **Where...**
```
✗ Where's the file at?
✓ Where's the file?
```

**Inside of** (literal) → **Inside**
```
✗ Inside of the container
✓ Inside the container
```

**Off of** → **Off**
```
✗ Get off of the server
✓ Get off the server
```

**Outside of** (literal) → **Outside**
```
✗ Outside of the building
✓ Outside the building
```

### C. Missing/Incorrect Prepositions

**Discuss** (transitive - no preposition)
```
✗ Let's discuss about the issue
✓ Let's discuss the issue
```

**Enter** (transitive - no preposition for physical space)
```
✗ Enter into the room
✓ Enter the room
Note: "Enter into" correct for agreements
```

**Reach** (transitive)
```
✗ Reach to the conclusion
✓ Reach the conclusion
```

**Marry** (transitive)
```
✗ Marry with someone
✓ Marry someone
```

## Integration Guidelines

**Apply Corrections Contextually**:
- Don't force every pattern into every sentence
- Prioritize clarity and naturalness
- Match formality level to context
- Preserve original meaning (precision principle)

**Frequency Balance (80/20)**:
Apply high-frequency corrections first: 90%+ (Spatial, Temporal) | 80% (Verb+Prep, Conciseness) | 75% (Adj+Prep, Abstract) | 70% (Noun+Prep, Direction) | 60% (Idioms) | 50% (Common Errors)

**Selective Application**:
- **Simple content**: Focus on correctness (at/on/in, verb+prep)
- **Complex content**: Add conciseness (eliminate wordy phrases)
- **Formal writing**: Prioritize precision (noun+prep, abstract relationships)
- **Conversational content**: Emphasize naturalness (idioms, contractions)

**Natural Integration**: Prepositions should be invisible—correct but not noticeable.

## Examples

### Example 1: Simple Correction

**Before**:
```
The meeting starts in Monday at 3PM. We'll discuss about the new features. 
The team is good in React and interested about AI.
```

**After** (Spatial + Temporal + Collocation):
```
The meeting starts on Monday at 3 PM. We'll discuss the new features.
The team is good at React and interested in AI.
```

**Changes**:
- in Monday → on Monday (temporal: day)
- at 3PM → at 3 PM (spacing)
- discuss about → discuss (verb+prep: discuss is transitive)
- good in → good at (adj+prep)
- interested about → interested in (adj+prep)

### Example 2: Conciseness Enhancement

**Before**:
```
In terms of performance, the system is slow due to the fact that we have 
a large number of database queries. For the purpose of optimization, we 
are in the process of implementing caching by means of Redis.
```

**After** (Conciseness + Precision):
```
The system performs poorly because we have many database queries. 
To optimize, we're implementing caching with Redis.
```

**Changes**:
- In terms of performance, the system is slow → The system performs poorly
- due to the fact that → because
- a large number of → many
- For the purpose of optimization → To optimize
- are in the process of implementing → are implementing / we're implementing
- by means of → with

---

### Example 3: Comprehensive Improvement

**Before**:
```
We need to have a discussion with regard to the solution of this problem. 
The issue is different than what we expected. In the event of a failure, 
the system should retry. Where's the configuration file at? The code is 
inside of the container. Success depends of many factors, and the team 
is responsible of the implementation. We arrived to the office in 9 AM.
```

**After** (Multiple patterns):
```
We need to discuss the solution to this problem. The issue differs from 
what we expected. If it fails, the system should retry. Where's the 
configuration file? The code is inside the container. Success depends on 
many factors, and the team is responsible for the implementation. We 
arrived at the office at 9 AM.
```

**Changes**:
- need to have a discussion with regard to → need to discuss (conciseness)
- solution of → solution to (noun+prep)
- different than → differs from (adj+prep → verb+prep, more concise)
- In the event of a failure → If it fails (conciseness)
- Where's... at? → Where's...? (redundant preposition)
- inside of → inside (redundant preposition)
- depends of → depends on (verb+prep)
- responsible of → responsible for (adj+prep)
- arrived to → arrived at (verb+prep: arrive at specific place)
- in 9 AM → at 9 AM (temporal: specific time)

## Summary

This framework provides **prepositional precision patterns** organized into **7 MECE categories**:

**I. Spatial Prepositions** (3 core: at/on/in + distinctions) - Location and position  
**II. Temporal Prepositions** (3 core: at/on/in + duration/deadline) - Time relationships  
**III. Directional Prepositions** (5 patterns: to/toward/into/from/through) - Movement and direction  
**IV. Abstract Prepositions** (5 patterns: about/on/of/for/with) - Conceptual relationships  
**V. Fixed Collocations** (3 types: verb+prep, adj+prep, noun+prep) - Applications of I-IV in fixed pairings  
**VI. Idiomatic Expressions** (4 types: time/manner/degree/state) - Natural phrases beyond basics  
**VII. Efficiency Patterns** (3 types: verbose→concise, redundant→eliminate, missing/incorrect→fix) - Error avoidance  

**Structure**: Sections I-IV are foundational (core prepositions), V-VII are applied (usage patterns)

**Coverage**: ~95% of prepositional usage in technical/professional English

**Balance**: Correctness (50%), Conciseness (30%), Naturalness (20%)

**Frequency**: Prioritized by 80/20 principle - high-frequency patterns emphasized

**Precision**: All patterns preserve semantic content, maintain factual accuracy

Apply these patterns contextually to create clear, concise, natural English that enhances structural clarity and native-like fluency.
