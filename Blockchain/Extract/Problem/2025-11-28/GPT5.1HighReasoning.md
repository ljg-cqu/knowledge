1. Q: In major public blockchains, the long-term security budget after scheduled issuance reductions is unclear, creating potential under-incentivization of validators and increased attack risk. Formulate a structured problem statement using the following [Input] fields.
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: Public blockchains that rely on declining block subsidies may face an insufficient long-term security budget from transaction fees alone, undermining validator incentives and increasing the probability of successful attacks compared with the economic value secured on-chain.
   - **Background and current situation**: Today’s leading proof-of-work and proof-of-stake chains often secure tens to hundreds of billions of dollars in asset value while paying out an annual security budget on the order of low single-digit percent of market cap, with a significant portion coming from inflationary issuance rather than sustainable fee revenue. As issuance schedules continue to reduce and user fees are volatile, there is no shared quantitative framework across ecosystems for what constitutes a “safe enough” security budget relative to value at risk.
   - **Goals and success criteria**: The ecosystem needs to clarify how much annual security spend (issuance + fees) is required, in absolute terms and as a percentage of economic value secured, so that 51% or similar attacks remain economically irrational under realistic scenarios. Success would mean, for example, modeling and agreeing on target ranges where expected honest validator revenue and attack costs remain at least 5–10x higher than plausible attacker payoffs over the next 5–10 years, with explicit thresholds for “minimum acceptable” vs “target” security budgets.
   - **Key constraints and resources**: Protocols are constrained by fixed or socially committed issuance schedules, political resistance to changing monetary policy, user intolerance for permanently high fees, and the need to remain competitive with other chains. Available resources include historical fee and issuance data, on-chain economic activity metrics, research on attack models, and the ability (in some ecosystems) to adjust parameters through governance.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: Key stakeholders include token holders who are diluted by issuance but benefit from security, validators / miners / stakers whose income depends on the security budget, protocol developers designing the monetary and fee mechanisms, large application teams whose users depend on chain security, and exchanges / custodians managing large balances that are exposed to consensus attacks.
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: The relevant horizon is 3–10 years, with decision windows in the next 1–3 years for potential monetary or fee-market changes. Impact spans the entire chain ecosystem: all users and assets, major DeFi protocols, and institutions holding large positions; even a single major attack could wipe out >10% of network value or temporarily freeze activity for millions of users.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Many chains have introduced fee-burning mechanisms, dynamic gas markets, or restaking / additional revenue sources, and security-budget modeling papers exist, but few ecosystems have reached consensus on quantitative targets or encoded them into protocol rules. So far, security has held mostly due to high token prices and social expectations rather than rigorously modeled, stress-tested economic incentives.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Issuance schedules and historical fee revenue are observable; major chains secure very large asset values compared with annual validator income. Assumptions: Rational attackers compare expected payoff with cost of acquiring or renting control; future transaction volume and fee levels can change by multiples up or down. Uncertainties: Future token prices, regulatory shocks, and novel attack or defense mechanisms could significantly change both attacker incentives and honest validator behavior.

   A:

Historical data shows multiple bridge exploits with losses in the hundreds of millions of dollars, and yet users and protocols still concentrate liquidity into a handful of bridges for convenience, creating large honeypots.

   - **Key constraints and resources**: Constraints include fundamental limitations of verifying one chain’s state on another, lack of universal standards, incentive misalignment between bridge operators and end users, and the difficulty of coordinating upgrades across chains. Resources include existing research on light clients, IBC-like protocols, threshold cryptography, and economic security models, as well as operational data from past bridge incidents.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: Stakeholders include end users bridging assets, DeFi protocols depending on bridged liquidity, bridge operators and validators, L1/L2 protocol teams whose ecosystems depend on inbound/outbound liquidity, auditors and security researchers, and regulators concerned with cross-chain money movement and consumer protection.
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: The problem is immediate (0–3 years), given active cross-chain usage and ongoing hacks, and affects any ecosystem whose assets or users rely on bridges. Impact includes direct financial losses, loss of trust in affected chains or tokens, contagion into DeFi protocols that hold bridged assets, and potential regulatory backlash.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Many bridge designs have been launched, from simple custodial bridges to sophisticated light-client-based systems, and some standards for message formats have emerged. Incident postmortems and best-practice checklists exist, but there is still no widely adopted framework for classifying bridge risk, limiting blast radius, or enforcing safety thresholds relative to value locked.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Documented exploits have repeatedly drained hundreds of millions from high-profile bridges; bridges concentrate a large share of inter-chain liquidity. Assumptions: Cross-chain activity will continue to grow as multi-chain application architectures proliferate; users will not manually evaluate complex security assumptions. Uncertainties: The pace at which more secure interoperability primitives (e.g., standardized light clients, cryptographic proofs) can be deployed at scale, and how quickly legacy bridges can be migrated or de-risked.

   - **Goals and success criteria**: The goal is to reduce harmful MEV (e.g., user-facing sandwich and frontrun attacks) while channeling unavoidable MEV into transparent mechanisms that either benefit protocol security or are fairly rebated to users.    - **Key constraints and resources**: Constraints include the need to preserve permissionless access and composability, the difficulty of proving intent or fairness, and the dependence of current validator income on MEV in some ecosystems.    - **Stakeholders and roles**: Stakeholders include retail and institutional users submitting transactions, DeFi protocols whose liquidity and pricing are affected, block producers and searchers who currently profit from MEV, infrastructure providers operating relays and builders, and regulators who may view some MEV behaviors as manipulative or abusive.

The impact includes billions of dollars in cumulative hidden costs to users, potential centralization of power among a small set of sophisticated MEV actors, and reputational damage to the promise of “fair and transparent” blockchains.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Approaches such as off-chain orderflow auctions, private transaction submission, fair-ordering relays, and protocol-level MEV auctions have been deployed in some ecosystems, but they remain partial, optional, and sometimes introduce new centralization or opacity. There is no widely accepted standard for what constitutes acceptable MEV vs harmful exploitation.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: On-chain data and research quantify substantial MEV revenues and document user-harming strategies like sandwich attacks. Assumptions: MEV cannot be fully eliminated in general-purpose composable systems, but its distribution and impact can be reshaped. Uncertainties: Which mix of protocol changes, market mechanisms, and social norms can sustainably align MEV incentives without overly centralizing power or harming liquidity.
