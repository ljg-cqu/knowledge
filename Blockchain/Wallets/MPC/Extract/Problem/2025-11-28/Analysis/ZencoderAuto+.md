   - **Key constraints and resources**: User devices (mobile phones, browsers) face significant constraints that make peer-to-peer coordination challenging: devices typically behind NAT and cannot accept incoming connections (requires NAT traversal techniques like STUN/TURN which themselves require coordination servers), mobile devices cannot act as always-on servers (battery, OS restrictions on background processes, intermittent connectivity), and complexity of peer-to-peer discovery without central relay (how do parties find each other's current IP addresses and establish connections?). Cryptographic protocol constraints: MPC signing ceremonies require multiple rounds of back-and-forth communication (typically 3-7 rounds depending on protocol) with sub-second to few-second timeouts, and coordination failures or delays can cause ceremony to abort and restart (adding latency and user frustration). Any fallback coordination mechanism must maintain: protocol correctness (cannot introduce security vulnerabilities or enable attacks on signing ceremony), privacy (ideally hide transaction metadata from coordinator), and acceptable performance (signing latency under 10 seconds for 95th percentile vs. 2-5 seconds for current centralized coordinator, representing 2-3x acceptable degradation). Engineering resources constrained: implementing decentralized coordination layer (peer-to-peer networking, relay network integration, fallback mechanisms, testing across network conditions) estimated at 6-12 months of focused engineering effort from team of 3-5 engineers, plus ongoing maintenance overhead. Existing MPC protocols and implementations not designed with decentralized coordination in mind (most assume reliable, low-latency, synchronous communication that centralized server provides), so retrofitting decentralization may require protocol modifications or careful engineering of timeout/retry logic. User experience concerns: decentralized mechanisms may introduce additional setup complexity (establishing peer-to-peer connections, configuring relay preferences, understanding fallback options) or increased signing latency (2-3x slower than centralized approach), and complex error cases (explaining to users why signing failed due to network conditions vs. vendor being down).    - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Various approaches have been attempted or proposed to address centralized coordination risk: "Emergency escape hatches" or recovery mechanisms where users can extract their key shares and reconstruct full key offline in emergency scenarios (complex to trigger, requires coordination of other key-share holders, may take days to weeks, and often requires vendor cooperation which defeats purpose if vendor is unavailable; adoption <10% of users actually test or could execute emergency recovery), redundant server regions with failover (vendor operates coordination servers in multiple geographic regions with automatic failover, reducing single-region outage risk but still maintaining centralized control; improves availability from 99.9% to 99.95% but doesn't address censorship or vendor failure risk), decentralized relay networks using libp2p, Waku, Nostr, or similar protocols (allows parties to discover each other and pass messages without central server; promising but adds complexity, latency typically 2-3x centralized approach, and requires critical mass of relay nodes; adoption still experimental with few production deployments), local network discovery using mDNS, Bluetooth, or WiFi Direct for cases where key-share holders are physically proximate (works for some use cases like multi-device personal wallets but doesn't help for geographically distributed parties; limited applicability), peer-to-peer WebRTC connections with STUN/TURN fallback (browsers and mobile apps can establish direct connections after initial discovery; requires signaling server for discovery which reintroduces some coordination dependency, and TURN fallback servers can become bottleneck), blockchain-based coordination using smart contracts or on-chain messages to coordinate signing rounds (fully decentralized and censorship-resistant but introduces high latency due to block times, 15-60 second delays per round, and transaction costs; poor UX for frequent signing), and federated coordination model where multiple independent operators run coordination servers and users can choose or rotate between them (reduces single-vendor risk but doesn't fully eliminate centralization; requires trust in federation membership and coordination between operators). Evaluation of historical attempts shows that most solutions trade off between decentralization, performance, complexity, and reliability. Pure decentralized coordination (P2P, blockchain-based) achieves strongest independence and censorship resistance but typically 2-10x latency overhead and significantly more complex implementation/operation. Federated approaches (multiple coordination servers) improve resilience and reduce single-vendor risk but retain some centralization. Redundancy within single-vendor infrastructure (multi-region servers) improves availability but doesn't address vendor-level risks (bankruptcy, legal action, deliberate shutdown) or censorship. No current production MPC wallet deployment has achieved true 100% independence from vendor coordination infrastructure while maintaining user experience parity with centralized coordination. Most "non-custodial" MPC wallets remain fully dependent on vendor coordination servers for transaction signing, with emergency recovery mechanisms rarely tested and poorly understood by users.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: **Facts**: Most consumer and institutional MPC wallets rely on centralized vendor-hosted coordination servers to facilitate signing ceremonies (message routing, session management, protocol orchestration), creating single point of failure where vendor infrastructure outage or shutdown prevents all users from signing transactions regardless of distributed key shares.
