3. Q: Concerning the regulatory compliance and auditability of Blockchain MPC Wallets, Formulate a structured problem statement using the following [Input] fields.
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: The inherent privacy of MPC protocols, while a security feature, can create challenges for demonstrating compliance with financial regulations (like Travel Rule, AML) and providing transparent audit trails.
   - **Background and current situation**: Regulators and auditors are accustomed to traditional key management where a single entity can be compelled to provide access. In MPC, no single party holds a complete key, making it difficult to prove control and ownership or to provide transaction signing histories in a familiar format.
   - **Goals and success criteria**: Design an MPC system that can generate verifiable and regulator-friendly audit logs without reconstructing the private key or compromising the security model. Success criteria: Pass third-party security audits and receive formal legal opinions stating the solution meets specific regulatory requirements (e.g., NYDFS BitLicense).
   - **Key constraints and resources**: Must operate within strict legal and regulatory frameworks. Cannot alter the core MPC security guarantee of no single point of failure. Resources include legal counsel, compliance teams, and cryptography experts.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: Regulators and law enforcement (need visibility), institutional customers (e.g., funds, banks requiring compliant partners), internal compliance officers, auditors (internal and external).
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: This is a strategic, long-term problem impacting the entire viability of MPC for regulated institutions. Failure to solve this could block access to a market segment worth billions in assets under management.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Attempts include using threshold signatures with on-chain transparency, but this conflicts with privacy. Some solutions involve secure multi-party computation for the logging and compliance checks themselves. Key lesson: Proactive engagement with regulators is more effective than retrofitting compliance.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: MPC obscures the traditional signing entity. Assumptions: Regulatory acceptance is a prerequisite for mass institutional adoption. Uncertainties: The evolving stance of global regulators on MPC technology and the specific evidence they will require to deem it compliant.

4. Q: On the operational risk of participant failure in Blockchain MPC Wallets, Formulate a structured problem statement using the following [Input] fields.
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: If one or more participants in an MPC protocol become unavailable (due to device loss, network outage, or system failure), it can block access to funds or critical operations, creating a single point of failure risk despite the distributed key shares.
   - **Background and current situation**: MPC systems typically have a threshold (e.g., 2-of-3) where a quorum of participants is needed to sign. If the number of available participants falls below this threshold, the wallet is effectively locked. This is an operational reliability issue.
   - **Goals and success criteria**: Ensure 99.99% service availability for signing operations. Define and implement robust backup and recovery procedures that can restore operations within a maximum of 4 hours (Recovery Time Objective) without compromising security.
   - **Key constraints and resources**: Constrained by the security model: any backup mechanism must not recreate a single point of failure. Resources include backup data centers, redundant communication links, and secure procedures for share recovery.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: End-users (risk losing access to assets), operations/SRE teams (responsible for system uptime), risk management officers (must quantify and mitigate this risk).
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: Impact is acute and immediate when a failure occurs. For a wallet service with 1 million users, even a 0.1% chance of individual user lockout affects 1,000 users, which is a significant customer support and reputational disaster.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Using a higher threshold (e.g., 3-of-5) to increase redundancy. Implementing "backup shares" stored in highly secure, offline environments, but this re-introduces a form of custodial risk. Lessons show that simplicity in recovery is key for user-operated wallets.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Participant unavailability is a matter of "when," not "if." Assumptions: Users will not perfectly manage their key shares. Uncertainties: The actual rate of device loss and long-term network outage for a globally distributed user base.

5. Q: Regarding the resource consumption of Blockchain MPC Wallets on consumer devices, Formulate a structured problem statement using the following [Input] fields.
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: The computational and battery load of performing MPC calculations on mobile devices (smartphones) can be significant, leading to poor user experience, rapid battery drain, and limited adoption for everyday transactions.
   - **Background and current situation**: MPC protocols involve complex cryptographic computations (e.g., elliptic curve operations). While manageable on servers, these operations can consume a disproportionate amount of a mobile device's CPU and battery, especially during key generation or signing.
   - **Goals and success criteria**: Limit the battery drain per MPC signing operation to less than 0.5% of a standard smartphone battery (current baseline may be 1-2% or more). Achieve signing times that are comparable to traditional "hot wallet" experiences.
   - **Key constraints and resources**: Constrained by the processing power and thermal limits of mobile devices. Must work within the memory and CPU constraints of iOS and Android platforms without requiring root access or specialized hardware.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: Mobile end-users (prioritize battery life and app responsiveness), mobile application developers (need efficient libraries), MPC protocol designers (must optimize for mobile constraints).
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: Impacts the daily use of the wallet application for all mobile users. For an app with 500,000 mobile users, inefficient resource usage could lead to a 10-20% uninstall rate due to performance issues.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Using more efficient cryptographic curves (e.g., Ed25519). Optimizing code with platform-specific native libraries (e.g., Rust, C++). Lessons learned: Protocol-level optimizations have a much greater impact than code-level micro-optimizations.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Cryptographic computations are CPU-intensive. Assumptions: High battery consumption is a top-three reason for users abandoning a mobile app. Uncertainties: The performance gap between the most and least efficient MPC schemes on mid-range mobile hardware.
