1.  Q: Formulate a structured problem statement regarding the risk of private key theft and compromise in Blockchain MPC wallets.
    A:
    -   **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: Blockchain MPC wallets are vulnerable to novel key-extraction attacks where compromising a single party can lead to the full exfiltration of the private key.
    -   **Background and current situation**: Multi-Party Computation (MPC) wallets are designed to enhance security by distributing parts of the private key among multiple parties. However, recent research indicates that practical attacks exist which can exfiltrate the entire private key even if only one party in the MPC setup is compromised. These vulnerabilities undermine the fundamental security premise of MPC wallets.
    -   **Goals and success criteria**: The primary goal is to achieve private key management that demonstrably prevents full key leakage, even under a scenario where a single party is compromised, aiming for zero single-point-of-failure vulnerabilities. Success would mean eliminating successful key extraction attempts in such scenarios by 100% within 6-12 months.
    -   **Key constraints and resources**: Constraints include the inherent complexity of cryptographic protocols, the need to maintain low latency for user transactions, and limited trust boundaries among the participating parties. Resources are limited by available cryptographic expertise and development budget.
    -   **Stakeholders and roles**: Wallet users face potential loss of billions of dollars in cryptocurrency assets. Wallet providers must ensure the security and integrity of their services. Security auditors are responsible for identifying and validating these protections.
    -   **Time scale and impact scope**: This is an immediate to short-term problem (within 6 months) with a global impact, affecting potentially billions of USD in cryptocurrency assets.
    -   **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Current MPC wallets often utilize threshold signature schemes, but novel attacks have exposed limitations in their resilience to key extraction.
    -   **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Key extraction attacks targeting MPC wallets have been demonstrated. Assumptions: Attackers possess the capability and motivation to corrupt a party. Uncertainties: The full extent of mitigations achievable without significantly impacting usability or performance is yet to be fully determined.

3.  Q: Formulate a structured problem statement on the difficulty of private key recovery and backup mechanisms in hardware and MPC wallets.
    A:
    -   **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: Secure and user-friendly recovery mechanisms for private keys in Blockchain MPC and hardware wallets are lacking, often compromising key security properties or being too complex for users.
    -   **Background and current situation**: Hardware wallets prioritize a unique hardware entity for ownership (unicity) to secure high-value assets, but traditional backups by cloning the root seed or private key compromise this unicity. MPC wallets, while distributing keys, still face challenges in designing robust, user-friendly recovery processes for shared keys without increasing the risk of exposure. The complexity of current recovery methods leads to user errors and potential asset loss.
    -   **Goals and success criteria**: The goal is to develop practical and secure key recovery mechanisms that preserve unicity in hardware wallets and offer robust, user-friendly options for MPC wallets, ensuring recovery success rates above 90% without compromising security. These mechanisms should reduce key leakage risks during recovery to near zero.
    -   **Key constraints and resources**: Recovery mechanisms must not expose private keys during the backup or recovery process. There are limitations in user technical expertise and the need for regulatory compliance for custody. Resources include advanced cryptographic techniques like Symmetric Secret Sharing and Smart Contract-based Registries.
    -   **Stakeholders and roles**: Asset holders require reliable access to their funds even in cases of device loss or personal incapacitation. Wallet manufacturers and key management service providers must offer these secure options.
    -   **Time scale and impact scope**: This is a medium-to-long-term problem (1-2 years) affecting millions of users and institutional actors globally, especially those with high-value digital assets.
    -   **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Proposed solutions include Symmetric Secret Sharing, Smart Contract-based Registries, and Passkey-based recovery mechanisms, with Biometrically Independent Fuzzy Signature (BIFS) being adapted for distributed keys. However, widespread adoption and robust, user-friendly implementations are still evolving.
    -   **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Cloning private keys for backup reduces security. Assumptions: Users require reliable recovery methods that are also easy to use. Uncertainties: The optimal balance between security, user-friendliness, and cost-effectiveness of various recovery schemes remains under active research.

5.  Q: Formulate a structured problem statement on the scalability and performance challenges of MPC protocols in heterogeneous environments for blockchain wallets.
    A:
    -   **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: Blockchain MPC wallets face significant performance bottlenecks and stall times when operating in heterogeneous computing environments due to the synchronous nature of MPC protocols under varied node capabilities.
    -   **Background and current situation**: MPC protocols require multiple, often synchronous, rounds of communication and computation among participants to generate signatures or execute tasks without revealing private inputs. When these computations are distributed across heterogeneous nodes with varying hardware, network speeds, or geographical locations, the overall process slows down to the pace of the slowest participant, leading to increased latency and reduced transaction throughput. This degradation impacts real-time transaction approval and overall user experience.
    -   **Goals and success criteria**: The goal is to optimize MPC performance to support real-time or near real-time transaction throughput with low latency (ideally in the milliseconds range) and manageable resource consumption, even over heterogeneous nodes. Success includes minimizing stall time, maintaining throughput proportional to user demand, and achieving transaction completion times comparable to single-key wallets.
    -   **Key constraints and resources**: Constraints include the inherent synchronous communication requirements of many MPC protocols, the variability of hardware and network conditions across participants, the computational complexity of cryptographic operations, and the need to preserve robust security guarantees. Resources are limited by available computational power and network bandwidth.
    -   **Stakeholders and roles**: Wallet users expect fast and reliable transaction signing. Wallet developers and protocol engineers are responsible for optimizing the efficiency of MPC protocols. Infrastructure providers need to support efficient node operations.
    -   **Time scale and impact scope**: This is a medium-term problem (6-18 months) relevant for MPC wallets serving large user bases globally and managing high volumes of transactions.
    -   **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Studies have identified the fundamental causes of stall time and performance limitations in MPC protocols. Research on optimizing MPC workflows and off-chain computations like MPC+ aim to reduce on-chain load and improve efficiency.
    -   **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Heterogeneity in computing nodes directly causes performance stalls in synchronized MPC protocols. Assumptions: Optimization of MPC workflows and protocol redesign can significantly reduce stall times and improve scalability. Uncertainties: The full extent of achievable performance improvements without compromising the security guarantees of MPC remains to be precisely determined.

11. Q: Formulate a structured problem statement regarding future risks posed by the advent of quantum computing to Blockchain MPC wallets.
    A:
    -   **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: The advent of quantum computing poses a significant future risk to Blockchain MPC wallets by threatening the underlying classical cryptographic schemes, potentially enabling adversaries to break key security and compromise digital assets.
    -   **Background and current situation**: Many commonly used cryptographic algorithms, such as Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA) and RSA, which underpin the security of current MPC wallets and blockchain transactions, are known to be vulnerable to quantum attacks like Shor's algorithm. The rapid progress in quantum technologies means that existing blockchain infrastructures, including MPC wallets, face a looming threat to their long-term security and integrity.
    -   **Goals and success criteria**: The goal is to develop and implement quantum-resistant cryptographic protocols (Post-Quantum Cryptography - PQC) and effective transition strategies to ensure the security of MPC wallets against future quantum threats. Success criteria include achieving zero quantum-related breaches and demonstrating seamless user transition to PQC-enabled wallets within a 1-3 year timeframe.
    -   **Key constraints and resources**: Constraints involve the technical complexity of developing and integrating new PQC algorithms, ensuring backward and forward compatibility with existing blockchain networks and MPC protocols, achieving regulatory acceptance for new cryptographic standards, and addressing potential computational performance impacts of PQC. Resources include ongoing research in PQC and available cryptographic development expertise.
    -   **Stakeholders and roles**: Wallet developers and cryptographers are responsible for researching, designing, and implementing quantum-resistant solutions. Blockchain users, including individual and institutional asset holders, require assurance of long-term asset security. Regulators must adapt security standards to account for quantum threats.
    -   **Time scale and impact scope**: This is a near-to-medium-term risk (1-3 years) with a global impact, potentially affecting millions of blockchain users and the security of all digital assets stored in MPC wallets.
    -   **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Initial research is ongoing in lattice-based PQC, hybrid cryptographic models that combine classical and quantum-resistant algorithms, and frameworks for migration. Experimental PQC wallet prototypes are also being developed.
    -   **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Quantum algorithms like Shor's can theoretically break current public-key cryptography used in blockchain. Assumptions: Practical quantum computers capable of breaking current cryptography will emerge within the next decade. Uncertainties: The precise timeline for large-scale quantum computer development, the performance overhead of PQC in real-world MPC implementations, and the pace of regulatory adaptation to these new threats.
