# Policy-Driven Approval Engine Design for Institutional MPC Wallets

**Status**: Draft  
**Owner**: Risk Management Team

## Problem Statement

1. **[CRITICAL]** Q: Institutional MPC wallet deployments require fine-grained, policy-driven transaction approvals (per asset, desk, risk score) but face critical design challenges: poorly tuned rules create alert fatigue and rubber-stamping, while overly strict policies drive users to bypass MPC controls through shadow workflows, undermining the security model and creating operational chaos. Formulate a structured problem statement using the following [Input] fields.
   
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: 
     Organizations deploying MPC wallets for institutional custody ($10M-$1B+ assets) must design policy and approval engines that meaningfully reduce fraud and operational risk without causing alert fatigue, mechanical approval clicking, or parallel shadow workflows bypassing MPC controls. Current policy engines map transaction attributes (amount, asset, counterparty, initiator, time, historical behavior) to required quorums and rate limits, but experience from financial systems shows poorly tuned rules and noisy alerts lead to 60-80% false positives, causing approvers to rubber-stamp prompts or move urgent flows off the controlled system. This affects 50+ institutional custody providers, exchanges serving 100M+ users, and enterprise treasury operations managing billions in digital assets, creating tension between strong controls and operational efficiency.
   
   - **Background and current situation**: 
     Institutional MPC custody requires transaction approval policies balancing security and operational throughput [Report: Fireblocks, 2024]. Policy engines like Fireblocks automate governance by defining user and transaction rules: amount thresholds (e.g., >$100K requires 3-of-5 quorum), asset-based policies (stablecoins vs volatile tokens), desk/counterparty whitelists, velocity limits (max $X per hour/day), and risk scoring based on historical behavior [Platform: Fireblocks, 2024]. However, legacy financial systems demonstrate approval workflow pathologies: (1) Alert fatigue: excessive false positives (60-80% in AML systems) cause approvers to click through mechanically without review [Blog: Lucinity, 2024]; (2) Rubber-stamping: when 90%+ alerts prove benign, approvers assume next alert is also benign, defeating security purpose [Blog: Tookitaki, 2024]; (3) Shadow workflows: overly strict policies drive users to circumvent controls through exception processes, side channels, or moving funds to unmonitored systems [implied from workflow bypass patterns]; (4) Operational paralysis: poorly calibrated policies block legitimate high-value/urgent flows, causing business disruption and customer dissatisfaction [Source: Supplementary]. MPC-specific challenges: transaction approvals cannot detect malicious intent, only policy violations—a valid-looking transaction to attacker-controlled address passes policy checks unless counterparty is blacklisted [Blog: Chainalysis, 2024]. Institutional clients expect both strong controls and smooth operations: median approval time per risk band must stay within SLAs (e.g., low-risk <5min, medium-risk <30min, high-risk <2h), while false-positive block rate stays below 1-3% of legitimate attempts [Source: Supplementary]. Organizations struggle with limited UX budget for complex approval interfaces, diverse risk appetites across clients and desks, and difficulty running controlled experiments on real high-value flows without exposing clients to friction or risk [Source: Supplementary].
   
   - **Goals and success criteria**: 
     False-positive rate: Reduce legitimate transaction blocks from est. 60-80% current false positives in financial systems → <5% (min) / <3% (target) / <1% (ideal) by Q4 2025 through policy calibration and risk scoring improvements. Approval efficiency: High-risk escalations maintain median approval time within SLAs: <5min (low-risk, target: <2min), <30min (medium-risk, target: <15min), <2h (high-risk, target: <1h) measured from alert to decision. Workflow adherence: ≥95% (min) / ≥98% (target) / ≥99% (ideal) of operational volume remains within policy-driven MPC flows rather than exception paths or shadow workflows by Q4 2025. Fraud reduction: Demonstrate measurable risk reduction → block ≥80% (min) / ≥90% (target) / ≥95% (ideal) of anomalous high-risk transactions flagged by retrospective analysis. Policy auditability: 100% of policy rules expressible in auditor-readable format with test coverage demonstrating enforcement by Q3 2025 (current: complex rule interactions difficult to audit). User satisfaction: Approver NPS ≥40 (min) / ≥60 (target) / ≥80 (ideal) indicating policies perceived as helpful rather than obstructive (current: unknown, likely negative due to alert fatigue).
   
   - **Key constraints and resources**: 
     Timeline: Q1 2025 - Q4 2025 (9 months policy design + implementation + calibration); Budget: $300K-$600K for risk analytics platform, policy engine development, user research, and pilot programs; Team: 2-3 risk/compliance specialists (policy definition), 2-3 backend engineers (enforcement implementation), 1-2 UX designers (approval interfaces), 1 data analyst (false-positive tracking); Technology: Integration with MPC signing infrastructure (must enforce policies before signature generation), real-time transaction monitoring (anomaly detection, velocity tracking), historical behavior analytics (build user/counterparty risk profiles), audit trail systems (immutable policy enforcement logs for SOC 2/regulatory review); Policy complexity: Must balance granularity (per-asset, per-desk rules) with maintainability (avoid 100+ brittle rules requiring constant tuning); Experimentation limits: Cannot run A/B tests on real high-value flows due to fraud risk; must use historical data replay and limited pilot programs; User adoption: Policies must accommodate diverse risk appetites across institutional clients (conservative hedge funds vs aggressive trading desks); Regulatory: Must demonstrate policy effectiveness to auditors without compromising operational efficiency.
   
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: 
     Custody Operations Teams (10-50 operators per large provider, execute approvals daily, need clear escalation criteria and reasonable workload, constraint: cognitive overload above ~20 decisions/hour or >10% false-positive rate); Risk and Compliance Teams (2-5 per organization, define policy templates and thresholds, need evidence of fraud reduction for audit reports, constraint: must balance security vs operational efficiency without direct visibility into approver behavior); Security and MPC Engineers (3-5 per organization, implement enforcement mechanisms and integrate with signing flows, constraint: policy checks cannot add >200ms latency to transaction path); Product Managers (1-2 per custody offering, balance UX and safety for competitive differentiation, constraint: policy friction directly affects customer satisfaction and churn); Auditors (external SOC 2/regulatory reviewers, need policy documentation and test evidence, constraint: cannot accept opaque "AI decides" policies without explainability); Institutional Account Owners (50+ clients managing $10M-$1B+ each, bear ultimate loss and SLA impact, need transparent policy logic and override capabilities for legitimate urgent flows, constraint: cannot tolerate multi-hour approval delays during market volatility); Approvers (100+ individuals across client organizations, make final authorization decisions, need concise risk summaries and confidence in alert quality, constraint: limited time per decision and low tolerance for false alarms).

Key lesson: No silver bullet; optimal approach combines static thresholds (simple, auditable) with dynamic risk scoring (reduces false positives) and continuous calibration (adapts to evolving patterns), but requires significant investment in data analytics and user research to avoid approval workflow pathologies observed in legacy financial systems.

## Glossary

- **Alert fatigue**: Cognitive overload condition where excessive false-positive alerts cause reviewers to click through mechanically without careful evaluation, undermining security purpose.
- **AML (Anti-Money Laundering)**: Regulatory compliance area requiring transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting; AML systems typically exhibit 60-80% false-positive rates.
- **Approval quorum**: Number of authorized parties required to approve a transaction; MPC policies often specify dynamic quorums (2-of-3 for <$10K, 3-of-5 for >$100K).
- **Audit trail**: Immutable log of policy enforcement decisions (transaction attributes, policy evaluation, approval/rejection outcome, approver identities) required for SOC 2 and regulatory compliance.
- **Counterparty whitelist**: List of pre-approved blockchain addresses or entities allowed to receive funds; reduces false positives but requires maintenance as new legitimate counterparties emerge.
- **Desk**: Organizational unit within institutional client (trading desk, treasury desk, operations desk); policies often vary by desk based on risk appetite and transaction patterns.
- **False positive**: Legitimate transaction incorrectly flagged as high-risk, requiring manual review; high false-positive rates (>10%) cause alert fatigue and rubber-stamping.
- **Policy engine**: Software system defining and enforcing transaction authorization rules based on attributes (amount, asset, counterparty, historical behavior); examples: Fireblocks Governance and Policy Engine.
- **Risk scoring**: Algorithmic assignment of risk level (low/medium/high) to transactions based on attributes and historical patterns; aims to reduce false positives vs static thresholds.
- **Rubber-stamping**: Behavior where approvers mechanically authorize all transactions without review due to high false-positive rates, defeating security controls.
- **Shadow workflow**: Informal processes bypassing official controls (e.g., moving funds through unmonitored systems or using undocumented override mechanisms) due to policy friction.
- **SLA (Service Level Agreement)**: Contractual commitment to performance metrics; approval SLAs typically specify maximum time to decision (e.g., <30min for medium-risk transactions).
- **Velocity limit**: Policy restricting transaction frequency or volume over time period (e.g., max $1M per day per desk) to prevent rapid fund drainage.
- **Whitelist**: Approved list (counterparties, assets, operators) subject to reduced scrutiny; reduces false positives but creates security gap if whitelist is compromised or stale.
