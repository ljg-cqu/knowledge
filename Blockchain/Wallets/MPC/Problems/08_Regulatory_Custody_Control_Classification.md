Traditional FinCEN 2019 guidance defined control as "total independent control" (unilateral access to move funds), under which many MPC wallets qualified as non-custodial, but MTMA Article XIII (adopted by California, Minnesota, North Dakota, with Florida, Rhode Island moving similarly) broadened definition to "power to execute unilaterally, or prevent indefinitely, a virtual currency transaction"—a functional control test that may capture MPC providers. FinCEN 2019 Guidance established four-factor test for money transmitter classification: (1) who owns value; (2) where value stored; (3) whether owner interacts directly with payment system; (4) whether intermediary has "total independent control" over value [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. Under 2019 guidance, unhosted multi-sig wallets not money transmitters if: (1) value owned and stored by user; (2) user initiates transactions with partial credentials; (3) co-signing party cannot unilaterally access or move funds [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. Many MPC providers relied on this interpretation (user initiates, provider co-signs, neither has complete key = non-custodial) to avoid money transmitter classification [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. However, 2020-2021 Conference of State Bank Supervisors (CSBS) developed Money Transmission Modernization Act (MTMA) as uniform framework states can voluntarily adopt, with Article XIII bringing virtual currency within money transmission regulation [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. MTMA Article XIII defines "control of virtual currency" as "power to execute unilaterally, or prevent indefinitely, a virtual currency transaction"—significantly broader than FinCEN's "total independent control" [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. California adopted MTMA language in Digital Financial Assets Law (DFAL), Minnesota formally enacted Article XIII, North Dakota embraced MTMA approach [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. Florida defines money transmitter including any intermediary with ability to unilaterally initiate or indefinitely block transaction; Rhode Island applies MTMA-style control test [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. Critical distinction: if MPC provider can unilaterally approve or block transactions without user consent (even lacking full key), may be deemed having "control" under MTMA standard, triggering money transmitter requirements [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. Current state: fragmented landscape with some states adopting MTMA, others maintaining pre-MTMA definitions, creating compliance burden for providers operating multi-state (must assess 50 jurisdictions independently) [Blog: S-Horowitz, 2025]. ## Glossary

- **Bank Secrecy Act (BSA)**: 1970 U.S. law requiring financial institutions to maintain records and report certain transactions to prevent financial crimes; establishes money transmitter requirements.
- **Conference of State Bank Supervisors (CSBS)**: National organization of state financial regulators that developed MTMA to harmonize money transmission frameworks across states.
- **Control**: Regulatory concept determining money transmitter classification; FinCEN 2019 defined as "total independent control" (unilateral access), MTMA broadened to "power to execute or prevent indefinitely" (functional control).
- **Custodial vs. Non-custodial**: Custodial services hold and manage user private keys; non-custodial services give users key control. MPC wallets blur this distinction (distributed control, no complete key).
- **FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network)**: U.S. Treasury bureau enforcing anti-money laundering (AML) regulations under Bank Secrecy Act, responsible for federal money transmitter oversight.
- **Money Services Business (MSB)**: FinCEN registration category including money transmitters; requires AML compliance, suspicious activity reporting, recordkeeping.
- **Money Transmission Modernization Act (MTMA)**: Uniform legal framework developed by CSBS (2020-2021) for states to voluntarily adopt; Article XIII addresses virtual currency with broadened control definition.
- **Money transmitter**: Entity engaged in business of transmitting currency, funds, or "other value that substitutes for currency"; requires federal MSB registration + state licensing.
- **No-action letter**: Regulatory guidance confirming specific business activities won't trigger enforcement action; non-binding but provides operational comfort; costly to obtain ($50K-$200K legal fees per state).
- **Total independent control**: FinCEN 2019 standard requiring unilateral ability to access, move, or manage value without user authorization to classify as money transmitter.
