1. Q: Analyze challenges in achieving secure and user-friendly decentralized custody blockchain wallets. Formulate a structured problem statement using the following fields.
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: Decentralized custody blockchain wallets struggle to deliver robust security while maintaining user-friendly interfaces, often presenting a trade-off that inhibits widespread adoption and exposes users to key management complexities.
   - **Background and current situation**: Current decentralized wallets, including multi-party computation (MPC) schemes and multi-signature setups, compel users to manage complex private keys, leading to potential loss or mishandling of digital assets. This design often sacrifices usability for security, while traditional custodial wallets centralize risk but offer simpler user experiences. Research is exploring solutions like MFKDF decentralized wallets and MPC-TSS wallets, but operational complexity and scalability challenges persist.
   - **Goals and success criteria**: The objective is to develop decentralized wallets that achieve security levels comparable to institutional custody, such as multi-layer key protection, while improving user adoption rates by at least 50% due to enhanced usability within 12 months. This includes reducing key loss incidents to below a 1% threshold for legitimate users.
   - **Key constraints and resources**: Constraints include the technological limitations of MPC and cryptographic protocols, existing blockchain frameworks, and the need to comply with evolving regulations. Available resources involve advances in cryptographic techniques, such as threshold signatures and distributed key generation, and the inherent transparency of blockchain technology.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: Key stakeholders include end-users who desire secure and accessible wallets, custodians managing digital assets, developers designing wallet architectures, and regulators enforcing compliance and security standards.
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: This problem has a medium-term impact (6-12 months) across millions of retail and institutional users worldwide, influencing the secure management of multi-chain assets valued in billions of USD.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Approaches like MFKDF decentralized wallets have been prototyped, offering trustless self-custody over existing solutions. MPC-TSS wallets aim to distribute private key control to enhance security, but face integration and latency challenges in real-world deployments. Biometrics are also being explored to simplify key management, but face compatibility issues.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Threshold signatures offer demonstrated security gains by eliminating single points of failure. Assumptions: Users are willing to adopt more complex yet secure wallet solutions if usability is sufficiently high. Uncertainties: The evolution of new attack vectors and the widespread adoption rates of advanced decentralized wallet architectures remain unpredictable.

4. Q: Formulate a structured problem statement on the challenge of key management and recovery in blockchain custody wallets.
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: Loss or compromise of cryptographic keys in blockchain custody wallets often results in irreversible asset loss or security breaches, compounded by limitations in current recovery and backup mechanisms that struggle to balance security, usability, and unicity.
   - **Background and current situation**: Key management techniques in wallets range from simple mnemonic seed phrases to more advanced biometric-based schemes. Hardware wallets offer strong security through "unicity" (unique key ownership tied to a device) but face a trade-off where creating a backup for recovery compromises this unicity. This means traditional backups create a security vulnerability, while the absence of recovery mechanisms can lead to permanent asset loss if the device is lost or damaged.
   - **Goals and success criteria**: The objective is to create key management and backup systems that preserve security unicity while allowing effective recovery, aiming to reduce asset loss incidents due to key issues by over 70% within one year. This also includes minimizing key leakage risks and facilitating cryptocurrency inheritance.
   - **Key constraints and resources**: Constraints include cryptographic algorithm compatibility with existing blockchain signature schemes (e.g., ECDSA, Schnorr Signatures), hardware limitations for secure recovery, diverse user education levels, and the need to comply with legal and regulatory frameworks. Resources involve advancements in biometric authentication, distributed key architectures, and smart contract-based recovery solutions.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: Stakeholders include individual wallet users who risk losing their assets, wallet developers tasked with implementing secure and user-friendly systems, custodians seeking to offer reliable recovery services, and regulators concerned with consumer protection.
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: This problem has a medium-term impact (up to 12 months), affecting millions of users worldwide who hold billions of USD in digital assets. The long-term implications involve establishing secure inheritance mechanisms for digital assets.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Biometrically Independent Fuzzy Signature (BIFS) schemes show promise by using biometric information as a distributed key, with extensions to support common blockchain signature algorithms. Other approaches include symmetric secret sharing, visual secret sharing schemes for key recovery, passkey-based recovery mechanisms, and smart contract-based recovery functionalities. However, many still face challenges in widespread adoption and balancing security with convenience.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Loss of a private key or seed phrase typically results in irreversible asset loss. Assumptions: Biometric authentication and distributed key architectures can significantly enhance both security and usability for key management and recovery. Uncertainties: The actual user adoption rates for novel hardware wallet recovery solutions and the long-term effectiveness of these methods against evolving threats remain to be seen.

6. Q: Formulate a structured problem statement regarding scalability and performance constraints in blockchain custody wallet architectures.
   A:
   - **Brief description of the problem to be analyzed**: Blockchain custody wallets face significant scalability and performance constraints, primarily due to the rapid growth of blockchain datasets and the proliferation of low-value Unspent Transaction Outputs (UTXOs), which impede transaction throughput, increase latency, and degrade overall asset management efficiency.
   - **Background and current situation**: Blockchain systems, particularly those like Bitcoin, have inherent limitations in transaction processing rates (e.g., ~4-7 transactions per second) and suffer from high confirmation latencies due to block creation intervals. As blockchain usage increases, the Unspent Transaction Output (UTXO) dataset grows, with a significant number of low-value UTXOs consuming main dataset space and affecting wallet performance and system efficiency. Multi-signature wallets, while enhancing security, can introduce performance overhead.
   - **Goals and success criteria**: The goal is to improve transaction throughput by at least 30%, reduce latency to sub-second or near real-time levels for transaction visibility, and optimize asset management efficiency by mitigating UTXO bloat. This should enhance wallet responsiveness and reduce associated transaction fees, all within a 12-month horizon, targeting millions of users globally.
   - **Key constraints and resources**: Technical constraints include immutable blockchain protocol limits, CPU and communication overhead from consensus algorithms, network propagation delays, and the computational cost of cryptographic operations, as well as the need for backward compatibility. Resources involve hardware capabilities, advanced consensus protocol optimizations, sophisticated UTXO selection algorithms like Multidimensional Space Simulated Annealing (MSA), and off-chain scaling solutions.
   - **Stakeholders and roles**: Key stakeholders include wallet users demanding swift and reliable transaction execution, wallet service providers responsible for ensuring scalability and performance, blockchain network operators maintaining protocol and consensus, and developers implementing wallet and infrastructure optimizations.
   - **Time scale and impact scope**: This problem has a medium-term impact (6-18 months), affecting millions of global users and necessitating the efficient management of high volumes of digital asset transactions with significant financial implications.
   - **Historical attempts and existing solutions (if any)**: Solutions like the Space-Efficient Digital Wallet (SEDW) service, employing the Multidimensional Space Simulated Annealing (MSA) algorithm, have been proposed to optimize UTXO selection and alleviate dataset expansion. Pipelined consensus protocols and multi-signature wallets optimized with threshold signatures also aim to improve throughput and reduce latency, but widespread adoption and integration are still evolving.
   - **Known facts, assumptions, and uncertainties**: Facts: Blockchain scalability inherently limits transaction throughput and latency, and an increasing UTXO dataset size degrades wallet performance. Assumptions: Incremental protocol and algorithmic optimizations can substantially improve scalability and efficiency. Uncertainties: Future blockchain protocol evolutions, adoption rates of advanced wallet architectures, dynamic network conditions, and regulatory changes affecting technical implementations remain uncertain.
