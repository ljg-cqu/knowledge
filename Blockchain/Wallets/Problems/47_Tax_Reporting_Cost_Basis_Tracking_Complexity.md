## Glossary

- **Form 1099-DA (Digital Asset Proceeds)**: IRS tax form (effective tax year 2025) requiring centralized exchanges to report gross proceeds from cryptocurrency sales, similar to Form 1099-B for stocks. Cost basis reporting NOT required until 2026 and only for same-platform transactions (purchased and sold on exchange, held entire time).
- **Cost Basis**: Original acquisition cost of asset used to calculate capital gains/losses upon sale. Crypto challenges: hard forks (allocation unclear), airdrops (free tokens â€” $0 basis or FMV at receipt?), staking rewards, DeFi yield, gas fees (add to basis or separate expense?). Essential for accurate tax calculation.
- **FIFO (First In, First Out)**: Accounting method assuming earliest purchased assets sold first. Conservative for appreciating assets (higher cost basis â†’ lower capital gain â†’ lower tax). IRS allows FIFO, LIFO, or specific identification for crypto; most software defaults to FIFO.
- **LIFO (Last In, First Out)**: Accounting method assuming most recently purchased assets sold first. Aggressive for appreciating assets (lower cost basis â†’ higher capital gain â†’ higher tax, but deferral benefit). Allowed by IRS but less common for crypto.
- **Specific Identification**: Accounting method allowing taxpayer to designate exact units sold (by transaction date, wallet address, exchange). Optimal for tax loss harvesting (sell high-basis units to minimize gain or maximize loss). Requires contemporaneous records; challenging for fungible crypto.
- **CARF (Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework)**: OECD international standard requiring exchanges to report user transactions to tax authorities across 100+ participating countries (similar to CRS for bank accounts). EU implements via DAC8 (effective 2026-2027). US participation requires Congressional authorization (timeline uncertain).
- **DAC8 (Directive on Administrative Cooperation)**: EU implementation of OECD CARF, requiring crypto exchanges operating in EU to report user transactions to member state tax authorities starting 2026-2027. Applies to 27 EU countries; data sharing via Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI).
- **Taxable Event**: Transaction triggering tax obligation. Crypto examples: selling crypto for fiat (capital gain/loss), trading one crypto for another (capital gain/loss on disposed asset), using crypto to purchase goods/services (capital gain/loss at FMV), receiving staking rewards or airdrops (ordinary income at FMV receipt). NOT taxable: transferring between own wallets (no disposition), gifting (recipient assumes donor's cost basis; donor may owe gift tax if >$18K/recipient 2024 limit).
- **Fair Market Value (FMV)**: Price asset would trade for on open market. Crypto challenge: 1,000+ tokens traded on 100+ exchanges with price discrepancies (which exchange's price? Volume-weighted average?). IRS requires reasonable method consistently applied.
- **Wash Sale Rule**: Tax provision disallowing loss deduction if substantially identical security purchased within 30 days before/after sale (prevents tax loss harvesting abuse). Currently does NOT apply to crypto (classified as property, not security), enabling aggressive tax loss harvesting, but IRS may extend wash sale rule via regulation or legislation (timeline uncertain).
- **Tax Loss Harvesting**: Strategy of selling depreciated assets to realize capital losses offsetting capital gains, reducing tax liability. Highly effective for crypto (no wash sale rule; can immediately repurchase). Optimization algorithms in tax software (TaxBit, CoinTracker) identify optimal selling opportunities.
- **Impermanent Loss**: DeFi concept where liquidity provider's token value diverges from simply holding tokens due to automated market maker price rebalancing. Tax treatment unclear: is rebalancing taxable trade? Realized only upon withdrawal? IRS guidance pending; affects Uniswap, Curve, Balancer users.
